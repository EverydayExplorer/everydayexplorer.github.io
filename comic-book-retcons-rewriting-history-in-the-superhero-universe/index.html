Comic Book Retcons: Rewriting History in the Superhero Universe


Comic books have been around for several decades, and there have been countless characters, storylines, and events that have defined the superhero universe. However, with so much content produced over the years, it is inevitable that there will be inconsistencies, plot holes, and other issues that require a solution. Enter the comic book retcon.

A retcon, or ‘retroactive continuity,’ is when writers or editors alter previously established events or character details to better fit within the current narrative or to fix continuity errors. Retcons can be controversial among fans, as they can alter beloved storylines or characters, but they are often necessary to keep the universe cohesive and consistent.

One of the most famous examples of a retcon is the resurrection of Jason Todd, the second Robin. In 1988, DC Comics held a phone poll asking readers whether they wanted Todd to live or die after being beaten by the Joker in the ‘Death in the Family’ storyline. The readers voted for Todd to die, and he was officially declared deceased. However, in 2005, he was brought back to life and revealed to have been resurrected by Ra’s al Ghul. This retcon allowed Todd to become the antihero Red Hood and added complexity to the Batman mythos.

Another notable retcon in the Marvel universe is the revelation that Peter Parker, aka Spider-Man, was not the only person to be bitten by the radioactive spider that gave him his powers. In the ‘Spider-Verse’ storyline, it is revealed that there are countless versions of Spider-Man from various alternate universes. This retcon allowed for the introduction of new and diverse Spider-People, such as Spider-Gwen and Spider-Man Noir, and expanded the Spider-Man mythos.

Retcons can also be used to address problematic or outdated elements in comic book history. For example, in the original X-Men comics, Jean Grey - one of the founding members of the team - is killed and later resurrected as the villainous Phoenix. However, in 2017, Marvel retconned Jean’s storyline to remove the idea that she was always destined to become a villain. This retcon allowed Jean to have agency over her actions and removed the problematic trope of a female character being corrupted by power.

While retcons may not always be popular with fans, they are a necessary tool for writers and editors to keep the superhero universe coherent and interesting. And with so much history to draw from, there will always be new retcons to explore and controversy to discuss. As long as they are used thoughtfully and with care, retcons can add depth, complexity, and diversity to the superhero genre.
