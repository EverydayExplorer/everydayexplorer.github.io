The Science behind Wine Faults: Recognizing and Understanding Common Flaws


Welcome to our blog, where we delve into the fascinating world of wine faults. Have you ever enjoyed a glass of wine only to find that it tastes off somehow? Maybe it had an unpleasant smell or an unusual taste that made it undrinkable. If you’ve experienced this, fear not, as today we are going to explore the science behind wine faults, helping you recognize and understand some of the most common flaws that can occur in this beloved beverage.

Wine faults can arise due to a variety of factors, including grape quality, winemaking practices, storage conditions, and even the natural aging process. These faults can significantly impact the overall quality and enjoyment of the wine, which is why it’s essential to be able to recognize and understand them.

One of the most common wine faults is cork taint, also known as ‘corked’ wine. This flaw occurs when the wine comes into contact with a cork that has been contaminated by a chemical compound called 2,4,6-trichloroanisole (TCA). When a wine is affected by cork taint, it will exhibit a distinct musty or moldy aroma, and the flavors will be muted or completely altered. It’s estimated that cork taint affects around 5-10% of all wines sealed with natural corks.

Another common wine fault is oxidation, which happens when the wine is overexposed to oxygen. This can occur during winemaking, bottling, or improper storage. Oxidation leads to a loss of freshness and vibrant flavors, resulting in a wine that tastes flat, dull, or even like vinegar. The discoloration of the wine, turning it brownish, can also be a visible sign of oxidation.

One more noteworthy fault is volatile acidity, often referred to as ‘VA’ in the wine world. This is caused by the presence of acetic acid, which is produced by bacteria during the winemaking process. In small amounts, volatile acidity can contribute to the complexity and flavor profile of the wine. However, when present in excessive levels, it becomes a flaw, imparting a vinegar-like aroma and taste that overwhelms the other characteristics of the wine.

A fault that is particularly frustrating for wine producers is Brettanomyces contamination. Brettanomyces, commonly known as ‘Brett,’ is a yeast strain that can thrive in winery environments. While some wines benefit from its presence, adding complexity and depth, in excess, it can lead to off-putting aromas described as ‘barnyard,’ ‘band-aid,’ or ‘sweaty horse.’ The perception of Brettanomyces can vary between individuals, but generally, it is considered a fault that takes away from the desired characteristics of the wine.

Lastly, we have a fault called reduction, which occurs when the wine is exposed to too little oxygen during the winemaking process. This can result in a wine with sulfurous, rotten egg-like aromas. Reduction is often associated with young wines or wines that have been bottled with excessive amounts of sulfur dioxide. While some wines may need time to ‘breathe’ and reduce these aromas, an excessive reduction can render the wine unpalatable.

Recognizing and understanding wine faults is crucial for both wine enthusiasts and industry professionals alike. By familiarizing yourself with the common flaws we’ve discussed today, you’ll be better equipped to identify them and make informed decisions when purchasing or serving wine. Remember, faults are not always indicative of poor winemaking but rather natural occurrences in the complex world of wine.
