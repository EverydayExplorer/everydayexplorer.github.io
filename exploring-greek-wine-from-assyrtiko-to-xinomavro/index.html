Exploring Greek Wine: From Assyrtiko to Xinomavro


When it comes to wine, Greece might not be the first country that comes to mind. However, Greece has a long and rich history of winemaking, dating back thousands of years. Even though Greek wines are not as well-known as French or Italian wines, they are definitely worth exploring. From the crisp and refreshing Assyrtiko to the bold and earthy Xinomavro, Greek wines have unique characteristics that set them apart from the rest.

One of the most popular Greek grape varieties is Assyrtiko. Grown primarily in Santorini, this white grape produces wines with high acidity and vibrant citrus flavors. Assyrtiko wines are often compared to Sauvignon Blanc or Chenin Blanc, but what makes them truly unique is the volcanic soil on which the vines grow. The volcanic soil imparts mineral notes and a distinct saline quality to the wines, giving them a refreshing and crisp character.

Another Greek grape variety worth exploring is Xinomavro. Native to the region of Macedonia, Xinomavro produces red wines that are bold, tannic, and age-worthy. Xinomavro is often described as Greece’s answer to Nebbiolo, the grape variety used in making Barolo and Barbaresco wines in Italy. Xinomavro wines are known for their complex aromas of ripe red fruits, spices, and earthy notes. With their firm tannins and high acidity, Xinomavro wines can age beautifully for many years, developing even more complexity and depth.

Apart from Assyrtiko and Xinomavro, Greece has a wide variety of other indigenous grape varieties that are worth exploring. Malagousia, for example, is a white grape that was almost extinct until it was rediscovered in the 1970s. Today, it has made a comeback and is known for producing aromatic and floral white wines.

Moschofilero, another Greek white grape, is grown primarily in the region of Mantinia. This grape variety produces wines with bright acidity, vibrant citrus flavors, and floral aromas. It is often compared to Gewürztraminer due to its aromatic profile.

When it comes to red wines, Agiorgitiko is a grape variety worth mentioning. Grown mainly in the region of Nemea in the Peloponnese, Agiorgitiko produces medium-bodied red wines with flavors of red fruits, spices, and a hint of sweetness. These wines are versatile and can pair well with a wide variety of dishes.

Exploring Greek wines not only allows you to discover unique grape varieties, but it also gives you the opportunity to learn about Greece’s rich winemaking history. Wine has been produced in Greece for thousands of years, and the techniques and traditions have been passed down through generations. Many wineries in Greece embrace both traditional and modern winemaking methods, resulting in wines that reflect the unique terroir of the region.

When it comes to pairing Greek wines with food, the possibilities are endless. Assyrtiko, with its high acidity, pairs well with seafood, grilled vegetables, and Greek salads. Xinomavro, with its bold and tannic character, pairs well with rich and flavorful dishes such as roasted lamb, stews, and hard cheeses.

In recent years, Greek wines have gained international recognition and are no longer a hidden gem. Winemakers in Greece have focused on quality, improving their techniques, and showcasing the unique characteristics of their grape varieties. As a result, Greek wines have gained a reputation for being both exciting and high quality.

If you are a wine lover looking to explore new and exciting flavors, do not overlook Greek wines. From the vibrant Assyrtiko to the bold Xinomavro, there is a Greek wine for every palate. So take a step off the beaten path and dive into the world of Greek wine – you might just discover your new favorite. Cheers!
