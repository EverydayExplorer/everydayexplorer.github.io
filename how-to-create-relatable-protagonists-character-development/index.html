How to Create Relatable Protagonists: Character Development


Creating relatable protagonists is vital for the success of any story. A protagonist is the main character who drives the story forward. It is essential to create a protagonist that is relatable because they are the character that the reader will follow throughout the story. If the reader cannot connect with the protagonist, they will not be invested in the story. Therefore, creating a relatable protagonist is crucial to keep the reader engaged.

Character development is the process of creating a well-rounded character. It involves adding depth, backstory, and motivation to the character. A well-developed character is one that feels real and human. A relatable protagonist should be complex, flawed, and have relatable qualities. The character should have strengths, weaknesses, and fears.

The following are some tips that will help writers create relatable protagonists:

1. Give the protagonist a goal or desire.

The protagonist should have a clear goal or desire that drives the story forward. The goal or desire should be relatable to the reader. This will help the reader connect with the protagonist and understand their motivations. The goal or desire should be something that the reader can empathize with.

2. Give the protagonist flaws.

A perfect protagonist who always makes the right decisions and never makes mistakes can be boring and unrelatable. Giving the protagonist flaws will make them more relatable. Flaws can be physical, emotional, or psychological. The flaws should be realistic and should add depth to the character.

3. Give the protagonist a backstory.

The protagonist’s backstory is important as it helps the reader understand the character’s motivations and personality. The backstory should be realistic and should add depth to the character. It should include details about the character’s childhood, family, and past experiences.

4. Give the protagonist a relatable personality.

The protagonist’s personality should be relatable and realistic. The character’s personality should be consistent throughout the story. The protagonist’s personality should also change as a result of their experiences and growth throughout the story.

5. Give the protagonist a relatable conflict.

The protagonist’s conflict should be something that the reader can relate to. It could be a personal conflict such as a relationship issue, or it could be a larger conflict such as a societal issue. The conflict should be something that the reader cares about and wants to see resolved.

6. Give the protagonist relatable flaws.

The protagonist’s flaws should be relatable. This means that they should be something that the reader can understand and relate to. For example, the protagonist could be stubborn, impulsive, or have a short temper.

7. Give the protagonist strengths.

The protagonist should have strengths that make them likable and admirable. The strengths should be something that the reader can aspire to. For example, the protagonist could be brave, intelligent, or compassionate.

8. Give the protagonist a relatable support system.

The protagonist should have a support system that is realistic and relatable. This could be a friend, family member, or mentor. The support system should help the protagonist grow and overcome challenges.

9. Ensure that the protagonist’s actions are realistic.

The protagonist’s actions should be realistic and consistent with their personality and motivation. The actions should also be consistent with the setting and time period.

In conclusion, creating a relatable protagonist requires a well-developed character with flaws, strengths, and a relatable goal or conflict. It is essential to spend time on character development to create a relatable protagonist that the reader will care about. By following these tips, writers can create protagonists that are realistic and engaging, making for a captivating story that readers won’t be able to put down.
