Writing Dialogue: Tips for Creating Authentic Conversations


Good dialogue is a key element of any great story. It can bring characters to life, deepen our understanding of them, and keep readers engaged. However, writing dialogue that feels authentic and believable can be challenging. In this post, we’ll explore some tips to help you create great dialogue that rings true for readers.

1. Listen to real conversations

One of the best ways to improve your dialogue is to pay attention to real-life conversations. Listen to how people talk, what vocabulary and phrases they use, and how they express themselves. This can help you write dialogue that feels natural and believable.

2. Use contractions

Using contractions in your dialogue is a great way to make it more authentic. People don’t typically speak in complete sentences, and they often use contractions when they talk. Using contractions can also help you establish a character’s voice and personality. For example, a character who speaks formally might avoid contractions, while a more laid-back character might use them frequently.

3. Show, don’t tell

One of the biggest mistakes writers make when writing dialogue is using it to convey information. Instead of telling readers what’s happening, show them through the characters’ conversations. For example, instead of having a character say ‘I’m really angry with you,’ show their anger through their tone of voice and word choice.

4. Avoid exposition

Exposition is information that the reader needs to know in order to understand the story, but it’s often delivered in a way that feels clunky and unnatural. Avoid delivering exposition through dialogue whenever possible. Instead, find other ways to convey this information, such as through narrative description or action.

5. Use subtext

Subtext is the underlying meaning behind what a character says. It’s what they really mean when they’re not saying it outright. Adding subtext to your dialogue can create tension and conflict, and it can also make your characters more interesting. For example, a character might say ‘I’m fine,’ but their tone of voice and body language might suggest otherwise.

6. Give each character a unique voice

Each character in your story should have a unique voice and way of speaking. This can be achieved by giving them distinct vocabulary, grammar, and sentence structure. For example, a character who’s a scientist might use technical jargon, while a character who’s from a different country might have an accent and use phrases unique to their culture.

7. Read your dialogue out loud

Reading your dialogue out loud can help you identify awkward phrasing, unrealistic conversations, and other issues. If you stumble over a line or it sounds awkward when it’s spoken out loud, it’s probably not going to work on the page.

8. Don’t be afraid of silence

Tip-to-toe dialogue can become tedious and distracting. It is okay to let characters stay silent in-between lines, as this can add meaning to the conversation. For example, one character might choose not to respond to another, indicating a taut exchange or tension between the two.

In conclusion, great dialogue is a crucial element of any successful story. By listening to real conversations, using contractions, showing instead of telling, avoiding exposition, using subtext, giving each character a unique voice, reading your dialogue out loud, and not being afraid of silence, you can create dialogue that feels authentic and believable. Remember, dialogue should never feel forced, contrived, or unnatural. It should flow easily, reflect the personalities of the characters, and add depth and texture to your story.
