The Science of Wine Tannins and Mouthfeel


Wine is often enjoyed for its complex flavors and aromas, but have you ever wondered why some wines have a smooth, velvety texture while others leave your mouth feeling dry and astringent? The answer lies in the science of wine tannins and mouthfeel.

Tannins are a group of naturally occurring compounds found in various plant parts, including grape skins, seeds, and stems. In wine, tannins can be extracted during the winemaking process, particularly during maceration when the grape skins are in contact with the juice. Tannins are responsible for several important characteristics of wine, including color stability, bitterness, and astringency.

When it comes to mouthfeel, tannins play a significant role. Mouthfeel refers to the tactile sensations experienced in the mouth when drinking wine, such as thickness, viscosity, and astringency. Tannins contribute to the astringency and structure of wine, creating the sensation of dryness and a puckering feeling in the mouth.

The perception of tannins in wine can vary depending on factors such as grape variety, ripeness of grapes, winemaking techniques, and aging. Red wines, in particular, are known for their higher tannin content compared to white wines. This is because red wines are fermented with their grape skins, allowing for greater tannin extraction.

Tannins are astringent compounds that bind with proteins and cause them to precipitate, resulting in a drying sensation in the mouth. This astringency is often described as a feeling of roughness or grittiness on the palate. However, it’s important to note that not all tannins are created equal. Some tannins can be round and silky, while others can be aggressive and harsh.

The level of tannin in wine can greatly affect its overall balance and enjoyment. Too much tannin can overwhelm the other flavors and make the wine unpleasantly astringent. On the other hand, too little tannin can result in a wine that lacks structure and depth. Winemakers carefully manage tannin extraction during the winemaking process to achieve the desired style and balance in their wines.

In addition to their impact on mouthfeel, tannins also contribute to the aging potential of wine. Over time, tannins can polymerize and soften, resulting in a smoother and more integrated mouthfeel. This is why many red wines, especially those made from Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Nebbiolo, benefit from aging. As the tannins mellow, the wine’s texture becomes more velvety and the flavors more harmonious.

So, how can you identify the level of tannin in a wine? One way is through a simple sensory evaluation. Take a sip of wine and pay attention to the sensations in your mouth. Is the wine dry and astringent, leaving your mouth feeling parched? Or is it smooth and supple, coating your palate with a silky texture? These clues can give you insights into the tannin content and mouthfeel of the wine.

Pairing wine with food can also influence the perception of tannins and mouthfeel. Tannins can interact with certain food components, such as proteins and fats, altering their perception. For example, the astringency of tannins can be softened by pairing them with foods high in fat, like cheese or meats. Similarly, the interaction between tannins and proteins can enhance the flavors and textures of both the food and the wine.

In conclusion, the science of wine tannins and mouthfeel provides insights into the complexities of wine enjoyment. Tannins contribute to the dryness and astringency in wine, affecting its overall balance and structure. By understanding the role of tannins and their impact on mouthfeel, you can better appreciate and savor the diverse array of wines available. So pour yourself a glass of your favorite red wine, pay attention to the tannins, and enjoy the unique sensory experience. Cheers!
