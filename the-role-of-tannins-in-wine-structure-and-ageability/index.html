The Role of Tannins in Wine: Structure and Ageability


The world of wine is a complex and fascinating one, filled with nuances that go far beyond just red or white. One of the critical components that contribute to a wine’s character and aging potential is tannins. These naturally occurring compounds play a pivotal role in shaping the structure, flavor, and ageability of wines. In this article, we will delve into the fascinating world of tannins, exploring their structure, sources, and the impact they have on wine aging.

Understanding Tannins

Tannins are a class of polyphenolic compounds found in various plant tissues, including grapes. In wine, they primarily come from the grape skins, seeds, and stems. Additionally, tannins can also be introduced through oak aging, as wooden barrels contain tannins that can leach into the wine. These compounds are responsible for the dry and puckering sensation often associated with red wines, particularly in their youth.

The term ’tannin’ may sound familiar to some, as it is also found in tea and certain fruits like persimmons, imparting a similar astringency. However, the tannins found in wine are chemically different from those in other sources, which contributes to their unique characteristics.

The Chemistry of Tannins

Tannins are part of a broader group of compounds known as polyphenols, which are naturally occurring antioxidants. The most common type of tannin in wine is called ‘condensed tannins’ or ‘proanthocyanidins.’ These compounds are made up of smaller units known as flavan-3-ols. The degree of polymerization, or the number of these smaller units linked together, affects a tannin’s structure and, consequently, its impact on the wine.

Tannins have a strong affinity for proteins and can bind to them, which is why they create that dry feeling in the mouth. This interaction between tannins and proteins can also influence the overall perception of a wine’s body and mouthfeel.

Tannins and Wine Aging

Tannins play a pivotal role in a wine’s aging potential. While many people enjoy young, fruit-forward wines, the magic of aging lies in the transformation of a wine over time, and tannins are central to this process.

During aging, tannins can undergo a series of chemical reactions. They gradually polymerize, linking together to form larger molecules. As a result, the harsh and astringent sensations that young wines often exhibit begin to mellow. This softening effect is known as ’tannin resolution’ and is one of the reasons why well-structured, high-tannin wines like Cabernet Sauvignon and Nebbiolo can improve with age.

As tannins evolve, they can also bind with other compounds in the wine, such as pigments, and help form sediment. This is why older wines often have a softer mouthfeel and display more complex aromas and flavors.

Tannins and Wine Varieties

Different grape varieties contain varying levels of tannins, which is why some wines are naturally better suited for aging than others. Red grape varieties like Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Nebbiolo are renowned for their high tannin content, making them excellent candidates for extended cellaring.

Conversely, white wines generally have lower tannin levels, as the juice is separated from the grape skins before fermentation. However, some white wines, particularly those aged in oak barrels, can also benefit from a certain level of tannin influence.

Winemaking Techniques and Tannins

Winemakers have several techniques at their disposal to manage tannins and tailor a wine’s structure to their desired style. For example:

  1. Maceration: The length of time grape skins, seeds, and stems are in contact with the juice during fermentation can be controlled. Longer maceration times result in more tannin extraction, leading to a bolder wine.

  2. Oak Aging: Wines aged in oak barrels can acquire additional tannins from the wood. Winemakers can choose different types of oak and levels of toasting to influence the tannin profile.

  3. Blending: Winemakers can blend different grape varieties to balance the tannin levels and create a more harmonious wine.

The Role of Tannins in Wine Tasting

When tasting wine, tannins are essential for evaluating a wine’s structure and potential for aging. Here are some key aspects to consider:

  1. Texture: Tannins impart texture to a wine. Young wines with pronounced tannins often feel grippy or astringent, while well-aged wines have a smoother, silkier texture.
