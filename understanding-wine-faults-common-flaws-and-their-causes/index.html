Understanding Wine Faults: Common Flaws and Their Causes


Wine is a complex and nuanced beverage, often celebrated for its array of flavors and aromas. However, like any agricultural product, wine is susceptible to a range of faults that can compromise its quality and enjoyment. In this article, we’ll explore some common wine faults, their causes, and how to identify them, empowering wine enthusiasts to recognize and appreciate wines at their best.

Oxidation
One of the most common faults encountered in wine is oxidation, which occurs when wine is excessively exposed to oxygen. Oxidized wines often exhibit a brownish hue and a stale, nutty aroma. This flaw can dull the wine’s fruit flavors and lead to a flat, lifeless palate.

Causes:
- Improper storage: Wine stored in corked bottles that have been compromised by air can quickly oxidize.
- Inadequate sealing: Poor-quality closures or faulty cork seals can allow oxygen to seep into the bottle.
- Extended exposure to air: Extended decanting or leaving wine in partially full bottles for too long can accelerate oxidation.

Reduction
Reduction is the opposite of oxidation and occurs when wine lacks exposure to sufficient oxygen during winemaking or bottling. Reduced wines often display off-putting aromas reminiscent of rotten eggs, rubber, or sewage.

Causes:
- Sulfur compounds: Excessive use of sulfur dioxide during winemaking can lead to reduction if not properly managed.
- Closed fermentation vessels: Wines fermented in closed or anaerobic conditions can develop reduction due to limited exposure to oxygen.
- Faulty closures: Poor-quality closures that do not allow for any oxygen exchange can exacerbate reduction.

Cork Taint (TCA)
Cork taint, caused by the presence of 2,4,6-trichloroanisole (TCA) in cork or other winemaking materials, is a pervasive fault that can render wine undrinkable. Wines affected by cork taint exhibit a distinct musty or moldy aroma, often described as ‘wet cardboard’ or ‘damp basement.’

Causes:
- Contaminated corks: TCA can originate from contaminated cork stoppers used in bottling.
- Winery equipment: TCA can also be introduced through contaminated winery equipment or storage environments.

Volatility
Volatility refers to the excessive evaporation of volatile compounds in wine, leading to an overpowering, solvent-like aroma. High volatility can make a wine smell harsh and alcoholic, overwhelming its more delicate flavors.

Causes:
- High temperatures during storage: Exposure to high temperatures can accelerate the evaporation of volatile compounds.
- Poorly controlled fermentation: Unstable fermentation conditions can lead to the formation of excessive volatile compounds.

Brettanomyces
Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘Brett,’ is a yeast that can contaminate wine and produce off-flavors such as barnyard, band-aid, or sweaty horse blanket. While some winemakers consider Brettanomyces to add complexity in small amounts, high levels can be considered a fault.

Causes:
- Poor sanitation: Contaminated winery equipment or unsanitary cellar conditions can lead to Brettanomyces contamination.
- Natural occurrence: Brettanomyces can also be present in vineyards and can infect grapes during winemaking.

Conclusion
Recognizing and understanding common wine faults is essential for both consumers and professionals in the wine industry. By identifying the causes and characteristics of these flaws, wine enthusiasts can better appreciate and enjoy wines at their optimal quality. Whether it’s preventing oxidation through proper storage or detecting cork taint before opening a bottle, being aware of wine faults empowers us to navigate the world of wine with confidence and discernment.
