Wine Faults: Common Issues and How to Recognize Them


Have you ever poured yourself a glass of wine, only to discover that it doesn’t taste quite right? You’re not alone. Wine faults are common issues that can affect the taste, aroma, and overall quality of a bottle of wine. In this article, we will discuss some of the most common wine faults and how to recognize them.

1. Cork taint:
Cork taint, also known as ‘corked wine,’ is caused by a compound called trichloroanisole (TCA) that can be found in natural corks. When a wine is affected by cork taint, it will have a musty, wet cardboard smell. The taste is often described as flat, lacking fruitiness, and with a slightly bitter aftertaste. To determine if a wine is corked, sniff the wine before tasting it. If you detect a musty odor, chances are it is corked.

2. Oxidation:
Oxidation occurs when wine comes into contact with too much oxygen. This can happen if a bottle is improperly sealed or if the wine is exposed to air for an extended period. Oxidized wine will have a brownish color and a stale, nutty aroma. On the palate, it may taste flat, lacking fruit flavors, and have a sherry-like character. If you suspect a wine is oxidized, check the color and aroma first and then taste it to confirm.

3. Volatile acidity:
Volatile acidity in wine is caused by the presence of acetic acid, which is created by bacteria during the winemaking process. In small amounts, volatile acidity can add complexity to a wine. However, if the levels are too high, it can result in an unpleasant smell, reminiscent of vinegar. It can also make the wine taste sharp, vinegary, and overly acidic. To detect volatile acidity, sniff the wine and look for a vinegary aroma. Then, taste it to confirm if it has a sharp, tart flavor.

4. Reduction:
Reduction is the opposite of oxidation and occurs when there is not enough exposure to oxygen during winemaking or storage. It can result in a wine that smells of rotten eggs, sulfur, or a struck match. Reduction can make the wine taste flabby, lacking fruitiness, and have a lingering bitter finish. To recognize reduction, swirl the wine in your glass to release any trapped sulfur compounds and take a sniff. If you detect any off-putting aromas, the wine may be affected by reduction.

5. Brettanomyces:
Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘brett,’ is a yeast that can infect wine and create off-flavors and aromas. While some winemakers embrace brett as it can add complexity to certain wines, others consider it a fault. A wine with brett may have aromas of barnyard, horse sweat, or Band-Aid. On the palate, it can show as a rustic, earthy character with a lingering bitterness. If you encounter a wine with unusual aromas, brett may be the culprit.

Knowing how to recognize these common wine faults can help you avoid disappointment when opening a bottle. If you suspect a wine is faulty, it’s worth contacting the producer or retailer to discuss a potential refund or replacement. By becoming familiar with these faults, you can make informed decisions when purchasing and drinking wine. Cheers to enjoying fault-free bottles every time!
