Survival Navigation: Using Maps and Compass Together


In today’s modern world, with GPS navigation systems readily available on our smartphones and in our vehicles, it’s easy to forget the importance of traditional navigation skills. However, in wilderness survival situations or when venturing off the grid, relying solely on electronic devices may not be feasible. That’s where the time-tested methods of using maps and compasses come into play. In this guide, we’ll explore the art of survival navigation and how to effectively use maps and compasses together to find your way in the great outdoors.

Understanding Maps

Maps are invaluable tools for navigation, providing a visual representation of terrain, landmarks, and geographical features. Before setting out on any journey, it’s essential to familiarize yourself with the map of the area you’ll be exploring. Pay attention to key details such as topography, elevation, water sources, and prominent landmarks. Understanding map symbols and legends will help you interpret the information presented on the map accurately.

Choosing the Right Compass

A reliable compass is an essential piece of equipment for wilderness navigation. When selecting a compass, opt for a quality model with a liquid-filled housing and a rotating bezel with degree markings. Ensure that the compass needle moves freely and accurately aligns with the Earth’s magnetic field. Familiarize yourself with the basic components of a compass, including the magnetic needle, orienting arrow, and direction-of-travel arrow.

Orienting the Map

To effectively use a map and compass together, you’ll need to orient the map to match the direction of the landscape. Begin by laying the map flat on a stable surface, ensuring that it’s free from folds or creases. Place the compass on the map with the edge of the baseplate aligned with the direction of travel. Rotate the map and compass together until the magnetic needle aligns with the orienting arrow on the compass housing. Once oriented, the map should accurately reflect the surrounding terrain.

Taking Bearings

A bearing is a precise direction measured in degrees clockwise from north. To navigate from one point to another using a map and compass, you’ll need to take bearings of your desired route. Align the edge of the compass baseplate with the starting point on the map and the destination. Rotate the compass housing until the orienting arrow is parallel with the map’s north-south grid lines. Read the degree marking where the direction-of-travel arrow points to obtain your bearing.

Following a Bearing

With your bearing determined, it’s time to follow your chosen route using the compass. Hold the compass level in front of you with the direction-of-travel arrow pointing away from your body. Rotate your body until the magnetic needle aligns with the orienting arrow inside the compass housing. Maintain this heading while navigating through the terrain, periodically checking your progress against prominent landmarks or features indicated on the map.

Adjusting for Declination

Declination is the angle between magnetic north and true north and varies depending on your location. To account for declination when using a map and compass, you’ll need to make adjustments to your bearings. Consult the declination information provided on the map or use online resources to determine the appropriate adjustment for your area. Apply the declination correction by either adding or subtracting the declination value from your bearing, ensuring accurate navigation.

Practicing Navigation Skills

Like any skill, proficiency in survival navigation requires practice and experience. Before embarking on a wilderness adventure, spend time honing your map and compass skills in familiar terrain. Start with simple navigation exercises and gradually increase the complexity as you become more confident. Familiarize yourself with alternative navigation techniques such as triangulation, dead reckoning, and terrain association to enhance your navigational toolkit.

Conclusion

In an age of advanced technology, the ability to navigate using maps and compasses remains a fundamental skill for outdoor enthusiasts and survivalists alike. By understanding how to effectively use these tools together, you can confidently navigate through unfamiliar terrain and find your way to safety. Remember to always carry a map and compass as backup navigation aids, even when relying on electronic devices. With practice and preparation, you’ll be well-equipped to tackle any navigational challenge that comes your way.
