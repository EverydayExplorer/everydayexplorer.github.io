Tiki Culture: The History and Cocktails


The Tiki culture is a phenomenon that swept across the United States in the mid-20th century. It was a time of thriving creativity and imagination, where exotic drinks, colorful clothing, and tropical themes were all the rage. Today, Tiki culture remains a style that is celebrated and continues to inspire those who are looking for an escape from the mundane.

The history of Tiki culture can be traced back to Hollywood in the 1930s. It was a time when Americans were fascinated with the exotic and the unknown, thanks to the Golden Age of Hollywood. The first Tiki bar, Don the Beachcomber, was opened in Hollywood in 1933 by former bootlegger Ernest Raymond Beaumont Gantt. He combined tropical decor with exotic drinks, including the famous Mai Tai, to create a new kind of drinking experience.

From there, Tiki culture grew in popularity and spread throughout the United States. During World War II, American GIs stationed in the South Pacific brought back with them stories of the tropical islands and the laid-back lifestyle they had experienced there. This only added fuel to the Tiki culture fire. Soon, Tiki bars and restaurants appeared in cities across the country, and everyone from Hollywood celebrities to everyday people embraced the trend.

The Tiki aesthetic was characterized by vibrant colors, palm trees, thatched roofs, carved tikis, and water features. It was a world of tropical paradise that transported people from their everyday lives. The signature drinks of Tiki culture were equally exotic and often included rum as the main ingredient. Some of the most famous cocktails include the Mai Tai, the Zombie, and the Pina Colada.

The Mai Tai is perhaps the most well-known and iconic Tiki cocktail. It was invented by Victor Bergeron, the owner of Trader Vic’s, in 1944. The name ‘Mai Tai’ means ‘out of this world’ in Tahitian, and the drink certainly lives up to its name. It is made with rum, lime juice, orange curacao, orgeat syrup, and a splash of grenadine. The Mai Tai is garnished with mint and a slice of pineapple.

The Zombie is another famous Tiki cocktail that was invented by Donn Beach, the owner of Don the Beachcomber. Legend has it that the drink was so potent that it turned one patron into a zombie for three days. The Zombie is made with several types of rum, falernum, lime juice, grapefruit juice, cinnamon syrup, and grenadine. It is garnished with mint and a cherry.

The Pina Colada is a sweet and creamy Tiki cocktail that is perfect for sipping on a hot summer day. It was invented in Puerto Rico in the 1950s and is made with rum, coconut cream, and pineapple juice. The Pina Colada is garnished with a slice of pineapple and a cherry.

Tiki culture had its heyday in the 1950s and 1960s but fell out of favor in the 1970s. However, in recent years, there has been a resurgence of interest in Tiki culture, with new Tiki bars and restaurants opening up all over the country. There are even Tiki-themed events, like Tiki Oasis, which is held annually in San Diego.

In conclusion, Tiki culture is a celebration of tropical paradise, exotic drinks, and laid-back living. It has a rich history that stretches back to Hollywood in the 1930s and has inspired generations of Americans to embrace the spirit of adventure and escape. Whether you’re sipping a Mai Tai at Trader Vic’s or lounging under a palm tree, Tiki culture is the perfect way to escape from the mundane and embrace the whimsical.
