Understanding Wine Faults: Common Problems and Solutions


Wine, a beverage steeped in history and culture, can offer a symphony of flavors and aromas. However, sometimes, a bottle can fall short of expectations due to faults that mar its enjoyment. Understanding these faults is crucial for wine enthusiasts and professionals alike, as it empowers them to identify and resolve issues, ensuring an optimal wine-drinking experience.

Common Wine Faults and Their Causes

Wine faults can arise at various stages of production, from grape cultivation to bottling. Some of the most common faults include:

  • Brettanomyces (Brett): Caused by a wild yeast, Brett imparts a distinctive ‘barnyard’ or ‘horse blanket’ aroma. While some winemakers embrace Brett character in specific styles, it can be considered a fault when it becomes overpowering.

  • Volatile Acidity (VA): An excess of acetic acid, the main component of vinegar, can lead to a sharp, vinegary aroma and taste. VA can originate from bacterial spoilage or improper winemaking practices.

  • Oxidation: Exposure to oxygen can prematurely age wine, resulting in a loss of freshness and fruitiness. Oxidized wines develop a ’nutty’ or ‘cardboard’ aroma and a dull, brown color.

  • Reduction: Insufficient oxygen exposure can create a ‘sulfury’ or ‘matchstick’ aroma. Reduction faults can occur during fermentation or storage and can be corrected with aeration.

  • Cork Taint (TCA): A chemical compound from cork stoppers, TCA can impart a musty, moldy aroma and taste to wine. TCA contamination is a significant concern in the wine industry.

  • Light Strike: Exposure to ultraviolet (UV) light can damage wine, causing a ‘skunky’ or ‘burnt rubber’ aroma. Light strike can occur during storage or transportation.

  • Premature Aging: Excessive heat or exposure to oxygen can accelerate the aging process, resulting in a loss of complexity and a ‘cooked’ or ‘raisiny’ character.

Identifying and Resolving Wine Faults

Identifying wine faults requires a keen sense of smell and taste. Once a fault is suspected, the next step is to determine its cause and find a solution:

  • Brettanomyces: Brett character can be managed by controlling fermentation conditions and using specific yeast strains. In some cases, a small amount of Brett can be desirable, adding complexity to certain wine styles.

  • Volatile Acidity: VA can be reduced by controlling bacterial growth during fermentation and storage. Proper cellar hygiene and temperature management are essential.

  • Oxidation: Oxidized wines cannot be restored. Prevention is key, involving proper bottle closures, minimal headspace, and controlled storage conditions.

  • Reduction: Reduction faults can be corrected by aeration. Exposing the wine to oxygen through decanting or gentle swirling can dissipate the sulfury aromas.

  • Cork Taint: TCA contamination is irreversible. Affected wines should be discarded. To prevent cork taint, winemakers use alternative closures or employ rigorous cork quality control measures.

  • Light Strike: Light strike damage is also irreversible. Storing wine in dark, UV-protected environments is crucial.

  • Premature Aging: Premature aging can be prevented by controlling storage conditions, such as temperature and humidity. Proper cellar management is essential to preserve wine quality.

Conclusion

Understanding wine faults empowers wine enthusiasts and professionals to identify and resolve issues that can compromise the enjoyment of this beloved beverage. By recognizing the common faults, their causes, and potential solutions, wine lovers can ensure an optimal wine-drinking experience, appreciating the full spectrum of flavors and aromas that wine has to offer.
