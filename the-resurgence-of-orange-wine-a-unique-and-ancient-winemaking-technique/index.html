The Resurgence of Orange Wine: A Unique and Ancient Winemaking Technique


Orange wine has been growing in popularity in recent years, captivating wine enthusiasts and sommeliers alike. This unique style of winemaking, which dates back thousands of years, involves leaving the grape skins and seeds in contact with the juice during fermentation, resulting in a beautiful amber hue. The resurgence of orange wine has shed light on this ancient technique and the intriguing flavors and aromas it produces.

The roots of orange wine can be traced back to Georgia, in the region of Kakheti, where winemaking has been an integral part of the culture for many centuries. This traditional method involves using large underground clay vessels called qvevri, which are buried in the ground. The grapes, often indigenous varieties such as Rkatsiteli or Kisi, are placed in the qvevri along with their skins, seeds, and stems. The vessels are then sealed and left to ferment for several months, sometimes even years, resulting in a unique and complex wine.

What sets orange wine apart is its rich and textural nature. The extended skin contact gives the wine its distinctive color and adds a tannic structure that is reminiscent of red wines. This tannic backbone is often accompanied by a pleasant grippy texture, making orange wine a fascinating sensory experience.

In terms of flavor profiles, orange wine is known for its complexity and range. The process of fermenting the juice with the skins imparts a myriad of aromas and flavors, including dried fruits, spices, nuts, and even tea-like characteristics. Orange wines can also exhibit floral and honeyed notes, along with a distinct oxidative character that adds depth and complexity.

Another reason for the resurgence of orange wine is its versatility when it comes to food pairing. Its slightly tannic structure allows it to stand up to robust dishes, while its acidity pairs well with a variety of foods. From cheeses and charcuterie to roasted meats and hearty vegetarian fare, orange wine has the ability to elevate a meal and create a memorable dining experience.

The increased interest in natural and minimal-intervention winemaking practices has also contributed to the renewed popularity of orange wine. Many producers who focus on organic or biodynamic viticulture embrace this ancient technique, as it aligns with their philosophy of minimal intervention in the winemaking process. By allowing the grapes to ferment with their skins and seeds, these winemakers are able to create wines that truly express the terroir and unique characteristics of the fruit.

For those looking to explore the world of orange wine, there are now a plethora of options available. Producers from various regions around the world, including Italy, Slovenia, and the United States, are experimenting with this ancient winemaking technique, resulting in a wide range of styles and flavors to discover. Whether you’re a seasoned wine enthusiast or a curious beginner, orange wine offers an exciting journey into the world of natural wines.

In conclusion, the resurgence of orange wine represents a return to tradition and a celebration of ancient winemaking techniques. Its distinct color, complex flavors, and ability to pair with a wide range of foods make it an intriguing choice for wine lovers who are seeking something a little different. As orange wine continues to gain recognition and popularity, it is sure to remain a fascinating and sought-after style in the world of wine.
