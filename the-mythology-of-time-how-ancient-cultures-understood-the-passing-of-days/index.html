The Mythology of Time: How ancient cultures understood the passing of days


Time is an ever-present force that governs our lives. It is the structure upon which our daily routines are built, and it provides us with a way to measure, organize, and make sense of the world around us. But have you ever wondered how different ancient cultures understood and interpreted the passing of days? In this blog, we will take a journey through the mythologies of various civilizations and explore how they perceived and conceptualized time. From the Babylonians to the Mayans, each culture had its own unique understanding of the rhythms of time.

The Babylonians and Their Celestial Clocks

One of the earliest civilizations to develop a complex understanding of time was the Babylonians. They believed that time was a cyclical phenomenon, with each day repeating the same celestial events as the day before. To keep track of these celestial events, the Babylonians built intricate celestial clocks known as ziggurats. These towering structures not only served as places of worship but also as observatories, enabling the Babylonians to track the movements of the sun, moon, and stars and predict celestial events such as eclipses and solstices. The Babylonians’ understanding of time was closely tied to their observations of the celestial bodies and their cycles.

The Egyptians and the Eternal Return

In ancient Egypt, time was seen as a perpetual cycle of life, death, and rebirth. The Egyptians believed in the concept of eternal return - the idea that everything in the universe, including time itself, followed a circular pattern of birth, death, and rebirth. This belief was central to their religious practices and was reflected in their creation myths and burial rituals. The Pharaoh, as the embodiment of the sun god Ra, was believed to journey through the underworld each night and be reborn at sunrise, ensuring the continuation of life and the passage of time.

The Greeks and the Linear Concept of Time

Unlike the Babylonians and the Egyptians, the ancient Greeks had a more linear concept of time. They believed that time had a definite beginning and would eventually come to an end. The Greek philosopher Heraclitus proposed the idea of ‘panta rhei,’ or ’everything flows,’ suggesting that time, like a river, constantly moved forward and never repeated itself. The Greek pantheon of gods and goddesses, with their own quirks and personalities, was believed to have a specific role in shaping the course of time through their actions and decisions.

The Mayans and the Long Count Calendar

The ancient Mayans of Central America developed one of the most complex and accurate systems of measuring time - the Long Count calendar. This calendar, based on astronomical observations and mathematical calculations, provided the Mayans with a way to track time over vast periods, including cycles that spanned thousands of years. The Long Count calendar was not only a practical tool for measuring time but also held great significance in Mayan mythology and religion. The end of a cycle, such as the famous ’end’ of the Mayan calendar in 2012, was seen as a time of transition and renewal, rather than an apocalyptic event.

Conclusion

Throughout the ancient world, different cultures devised their own intricate mythologies to explain the passing of days and the nature of time. From the Babylonians’ celestial clocks to the Mayans’ Long Count calendar, each civilization had its unique understanding of time’s rhythms and cycles. These mythological interpretations not only provided practical ways to track time but also served as a way to connect the everyday with the divine and to make sense of the seemingly immeasurable force that governs our lives. Today, as we continue to measure time with precision, it is fascinating to reflect on how ancient cultures perceived the passing of days and the mysteries that lay beyond the veil of time.
