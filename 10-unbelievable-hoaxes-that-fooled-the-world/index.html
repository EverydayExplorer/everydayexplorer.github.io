10 Unbelievable Hoaxes That Fooled the World


Hoaxes have long been a part of human history, tricking people into believing the unbelievable. From the infamous Piltdown Man to the recent Balloon Boy hoax, here are ten unbelievable hoaxes that fooled the world.

  1. The Cardiff Giant
    In 1869, workers in Cardiff, New York, uncovered a giant petrified man while digging a well. The ‘Cardiff Giant’ drew massive crowds and was touted as evidence of giants in ancient America. However, it was later revealed to be a hoax created by a businessman seeking to make money from curious onlookers.

  2. The Cottingley Fairies
    In the early 20th century, two young girls from Yorkshire, England, claimed to have photographed fairies in their garden. The photos, which gained widespread attention, were eventually revealed to be elaborate cardboard cutouts. Despite this, the girls maintained their story for years, fooling many, including Sir Arthur Conan Doyle.

  3. The Hitler Diaries
    In 1983, German magazine Stern published excerpts from what they claimed were Adolf Hitler’s personal diaries. The diaries were later revealed to be forgeries created by Konrad Kujau, a notorious fraudster. The incident was a major embarrassment for Stern and highlighted the dangers of not thoroughly verifying sources.

  4. The War of the Worlds Radio Broadcast
    In 1938, Orson Welles aired a radio adaptation of H.G. Wells’ ‘War of the Worlds,’ presenting it as a series of news bulletins. Many listeners mistook the broadcast for a real alien invasion, leading to panic in some areas. While exaggerated, the extent of the panic is still debated by historians.

  5. The Piltdown Man
    One of the most famous hoaxes in scientific history, the Piltdown Man was a supposed missing link between humans and apes. It was discovered in 1912 in England and hailed as a major archaeological find. However, it was later revealed to be a forgery, with the skull being a human and the jaw belonging to an orangutan.

  6. The Loch Ness Monster
    The legend of the Loch Ness Monster has persisted for centuries, with numerous sightings and photographs claiming to prove its existence. However, most evidence has been debunked as hoaxes or misidentifications, leading many to believe the monster is merely a myth.

  7. Balloon Boy
    In 2009, a story captivated the world as a six-year-old boy was thought to be trapped in a helium balloon floating over Colorado. The balloon eventually landed, but the boy was not inside. It was later revealed to be a hoax orchestrated by the boy’s parents to gain publicity.

  8. The Fiji Mermaid
    In the 19th century, showman P.T. Barnum displayed a ‘Fiji Mermaid,’ claiming it was a real creature. In reality, it was a grotesque hoax, created by sewing the upper body of a monkey to the lower body of a fish. Despite its obvious fakery, the Fiji Mermaid drew large crowds eager to see the mythical creature.

  9. The Cottingley Fairies
    In the early 20th century, two young girls from Yorkshire, England, claimed to have photographed fairies in their garden. The photos, which gained widespread attention, were eventually revealed to be elaborate cardboard cutouts. Despite this, the girls maintained their story for years, fooling many, including Sir Arthur Conan Doyle.

  10. The Hitler Diaries
    In 1983, German magazine Stern published excerpts from what they claimed were Adolf Hitler’s personal diaries. The diaries were later revealed to be forgeries created by Konrad Kujau, a notorious fraudster. The incident was a major embarrassment for Stern and highlighted the dangers of not thoroughly verifying sources.

In conclusion, these hoaxes remind us of the importance of skepticism and critical thinking. While some hoaxes are harmless fun, others can have serious consequences. It’s crucial to always question information and seek out reliable sources before accepting something as true.
