Wine Faults and Flaws: Identifying and Preventing Common Issues


When it comes to enjoying a glass of wine, nothing can be more disappointing than discovering that it has a fault or flaw. These issues can range from unpleasant aromas and flavors to an overall lack of quality. As a wine enthusiast, it is essential to understand the common faults and flaws that can occur and how to prevent them. In this blog post, we will explore some of the most common wine faults and flaws, and provide you with tips on how to identify and prevent them.

1. Cork Taint: One of the most well-known wine faults is cork taint, also known as ‘corked’ wine. This occurs when a compound called TCA (2,4,6-trichloroanisole) is present in the cork, resulting in a musty or moldy aroma and a loss of fruit flavors. To identify cork taint, simply give the wine a sniff. If it smells like wet cardboard or a dank basement, chances are you have a corked bottle. To prevent cork taint, store your wine in a cool, dry place and consider investing in alternative closures such as screw caps or synthetic corks.

2. Oxidation: Oxidation can occur when wine is exposed to excessive amounts of oxygen, resulting in a loss of freshness and vibrancy. Signs of oxidation include a brownish color, a flat taste, and a lack of fruit aromas. To prevent oxidation, ensure proper storage by keeping wine bottles upright and away from direct sunlight. Additionally, consider investing in a vacuum pump or inert gas system to remove excess oxygen from opened bottles.

3. Sulfur Compounds: Sulfur compounds can contribute to off-putting aromas and flavors in wine. These compounds can result from excessive use of sulfur dioxide during winemaking or improper handling during storage. Common sulfur-related faults include ‘reduced’ aromas (smelling like rotten eggs or burnt rubber) or ‘oxidized’ aromas (smelling like burnt matches). To prevent sulfur-related faults, winemakers should use sulfites in moderation and ensure proper hygiene during winemaking. As a consumer, it is important to store your wine in a cool, dark place and avoid exposure to extreme temperatures.

4. Volatile Acidity: Volatile acidity refers to the presence of acetic acid in wine, which can result in a vinegary aroma and taste. While a small amount of volatile acidity can contribute to complexity in certain wines, excessive levels can be a fault. To identify volatile acidity, look out for a strong vinegar-like smell or a sharp, acidic taste. To prevent volatile acidity, winemakers should control fermentation temperatures and practice good hygiene in the winery. As a consumer, it is crucial to store your wine at a cool temperature and avoid exposing it to excessive heat.

5. Brettanomyces: Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘Brett,’ is a yeast that can produce off-putting aromas and flavors in wine. These can range from barnyard-like aromas to medicinal or band-aid-like notes. To identify Brettanomyces, look out for these distinctive aromas in your wine. To prevent Brettanomyces, winemakers should maintain strict hygiene protocols in the winery and monitor the presence of this yeast. As a consumer, it is important to be aware that some wines may intentionally have a small amount of Brettanomyces as a stylistic choice.

By understanding these common wine faults and flaws and implementing preventative measures, you can enhance your wine drinking experience and avoid disappointment. Whether you are a wine enthusiast or a casual consumer, being able to identify and prevent these issues will help you appreciate the full potential of your favorite wines. Cheers to enjoying a fault-free glass of wine!
