The Debate on Moral Relativism vs. Moral Absolutism


As human beings, we have been debating the concept of morality for centuries now. While some argue that morality is subjective and relative, others believe that it is an objective entity that exists across time and space. The debate on moral relativism vs. moral absolutism is one that has continued to gain traction over the years. While both sides of the argument have their own merits and demerits, let us take a closer look at what the debate actually involves.

Moral relativism is the belief that morality is subjective and therefore varies from one individual to another. According to this view, there are no universal moral laws and what is considered right or wrong is relative to a person’s individual belief system and cultural background. In essence, what may be considered taboo in one culture may be perfectly acceptable in another culture. For instance, some cultures may endorse infanticide or female genital mutilation, while others may denounce these acts as barbaric and inhumane.

On the other hand, moral absolutism is the belief that morality is objective and universal. This viewpoint contends that there are certain moral laws that are applicable to all individuals, regardless of their cultural or social background. These moral laws are seen as axiomatic and non-negotiable. From this perspective, certain acts are just wrong, no matter what contextual nuances may exist. In short, it is a black and white view of morality, devoid of any shades of grey.

One of the strongest proponents of moral relativism is David Wong, a professor of philosophy at Duke University. Wong contends that moral relativism allows for the celebration of diversity and respect for different cultural values. Wong’s argument is grounded in the idea that different cultures may have differing views on what is regarded as moral or ethical behaviour. For example, in some cultures, it may be acceptable to sacrifice animals for religious purposes, while in others it may be seen as cruel and inhumane.

However, critics of moral relativism argue that the idea of morality being culturally relative is deeply flawed. Moral relativists assume that every cultural or social group shares the same moral values. In reality, moral values within the same cultural group can vary from individual to individual. For instance, while a particular culture may condone female genital mutilation, it does not mean that all members of that culture necessarily engage in or support such practices.

Moral absolutists, on the other hand, argue that morality is a universal and objective entity that applies across all cultures and times. They contend that certain moral values are inherent to the human condition, and that it is our responsibility as humans to adhere to them. Absolute moral values are not simply arbitrary rules, but reflect the fundamental principles of our existence. For instance, most people would agree that it is wrong to kill innocent individuals or to steal.

However, some argue that moral absolutism can also lead to a lack of empathy and understanding. Moral absolutists can sometimes be accused of using morality as a tool to enforce conformity and suppress dissent. For instance, in some cases, moral absolutism has been used to justify colonialism, imperialism and other forms of oppression on the grounds that the West had a moral duty to civilize and enlighten ‘primitive’ cultures.

In conclusion, the debate on moral relativism vs. moral absolutism is one that continues to divide opinion in the world of philosophy and ethics. While both sides have their own merits and demerits, what is clear is that morality is not a simple or straightforward issue. The nature of morality is complex and multifaceted, and the debate on its relativity or absoluteness is likely to continue for many years to come. Ultimately, it is up to each of us to decide where we stand on this issue and how we choose to approach the moral dilemmas that we face in our daily lives.
