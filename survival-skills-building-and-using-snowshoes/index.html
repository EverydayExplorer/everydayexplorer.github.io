Survival Skills: Building and Using Snowshoes


When the snow starts to fall and the landscape is blanketed in white, it’s time to embrace the beauty of winter and all the outdoor activities it offers. For outdoor enthusiasts and survivalists, mastering the skill of building and using snowshoes is not only a practical necessity but also a rewarding craft that connects us to the traditions of indigenous peoples and early explorers.

Understanding the Importance of Snowshoes

Snowshoes are indispensable tools for traversing deep snow, especially in regions where snowfall is a regular occurrence. They distribute the weight of the wearer across a larger surface area, preventing them from sinking deep into the snow and making travel much easier. Whether you’re an avid hiker, a hunter, or someone who simply finds themselves in snowy conditions, the ability to make and use snowshoes can be a lifesaver.

Crafting Your Own Snowshoes

The art of making snowshoes has been passed down through generations and varies across cultures, each with its own unique design and materials. However, a common feature among all traditional snowshoes is the use of wood and rawhide. Here’s a basic guide to crafting your own traditional wooden snowshoes:

Materials Needed

  • Ash or white oak wood for the frame
  • Rawhide for the lacing
  • Wood glue
  • Nails or screws
  • Saw, knife, and sandpaper

Steps to Build Snowshoes

  1. Frame Construction: Start by cutting the wood into the desired shape and size for the snowshoe frame. Traditionally, a long, narrow shape is preferred to provide better flotation on snow.

  2. Weaving the Rawhide: Once the frame is constructed, the next step is to weave the rawhide lacing across the frame in a pattern that maximizes surface area and strength. This requires precision and patience, as the rawhide needs to be tightly woven to create a durable base for the snowshoe.

  3. Finishing Touches: After the rawhide is woven, secure the ends of the lacing and trim any excess material. Sand down any rough edges and apply a weather-resistant finish to protect the wood and rawhide from moisture.

Mastering the Art of Snowshoeing

With your handmade snowshoes ready, it’s time to venture into the snowy wilderness and put your skills to the test. Here are a few tips for using snowshoes effectively:

  • Walking Technique: When wearing snowshoes, it’s important to adopt a wider stance to prevent stepping on the frames. Walking with a slightly wider gait will help you maintain balance and avoid tripping.

  • Uphill and Downhill Travel: When tackling uphill terrain, use the crampons or cleats on the snowshoes to gain traction. For downhill travel, lean back slightly to prevent the tips of the snowshoes from digging into the snow.

  • Terrain Awareness: Be mindful of the terrain and potential hazards such as hidden tree branches or uneven ground. Snowshoes provide flotation, but they don’t eliminate the need for caution and awareness.

Embracing a Time-Honored Tradition

The art of snowshoe making and the skill of using them effectively is a testament to human ingenuity and adaptability in harsh environments. By learning and practicing these survival skills, we not only connect with nature but also gain a deeper understanding of the challenges faced by those who came before us. So, next time you find yourself in a winter wonderland, consider embracing the art of snowshoe making and experiencing the world from a different perspective.

In conclusion, the ability to build and use snowshoes is a valuable survival skill that opens up new possibilities for outdoor exploration and adventure. By mastering this ancient craft, you’ll be better prepared to tackle the winter wilderness with confidence and grace. So, gather your materials, embrace the challenge, and step into the world of snowshoe making and survival skills.
