Understanding Wine Faults: How to Identify and Prevent Them


Wine is a complex and fascinating beverage that has been enjoyed by humans for centuries. From its rich history to the intricacies of its production, there is much to learn and appreciate about wine. However, even the most experienced wine enthusiasts can encounter faults in their favorite bottles. Understanding wine faults is crucial for anyone who wishes to fully appreciate and enjoy this timeless libation. In this blog post, we will explore the various wine faults, how to identify them, and most importantly, how to prevent them.

First, let’s define what we mean by ‘wine fault.’ A wine fault refers to any undesirable characteristic or flaw found in a bottle of wine. These flaws can manifest in different ways, affecting the aroma, taste, and appearance of the wine. It is important to note that not all faults are synonymous with poor quality; some wine faults can be intentional or even preferred in certain styles of wine. However, for the purposes of this discussion, we will focus on the faults that are generally considered negative.

1. Cork Taint (TCA): One of the most common wine faults, cork taint is caused by a compound called trichloroanisole (TCA). TCA can contaminate a bottle of wine when it comes into contact with a faulty or contaminated cork. The resulting wine will have a distinct musty odor, often described as smelling like wet cardboard or a damp basement. To identify cork taint, simply sniff the wine before tasting. If you detect an off-putting aroma, chances are the wine is affected. To prevent cork taint, ensure that your wine is properly stored in a cool, dry place, and consider alternative closures such as screw caps or synthetic corks.

2. Oxidation: Oxidation occurs when wine is exposed to excessive amounts of oxygen. This can happen during the winemaking process, as well as after the bottle has been opened. Oxidized wine will have a brownish color and a flat, lackluster taste. It may also exhibit aromas reminiscent of vinegar or sherry. To prevent oxidation, store your wine in a cool, dark place and avoid unnecessary exposure to air. Once a bottle has been opened, consider using a wine preservation system or transferring the remaining wine into a smaller container to reduce the amount of oxygen contact.

3. Sulfur Compounds: Sulfur dioxide (SO2) is commonly used in winemaking as a preservative. However, excessive amounts of sulfur compounds can result in unpleasant odors and flavors. The aroma of rotten eggs (hydrogen sulfide) or burnt rubber (mercaptans) is a clear indication of sulfur-related faults. Proper hygiene and sanitation practices during winemaking can help prevent the development of these compounds. Additionally, winemakers should carefully monitor and control the levels of SO2 added to their wines.

4. Volatile Acidity (VA): Volatile acidity refers to the presence of acetic acid in wine. In small amounts, it can contribute to the wine’s complexity and aging potential. However, excessive volatile acidity can result in a vinegary smell and taste. High temperatures during fermentation, poor hygiene, and extended exposure to air can all contribute to the development of volatile acidity. To prevent this fault, winemakers must maintain proper temperature control during fermentation and ensure the cleanliness of equipment and storage vessels.

5. Reduction: While oxidation represents an excess of oxygen, reduction occurs when there is a lack of oxygen during winemaking or storage. Reduced wines can have unpleasant aromas, often described as smelling like rotten eggs, garlic, or cabbage. A quick remedy for reduction is to expose the wine to air by decanting it or vigorously stirring it. Prevention involves ensuring that the wine has regular access to oxygen during fermentation and aging. Additionally, proper hygiene practices are essential to prevent the growth of harmful sulfur compounds.

6. Brettanomyces: Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘Brett,’ is a yeast strain that can contribute to both desirable and undesirable characteristics in wine. In low levels, Brett can add complexity and earthy aromas. However, in excessive amounts, it can produce unpleasant aromas reminiscent of barnyard, wet dog, or band-aids. To prevent the overgrowth of Brettanomyces, winemakers must maintain strict sanitation practices and monitor the wine’s pH and sulfur dioxide levels.

In conclusion, understanding wine faults is vital for wine lovers and professionals alike. By familiarizing yourself with the various faults and their causes, you can enhance your wine tasting experience and ensure that you enjoy every bottle to its fullest potential. Remember to trust your senses when identifying faults and take the necessary steps to prevent them. Whether it’s properly storing your wine, maintaining hygiene during winemaking, or controlling exposure to oxygen, these preventive measures will help you appreciate wine in all its glory. So raise a glass and savor the beauty of fault-free wine!
