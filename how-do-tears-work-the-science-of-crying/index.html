How Do Tears Work? The Science of Crying


Tears. We all shed them at some point in our lives, whether it’s from joy or heartbreak. But have you ever wondered how exactly tears work? What triggers them to flow out of our eyes? In this blog post, we will delve into the science behind crying and explore the various reasons for it.

First, let’s start with the basics. Tears are made up of a combination of water, salt, and various proteins. They are produced by the lacrimal glands, located above each eye. These glands continuously produce tears, which are spread across the surface of the eye each time we blink. This helps to lubricate the eye and keep it from drying out.

However, there are three types of tears: basal tears, reflex tears, and emotional tears. Basal tears are the ones that are constantly produced to keep the eye moist. Reflex tears are the ones that flow when something irritates the eye, such as smoke or dust. Emotional tears, on the other hand, are triggered by our feelings and emotions.

When we experience an intense emotion such as sadness, happiness, or even frustration, the limbic system in our brain is activated. This system is responsible for regulating our emotions and triggering the release of hormones such as cortisol and adrenaline. These hormones then stimulate the lacrimal gland to produce tears.

But why do we cry? There are several theories to explain why we shed tears. One is that tears help to release built-up stress and tension by flushing out stress hormones and toxins from the body. Another theory is that tears are a way for us to communicate our emotions and seek comfort and support from others.

Interestingly, research has also shown that emotional tears contain higher levels of certain chemicals, such as the painkiller endorphin, which could explain why we feel better after a good cry. Additionally, tears also contain antibodies and enzymes that can help to fight off infections and improve our overall immune system.

So, how do tears work? In short, they are produced by the lacrimal gland and are made up of water, salt, and proteins. Emotional tears are triggered by our limbic system, which is responsible for regulating our emotions. There are several theories as to why we cry, including the release of stress and toxins from the body, the communication of our emotions, and the presence of chemicals that can improve our mood and immune system.

In conclusion, crying is a natural and essential part of the human experience. It helps us to release our emotions, communicate our feelings, and even improve our overall health. So, the next time you find yourself shedding a tear, remember that it’s all just a part of the fascinating science of crying.
