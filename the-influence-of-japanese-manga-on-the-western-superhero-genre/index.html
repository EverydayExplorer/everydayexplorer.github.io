The Influence of Japanese Manga on the Western Superhero Genre


The world of comics has been thriving for over a century, but it wasn’t until the mid-twentieth century that the superhero genre emerged – first in the United States and then expanding all over the world. The comic book medium has gone through several transformations with artists and writers finding innovative ways to invent new stories and characters. One of the biggest influences on the western superhero genre came from Japanese manga. The popularity of manga has exploded worldwide and has had a significant impact on the American comic book industry – leading to the creation of new superheroes.

The influence of Japanese manga on western comics dates back to the 1970s and 80s when Japanese comic books started making appearances in the United States. Manga at that time was somewhat of a novelty, with readers being particularly drawn to the unique visual storytelling and intense action scenes. Western artists and writers began taking inspiration from the Japanese style of storytelling, with the most significant changes coming to the superhero genre itself.

One of the most obvious influences is the distinctive visual style that manga brings to comics – larger than life characters with exaggerated facial features and emotive expressions. This style has made its way into western comics, giving comics a fresh, modern look. This is seen in many popular comics such as Spider-Man, Batman, and Superman. This was further compounded by the popularity of anime in the 90s and early 2000s, which also influenced the design of characters, making them appear more appealing to younger audiences.

Japanese manga also introduced a new approach to storytelling, an approach focused on character development and an ever-evolving storyline. In earlier western comics, superhero stories were more episodic; with each issue telling a different adventure. In contrast, Japanese manga had a long-form narrative with continuous story arcs. This format allowed western comic book writers to tell more complex stories, including character-driven arcs and long plotlines.

The influence of Japanese manga extends beyond the visual storytelling of comics. The concept of superheroes teaming up, a popular trope in western comics, was also present in Japanese manga. Japanese superhero teams like Battle of the Planets/G-Force and Super Sentai are perfect examples. These ideas were later adopted by western comics, giving rise to some of the most iconic superhero teams, including the X-Men and the Teen Titans.

Incorporating elements of manga has allowed western comics to explore new narrative structures and focus on topics beyond superheroic fighting. For instance, East of West by Jonathan Hickman, a critically acclaimed comic series, is a mashup of western comics and the manga format. The story explores what a post-apocalyptic America would look like, and the majority of issues have a clear beginning, middle, and end, with each issue focused on a different character.

From visual storytelling to narrative structure, Japanese manga has revolutionized the Western superhero genre. The influence is vast and undeniable. It has enabled comic book writers and artists to expand their creative scope, and has resulted in some of the most iconic characters of both Japanese and Western comics. Moreover, demand for manga-inspired comics has allowed publishers such as Marvel and DC Comics to expand their markets worldwide, making the genre more accessible to a global audience. It is safe to say that the influence of Japanese manga has always been, and will continue to be, a crucial part of the Western superhero canon.
