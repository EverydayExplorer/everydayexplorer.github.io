Turkey Calling Techniques: Mastering the Art of Mimicking Turkey Sounds


Are you an avid hunter looking to improve your turkey hunting game? Or are you simply interested in the fascinating world of mimicking turkey sounds? In either case, mastering the art of turkey calling can be a game-changer. It not only increases your chances of luring in a turkey but also enhances your overall hunting experience. In this blog post, we’ll explore the different turkey calling techniques and tips that will help you become a master at mimicking turkey sounds.

The Need for Turkey Calling

Turkey calling is an essential skill for any turkey hunter. Turkeys are highly vocal creatures with a wide range of distinct sounds. By mastering the technique of turkey calling, you can engage in a dialogue with these birds, imitating the different vocalizations they make. This ability allows you to create the illusion of a turkey in distress, a potential mate, or a contented group, thus attracting them towards you.

Types of Turkey Sounds

Before diving into the techniques, let’s first familiarize ourselves with the different types of turkey sounds.

  1. Gobbles: The gobble is the male turkey’s primary vocalization during the mating season. It is a loud and deep sound, often referred to as a ‘gobble’ or ‘gobble-gobble.’ Gobbling is an effective method for locating toms.

  2. Yelps: Yelps are the most commonly heard turkey vocalization. They are soft, high-pitched, and melodic sounds made by both male and female turkeys. Yelps communicate various messages, such as contentment, frustration, or seeking contact with other turkeys.

  3. Purrs: Purrs are soft, rolling, and continuous sounds made by turkeys. They convey a sense of relaxation, contentment, or a call for other turkeys to gather together.

  4. Clucks: Clucks are short, staccato-like sounds made by both male and female turkeys. They are gentle and often used for maintaining contact within a flock.

  5. Cutting: Cutting is a fast and erratic series of sharp clucks and yelps. It represents excitement, agitation, or urgency.

  6. Kee-kee runs: Kee-kee runs are primarily made by young turkeys. They sound like a high-pitched ‘kee-kee’ repeated multiple times, followed by a series of yelps. This vocalization attracts other turkeys of similar ages or curious older birds.

Now that we have a grasp on the different turkey sounds, let’s explore the techniques that will allow you to mimic them effectively.

Turkey Calling Techniques

1. Mouth Calls

Mouth calls, also known as diaphragm calls, are the most popular and versatile turkey calls. They are placed in the roof of the mouth and manipulated by the tongue and airflow to produce various turkey sounds. Here’s how to use them:

  • Choose the right mouth call: Mouth calls come in a variety of designs, each producing slightly different sounds. Experiment with different calls to find one that suits your style and produces the desired sound.

  • Position the mouth call: Place the mouth call in the roof of your mouth, ensuring the reeds are facing down and touching your tongue. Adjust the call until it feels comfortable and secure.

  • Practice airflow and tongue control: To produce turkey sounds, control the amount of air passing through the call while manipulating your tongue to create different tones. Start with basic yelps and clucks, then move on to more advanced sounds like purrs and cuts.

  • Master realistic cadence and rhythm: Turkeys have a unique cadence and rhythm in their calls. Practice mimicking these patterns and replicating the natural pauses and breaks within each sound.

2. Friction Calls

Friction calls, also known as slate calls or pot calls, produce turkey sounds by rubbing a striker against a surface. The most common types of friction calls are slate, glass, and aluminum. Here’s how to effectively use them:

  • Choose the right friction call: Experiment with different surface materials and striker types to find the combination that produces the most realistic turkey sounds.

  • Grip the call and the striker: Hold the call between your thumb and fingers, ensuring a stable grip. Hold the striker as you would a pen or pencil, with a firm but relaxed grip.

  • Apply the right amount of pressure: Applying too much pressure can result in a harsh sound, while too little pressure may produce a weak sound. Find the sweet spot and adjust your pressure accordingly.

  • Create friction with smooth strokes: Glide the striker across the call’s surface, using smooth and consistent strokes. Vary your speed and pressure to create different turkey sounds.

  • Practice different techniques and angles: Experiment with various striking techniques and angles to produce different sounds. Some common techniques include circles, zigzags, and figure eights.

3. Box Calls

Box calls consist of two parts: a wooden box and a paddle that slides across it to create turkey sounds. They are relatively easy to use and can produce a range of turkey sounds, from soft yelps to loud cutts. Here’s how to use them effectively:

  • Hold the box call: Place the box call in your non-dominant hand, with your thumb on one side and your fingers on the other. Ensure a secure grip on the box.

  • Slide the paddle: Hold the paddle with your dominant hand and slide it across the box call’s surface. The speed and pressure of the slide will determine the pitch and volume of the turkey sound.

  • Experiment with different strokes: Vary the speed, pressure, and length of the paddle strokes to create different turkey sounds. Practice smooth and consistent movements to produce realistic calls.

  • Mimic natural pauses and breaks: Just like with mouth calls, try to replicate the natural cadence and rhythm of turkey calls. Introduce small pauses and breaks within your sequences to mimic the behavior of real turkeys.

Tips for Mastering Turkey Calling

  • Practice regularly: Consistent practice is key to mastering turkey calling. Set aside dedicated practice sessions and focus on improving specific sounds and techniques.

  • Listen to real turkeys: Spend time in the field observing and listening to real turkeys. Pay attention to their vocalizations, cadence, and rhythms. Try to imitate their sounds as closely as possible.

  • Start with basic sounds: Master the basic sounds like yelps, clucks, and purrs before moving on to more advanced techniques like cutting and kee-kee runs. Building a strong foundation will lead to more realistic and effective calling.

  • Use the right call for the situation: Different turkey sounds are effective in different hunting scenarios. Choose the right call for the situation to maximize your chances of success.

  • Be patient and adaptable: Not all turkeys will respond to your calls. If one technique doesn’t work, try another or change your calling strategy. Adaptability is key in turkey hunting.

  • Seek guidance from experienced hunters: Learning from experienced hunters can accelerate your progress. They can provide valuable insights, share tips and tricks, and help refine your calling techniques.

Conclusion

Mastering the art of turkey calling is a skill that can greatly enhance your turkey hunting experience. By becoming proficient in imitating turkey sounds, you can effectively communicate with these fascinating creatures and increase your chances of a successful hunt. Remember to practice consistently, listen to real turkeys, and experiment with different techniques and calls. With dedication and patience, you’ll soon become a master at mimicking turkey sounds, taking your hunting skills to the next level. Happy hunting!
