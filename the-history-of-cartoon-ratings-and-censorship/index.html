The History of Cartoon Ratings and Censorship


Cartoons have been a beloved form of entertainment for both children and adults for many decades. They have brought laughter, joy, and even life lessons to millions of people around the world. However, as with any form of media, cartoons have also faced their fair share of challenges when it comes to ratings and censorship. In this blog post, we will dive into the history of cartoon ratings and censorship, exploring how it has evolved over time.

Early Years: The Birth of Cartoon Ratings

The concept of rating cartoons initially emerged in the early 20th century with the rise of animated shorts in movie theaters. These early cartoons, such as those produced by animation pioneers like Walt Disney and Max Fleischer, were intended for general audiences. However, as the popularity of cartoons grew, concerns arose about their potential impact on young viewers.

To address these concerns, various organizations and regulatory bodies were established to establish guidelines for the content and appropriateness of cartoons. One of the earliest examples was the Hays Code in the 1930s, which focused primarily on live-action films but also included some provisions for animated content.

The Hays Code era brought about a more cautious approach to cartoon production, leading many animators to create stories that were light-hearted, innocent, and devoid of controversial themes. However, as the landscape of the media industry changed, these regulations needed to be revisited to adapt to the evolving tastes and norms of society.

Modern Times: The Evolution of Cartoon Ratings

In the 1960s, with the advent of television, cartoons began to air during morning and afternoon time slots specifically targeting children. This led to the creation of the Saturday morning cartoon phenomenon, which attracted a large audience of young viewers. With the rise of television, new challenges emerged surrounding the content and appropriateness of cartoons.

In response to the changing landscape, the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) in the United States introduced the Children’s Television Act in 1990. This act mandated that broadcasters provide educational and informational programming for children, setting the stage for stronger regulation of cartoon content.

At the same time, independent organizations like the Motion Picture Association (MPA) developed rating systems to provide guidance to parents and viewers. The most well-known of these is the MPAA film rating system, which assigns ratings such as G (General Audiences), PG (Parental Guidance), and PG-13 (Parents Strongly Cautioned).

The animation industry also took its own steps toward self-regulation. The creation of Nickelodeon’s Nicktoons in the 1990s, for example, sparked a new wave of creativity and originality in cartoons targeted at children. These cartoons often pushed boundaries and explored more mature themes, leading to the development of specific ratings within the cartoon industry itself.

In recent years, streaming platforms like Netflix and Hulu have become major players in the distribution of cartoon content. These platforms have introduced their own rating systems, allowing viewers to assess the appropriateness of the content for different age groups. Additionally, they often provide parental control features to help parents monitor and restrict access to certain shows.

The Impact on Cartoons and Society

The history of cartoon ratings and censorship has undoubtedly had a significant impact on the content and messaging of these beloved forms of entertainment. While the goals of these regulations are often noble, striking a balance between protecting vulnerable viewers and promoting artistic freedom can be challenging.

On one hand, ratings and censorship can help parents make informed decisions about the appropriateness of cartoons for their children. They can provide an extra layer of protection against potentially harmful or inappropriate content. On the other hand, stringent regulations can stifle creativity and limit the storytelling potential of cartoons.

As we move forward, it is crucial to continue having discussions about the role of ratings and censorship in the cartoon industry. Striking a balance between protection and creative freedom will be key to providing viewers with the highest quality and most diverse range of animated content.

In conclusion, the history of cartoon ratings and censorship is a complex and ever-evolving topic. From the earliest days of animated shorts in movie theaters to the modern era of streaming platforms, cartoons have faced numerous challenges in determining appropriate content for audiences. While rating systems and regulatory bodies have played a valuable role in protecting viewers, it is essential to find a delicate balance that ensures artistic freedom while safeguarding vulnerable audiences. By understanding and appreciating this history, we can continue to enjoy and support cartoons for generations to come.
