Cartoons and Cultural Representation: Celebrating Diversity through Animation


Cartoons and cultural representation have come a long way since the early days of animation. Though the golden age of cartoons saw the rise of animated classics such as Tom and Jerry, Looney Tunes, and Mickey Mouse, it is important to acknowledge the lack of diversity in these programs. The characters and stories were predominantly centered around the experiences and perspectives of white, male characters.

Thankfully, animation has come a long way since then. Today, animated shows and movies have become a powerful medium for celebrating diversity and representation. With characters and stories from all different cultures, races, religions, and backgrounds, a range of stories and perspectives are finally getting their chance to shine.

One of the most iconic examples of multicultural representation in animation is Disney’s Moana. The story of a Polynesian princess who sets out on a quest to save her island and her people, Moana is a shining example of how animation can bring a variety of cultures to a wider audience. It was praised for its accurate portrayal of Polynesian culture, and went on to become a critical and commercial success.

Similarly, the popular cartoon series Arthur has also been lauded for its representation of diverse cultures. With a cast of characters hailing from all different backgrounds, including African American, Latino, Asian American, and more, the show is a great example of how to normalize cultural diversity in media.

The way animation can be used to showcase different cultures has also been recognized by many governments. UNESCO, for instance, declared 2005 the International Year of Languages, and one of the tools they recommended to promote cultural diversity and multilingualism was animation. By using animation to tell stories in different languages and from different perspectives, we can promote cultural understanding and empathy.

One great example of how animation can promote cultural understanding is the Brazilian animated film Boy and the World. The film portrays the harsh reality of poverty in Brazil, through the eyes of its young protagonist. Through stunning visuals, the film showcases the beauty and challenges of Brazilian culture, and has won numerous awards for its representation of the country.

Another example of celebrating diversity through animation is the recent animated short film Hair Love. The film tells the story of a black father trying to style his daughter’s hair for the first time. The film takes on important themes of black fatherhood and the complexities and beauty of black hair, all while reminding viewers of the importance of representation and seeing themselves reflected in media.

In conclusion, cartoons and cultural representation have come a long way and are finally beginning to celebrate diversity through animation. From Disney’s Moana to Arthur, animation is providing an excellent opportunity to promote cultural understanding, empathy, and representation. With continued efforts in this field, we can ensure animated films and shows of all types are reflective of the multicultural, multilingual, and diverse world we live in.
