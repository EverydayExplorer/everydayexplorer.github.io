Surviving Avalanches: Safety Tips for Winter Backcountry Travel


Winter backcountry travel can be an exhilarating and rewarding experience, but it also comes with its fair share of risks. One of the most dangerous threats in the backcountry is avalanches. These powerful forces of nature can strike unexpectedly and have devastating consequences. However, with proper knowledge and preparation, you can increase your chances of surviving avalanches and enjoying your winter adventures safely. In this blog post, we will discuss essential safety tips for winter backcountry travel to help you navigate the snowy terrain with confidence.

1. Get educated: Before heading into the backcountry, it is crucial to educate yourself about avalanches. Take an avalanche safety course to learn about the different types of avalanches, how they form, and how to recognize warning signs. Understanding the basics of snow science and avalanche terrain will empower you to make informed decisions in the field.

2. Check the avalanche forecast: Stay updated on the current avalanche conditions by checking the local avalanche forecast. This information is usually provided by regional avalanche centers and can give you a good idea of the potential avalanche danger in the area. Avoid traveling in high-risk areas when the danger rating is elevated.

3. Travel with a group: Never venture into the backcountry alone, especially in avalanche terrain. Traveling with a group not only enhances the overall experience but also increases safety. Make sure everyone in your group has the necessary avalanche safety gear and knows how to use it.

4. Carry essential avalanche safety gear: Always carry the essential avalanche safety gear, including an avalanche transceiver (also known as a beacon), a collapsible shovel, and a probe. These tools are vital for locating and rescuing buried victims in the event of an avalanche. Practice using them regularly to maintain proficiency.

5. Assess the terrain: When traveling in the backcountry, pay close attention to the terrain features that may increase the risk of avalanches. Steep slopes, convex rolls, and areas with recent avalanche activity are all red flags. Avoid traveling on or below slopes with these characteristics.

6. Practice safe route-finding: Plan your route carefully, taking into account the current avalanche conditions and the terrain features. Opt for low-angle terrain when possible and avoid traveling in or under avalanche runout zones. If in doubt, it’s always better to choose a safer alternative route.

7. Use safe travel techniques: When traveling in avalanche terrain, use safe travel techniques to minimize your exposure. This includes traveling one at a time on suspect slopes, spreading out to reduce the impact of a potential avalanche, and avoiding unnecessary stops in hazardous areas.

8. Stay alert and observant: Constantly assess the snowpack and the changing conditions as you travel. Look for signs of instability, such as recent avalanches, cracking or collapsing of the snowpack, and whumpfing sounds. Trust your instincts and be prepared to alter your plans if conditions deteriorate.

9. Be prepared for self-rescue: Despite taking all necessary precautions, avalanches can still happen. If caught in an avalanche, try to stay on the surface by swimming or ‘fighting’ against the moving snow. As soon as it comes to a stop, try to create an air pocket in front of your face. Carry a whistle to alert rescuers and conserve your energy until help arrives.

10. Practice regular drills: Regularly practice avalanche rescue drills with your group to ensure everyone is familiar with the procedures and can respond effectively in an emergency. This includes searching with avalanche transceivers, probing, and digging.

Remember, surviving avalanches is a combination of knowledge, preparation, and sound decision-making. By following these safety tips and staying informed, you can minimize the risks and enjoy your winter backcountry travels to the fullest. Stay safe and happy exploring!
