Demystifying Wine Faults: Common Issues and How to Identify Them


Have you ever sipped a glass of wine and noticed something off about its taste or aroma? If so, you may have encountered a wine fault. Wine faults are undesirable characteristics that can occur during the winemaking process, affecting the overall quality and enjoyment of the wine. In this blog post, we will demystify some common wine faults, helping you identify them and understand their causes.

1. Cork Taint: One of the most well-known wine faults is cork taint, which is caused by the presence of a chemical compound called TCA (2,4,6-trichloroanisole). This compound can develop when natural cork interacts with certain bacteria and chlorine-based cleaning agents. The result is a musty, moldy aroma that can overpower the wine’s natural flavors.

To identify cork taint, pay attention to any damp or moldy smells when you open the bottle. If the wine smells like wet cardboard or a dank basement, it’s likely affected by cork taint.

2. Oxidation: Oxidation occurs when wine is exposed to excessive amounts of air. This can happen during the winemaking process or due to faulty storage conditions. Oxidized wine often loses its vibrant colors and develops a brownish hue. The flavors and aromas can become flat and dull, reminiscent of sherry or vinegar.

To identify oxidation, look for discoloration in the wine or a lack of fruitiness in its aroma and taste. If the wine tastes flat or vinegary, it’s likely oxidized.

3. Sulfur Compounds: Sulfur is commonly used in winemaking as a preservative, but when used excessively, it can lead to wine faults. Two common sulfur-related faults are hydrogen sulfide and mercaptans. Hydrogen sulfide produces a rotten egg smell, while mercaptans create aromas similar to burnt rubber or garlic.

To identify sulfur-related faults, trust your sense of smell. If the wine has any off-putting odors like rotten eggs or burnt rubber, it may be affected by sulfur compounds.

4. Volatile Acidity: Volatile acidity (VA) refers to the presence of acetic acid and other volatile compounds in wine. In small amounts, VA can contribute to a wine’s complexity, but excessive levels can result in unpleasant aromas, such as vinegar or nail polish remover.

To identify volatile acidity, pay attention to any sharp or vinegary smells. If the wine has a strong, pungent aroma reminiscent of vinegar, it may have high levels of volatile acidity.

5. Brettanomyces: Brettanomyces, often called ‘brett’ for short, is a yeast that can grow in wine and produce various aromas, including barnyard, Band-Aid, or sweaty horse blanket. Some wine enthusiasts enjoy these flavors in moderation, as they can add complexity. However, excessive brettanomyces can overpower the wine’s natural characteristics.

To identify brettanomyces, look for any unusual or funky smells. If the wine reminds you of a barn, a hospital, or a stable, it may be affected by brettanomyces.

Understanding these common wine faults and how to identify them can help you make informed decisions when purchasing, storing, and enjoying wine. Remember, wine faults are not necessarily a sign of poor-quality wine, but rather a result of various factors during the winemaking process. By being aware of these faults, you can enhance your wine tasting experience and appreciate the complexities of each bottle.

So, the next time you encounter an off-putting aroma or taste in your wine, you can confidently identify the fault and determine whether it’s a characteristic you enjoy or one that hinders your overall enjoyment. Cheers to demystifying wine faults and expanding your wine knowledge!
