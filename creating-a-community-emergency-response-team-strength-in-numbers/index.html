Creating a Community Emergency Response Team: Strength in numbers


Disasters can strike at any time, often without warning. Whether it’s a natural disaster like a hurricane, earthquake, or wildfire, or a man-made crisis such as a terrorist attack or industrial accident, communities need to be prepared to respond swiftly and effectively. This is where Community Emergency Response Teams (CERTs) come into play. In this blog, we’ll explore the importance of creating a Community Emergency Response Team and how the strength in numbers can make a significant difference in disaster response and recovery efforts.

What is a Community Emergency Response Team (CERT)?

A Community Emergency Response Team, or CERT, is a group of trained volunteers who assist emergency services and first responders during disasters. CERT programs were developed by the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) in the United States but have been adopted by communities worldwide. The primary goal of CERTs is to provide immediate assistance to victims and help organize volunteers at the disaster site when professional responders are not immediately available.

The Importance of CERTs

  1. Rapid Response: In the critical moments immediately following a disaster, professional responders may be overwhelmed or delayed. CERT members, who are trained to assess situations and provide basic medical treatment, can fill this gap, ensuring that those in need receive timely assistance.

  2. Local Expertise: CERT members are often residents of the community they serve. Their local knowledge is invaluable for navigating the area, identifying hazards, and understanding the specific needs of their neighbors.

  3. Resource Conservation: By having a CERT in place, professional responders can focus on more complex tasks, knowing that trained volunteers are handling less critical but still important functions. This efficient allocation of resources can save lives.

  4. Community Resilience: CERTs play a significant role in building community resilience. Training and preparing for disasters together foster a sense of community and solidarity among members. This can lead to a faster and more coordinated response during a crisis.

  5. Extended Reach: With the involvement of local volunteers, CERTs can reach areas that might be difficult for professional responders to access quickly, especially in large-scale disasters.

How to Create a CERT

Creating a CERT in your community involves several key steps:

1. Community Engagement

Begin by gauging interest and support within your community. Hold informational meetings or workshops to educate residents about CERTs and the benefits of volunteering.

2. Training

Training is a fundamental component of CERTs. Seek out local emergency management agencies or organizations that offer CERT training programs. Training typically covers disaster preparedness, fire safety, disaster medical operations, and more.

3. Recruitment

Recruit volunteers from your community who are willing to complete the training and commit to being part of the CERT. Diverse skills and backgrounds among members can be beneficial.

4. Organization

Establish a leadership structure within your CERT, including a coordinator, assistant coordinator, and other necessary roles. Develop clear communication and reporting protocols.

5. Equipment and Supplies

Secure the necessary equipment and supplies for your CERT. This may include first aid kits, communication tools, personal protective gear, and basic rescue equipment.

6. Exercises and Drills

Regularly conduct exercises and drills to ensure that CERT members are well-prepared and can respond effectively in various disaster scenarios.

7. Collaboration

Collaborate with local emergency services, government agencies, and community organizations to integrate your CERT into the larger emergency response framework.
