Why Does Cutting Onions Make You Cry? Understanding the Chemistry of Tears


Have you ever wondered why cutting onions can bring tears to your eyes? This seemingly simple kitchen task can turn into a teary affair, leaving you with red eyes and a runny nose. The culprit behind this crying phenomenon lies in the chemistry of tears. In this article, we will explore the fascinating science behind why onions evoke such an emotional response and discover ways to minimize the tears.

The tear-inducing effects of onions can be attributed to a compound called syn-propanethial-S-oxide. When an onion is cut or crushed, the cells of the vegetable are damaged, releasing this volatile sulfur compound into the air. As the syn-propanethial-S-oxide comes into contact with the surface of your eyes, it stimulates the lacrimal glands, causing them to produce tears.

But why do we cry when our eyes are exposed to certain chemicals? Tears play a crucial role in maintaining the health and lubrication of our eyes. They contain a variety of substances, including water, electrolytes, enzymes, and proteins. Tears are produced in three different layers: the mucous layer, the aqueous layer, and the lipid layer. Each layer serves a unique purpose in protecting and nourishing our eyes.

When irritants like syn-propanethial-S-oxide come into contact with our eyes, they disrupt the delicate balance of these tear layers. As a defense mechanism, our eyes activate a reflex arc that leads to an increased production of tears. This surge in tear production helps to dilute and flush out the irritants, thus protecting the eyes from potential harm.

In addition to syn-propanethial-S-oxide, other compounds found in onions can also contribute to the tear-inducing effects. Enzymes such as alliinase and alliin are released when an onion is cut, forming sulfenic acids. These sulfenic acids then react with each other to form a volatile gas known as propanethial-S-oxide. This gas is released into the air, where it can reach our eyes and trigger tear production.

While it may seem impossible to prevent tears while cutting onions, there are a few strategies you can try to minimize the discomfort. One method involves chilling the onion before cutting. By placing the onion in the refrigerator or freezer for 30 minutes prior to cutting, the cold temperature can help inhibit the release of syn-propanethial-S-oxide, reducing its concentration in the air.

Another technique is to cut onions under running water or in a bowl filled with water. Water can help dissolve and absorb the syn-propanethial-S-oxide, minimizing its contact with your eyes. However, it’s important to note that this method may not completely eliminate tears, as other onion compounds may still contribute to tear production.

Wearing protective eyewear, such as goggles or glasses, can also serve as a barrier between your eyes and the irritant compounds. This can be especially helpful if you frequently find yourself cutting onions or if you have a heightened sensitivity to the tear-inducing effects.

In conclusion, the tears we shed while cutting onions are a result of a complex chemical reaction. The release of syn-propanethial-S-oxide and other compounds from onions triggers a reflex response in our eyes, leading to increased tear production. Understanding the chemistry behind this teary process can help us find effective ways to minimize the discomfort. Whether it’s chilling the onion, cutting under water, or wearing protective eyewear, experimenting with different methods can make your next onion-cutting experience a more pleasant one. So the next time you reach for that knife and onion, arm yourself with the knowledge of tears and tackle those culinary tasks with confidence.
