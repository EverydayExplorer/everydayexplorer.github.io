The Chemistry of Breathalyzers: Measuring Blood Alcohol Levels


When it comes to measuring blood alcohol levels, breathalyzers play a crucial role in ensuring safety on the roads and maintaining law and order. These handheld devices have become a staple in the arsenal of law enforcement officers, allowing them to quickly and easily determine if a driver is under the influence of alcohol. But have you ever wondered how breathalyzers work? What goes on behind the scenes to provide an accurate measurement of blood alcohol content (BAC)? In this blog post, we will delve into the chemistry of breathalyzers and explore the science behind these innovative devices.

Breath alcohol testing is based on the principle of ethanol metabolism in the human body. When alcohol is consumed, it is absorbed into the bloodstream through the walls of the stomach and small intestine. From there, it is distributed throughout the body, including the lungs. As blood circulates through the lungs, a portion of the alcohol evaporates into the air sacs (alveoli) within the lungs. These alcohol molecules then pass into the breath, providing a direct link between blood alcohol levels and the concentration of alcohol in the breath.

Breathalyzers utilize a chemical reaction known as oxidation to measure the amount of alcohol present in a person’s breath. One of the main chemicals used in this reaction is potassium dichromate (K2Cr2O7). When alcohol is introduced to the breathalyzer, it comes into contact with the potassium dichromate, resulting in a chemical reaction.

During this reaction, the ethanol in the breath is oxidized by the dichromate ion (Cr2O7-2) to form acetic acid (CH3COOH) and water (H2O). This oxidation process leads to a change in the color of the dichromate solution, which serves as the basis for determining the blood alcohol content. The more alcohol present in the breath, the greater the color change in the solution.

The color change is typically measured using a photometer, which detects the intensity of light passing through the solution. As the amount of alcohol increases, the intensity of light passing through the solution decreases. By comparing the intensity of light before and after the reaction, the breathalyzer can calculate the blood alcohol content.

It’s important to note that breathalyzers are calibrated to provide an estimate of blood alcohol content rather than a precise measurement. There are several factors that can influence the accuracy of a breathalyzer reading, including the temperature of the breath, the presence of substances other than alcohol in the breath, and the individual’s physiology. As a result, breathalyzer results are often supplemented with blood tests for definitive measurements of blood alcohol levels.

In recent years, advancements in technology have led to the development of more accurate and reliable breathalyzers. Some devices now use fuel cell sensors instead of chemical reactions to measure blood alcohol content. Fuel cell sensors rely on the electrochemical reaction between ethanol and oxygen to generate an electrical current. The magnitude of this current is directly proportional to the amount of alcohol in the breath, allowing for precise measurements.

In conclusion, breathalyzers represent an important tool in detecting and deterring drunk driving. The chemistry behind these devices involves the oxidation of ethanol in the breath, leading to a color change that can be measured and used to estimate blood alcohol content. While breathalyzers provide a valuable initial assessment of a driver’s intoxication level, it’s crucial to remember that they are not infallible and can be influenced by various factors. Therefore, it’s always advisable to exercise caution and never drink and drive.
