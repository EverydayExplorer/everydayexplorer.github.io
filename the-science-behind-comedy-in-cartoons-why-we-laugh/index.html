The Science Behind Comedy in Cartoons: Why We Laugh


Comedy has been an integral part of our entertainment industry for decades, and cartoons have consistently tickled our funny bones with their clever gags, witty dialogue, and humorous situations. Have you ever wondered why we find certain things funny? Is there a science behind comedy in cartoons that makes us laugh?

The answer is yes! Comedy, including that found in cartoons, is rooted in various scientific principles that tap into our psychology, cognition, and emotions. Let’s delve into the science behind comedy in cartoons and explore why we find them so amusing.

1. Surprise and Incongruity: One of the key elements of comedy is surprise. Cartoons often present unexpected situations, absurd scenarios, or sudden twists that catch us off guard. Our brains are wired to seek patterns and predict outcomes, so when something unexpected happens, it triggers a momentary cognitive dissonance. This incongruity between our expectations and reality is what makes us laugh.

2. Incongruity-resolution Theory: Developed by psychologists, the incongruity-resolution theory suggests that humor arises when there is a conflict between two mental representations. In cartoons, this conflict can be between visual cues, such as exaggerated movements or distorted proportions, and the established norms of reality. The resolution of this incongruity through a punchline or comedic twist brings about a sense of relief and amusement.

3. Superiority Theory: Another theory states that comedy in cartoons often relies on the concept of superiority. We find humor in situations where we feel superior to the characters or situations depicted. This theory explains why slapstick comedy, where characters endure embarrassing or physically comical situations, evokes laughter. It allows us to momentarily feel better about ourselves by comparing ourselves favorably to the characters’ misfortunes.

4. Release of Tension: Laughter acts as a release valve for pent-up tension or stress. Cartoons often incorporate tense situations or conflicts that are resolved in a humorous way. The relief we experience when tension is diffused through comedy triggers laughter, providing us with a sense of catharsis and emotional release.

5. Incongruity and Benign Violation: According to the incongruity-based theories, comedy arises from benign violations of societal norms or expectations. Cartoons often depict outrageous situations, absurdities, or playful transgressions that would be considered inappropriate in real life. By breaking these norms in a harmless and non-threatening manner, cartoons make us laugh by challenging our assumptions about what is acceptable.

6. Mirror Neurons and Empathy: Mirror neurons in our brains are responsible for imitating and understanding the actions and emotions of others. When we watch cartoons, especially those that feature relatable characters, our mirror neurons fire, enabling us to empathize with the humorous situations on screen. This empathetic connection enhances the comedic effect, as we laugh not only at the characters but also at ourselves.

7. Wordplay and Linguistic Humor: In addition to visual humor, language plays a significant role in comedy. Cartoons often employ puns, wordplay, double entendre, and clever dialogue to evoke laughter. These linguistic devices exploit the multiple meanings of words, create ambiguity, and rely on our cognitive abilities to decipher the intended humor.

As we can see, the science behind comedy in cartoons is a fascinating subject that intertwines various psychological, cognitive, and emotional factors. From surprise and incongruity to theories of superiority and relief, these elements work together to create hilarious moments that make us laugh.

Next time you find yourself chuckling at a cartoon, remember that it’s not just a random occurrence. It’s the result of a carefully crafted blend of scientific principles aimed at tickling your funny bone. So sit back, relax, and enjoy the scientific marvel that is comedy in cartoons!
