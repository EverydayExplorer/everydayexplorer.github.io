The Green Children of Woolpit: Medieval Enigma


In the small village of Woolpit, located in the county of Suffolk, England, a strange and perplexing mystery unfolded during the 12th century. The case of the Green Children of Woolpit has fascinated historians, researchers, and enthusiasts for centuries, as it defies explanation and adds an air of mystique to the medieval era.

According to the folklore surrounding this enigmatic story, the events unfolded in the village of Woolpit in the year 1150. It is said that two children, a brother and sister, suddenly appeared near a local wolf pit, hence the name ‘Woolpit.’ What made them unique and truly bizarre was their unusual green skin color.

The children, who were later named Agnes and William, had never been seen before in Woolpit, and their foreign appearance startled the villagers. However, their striking skin tone was not the only peculiar aspect of their arrival. The children spoke an entirely unfamiliar language and seemed to be disoriented by their unfamiliar surroundings.

It is alleged that the bewildered siblings were found by a local landowner, Sir Richard de Caine, who immediately took them into his care. At first, the children refused to eat any food other than raw broad beans, which further contributed to their mysterious nature.

Despite their oddities, Agnes and William managed to adapt to their new environment over time. As they learned the English language and the customs of Woolpit, they eventually shared an intriguing tale of their origins. According to the children, they hailed from a place called ‘St. Martin’s Land,’ situated underground.

The young siblings described their homeland as being perpetually twilight, with a luminous green landscape. They claimed to have inadvertently found themselves in Woolpit after following the sound of bells. The children stated that they emerged from a cavernous opening into the daylight and soon found themselves in a place unfamiliar and bewildering to them.

As the story spread throughout Woolpit, it attracted the attention of neighboring villages and eventually reached the ears of King Henry II himself in Westminster. Intrigued by the mysterious green children, the king had them relocated to his court, where they could be closely examined.

Sadly, history does not provide us with a record of what became of Agnes and William beyond their time at court. It is believed that both children eventually adapted to their new lives and were able to integrate into society.

Numerous theories have emerged over the centuries to explain the origins and nature of the Green Children of Woolpit. Some speculations suggest that the children may have been extraterrestrial beings or supernatural creatures trapped between realms. Others propose that they were simply suffering from a rare medical condition that caused their skin to take on a greenish hue.

One particularly intriguing theory suggests that the children may have been survivors from the lost realm of Atlantis. Proponents of this theory argue that the children’s description of St. Martin’s Land, with its unusual twilight and green landscape, parallels ancient accounts of the mythical island civilization.

Regardless of the truth behind their origins, the story of the Green Children of Woolpit continues to captivate the minds of historians and enthusiasts alike. The enigma surrounding their appearance and disappearance serves as a reminder of the vast mysteries that still remain hidden within the annals of history.

In conclusion, the tale of the Green Children of Woolpit stands as an enduring medieval enigma. The inexplicable green skin color, unfamiliar language, and peculiar origin story have baffled generations of scholars and remain a topic of fascination today. While we may never uncover the truth behind this mysterious event, the story serves as a testament to the boundless nature of the human imagination and the enduring allure of ancient mysteries.
