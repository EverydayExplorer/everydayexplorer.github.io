The Cognitive Psychology of Stress and Coping


Stress is an inevitable part of everyday life. We all experience it, regardless of our status, age, or occupation. While a moderate amount of stress can motivate us to perform better, too much stress can take a toll on our emotional, physical, and cognitive well-being. This leads us to coping mechanisms, which can either be adaptive or maladaptive. In this blog, we will delve into the cognitive psychology of stress and coping, exploring the different theories used to explain the causes of stress, coping strategies, and their outcomes.

Cognitive Appraisal Theory

According to the cognitive appraisal theory, stress is not solely caused by external events but also, perceptions. Stress results from an individual’s cognitive evaluation of the situation and their response to it. Two main appraisals are used to explain stress; primary appraisal and secondary appraisal.

Primary appraisal involves an initial evaluation of the situation, critically answering whether it is relevant to one’s well-being and what the implications are. This evaluation then leads to two possible appraisals; harm or loss or potential harm or gain. For instance, losing a loved one can result in harm or loss appraisal, while a job interview can lead to potential harm or gain appraisal.

Secondary appraisal involves an assessment of the resources available to cope with the situation at hand. This includes physical, emotional, social, and cognitive resources. People who perceive more resources have better control over their stressors, and the risk of experiencing chronic stress is reduced.

Transactional Model of Stress

This theory highlights the reciprocal relationship between stressors and coping mechanisms. Stressors are events or situations that are perceived as demanding and exceed a person’s available resources. Coping mechanisms refer to the strategies used to manage or alleviate these stressors. The transactional model of stress emphasizes the importance of cognitive appraisal and coping skills in managing stress effectively.

Coping Mechanisms

Individuals use several coping mechanisms to manage stress. These mechanisms are either maladaptive or adaptive. Maladaptive coping mechanisms worsen the stress response and lead to negative outcomes, while adaptive coping mechanisms result in positive outcomes.

Examples of maladaptive coping mechanisms are; avoidance, substance use, and self-blame. Avoidance coping involves withdrawing from the situation. Substance use or abuse involves masking the stressor’s pain with drugs or alcohol. Self-blame involves taking undue responsibility for a situation, leading to low self-efficacy and lower coping skills.

Examples of adaptive coping mechanisms are; problem-solving, social support, and emotional regulation. Problem-solving involves identifying the stressor, brainstorming possible solutions, evaluating the pros and cons of each solution, and selecting the best outcome. Social support refers to the emotional, practical, and social help an individual receives from others. Emotional regulation involves managing one’s emotions in a way that promotes well-being, such as meditation and mindfulness.

Conclusion

Stress and coping are inevitable parts of life. While stress is inevitable, how an individual responds to it can be controlled. The cognitive psychology of stress and coping emphasizes the importance of cognitive appraisals in managing stressors and the importance of coping skills in promoting positive outcomes. Identifying adaptive and maladaptive coping mechanisms is essential in managing stressors effectively. It is vital that individuals learn to evaluate their stressors effectively and select the best adaptive coping mechanism to alleviate their stress.
