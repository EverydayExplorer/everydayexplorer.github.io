Understanding Wine Faults: How to Identify and Rectify Common Issues


Wine is a beautiful and complex beverage that is enjoyed by people all over the world. From its creation in the vineyard to its fermentation and aging process, the journey of wine is filled with intricacies that contribute to its unique flavors and aromas. However, sometimes things don’t go as planned, and wine can develop faults that affect its taste and quality. In this blog post, we will explore some common wine faults, how to identify them, and what can be done to rectify these issues.

1. Oxidation: Oxidation occurs when wine is overexposed to oxygen, resulting in a loss of freshness and vibrancy. Signs of oxidation include a brownish tint in white wines, a browning rim in red wines, and a flat or dull taste. To prevent oxidation, it is crucial to store wines properly by keeping them away from excessive heat and light. Once oxidation has occurred, there is no way to reverse it completely. However, you can mask the effects by serving the wine with food or using it for cooking.

2. Cork Taint: Cork taint is one of the most well-known wine faults and is caused by a compound called TCA (2,4,6-trichloroanisole). TCA can develop in corks or other wooden surfaces and contaminate the wine, leading to a musty, damp cardboard smell. To identify cork taint, look for a moldy or wet cardboard aroma when you sniff the wine. Unfortunately, there is no way to fix cork-tainted wine, and the only solution is to open a new bottle.

3. Reduction: Reduction occurs when a wine has been exposed to too little oxygen, leading to a ‘stinky’ or rotten egg-like smell. This fault is more common in wines that have been bottled under a screw cap or a very tight cork. To rectify reduction, you can pour the wine into a decanter and let it ‘breathe’ for a while. In some cases, swirling the wine in the glass can also help release some of the volatile compounds causing the odor.

4. Volatile Acidity: Volatile acidity (VA) is the presence of excessive acetic acid in a wine, resulting in a vinegar-like smell. While a slight amount of VA can contribute to the complexity of certain wines, too much can be off-putting. To identify VA, look for a sharp, vinegary aroma. If the VA is too high, there is not much that can be done to rectify it. However, blending the wine with another less acidic wine can help balance the flavors.

5. Sulfur Compounds: Sulfur compounds can develop in wine due to the addition of sulfur dioxide during winemaking. In small amounts, sulfur can act as a preservative, but excessive amounts can result in off-putting aromas, such as burnt rubber or rotten eggs. If you detect these smells, you can try decanting the wine or swirling it vigorously to dissipate the sulfur compounds.

6. Bacterial Contamination: Bacterial contamination can lead to various faults in wine, such as the development of hydrogen sulfide (H2S) or a sour taste due to the presence of acetic acid. These faults can be caused by poor hygiene during winemaking or inadequate sanitation of equipment. To prevent bacterial contamination, winemakers need to follow proper hygiene practices and ensure that all equipment is thoroughly cleaned and sanitized.

In conclusion, understanding wine faults is essential for both wine enthusiasts and professionals. By knowing how to identify and rectify common wine faults, you can maximize your enjoyment of this beautiful beverage. Remember to store your wines correctly, pay attention to any off-putting aromas or tastes, and take appropriate actions to rectify the issues when possible. Cheers to a world of fault-free wine enjoyment!
