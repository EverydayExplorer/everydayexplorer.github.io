The Spanish Inquisition: Persecution and Religious Control


The Spanish Inquisition stands as one of the most notorious chapters in European history, characterized by its ruthless pursuit of religious purity and control. Spanning over three centuries from the late 15th to the early 19th century, this institution aimed to eradicate any form of deviation from Catholic orthodoxy in Spain. The consequences were devastating for countless individuals who fell victim to persecution, torture, and even death.

Origins and Establishment
The origins of the Spanish Inquisition can be traced back to Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile, often referred to as the ‘Catholic Monarchs.’ Seeking a unified Christian kingdom after centuries of Muslim rule on the Iberian Peninsula, they established their authority through various means including religious uniformity. Thus, in 1478, Pope Sixtus IV granted them permission to establish an ecclesiastical tribunal known as ‘La Santa Hermandad’ (the Holy Brotherhood), which later became infamous as the Spanish Inquisition.

Persecution Targets: Conversos and Moriscos
One primary target for persecution during the Spanish Inquisition was conversos - Jews who had converted to Christianity under duress or voluntarily but faced suspicion regarding their sincerity. Many conversos held influential positions within society due to their wealth or education. They were accused of secretly practicing Judaism or engaging in heretical activities that threatened Catholic dominance. The trials against conversos often relied on coerced confessions obtained through torture.

Similarly targeted were moriscos – Muslims who had also been forcibly converted or their descendants living within Christian territories after Islamic rule ended in Spain. Their cultural practices like language use or dress choices were seen as indications that they maintained Muslim beliefs clandestinely.

Methods Employed: Fear Tactics and Torture
To maintain control over society’s religious fabric, fear tactics played a crucial role throughout this dark period. Informants were encouraged to report suspected heretics, and anonymous accusations often led to arrests. Once apprehended, individuals accused of heresy or blasphemy faced a range of interrogation techniques designed to extract confessions. These methods included torture devices such as the strappado (a painful suspension from the ceiling), waterboarding, or even burning at the stake.
