Exploring the Different Types of IPAs: From West Coast to Hazy


Craft beer enthusiasts are no strangers to the world of IPAs. These hop-forward beers have taken the brewing scene by storm, with their bold flavors and aromatic profiles. While IPAs share a common foundation, there are distinct variations that cater to different palates. In this blog post, we will dive into the world of IPAs and explore the different types, from the classic West Coast IPA to the trendy Hazy IPA.

1. West Coast IPA: Let’s start with the OG - the West Coast IPA. This style emerged in the 1970s and quickly became synonymous with American craft beer. West Coast IPAs are known for their strong hop bitterness and piney, citrusy flavors. The hops used in these beers are typically sourced from the Pacific Northwest, giving them a distinct aroma and resinous character. The West Coast IPA is a favorite among hop heads who crave that bitter punch and crisp finish.

2. East Coast IPA: As the craft beer movement spread across the United States, the East Coast put its own spin on the IPA style. East Coast IPAs, also known as New England IPAs or Juicy IPAs, prioritize juicy, tropical fruit flavors and a hazy appearance. These beers are often less bitter than their West Coast counterparts, with a smooth mouthfeel and a creamy, almost pulpy texture. East Coast IPAs are a hit among those seeking a fruit-forward, less aggressive IPA experience.

3. English IPA: Before the American craft beer revolution, there was the English IPA. This style has a rich history dating back to the 19th century. English IPAs are known for their balance between malt sweetness and hop bitterness. They often showcase earthy, floral hop flavors and a biscuity malt backbone. While not as hop-forward as their American counterparts, English IPAs offer a more nuanced and traditional take on the IPA style.

4. Belgian IPA: Belgium, the land of Trappist ales and farmhouse beers, couldn’t resist putting its own twist on the IPA. Belgian IPAs combine the fruity esters and spicy phenols of Belgian yeast with the hop-forward nature of an IPA. The result is a unique beer that intertwines floral, herbal hop flavors with the complex yeast characteristics found in Belgian ales. Belgian IPAs strike a delicate balance between the two worlds, offering a refreshing and intriguing drinking experience.

5. Session IPA: For those looking for a lighter, more sessionable IPA, the Session IPA is the answer. These beers maintain the hop-forward nature of IPAs but with a lower alcohol content, making them perfect for extended drinking sessions. Session IPAs often have a more balanced malt profile, allowing the hop flavors to shine without overwhelming the palate. These beers are a great option for those who want the hoppy goodness of an IPA without the high ABV.

6. Double IPA: If you thought regular IPAs were bold, then brace yourself for the Double IPA. Also known as Imperial IPAs, these beers take everything you love about IPAs and amplify it. Double IPAs feature an intense hop bitterness, higher alcohol content, and a robust malt backbone to balance out the hop onslaught. These beers are not for the faint of heart, but for those seeking a hop bomb that packs a punch.

As you can see, the IPA family is diverse and offers something for everyone. Whether you prefer the classic West Coast IPA with its bitter bite or the hazy, juicy goodness of a New England IPA, there’s an IPA out there waiting to tantalize your taste buds. So go ahead, explore the different types of IPAs and embark on a hop-filled adventure. Cheers!
