A Guide to Regional American Whiskey: From Kentucky Bourbon to Tennessee Whiskey


When it comes to American whiskey, there’s a rich tapestry of flavors and traditions that vary from region to region. From the iconic Kentucky bourbon to the smoothness of Tennessee whiskey, each style has its unique characteristics that make it distinct and beloved by whiskey enthusiasts worldwide. In this guide, we will take you on a journey through the different regions of American whiskey, exploring their history, production methods, and notable distilleries. So grab a glass and let’s dive into the world of regional American whiskey.

Kentucky Bourbon: The King of American Whiskey

Kentucky bourbon is often considered the pinnacle of American whiskey. Its roots can be traced back to the late 18th century when Scottish, Irish, and German immigrants settled in Kentucky and brought their distilling traditions with them. However, it wasn’t until the 19th century that bourbon as we know it today began to take shape.

What sets Kentucky bourbon apart is its strict production regulations. By law, it must be made from a grain mixture containing at least 51% corn and aged in new, charred oak barrels. It also needs to be distilled to no more than 160 proof (80% alcohol by volume) and entered into the barrel for aging at no more than 125 proof (62.5% alcohol by volume). The aging process imparts the deep amber color and complex flavors that bourbon is known for.

The limestone-rich water found in Kentucky is also believed to contribute to the unique flavor profile of the bourbon produced in the state. Known as the ‘Bourbon Belt,’ Kentucky is home to some of the most renowned distilleries, including Maker’s Mark, Woodford Reserve, and Jim Beam. Each distillery puts its own spin on the classic bourbon recipe, resulting in a wide range of flavors and styles within the Kentucky bourbon category.

Tennessee Whiskey: Charcoal Mellowing for Unmatched Smoothness

Just south of Kentucky lies Tennessee, another region with a rich whiskey-making history. Tennessee whiskey shares many similarities with bourbon, but it has its own distinctive twist. The key difference lies in the charcoal filtering process known as the ‘Lincoln County Process.’

Before aging, Tennessee whiskey is filtered through charcoal made from sugar maple trees. This process, named after Lincoln County, Tennessee, where it was originally developed, removes impurities and imparts a distinctly smooth character to the whiskey. The filtered spirit is then aged in new, charred oak barrels, much like bourbon.

Jack Daniel’s is the most famous name associated with Tennessee whiskey. Founded in 1866, the Jack Daniel’s Distillery has become an American icon. Their Old No. 7 brand is known for its signature charcoal-mellowed flavor, which sets it apart from bourbon and other whiskeys. Other notable Tennessee whiskey producers include George Dickel and Nelson’s Green Brier Distillery.

Other Regional American Whiskeys

While Kentucky bourbon and Tennessee whiskey dominate the American whiskey scene, there are other regional varieties worth exploring:

1. Rye Whiskey: Popularized in the northeastern United States, rye whiskey is made primarily from rye grain. It has a spicier and drier flavor compared to bourbon and Tennessee whiskey. Some well-known rye whiskey brands include Rittenhouse, Bulleit, and Sazerac.

2. Corn Whiskey: As the name suggests, corn whiskey is made predominantly from corn. It has a sweeter and lighter taste compared to bourbon. Corn whiskey does not have the aging requirements that bourbon does, resulting in a more straightforward and youthful spirit.

3. Malt Whiskey: While Scotland is renowned for its malt whisky, the United States also produces its own version. Malt whiskey is made primarily from malted barley and often showcases rich, toasty flavors. Stranahan’s Colorado Whiskey and Westland Distillery are leading names in American malt whiskey production.

4. Craft Distilleries: In recent years, the craft distilling movement has gained significant momentum across the United States. Small-scale, artisanal distilleries are popping up in various states, offering unique and innovative expressions of American whiskey. These distilleries often experiment with different grains, aging techniques, and flavor profiles, adding a refreshing diversity to the American whiskey landscape.

In Conclusion

American whiskey is a testament to the rich distilling heritage of the United States. From the iconic Kentucky bourbon to the smoothness of Tennessee whiskey and the individuality of regional variations, there’s a whiskey for every palate. Whether you prefer the robust flavors of bourbon or the charcoal-mellowed smoothness of Tennessee whiskey, exploring the diverse world of regional American whiskey is an adventure worth embarking on. So raise your glass, savor the flavors, and enjoy the journey through the fascinating history and traditions of American whiskey.
