Hunting Turkeys: Strategies for Spring Gobblers


Spring is an exciting time for hunters, as it marks the beginning of turkey season. The challenge of hunting these elusive birds and the reward of a successful hunt make it a favorite pastime for many outdoor enthusiasts.

In this blog post, we will explore some effective strategies for targeting spring gobblers. Whether you are a seasoned turkey hunter or a beginner, these tips will help you improve your chances of bagging that trophy tom.

1. Scout the Area

Scouting is an essential part of any successful hunt. Before heading out, take the time to observe the turkeys’ patterns and habits in your hunting area. Look for signs such as scratch marks, droppings, and feathers. Pay attention to their roosting sites, feeding areas, and travel routes. This information will give you a better understanding of where the birds are most likely to be during different times of the day.

2. Use Locating Calls

Once you have identified a potential hunting spot, it’s time to locate the turkeys. Using locator calls, such as owl hooters or crow calls, can help you pinpoint their locations. Turkeys are naturally curious birds and will often respond to these calls by gobbling. This will give you an idea of where to set up your blind or position yourself for an ambush.

3. Master the Art of Turkey Calling

Turkey calling is an integral part of hunting turkey and can be a make-or-break factor for success. There are various turkey calls available, including box calls, slate calls, diaphragm calls, and mouth calls. Take the time to practice different techniques to create realistic hen calls, gobbling sounds, and clucks. Mimicking the sounds of a turkey can attract nearby gobblers and increase your chances of a successful hunt.

4. Set Up Decoys

Decoys are an excellent tool for luring turkeys into your shooting range. A lifelike decoy setup can make a gobbler believe that there is another turkey in the area, sparking its territorial instincts. Use a hen decoy to attract the attention of a tom and position it strategically, with the wind in your favor. Decoys coupled with realistic calls can be a deadly combination.

5. Patience is Key

Turkey hunting requires a lot of patience. After setting up your blind or positioning yourself, it’s crucial to remain still and quiet. Turkeys have incredible eyesight and can detect the slightest movement. Avoid unnecessary fidgeting or rustling, as this can scare off the birds. It’s also important to wait for the perfect shot. Gobblers can be wary and may hang up just out of range. Be patient and let them come to you.

6. Hunt During the Right Time of Day

Turkeys are most active early in the morning and late in the afternoon. These are the prime times for gobblers to strut, display, and search for hens. Plan your hunts accordingly, and be in your preferred hunting spot before first light. When it comes to turkey hunting, the early bird truly does get the worm.

7. Understand Turkey Behavior

Knowing how turkeys behave can give you a significant advantage during your hunt. In the spring, gobblers search for hens to breed with, and they will often respond to calls made by hen decoys or hunters. Understanding their mating rituals and behavior patterns will help you choose the best tactics for attracting them.

8. Stay Concealed and Practice Safety

Hunting turkey requires a high level of concealment. Wear camouflage clothing that matches your surroundings, and make sure to cover your face and hands. Turkeys have excellent vision and can easily spot a hunter if they are not properly hidden. Additionally, always practice firearm safety and be aware of your surroundings, especially when other hunters are nearby.

In conclusion, hunting turkeys during the spring season can be an exhilarating experience for any hunter. By scouting the area, mastering turkey calls, using decoys, and understanding turkey behavior, you can significantly increase your chances of success. Remember to stay patient, remain concealed, and practice safety at all times. Now, get out there and bag that trophy tom!
