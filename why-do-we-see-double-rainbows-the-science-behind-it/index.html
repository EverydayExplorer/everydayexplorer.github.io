Why do we see double rainbows? The science behind it


Have you ever looked up at the sky after a rainstorm and been mesmerized by the sight of not just one, but two vibrant rainbows? This phenomenon, known as a double rainbow, is a fascinating display of nature’s wonders. Double rainbows have been the subject of folklore, poetry, and even song lyrics, but have you ever wondered why we see them? In this blog post, we will uncover the science behind double rainbows and explore the factors that contribute to their formation.

To understand the science behind double rainbows, we need to start with the basics of how rainbows form. Rainbows occur when sunlight is refracted, or bent, by water droplets in the air. As sunlight passes through a raindrop, it undergoes both refraction and internal reflection. This causes the light to split into its constituent colors, forming a circular arc of vivid hues.

Now, let’s dive deeper into the formation of double rainbows. Double rainbows occur when there is a secondary reflection within raindrops, resulting in a second, fainter rainbow appearing outside the primary rainbow. This secondary reflection creates an additional ray of light that refracts back towards the observer.

To understand why the secondary reflection occurs, we must consider how light behaves within a water droplet. When light enters a raindrop, it undergoes refraction and is reflected internally multiple times before exiting the droplet. This creates a circular path for the light, with the primary reflection forming the primary rainbow.

However, for the secondary reflection to occur, the light must undergo an additional internal reflection before leaving the droplet. This secondary reflection follows a more elongated path, resulting in a larger radius for the secondary rainbow. The additional reflection also causes the colors in the secondary rainbow to appear in reverse order compared to the primary rainbow.

The key factor in the formation of double rainbows is the angle at which the sunlight enters the raindrops. The angle of incidence plays a crucial role in determining the size, intensity, and visibility of the rainbows. If the light enters the raindrops at a high angle, the secondary reflection becomes more prominent and a double rainbow may be observable. However, if the angle is too low, the secondary reflection may be washed out, resulting in only a single rainbow being visible.

The colors of a double rainbow are also worth noting. The primary and secondary rainbows consist of the same colors, but the secondary rainbow appears dimmer due to the additional reflection and dispersion of light. The secondary rainbow also has its colors in reverse order, with red on the outer edge and violet on the inner edge. This reversal occurs because the light undergoes an extra refraction when leaving the droplet on its elongated path.

In conclusion, double rainbows are a breathtaking phenomenon that occurs when sunlight is refracted and internally reflected multiple times within raindrops. The secondary reflection creates a second, fainter rainbow with reversed colors. The angle of incidence of the sunlight, as well as the path length of the internal reflection, play key roles in the formation and visibility of double rainbows. So next time you spot a vibrant double rainbow after a rain shower, take a moment to appreciate the intricate science behind this captivating sight.
