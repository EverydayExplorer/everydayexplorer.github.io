The Challenges of Protecting Our Planet from Space Debris and Near-Earth Objects: Prevention and Mitigation Strategies


For millennia, humans have gazed at the stars and wondered about their nature and role in the universe. But with the advent of space exploration and exploitation, we have also started to affect the space environment and the objects that inhabit it, sometimes unintentionally or negligibly. One of the most pressing challenges of our time is the protection of our planet from space debris and near-Earth objects (NEOs), which can pose serious risks to our lives, property, and activities in space and on the ground. In this blog, we will discuss some of the key issues and strategies involved in preventing and mitigating this danger.

What are space debris and NEOs?

Space debris, also known as space junk, refers to human-made objects that orbit the Earth but no longer serve a useful purpose. They can range from tiny fragments of paint, wires, and screws to large rocket stages, satellites, and even space stations. According to the European Space Agency (ESA), there are over 34,000 objects larger than 10 cm in low Earth orbit (LEO), and millions of smaller ones that can still cause damage to spacecraft or satellites. Each year, thousands of new debris objects are created through collisions, explosions, or deliberate destruction. The density of debris in some regions of space, such as the geostationary orbit (GEO) or the polar orbit, is already high enough to pose a serious hazard to any spacecraft that ventures there.

NEOs, on the other hand, are natural or artificial objects that cross or come close to the Earth’s orbit. They can be asteroids, comets, or fragments of them, ranging in size from a few meters to kilometers. Some NEOs, such as the famous Chelyabinsk meteor that exploded over Russia in 2013, can cause significant damage or injuries if they collide with the Earth’s atmosphere or surface. Larger NEOs can pose existential threats to our civilization if they hit us, as evidenced by the extinction of the dinosaurs and other species 66 million years ago. NASA estimates that there are over 25,000 NEOs that are at least 140 meters in diameter, and only a fraction of them have been detected or tracked.

Why do we need to protect ourselves from space debris and NEOs?

The most obvious reason is safety. If a spacecraft or satellite collides with debris or a NEO, it can experience severe damage or destruction, leading to loss of mission, data, and investment. Moreover, debris and NEO impacts can create more debris or secondary fragments, which can exacerbate the problem for future missions or satellites. The danger is not limited to space activities, as debris may also fall to the Earth’s surface, causing harm to people, animals, or property. Some debris objects, such as nuclear-powered satellites or rocket stages, can also pose environmental and health risks if they re-enter the atmosphere and release their radioactive or toxic contents.

Another reason to protect ourselves from space debris and NEOs is economic and strategic. Satellites and other space-based technologies are crucial for many fields, such as telecommunication, weather forecasting, navigation, surveillance, and scientific research. The disruption or loss of these assets can have ripple effects on many industries and societies. Moreover, space-based assets are vulnerable to intentional or accidental interference, such as cyberattacks, jamming, or spoofing, which can compromise their integrity, confidentiality, or availability. Ensuring the safety, sustainability, and security of the space environment is therefore essential for maintaining our technological and strategic advantage.

What are the challenges of protecting ourselves from space debris and NEOs?

There are several challenges that make the protection of our planet from space debris and NEOs a complex and evolving task. Some of the main challenges are:

- Tracking and detecting the objects: To prevent collisions or impacts with debris or NEOs, we need to know where they are and where they are going. However, detecting, tracking, and characterizing these objects is not easy, as they can move fast, change course, or be hidden from our sensors. Furthermore, tracking requires sharing data and coordination among many stakeholders, including the government, industry, academia, and the public. Differences in priorities, interests, or capabilities can hinder such collaboration, especially in sensitive or competitive areas.
- Removing or mitigating the objects: Once we have detected a debris object or a NEO that poses a significant risk, we need to decide what to do with it. Ideally, we should remove or deflect it from its collision course with the Earth or other sensitive targets. However, such operations require specialized technology, resources, and skills, and may generate more debris or NEO fragments. Moreover, removing or deflecing a NEO may be seen as a military or political act, as it can alter the trajectory or composition of the object, which may have implications for other countries or stakeholders.
- Preventing future debris and NEOs: The best way to deal with space debris and NEOs is to prevent their creation in the first place. This can be done through better design and disposal of spacecraft and rockets, avoiding unnecessary launches or collisions, and regulating or incentivizing space activities that generate debris or NEOs. However, preventing debris and NEOs requires collective action and responsible behavior by all actors in the space domain, including governments, companies, scientists, and citizens. Moreover, preventing NEO impacts requires detecting and characterizing NEOs before they reach the Earth, which requires more resources and coordination than detecting debris.

What are the prevention and mitigation strategies?

Despite the challenges, there are many strategies that have been proposed, developed, or tested to protect our planet from space debris and NEOs. Some of the most promising ones are:

- Better space situational awareness (SSA): SSA refers to the capability to detect, track, and predict the movements of space objects. Improving SSA can enable us to avoid collisions, predict close approaches, and mitigate debris objects by changing their orbits or reducing their size. SSA can be enhanced through better sensors, algorithms, data sharing, and international cooperation. The U.S. Space Force Space Surveillance Network, the ESA Space Surveillance and Tracking program, and the UN Office for Outer Space Affairs Space Object Registry are examples of SSA initiatives.
- Active debris removal (ADR): ADR refers to the capability to remove debris from the space environment through mechanical, chemical, or electrical means. ADR can reduce the risk of collisions and the amount of debris in the long run, but it requires specialized technology and resources. The Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency’s Kounotori spacecraft and the European Clean Space initiative are examples of ADR projects.
- NEO detection and deflection: NEO detection refers to the capability to find and track NEOs that may pose a significant risk to the Earth. NEO deflection refers to the capability to change the trajectory or composition of a NEO to avoid a collision. NEO detection can be enhanced through better telescopes, radars, and space probes. NEO deflection can be achieved through technologies such as kinetic impactors, gravity tractors, and nuclear devices. NASA’s Double Asteroid Redirection Test mission and the ESA’s Hera mission are examples of NEO deflection projects.
- Responsible space behavior: Responsible space behavior refers to the commitment of all actors in the space domain to minimize the creation and impact of space debris and NEOs. This can be achieved through voluntary or mandatory guidelines, standards, and regulations that promote sustainable and safe space activities. The UN Space Debris Mitigation Guidelines, the International Organization for Standardization Space Systems Standards, and the EU Space Surveillance and Tracking Regulations are examples of responsible space behavior initiatives.

Conclusion

The protection of our planet from space debris and NEOs is a complex and urgent challenge that requires the collaboration and innovation of many stakeholders. Preventing and mitigating the risks of these objects require tracking and detecting them, removing or deflecting them, preventing future ones, and promoting responsible behavior in space. While many strategies have been proposed or implemented, there is still much to do to ensure the safety, sustainability, and security of the space environment. By working together and investing in space science and technology, we can continue to explore and benefit from space while minimizing the impacts and risks of our presence.
