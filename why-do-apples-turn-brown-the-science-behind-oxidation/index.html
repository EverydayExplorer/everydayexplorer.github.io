Why Do Apples Turn Brown? The Science Behind Oxidation


Have you ever wondered why apples turn brown when you slice them? It’s a common phenomenon that many of us have witnessed, but have you ever stopped to consider the science behind it? In this blog post, we will delve into the fascinating world of oxidation and uncover the reasons behind the browning of apples.

Oxidation is a chemical process that occurs when certain substances come into contact with oxygen. It is a natural reaction that happens in a wide range of biological and non-biological systems, including the browning of apples. When an apple is cut or bruised, the cells in the fruit are damaged, exposing the inner flesh to oxygen in the air.

The browning of apples is primarily caused by an enzyme called polyphenol oxidase (PPO). This enzyme is present in the cells of the apple and is activated when it comes into contact with oxygen. When PPO reacts with oxygen, it catalyzes a series of chemical reactions that result in the formation of brown pigments known as melanins. These pigments give the apple its characteristic brown color.

But why does PPO react with oxygen in the first place? Well, it turns out that the enzyme is not the only player in this process. Apples also contain phenolic compounds, which are naturally occurring chemicals found in many fruits and vegetables. When an apple is cut, the damaged cells release these phenolic compounds, which then react with the oxygen in the air. This reaction triggers the activation of PPO and sets off the browning process.

Interestingly, the rate at which an apple turns brown can be influenced by various factors. For example, the pH level of the apple plays a significant role. Apples with a higher pH level, such as Granny Smiths, are less prone to browning because the alkaline environment inhibits the activity of PPO. On the other hand, apples with a lower pH level, like Red Delicious, have a more acidic environment that promotes browning.

Temperature also affects the browning process. Cold temperatures slow down the chemical reactions involved in oxidation, which is why refrigerating apples can help delay browning. On the contrary, warm temperatures accelerate the process, causing apples to brown more quickly.

Now that we understand the science behind the browning of apples, you might be wondering if there are ways to prevent or slow down this process. One common method is to treat the apple slices with an acid, such as lemon juice or vinegar. The acid lowers the pH level of the apple, creating an acidic environment that inhibits the activity of PPO and slows down the browning process.

Another approach is to coat the apple slices with sugar or honey. The sugar forms a protective barrier that limits the exposure of the apple to oxygen, thus reducing the rate of browning. Additionally, you can also use specialized products that contain antioxidants, which help prevent oxidation and keep the apple slices looking fresh and appealing for longer.

In conclusion, the browning of apples is a natural process driven by oxidation. When an apple is cut or bruised, the damaged cells release phenolic compounds that react with oxygen, activating the enzyme polyphenol oxidase and resulting in the formation of brown pigments. Factors such as pH level and temperature can influence the browning process, and there are various methods available to prevent or slow down the browning of apple slices. So, the next time you enjoy a crisp, juicy apple, you can appreciate the science behind its transformation into a brown delight.
