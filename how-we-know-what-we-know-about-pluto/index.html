How We Know What We Know About Pluto


Pluto, the dwarf planet, has been one of the most mysterious objects in our solar system for decades. Its distance, small size, and unique orbit have made it a difficult object to study; however, scientists have been able to uncover a great deal of information about Pluto in the past few years. With the help of advanced technology and innovative research techniques, we now know more about Pluto than ever before.

The first milestone in our understanding of Pluto was the discovery of its existence in 1930 by astronomer Clyde Tombaugh. For several decades, Pluto was considered to be the ninth planet in our solar system, until further research categorized it as a dwarf planet. One of the key factors in this decision was the discovery of several other small objects in the same region of our solar system, known as the Kuiper Belt.

The discovery of these objects gave rise to the understanding that Pluto was not a unique entity in our solar system. It is one of many small bodies that exist in the Kuiper Belt, a vast region beyond the orbit of Neptune that is home to a myriad of icy worldlets. The Kuiper Belt is also believed to be the origin of many comets that occasionally visit our solar system.

After the discovery of the Kuiper Belt, it became clear that studying Pluto was the key to understanding this region of our solar system. Several missions were launched with the goal of studying Pluto up close, including the New Horizons mission, which became the first spacecraft to visit Pluto in 2015.

The New Horizons mission provided scientists with a wealth of new information about Pluto. The spacecraft’s cameras captured stunning images of the dwarf planet’s surface, revealing that it was more diverse and complex than previously thought. The images showed craters, mountains, and even a heart-shaped feature on the surface of Pluto.

In addition to these visuals, New Horizons also collected data on Pluto’s atmosphere, which helped to reveal the presence of nitrogen, methane, and other gases. The information obtained by New Horizons has allowed scientists to better understand the geology and evolution of Pluto, providing new insights into the history of our solar system as a whole.

Beyond New Horizons, scientists continue to make discoveries about Pluto. For example, in 2018, a study conducted by researchers at the Southwest Research Institute revealed the presence of a large underground ocean on the dwarf planet. The study used data from the New Horizons mission to confirm the presence of the ocean, which is believed to be a remnant of Pluto’s early formation.

In conclusion, our understanding of Pluto has advanced significantly in recent years, thanks to advances in technology and innovative research techniques. The discovery of the Kuiper Belt and subsequent missions to study Pluto have provided us with unprecedented insights into this mysterious world, revealing its complex geology and evolution. As our understanding of Pluto and the Kuiper Belt continue to expand, we can expect even more exciting discoveries in the future.
