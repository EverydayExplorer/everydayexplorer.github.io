Understanding Wine Faults: Common Issues and How to Identify Them


Have you ever been sipping on a glass of wine and noticed something off about its taste or aroma? If so, you may have encountered a wine fault. Understanding wine faults and knowing how to identify them can help you appreciate wine even more and avoid disappointing experiences. In this blog post, we will discuss some common wine faults, their causes, and how to identify them.

1. Cork Taint (TCA)
Cork taint, also known as 2,4,6-Trichloroanisole or TCA, is one of the most well-known wine faults. It occurs when natural cork interacts with chlorine compounds, resulting in a musty, moldy aroma and a muted flavor profile. If you detect a wet cardboard or damp basement smell in your wine, chances are it has been affected by cork taint.

2. Oxidation
Oxidation is another common wine fault. It happens when wine is exposed to too much oxygen, causing it to lose its freshness and develop off-putting aromas and flavors. Signs of oxidation include a brownish color, a sherry-like aroma, and a flat taste. This fault can occur due to faulty closures, overexposure to air, or improper storage conditions.

3. Reduction
Reduction is the opposite of oxidation and occurs when wine is deprived of oxygen. This fault often presents itself as a rotten egg or sulfur-like smell, known as hydrogen sulfide (H2S). Reduction can also result in a flinty or reductive odor, making the wine seem unappealing. Decanting or swirling the wine vigorously can help dissipate these aromas.

4. Volatile Acidity (VA)
Volatile Acidity, or VA, refers to the presence of acetic acid in wine. While a minimal amount is desirable for some wine styles, high levels of VA can make the wine smell like vinegar or nail polish remover. Some people may perceive a slightly vinegary aroma as a positive attribute, but excessive levels can be unpleasant.

5. Sulfur Compounds
Certain sulfur compounds can cause significant wine faults. For example, mercaptans can produce aromas reminiscent of rotten eggs or garlic. Additionally, sulfur dioxide (SO2) can create a pungent smell and cause allergic reactions in some individuals. While sulfur compounds are often added to wine as preservatives, excessive amounts can lead to faults.

6. Brettanomyces
Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘Brett,’ is a yeast strain that can spoil wine if it grows unchecked. It produces a range of aromas, including barnyard, band-aid, and wet dog. These aromas may be appreciated in small amounts by some wine enthusiasts, but strong Brett aromas can dominate the wine and diminish its quality.

So, how can you identify these wine faults? Here are a few tips:

- Trust your senses: Developing a keen sense of smell and taste is crucial for identifying wine faults. Train your palate by smelling and tasting different foods and ingredients, such as fruits, spices, and herbs. This will help you pick up on subtle nuances in wine.
- Observe the wine’s appearance: Check for any unusual colors or haziness in the wine. Visual cues can sometimes indicate a fault.
- Pay attention to off-putting aromas: Sniff the wine and look out for any unpleasant or unusual smells. If something smells off or overpowering, there may be a fault present.
- Evaluate the taste: Take small sips and note any unexpected flavors or sensations. Does the wine taste dull or muted? Is there an overwhelming presence of a particular flavor?

If you suspect a wine fault, it’s essential to trust your judgment and potentially seek a second opinion. Winemakers and wine professionals can be valuable resources in identifying and understanding faults.

Remember, not all wines are faultless, and experiencing wine faults is part of the learning process. By familiarizing yourself with common wine faults and sharpening your sensory skills, you’ll be able to appreciate wine more deeply and make informed decisions when selecting and enjoying your next bottle.
