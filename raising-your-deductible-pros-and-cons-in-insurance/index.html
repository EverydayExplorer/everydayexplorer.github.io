Raising Your Deductible: Pros and Cons in Insurance


When it comes to insurance, many people are familiar with the term ‘deductible.’ The deductible is the amount that policyholders must pay out of pocket before their insurance coverage kicks in. It’s an important factor to consider when purchasing insurance policies, as it directly affects the cost of premiums and the potential financial impact of filing a claim.

One option that policyholders have is to raise their deductible in order to lower their insurance premiums. While this may seem like an attractive choice, it’s important to understand the pros and cons associated with raising your deductible.

The Pros of Raising Your Deductible

1. Lower Premiums

One of the most significant advantages of raising your deductible is the potential to lower your insurance premiums. By choosing a higher deductible, you are essentially taking on more risk yourself, which the insurance company compensates for by reducing the cost of your coverage. This can be especially beneficial for individuals and families looking to save money on their monthly expenses.

2. Financial Flexibility

Having a higher deductible means you are responsible for a larger portion of any potential claims. As a result, insurance companies may view you as a less risky policyholder, and you may have more flexibility in choosing the scope of your coverage. This can allow you to customize your policy to meet your specific needs and budget.

3. Avoiding Small Claims

Insurance policies are not designed to cover minor or routine expenses. With a higher deductible, you are less likely to file claims for small losses or damages, such as a minor fender bender or a small water leak. By avoiding small claims, you may be able to maintain a claims-free history, which can result in lower premiums over time.

The Cons of Raising Your Deductible

1. Higher Out-of-Pocket Expenses

Perhaps the most obvious downside of raising your deductible is the increased out-of-pocket costs if you need to file a claim. While a higher deductible can save you money on your monthly premiums, it also means that you will be responsible for a larger portion of any potential damages or losses. This can be a substantial financial burden, especially in the event of a major accident or catastrophe.

2. Risk of Underinsurance

By raising your deductible, you are essentially reducing the coverage for smaller claims. While this may save you money in the short term, if you encounter a series of small losses, you may find yourself paying for repairs or replacements out of pocket. It’s important to carefully consider your financial situation and the potential risks associated with higher deductibles to ensure you are adequately covered.

3. Potential Impact on Claim Frequency

Having a higher deductible may discourage policyholders from filing smaller claims. While this can save money on premiums in the short term, it can also lead to a decrease in overall claim frequency. This may make it more difficult to maintain a claims-free history and could result in larger claims in the future. It’s important to strike a balance between a deductible that saves you money and a deductible that accommodates your potential claims.

Considerations before Raising Your Deductible

Before making any changes to your insurance deductible, it’s crucial to consider various factors and evaluate your personal circumstances.

  1. Financial Stability: Assess your financial stability and ability to cover a higher out-of-pocket expense in the event of a claim. If you cannot comfortably afford a higher deductible, it may not be the right choice for you.

  2. Claims History: Evaluate your past claims history and predict future claims. If you have a history of filing frequent small claims, a higher deductible may not be beneficial in the long run.

  3. Type of Coverage: Different types of insurance have different deductibles. While raising your deductible on one policy may be financially feasible, it may not be the case for another policy.

  4. Premium Savings vs. Potential Claims: Consider the potential savings on premiums versus the potential costs of claims. It’s important to strike a balance that fits your financial situation and risk tolerance.

  5. Consult with an Expert: Talk to an insurance professional or advisor who can provide guidance based on your specific needs and circumstances.

In conclusion, raising your deductible in insurance policies can have both pros and cons. It is important to carefully evaluate your financial situation, risk tolerance, claims history, and type of coverage before making a decision. While raising your deductible can lead to lower premiums and increased financial flexibility, it also comes with the risk of higher out-of-pocket expenses and potential underinsurance. Consider the potential impact on claim frequency and consult with an expert to make an informed decision that aligns with your needs and budget.
