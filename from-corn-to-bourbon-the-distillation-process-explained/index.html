From Corn to Bourbon: The Distillation Process Explained


The distillation process is a fascinating journey that transforms simple ingredients into complex spirits with distinct flavors and aromas. One prime example of this is the journey from corn to bourbon. Bourbon, known as America’s native spirit, is a beloved beverage that has gained popularity worldwide. In this blog post, we will take a deep dive into the distillation process and explore how corn is transformed into the golden elixir we know and love.

Before we delve into the details, it’s important to understand what bourbon is and its significance in the world of spirits. Bourbon is a type of whiskey that must meet specific legal requirements to be labeled as such. It must be produced in the United States, made from a grain mixture that is at least 51% corn, aged in new charred oak barrels, and distilled to no more than 160 proof (80% alcohol by volume). These regulations ensure the authenticity and quality of bourbon.

The distillation process begins with the selection and preparation of the corn. Commonly, distillers use yellow dent corn, a type of field corn known for its sweetness and high starch content. The corn kernels are milled, which involves grinding them into a coarse powder, to expose the starches present in the grain.

Once the corn is milled, it is cooked in hot water to transform the starches into fermentable sugars. This process is called mashing. The hot water activates enzymes in the corn, breaking down the large starch molecules into smaller sugar molecules, such as glucose and maltose. These sugars will serve as food for the yeast during fermentation.

The next step in the distillation process is fermentation. The corn mash, along with water and yeast, is transferred to large fermentation vessels, also known as fermenters or mash tuns. The yeast consumes the sugars present in the mash and converts them into alcohol through a process called anaerobic fermentation. This process typically takes several days, during which the yeast converts most of the sugars into alcohol, creating what is known as the distiller’s beer or wash.

Once fermentation is complete, it’s time for distillation. Distillation is the process of separating and purifying alcohol through heat and vaporization. The wash is transferred to a still, a large copper or stainless steel vessel equipped with a heating system and a condenser. The still is heated, causing the alcohol to vaporize and rise through the still’s neck or column.

As the alcohol vapor rises through the still, it enters the condenser, which cools the vapor and turns it back into a liquid. This liquid, known as distillate, is collected and separated into different fractions based on their boiling points. The first fraction, called the foreshots, contains undesirable impurities and is discarded. The middle fraction, known as the hearts, contains the desired ethanol (alcohol) and flavorful compounds. The final fraction, called the feints, contains lower-quality alcohols and is also discarded.

After distillation, the hearts are aged in charred oak barrels, as required by law, to undergo maturation. This aging process imparts the characteristic flavors and colors associated with bourbon. The charred oak barrels provide the bourbon with notes of caramel, vanilla, and oak, while also filtering out impurities and smoothing the spirit’s taste.

The length of aging varies for each bourbon, with a minimum requirement of two years to be labeled as ‘straight’ bourbon. Some bourbons are aged for several years, allowing them to develop complex flavors and mellow out over time.

The final step in the distillation process is bottling and labeling. Once the bourbon has reached its desired age, it is diluted with water to reach the desired proof, usually around 80 proof (40% alcohol by volume). The bourbon is then filtered and bottled, ready for distribution and consumption.

From corn to bourbon, the distillation process is a beautiful combination of science, artistry, and tradition. It takes simple ingredients and transforms them into a spirit that has become deeply ingrained in American culture. The next time you enjoy a glass of bourbon, take a moment to appreciate the intricate process that went into creating the flavorful elixir in your hand.
