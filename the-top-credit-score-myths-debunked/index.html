The Top Credit Score Myths Debunked


When it comes to credit scores, there are so many myths and misconceptions out there that it can be hard to separate fact from fiction. Your credit score is an important financial tool that lenders use to determine your creditworthiness, so it is crucial to have a clear understanding of how it works. In this blog post, we will debunk some of the most common credit score myths and provide you with accurate information to help you make informed financial decisions.

Myth 1: Checking your credit score will negatively impact it.

One of the most prevalent myths about credit scores is that checking your own credit score will negatively impact it. This is categorically false. When you check your own credit score, it is considered a ‘soft inquiry’ and does not affect your credit score in any way. In fact, regularly monitoring your credit score is essential for maintaining good financial health.

Myth 2: Closing credit cards will improve your credit score.

Another widely believed myth is that closing credit cards will improve your credit score. This myth stems from the misconception that having fewer credit cards means less credit risk. However, closing credit card accounts can actually harm your credit score. This is because it reduces your available credit, which increases your credit utilization ratio. Having a higher credit utilization ratio can lower your credit score. It’s generally better to keep your credit card accounts open, even if you’re not using them, to maintain a healthy credit utilization ratio.

Myth 3: Carrying a balance on your credit card will boost your credit score.

Contrary to popular belief, carrying a balance on your credit card does not boost your credit score. In fact, carrying a balance can actually have a negative impact on your credit score. Your credit utilization ratio, which is the percentage of your available credit that you are using, plays a significant role in determining your credit score. Keeping your credit card balances low and paying them off in full each month is the best practice for maintaining a good credit score.

Myth 4: Closing old accounts will remove them from your credit report.

Many people believe that closing old accounts will remove them from their credit report. However, this is not true. Closed accounts will remain on your credit report for a certain period of time, typically up to 10 years. Closing old accounts may actually have a negative impact on your credit score, as it shortens the average age of your accounts. It’s generally better to keep your old accounts open, especially if they have a positive history and contribute to the overall length of your credit history.

Myth 5: Your income has an impact on your credit score.

While it is true that lenders may consider your income when determining your creditworthiness, your income does not directly impact your credit score. Your credit score is based on factors such as your payment history, credit utilization, length of credit history, types of credit, and new credit applications. Your income is not factored into these calculations.

Myth 6: Paying off a debt will immediately improve your credit score.

While paying off a debt is certainly a responsible financial move, it does not guarantee an immediate improvement in your credit score. Your payment history, which includes late payments and accounts in collections, plays a significant role in determining your credit score. It takes time and consistent responsible financial behavior to see a positive impact on your credit score. However, paying off debts is an important step in improving your overall financial health.

Myth 7: Having a perfect credit score guarantees loan approval.

Having a perfect credit score does not guarantee loan approval. While a high credit score is definitely an advantage, lenders consider various factors when deciding whether to approve a loan. These factors may include your income, employment history, debt-to-income ratio, and more. A perfect credit score certainly helps, but it is not the sole determinant for loan approval.

In conclusion, understanding the truth behind credit score myths is essential for making informed financial decisions. Checking your own credit score, maintaining a healthy credit utilization ratio, and having a long credit history are some of the key factors that contribute to a good credit score. Remember, your credit score is a reflection of your financial health, so it’s important to nurture and maintain it responsibly.
