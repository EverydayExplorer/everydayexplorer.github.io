Survival Skills: Finding and Using Natural Cordage


Are you an outdoor enthusiast or someone who loves spending time in nature? If so, then you understand the importance of being prepared for any situation that may arise. One essential survival skill that often goes overlooked is the ability to find and use natural cordage. In this blog post, we will explore the various types of natural cordage and how you can utilize them in a survival situation.

Types of Natural Cordage

Natural cordage refers to any type of rope or string that is made from materials found in the environment. These materials can include plant fibers, animal tendons, and even certain types of tree bark. Let’s take a closer look at some common types of natural cordage:

1. Plant Fibers:

Plant fibers are a popular choice for making natural cordage due to their abundance and versatility. Some commonly used plant fibers include:

  • Nettle: Nettle fibers are strong and flexible, making them ideal for creating sturdy cordage. They can be found in various regions and can be harvested by carefully plucking the leaves from the plant.

  • Yucca: Yucca plants are native to arid regions and have long, fibrous leaves that can be stripped and twisted into cordage. Yucca cordage is known for its durability and strength.

  • Milkweed: Milkweed plants produce silky fibers that can be collected from the seed pods. These fibers are lightweight and have a high tensile strength, making them suitable for various applications.

2. Animal Tendons:

In a survival situation, animal tendons can serve as an excellent source of natural cordage. Tendons are tough and resilient, making them ideal for tasks that require a high level of strength. Some animals that provide suitable tendons for cordage include:

  • Deer: Deer tendons are readily available and can be harvested from the legs of freshly hunted deer. They can be processed by removing the excess tissue and drying them before use.

  • Fish: Fish tendons, also known as fish sinew, can be obtained by carefully separating them from the fish’s backbone. They can be used as cordage or turned into fishing lines.

3. Tree Bark:

Certain types of tree bark can be stripped and processed into natural cordage. This method requires a bit more effort, but it can be a valuable skill to have in survival situations. Some trees known for their bark cordage potential include:

  • Birch: Birch bark can be easily peeled off the tree in large sheets. These sheets can then be cut into strips and twisted into cordage. Birch bark cordage is known for its strength and durability.

  • Cedar: Cedar bark is fibrous and flexible, making it ideal for cordage. The inner bark can be separated from the outer bark and processed into usable strands.

Utilizing Natural Cordage

Now that you are familiar with some types of natural cordage, let’s explore how you can effectively utilize them in a survival situation. Here are a few practical applications:

Shelter Building:

When constructing a shelter, natural cordage can be used to secure branches and bind them together. This ensures the stability and durability of your shelter, providing you with a safe place to rest and protect yourself from the elements.

Hunting and Fishing:

Natural cordage can also be used for crafting tools and weapons. For example, you can create snares or traps using cordage made from plant fibers or animal tendons. Additionally, cordage can be used to fashion fishing lines or bowstrings, enhancing your ability to procure food in the wild.

First Aid:

In emergency situations, natural cordage can be used to create makeshift splints or tourniquets. By securely fastening branches or fabric with cordage, you can immobilize injured limbs or stop severe bleeding, potentially saving lives.

Conclusion

Mastering the skill of finding and using natural cordage is a valuable asset for anyone who spends time in the great outdoors. By understanding the different types of natural cordage and their applications, you can enhance your survival capabilities and increase your chances of thriving in challenging environments. So, next time you venture into nature, remember to keep an eye out for potential cordage materials – you never know when this skill might come in handy!
