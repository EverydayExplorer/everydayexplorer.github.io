Why Do We Get Chills? The Physiology and Psychology of Goosebumps


Have you ever wondered why you get chills? That strange sensation that sends shivers down your spine and makes your hair stand on end. Whether it’s from listening to your favorite song, watching a suspenseful movie, or experiencing a sudden drop in temperature, getting chills is a universal human experience. But what causes them, and what is their purpose? In this blog post, we will explore the physiology and psychology behind goosebumps.

Physiology of Goosebumps:

Goosebumps, scientifically known as cutis anserina, occur when the muscles attached to your hair follicles contract, causing the hair to stand upright. This reaction is a remnant from our evolutionary past when our ancestors had much more body hair. Back then, when faced with a threat or extreme cold, the raised hair helped to insulate their bodies and make them appear larger, intimidating potential predators.

The mechanism behind goosebumps is controlled by the autonomic nervous system, which is responsible for regulating involuntary bodily functions. When we experience certain emotions, such as fear or excitement, the sympathetic branch of the autonomic nervous system is activated. This triggers the release of adrenaline, which in turn causes our muscles to contract, including those attached to our hair follicles, resulting in goosebumps.

Psychology of Goosebumps:

While the physiological explanation behind goosebumps is fascinating, the psychological aspect is equally intriguing. Chills often accompany intense emotions, whether positive or negative. Have you ever listened to a powerful piece of music that gave you goosebumps? Or perhaps you’ve watched a heartwarming scene in a movie that made you feel a surge of emotions? These experiences trigger the release of neurotransmitters like dopamine and oxytocin, which are associated with pleasure and bonding.

Goosebumps can also be triggered by a sense of awe and wonder. When we witness something extraordinary or experience a profound moment, such as gazing at a breathtaking sunset or standing in the presence of a majestic natural wonder, our brains release endorphins and other chemicals that evoke a sense of awe. This emotional response can manifest as goosebumps, serving as a reminder of the profound impact that these moments have on us.

Furthermore, research has shown that individuals with a higher level of empathy are more likely to experience goosebumps. This suggests that the ability to feel chills in response to emotional stimuli may be linked to our capacity for empathy and our ability to connect with others on an emotional level.

In conclusion, the phenomenon of getting chills is a fascinating interplay between our physiology and psychology. Goosebumps serve as a vestigial response from our evolutionary past, while also enhancing our emotional experiences and connecting us to the world around us. So, the next time you feel those familiar shivers, embrace them and appreciate the incredible complexity of the human body and mind.
