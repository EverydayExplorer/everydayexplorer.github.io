The World's Most Unusual Wine Traditions: From Stomping to Aging


When it comes to wine, we often think about vineyards, barrels, and a refined tasting experience. But did you know that around the world, there are some truly unique and unusual wine traditions that have been passed down through generations? From stomping grapes with bare feet to aging wine in peculiar containers, these traditions add a touch of fascinating history and culture to the world of winemaking. In this blog post, we will explore some of the most intriguing and unconventional wine traditions from different corners of the globe.

Let’s start with the classic tradition that most of us have heard of - grape stomping. This age-old method involves crushing grapes with bare feet to extract the juice. While it may sound like a scene straight out of a movie, grape stomping is still practiced today in certain regions, particularly during harvest festivals. Not only does this method connect people to the land and the fruits of their labor, but it also allows the grape juice to come into contact with the natural yeasts present on human skin, contributing to the fermentation process.

Moving on to aging methods, we encounter the use of amphorae. Originating in ancient Greece, these clay vessels have made a comeback in recent years. Amphorae provide a unique environment for wine to age, as the clay allows for gentle oxygenation while preserving the wine’s freshness. The resulting wines often have distinct mineral qualities and a complex flavor profile.

In Georgia, winemakers have mastered the art of making wine in qvevri - large earthenware vessels buried underground. This ancient winemaking tradition, dating back over 8,000 years, involves fermenting and aging grapes, skins, seeds, and stems together. The qvevri method creates wines with robust tannins, vibrant acidity, and a rich amber color. This traditional technique has even been recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.

Heading to Spain, we discover the unique tradition of the solera system. This method is commonly used in sherry production, where barrels are stacked on top of each other in a pyramid-like structure. The oldest and most mature wine is stored in the bottom row, while the younger wines are gradually blended with the older ones as they move up the pyramid. The result is a consistent and complex wine with layers of flavors and aromas.

In Japan, the art of winemaking takes on a whole new level of precision and dedication. Winemakers in the Yamanashi Prefecture have adopted the practice of playing classical music to their vineyards. It is believed that the vibrations and harmonies from the music help stimulate the growth of the grapes and enhance the overall quality of the wine. While the scientific evidence behind this tradition is debatable, the idea of harmonizing nature and music is undeniably captivating.

These are just a few examples of the world’s most unusual wine traditions. Each one brings a distinct flavor and story to the wine we enjoy. As we raise our glasses and savor the complexities of these unconventional techniques, let’s toast to the diversity and richness that wine brings to our lives. Cheers!
