Understanding Wine Faults: Detecting and Assessing Common Wine Flaws


Wine is often seen as a sophisticated drink, symbolizing refinement, pleasure, and celebration. However, just like any other product, wine can develop flaws or faults that can significantly affect its taste and quality. Understanding and detecting common wine faults is crucial for both wine professionals and enthusiasts alike. In this blog post, we will explore the most common wine flaws, their causes, and how to assess them.

1. Oxidation

Oxidation is one of the most well-known wine faults. It occurs when wine is overexposed to oxygen, resulting in a loss of freshness, color, and flavor. Oxidized wines often have a brownish tint and can exhibit aromas of nuts, sherry, or stewed fruits.

Causes: Oxygen can sneak into the wine through a faulty closure, excessive headspace in the bottle, or poor storage conditions.

Detection: Oxidation can be detected by visual cues such as brownish color or a yellowish tint in white wines. Aromas of sherry, cardboard, or a lack of fruitiness can also indicate oxidized wine.

Assessment: Oxidized wine is considered a major fault and is generally undesirable. However, in some cases, oxidation can contribute to the complexity of certain styles of wine, particularly fortified wines like Madeira or some sherries.

2. Cork Taint (TCA)

Cork taint, often referred to as TCA (2,4,6-trichloroanisole), is another significant wine flaw. It is caused by a chemical compound that can contaminate a wine as a result of a faulty cork. Wines affected by cork taint exhibit aromas and flavors reminiscent of wet cardboard, musty cellar, or moldy newspaper.

Causes: Cork taint occurs when the wine comes into contact with a cork contaminated by TCA. TCA can originate from cork bark or from various winery sources.

Detection: The telltale sign of cork taint is a musty, damp, or moldy aroma. It is often more pronounced upon opening the bottle and can negatively affect the wine’s taste.

Assessment: Cork taint is a fault that cannot be fixed or reversed, causing the affected wine to be undrinkable. It is estimated that around 2-5% of wines sealed with natural corks are affected by cork taint.

3. Volatile Acidity (VA)

Volatile Acidity, or VA, refers to the presence of acetic acid in wine. In small amounts, acetic acid can contribute to the wine’s complexity and add a hint of vinegar-like aroma. However, excessive levels of VA can result in a wine that smells like nail polish remover or vinegar.

Causes: Excessive levels of VA can occur due to bacterial or yeast contamination during winemaking or inadequate temperature control during fermentation.

Detection: A persistent, vinegar-like aroma is the most common indicator of high levels of VA. However, some wines with higher VA can also exhibit a slight sharpness on the palate.

Assessment: While low levels of VA can be tolerated in some wines, high levels can be overpowering and considered a flaw. The acceptable level of VA varies depending on the wine style and personal preferences.

4. Reduction

Reductive faults occur when wines are deprived of oxygen, resulting in a lack of freshness, unpleasant smells, and off-putting flavors. Reduction can present itself as aromas of rotten eggs, burnt matchsticks, or rubber.

Causes: Reduction can occur during fermentation or aging if the winemaking process limits exposure to oxygen.

Detection: The presence of sulfur-related aromas, such as rotten eggs or rubber, can indicate reduction. However, these aromas may dissipate with aeration, revealing more pleasant characteristics.

Assessment: Some wines benefit from reductive winemaking practices, contributing to unique flavor profiles. However, excessive reduction can result in unappealing aromas and flavors, making the wine less enjoyable.

Conclusion

Understanding and detecting common wine faults is essential for appreciating wine and ensuring a high-quality drinking experience. By being familiar with common wine flaws such as oxidation, cork taint, volatile acidity, and reduction, wine enthusiasts can assess their wines and make informed decisions about whether they are still enjoyable or need to be discarded.

It is important to note that taste is subjective, and what one person may consider a fault, another may enjoy. Ultimately, it is up to the individual to decide their personal preferences and tolerance for wine faults. By developing a discerning palate and understanding these flaws, wine lovers can navigate the world of wine with confidence and appreciation.
