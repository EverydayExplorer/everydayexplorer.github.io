Hunting for Wild Turkeys: Calls and Setups for a Successful Hunt


When it comes to hunting wild turkeys, the right calls and setups can make all the difference. Whether you’re a seasoned hunter or just starting out, mastering these techniques is key to a successful hunt. In this blog post, we’ll dive into the world of turkey hunting and explore the various calls and setups you can employ to lure in those elusive gobblers.

1. The Art of Calling

Calling is an essential skill for any turkey hunter. It involves mimicking the sounds turkeys make to communicate with each other. There are several types of calls you can use, each serving a specific purpose:

a) Box Calls: Box calls are popular among hunters due to their versatility and ease of use. They consist of a wooden box with a paddle that produces turkey sounds when scraped against the side. With a box call, you can create a range of turkey vocalizations, from soft purrs to loud yelps.

b) Slate Calls: Slate calls, also known as pot calls, are another common choice. They consist of a slate or glass surface that you strike with a striker to produce turkey sounds. Slate calls offer a realistic and soft tone, making them effective for drawing in wary gobblers.

c) Mouth Calls: Mouth calls, or diaphragm calls, require more practice to master but offer hands-free operation. These calls are placed in the roof of your mouth, and you use your tongue and airflow to create turkey sounds. Mouth calls are great for creating realistic hen sounds, including clucks, purrs, and yelps.

2. Setting Up for Success

Once you’ve mastered the art of calling, it’s time to focus on your setup. A good setup can make or break your hunt, so pay close attention to the following tips:

a) Scouting: Before your hunt, scout the area to locate roosting sites, feeding areas, and travel routes. Look for turkey droppings, tracks, and feathers to determine where turkeys are active. This information will help you choose the best setup locations.

b) Concealment: Turkeys have sharp eyesight, so it’s crucial to blend into your surroundings. Wear camouflage clothing that matches the terrain and use natural cover, such as bushes or trees, to break up your silhouette. A well-concealed hunter is less likely to be detected by wary gobblers.

c) Decoys: Decoys can be a valuable asset when setting up for a turkey hunt. Place a hen decoy in a feeding or breeding position to attract gobblers. Use a jake or tom decoy sparingly, as they might intimidate dominant gobblers. Experiment with different decoy setups to see what works best in your hunting area.

d) Calling Sequences: When setting up, start with soft and subtle calls to pique the curiosity of nearby turkeys. Once you’ve grabbed their attention, gradually increase the volume and intensity of your calls. Remember to pause between calls to mimic the natural rhythm of turkey communication.

e) Patience and Persistence: Turkey hunting requires patience and persistence. Stay in your setup location for at least 30 minutes before considering a move. Sometimes, gobblers take longer to respond, and a sudden move might spook them. If you don’t get any response, change your location and try again.

In conclusion, successful turkey hunting revolves around mastering the art of calling and setting up strategically. By utilizing the right calls and setups, you can increase your chances of luring in those elusive gobblers. Remember to practice your calls, scout the hunting area, and practice patience and persistence. So grab your gear, head into the woods, and get ready for an exhilarating turkey hunting adventure!
