Hunting Geese: Decoy Spreads and Calling Techniques


When it comes to hunting geese, having the right decoy spreads and calling techniques can make all the difference. Whether you’re a seasoned hunter or just starting out, understanding how to set up your decoys and effectively call geese can greatly increase your chances of success.

Decoy Spreads:

The first step in creating an effective decoy spread is understanding the behavior and patterns of geese. Geese are social birds and tend to gather in large flocks. Setting up your decoys to mimic this natural behavior is crucial.

One common decoy spread is the ‘U’ shape. This spread mimics a flock of geese landing or feeding. Start by placing a few decoys close together to create the center of the ‘U.’ Gradually spread out the rest of the decoys in a semi-circle shape, with the open end of the ‘U’ facing into the wind. This spread creates a natural-looking landing zone for geese and can be highly effective.

Another popular spread is the ‘V’ shape. This spread mimics geese flying in formation. Start by placing a few decoys at the front of the ‘V’ to represent the lead geese. Gradually spread out the rest of the decoys in a V-shape, with the open end of the ‘V’ facing into the wind. This spread can attract geese flying overhead, as it gives the illusion of other geese already on the ground.

Experimenting with different spread patterns and sizes can help you find what works best for your hunting location and the specific behavior of the geese you’re targeting. Remember to pay attention to wind direction and adjust your spread accordingly, as geese prefer to land into the wind.

Calling Techniques:

Calling is an essential skill for any goose hunter. The goal of calling is to imitate the sounds geese make to communicate with each other. There are various calling techniques you can use to attract geese and make them believe that your decoy spread is a safe and inviting place to land.

One common calling technique is the ‘hail call.’ This call is used to grab the attention of geese flying overhead. It consists of a series of loud, long notes that mimic the sound of a large flock of geese. The hail call can be effective in getting geese to take notice of your spread and potentially change their flight path to investigate further.

Another effective calling technique is the ‘comeback call.’ This call is used after geese have shown some interest in your spread but are hesitant to commit. It consists of a series of short, fast notes that mimic the sound of a goose trying to get the attention of other geese. The comeback call can create a sense of urgency and convince geese to make a final approach to your decoys.

It’s important to practice your calling techniques to ensure they sound realistic. There are many instructional videos and audio recordings available online that can help you fine-tune your skills.

In conclusion, hunting geese requires a combination of effective decoy spreads and calling techniques. By understanding the behavior of geese and mimicking their natural patterns, you can increase your chances of a successful hunt. Remember to experiment with different spread patterns and sizes, pay attention to wind direction, and practice your calling techniques. Happy hunting!
