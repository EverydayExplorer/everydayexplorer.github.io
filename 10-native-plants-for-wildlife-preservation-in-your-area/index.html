10 Native Plants for Wildlife Preservation in Your Area


Are you passionate about wildlife preservation? Do you want to create a vibrant habitat for local fauna in your area? One of the most effective ways to support wildlife is by planting native plants in your garden or outdoor space. Native plants provide a variety of benefits to wildlife, including food, shelter, and habitat. In this blog post, we will explore 10 native plants that are fantastic for attracting and supporting wildlife in your area.

1. Milkweed (Asclepias spp.)
Milkweed is a crucial plant for supporting the declining population of monarch butterflies. This plant is the sole host for monarch caterpillars, and the nectar-rich flowers attract several other butterfly species as well. By planting milkweed in your garden, you can play a vital role in providing a breeding ground and food source for these beautiful creatures.

2. Coneflower (Echinacea spp.)
Coneflowers are known for their stunning and long-lasting blooms. They are a favorite among bees, butterflies, and other pollinators. The large, cone-shaped flowers provide nectar for bees, while the seed heads attract finches and other birds. Additionally, coneflowers are low-maintenance and create a beautiful focal point in any garden.

3. Sunflower (Helianthus spp.)
Sunflowers are not only a delight to the human eye but also a haven for wildlife. These tall plants provide abundant nectar and seeds, attracting a wide range of birds, bees, and butterflies. The dense flower heads also offer shelter for insects and small mammals.

4. Wild Bergamot (Monarda fistulosa)
Wild bergamot, also known as bee balm, is a native perennial with beautiful clusters of lavender flowers. This plant attracts a variety of pollinators, including bees, butterflies, and hummingbirds. The aromatic leaves and flowers also have medicinal properties and can be used for teas and infusions.

5. Butterfly Weed (Asclepias tuberosa)
Similar to milkweed, butterfly weed is a host plant for monarch butterflies. Its bright orange flowers are not only visually appealing but also serve as a nectar source for many butterfly species. This low-maintenance plant is an excellent addition to any wildlife-friendly garden.

6. Redbud (Cercis canadensis)
Redbud trees are native to many areas and are highly beneficial for wildlife. They provide early-season nectar for bees and butterflies when few other plants are blooming. The distinctive heart-shaped leaves and pink flowers add beauty to any landscape, while the seeds attract birds throughout the year.

7. Serviceberry (Amelanchier spp.)
Serviceberry, also known as shadbush, is a multi-stemmed shrub or small tree with beautiful white flowers in the spring. The berries that follow are highly favored by birds, including thrushes, waxwings, and robins. Additionally, the dense branching structure of serviceberry provides nesting sites and cover for various wildlife species.

8. Goldenrod (Solidago spp.)
Goldenrod is a common wildflower that adorns fields and meadows with its bright yellow flowers. Contrary to popular belief, goldenrod does not cause hay fever. Its pollen is heavy and is not easily wind-dispersed. Goldenrod attracts bees, butterflies, and other insects, providing a vital food source as summer turns into fall.

9. Virginia Creeper (Parthenocissus quinquefolia)
Virginia creeper is a fast-growing vine with beautiful red foliage in the fall. Its berries are highly sought after by many bird species, including cardinals, robins, and bluebirds. As a vine, Virginia creeper also provides cover and shelter for small mammals and birds.

10. Oak Trees (Quercus spp.)
If you have enough space, planting native oak trees is highly beneficial for wildlife. Oak trees support numerous moth and butterfly species, including the stunning regal moth. Acorns produced by oak trees are a vital food source for squirrels, deer, and many bird species. The dense canopy of oak trees also provides shelter for wildlife.

In conclusion, incorporating native plants into your landscape is a win-win situation. By planting these 10 native plants, you can create a welcoming habitat for wildlife in your area while enjoying the beauty and diversity they bring to your garden. Remember, wildlife preservation starts in our own backyards. Start small and watch as your garden becomes a thriving ecosystem that supports a wide range of local fauna.
