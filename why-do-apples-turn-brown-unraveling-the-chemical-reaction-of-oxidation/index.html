Why Do Apples Turn Brown? Unraveling the Chemical Reaction of Oxidation


Have you ever wondered why apples turn brown after being sliced? It’s a common phenomenon that many of us have observed while preparing or eating this beloved fruit. While the browning may not affect the taste or nutritional value of the apple, it certainly impacts its visual appeal. In this blog post, we will delve into the science behind this intriguing process, known as oxidation.

Oxidation is a chemical reaction that occurs when certain substances come into contact with oxygen. It is a process that happens naturally and is responsible for the browning of apples, as well as many other fruits and vegetables. To better understand the science behind this reaction, let’s break it down step by step.

When an apple is cut or bruised, its cells are damaged, and the enzyme polyphenol oxidase (PPO) is released. This enzyme is abundantly found in the cells of apples and is responsible for catalyzing the oxidation process. Once exposed to oxygen in the air, the PPO enzyme reacts with phenolic compounds present in the apple’s cells, resulting in the formation of brown pigments called melanins.

Melanins are a type of pigment that gives the apple its characteristic brown color. They are formed through a series of complex chemical reactions involving the oxidation of phenols by the PPO enzyme. The exact mechanism and pathway of the reaction are still not fully understood, but it is believed to involve the conversion of colorless phenolic compounds into brown-colored compounds by the action of oxygen.

The browning reaction can be slowed down or prevented through various methods. One common way to slow down the oxidation process is to coat the sliced apples with lemon juice or citric acid. The acid in these substances inhibits the activity of the PPO enzyme, reducing the amount of browning that occurs. Another method is to minimize the exposure of the apple to oxygen by storing it in airtight containers or using plastic wrap.

Understanding the science behind the browning of apples has practical implications beyond preserving their visual appeal. This knowledge has led to the development of various techniques to prevent or delay the browning process in commercial food practices. For example, apple slices used in salads or desserts are often treated with antioxidant solutions that impede oxidation. Additionally, some apple varieties have been bred to naturally contain lower levels of PPO enzymes, resulting in reduced browning.

The browning reaction in apples is not only a visual change but also an indication of chemical changes occurring within the fruit’s cells. As the phenolic compounds oxidize, other chemical reactions take place, leading to changes in texture, flavor, and aroma. In some cases, these changes may be desirable, such as in the production of apple cider or apple juice, where the browning reaction contributes to the overall taste profile. However, in other instances, such as in fresh fruit salads or apple slices for snacking, the browning reaction is less desirable.

In summary, the browning of apples is a result of the oxidation process catalyzed by the enzyme polyphenol oxidase (PPO). When an apple is cut or bruised, the PPO enzyme reacts with phenolic compounds, resulting in the formation of brown pigments called melanins. Understanding the science behind this reaction has practical applications in food preservation and has led to the development of various methods to slow down or prevent browning. So, next time you slice an apple and it starts to turn brown, you’ll know the fascinating chemical process that’s taking place!
