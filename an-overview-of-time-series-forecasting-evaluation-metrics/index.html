An Overview of Time Series Forecasting Evaluation Metrics


Time series forecasting is a valuable tool for predicting future values based on historical data. It has numerous applications across various industries, such as finance, meteorology, and sales forecasting. When it comes to evaluating the accuracy and performance of time series forecasting models, there are several metrics that are commonly used.

These evaluation metrics provide a way to measure how well a forecasting model is performing and allow researchers and practitioners to compare different models or assess the performance of a single model over time. In this blog post, we will provide an overview of some of the most commonly used time series forecasting evaluation metrics.

Mean Absolute Error (MAE):
The Mean Absolute Error (MAE) is a commonly used metric that measures the average absolute difference between the predicted and actual values. It is calculated by taking the average of the absolute differences between the predicted and actual values. The MAE provides a straightforward measure of the average magnitude of the forecasting errors.

Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE):
The Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) is another widely used metric that measures the square root of the average squared difference between the predicted and actual values. It is calculated by taking the square root of the average of the squared differences between the predicted and actual values. The RMSE penalizes larger errors more heavily than the MAE, making it a popular choice for evaluating forecasting models.

Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE):
The Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) is a metric that measures the average percentage difference between the predicted and actual values. It is calculated by taking the average of the absolute percentage differences between the predicted and actual values. The MAPE provides a measure of the relative magnitude of the forecasting errors.

Symmetric Mean Absolute Percentage Error (SMAPE):
The Symmetric Mean Absolute Percentage Error (SMAPE) is similar to the MAPE, but it takes into account the asymmetry in forecasting errors. It is calculated by taking the average of the absolute percentage differences between the predicted and actual values, divided by the sum of the predicted and actual values. The SMAPE provides a balanced measure that is suitable for comparing forecasting models.

Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE):
The Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE) is a metric that measures the relative performance of a model compared to a naive baseline model. It is calculated by taking the average of the absolute differences between the predicted and actual values, divided by the average absolute difference between consecutive observations in the historical data. The MASE allows for the comparison of models across different time series data.

Forecast Bias:
The forecast bias measures the average difference between the predicted and actual values. A positive bias indicates that the forecasts tend to be higher than the actual values, while a negative bias indicates that the forecasts tend to be lower. The forecast bias provides insight into the systematic over- or under-estimation of the forecasting model.

Forecast Interval Coverage:
The forecast interval coverage measures the proportion of observed values that fall within a specified prediction interval. It provides a measure of the model’s ability to capture the uncertainty of the forecasts. The forecast interval coverage is particularly useful when dealing with probabilistic forecasting models.

These are just a few of the many evaluation metrics available for time series forecasting. Each metric provides a different perspective on the accuracy and performance of forecasting models. It is important to consider multiple metrics when evaluating models to get a comprehensive understanding of their performance.

In conclusion, time series forecasting evaluation metrics allow researchers and practitioners to assess the accuracy and performance of forecasting models. Metrics such as MAE, RMSE, MAPE, SMAPE, MASE, forecast bias, and forecast interval coverage provide different measures of forecasting accuracy, relative performance, and interval estimation. By utilizing these metrics, analysts can make informed decisions about the suitability of forecasting models for their specific applications.
