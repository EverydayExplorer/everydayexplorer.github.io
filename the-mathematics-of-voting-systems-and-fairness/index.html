The Mathematics of Voting Systems and Fairness


When it comes to making decisions as a group, whether it be electing a leader, choosing a candidate for a job, or deciding on a course of action, a voting system is often employed. But have you ever stopped to consider the mathematical principles behind these voting systems and whether they truly represent the will of the people? In this blog post, we will delve into the mathematics of voting systems and explore the concept of fairness.

Voting systems are designed to aggregate individual preferences into a collective decision. However, different voting systems can produce different outcomes, even when the same set of preferences is considered. This has led to debates about the fairness of different voting systems and the quest to find the most representative and fair method.

One of the fundamental principles in voting system design is known as the Condorcet criterion. This criterion states that if there is a candidate who would win in a head-to-head matchup against every other candidate, then that candidate should ultimately win the election. This seems intuitive, as the Condorcet winner can be seen as the most generally preferred candidate.

However, it is often the case that no candidate satisfies the Condorcet criterion, leading to the need for alternative methods. This is where mathematical techniques such as mathematical modeling, probability theory, and game theory come into play.

One popular voting system that addresses the limitations of the Condorcet criterion is the Borda count method. In the Borda count, voters rank candidates in order of preference, and points are assigned to each candidate based on their rank. The candidate with the highest total points wins the election. The idea behind this method is to reward candidates who have broad support across the electorate.

While the Borda count is a step towards fairness, it is not without its flaws. One of the criticisms of the Borda count is that it is susceptible to strategic voting. Strategic voting occurs when voters strategically rank candidates in order to manipulate the outcome of the election. This can lead to results that do not accurately reflect the true preferences of the voters.

Another popular voting system that addresses strategic voting is the Instant Runoff Voting (IRV) method. In IRV, voters rank candidates in order of preference, and the candidate with the fewest first-choice votes is eliminated. The votes of the eliminated candidate are then redistributed to the remaining candidates based on the voters’ second-choice preferences. This process continues until a candidate receives a majority of the votes.

One of the advantages of IRV is that it incentivizes voters to vote honestly, as there is no benefit to strategically ranking candidates. However, it is not without its critics. Some argue that IRV can still lead to non-monotonicity, where ranking a candidate higher can actually lower their chances of winning.

These are just a few examples of the many different voting systems that have been devised and studied by mathematicians and political scientists over the years. Each system has its strengths and weaknesses, and the quest for the perfect voting system continues.

In addition to the specific voting systems themselves, mathematical principles can also be applied to evaluate the fairness of elections. One such principle is proportionality, which seeks to ensure that the distribution of seats or representation matches the distribution of votes. For example, if a party receives 40% of the votes, they should ideally receive 40% of the seats.

Another aspect of fairness is the concept of monotonicity. Monotonicity ensures that increasing the support for a candidate should never harm their chances of winning. This principle is crucial in maintaining the integrity of the voting process.

Mathematics can also be used to explore the concept of gerrymandering, the practice of manipulating district boundaries to provide an advantage to a particular political party. By employing mathematical models, researchers are able to detect and quantify the extent of gerrymandering and propose alternative districts that are more representative.

Overall, the mathematics of voting systems and fairness is a fascinating field of study. It combines mathematics, probability theory, game theory, and political science to evaluate and design voting systems that accurately represent the preferences of the electorate. By understanding the underlying mathematical principles, we can strive for more fair and representative elections. So the next time you cast your vote, remember that mathematics plays a crucial role in shaping the outcome.
