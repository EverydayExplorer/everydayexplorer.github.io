Discovering Wine Faults: How to Identify and Handle Wine Flaws


Wine is a fascinating beverage that has been enjoyed by millions of people around the world for centuries. From its rich and diverse flavors to its ability to enhance meals and social gatherings, wine has become an integral part of many cultures. However, like any other natural product, wine is not immune to flaws. Understanding these flaws and knowing how to identify and handle them is essential for both wine enthusiasts and professionals in the industry. In this blog post, we will delve into the world of wine faults, exploring the common flaws that can occur in wine and providing you with the knowledge needed to detect and address them.

Why is it important to understand wine faults?

Wine faults can significantly impact the overall enjoyment and quality of a wine. Whether you are a consumer looking to purchase a bottle of wine or a sommelier responsible for curating a wine list, being able to detect and address wine flaws is crucial. By understanding wine faults, you will be armed with the knowledge needed to differentiate between a flawed wine and a wine that simply doesn’t meet your personal taste preferences. It can also help you make informed decisions about aging potential, storage conditions, and proper serving techniques.

Common Wine Faults:

1. Cork Taint (TCA):

Cork taint, also known as Trichloroanisole (TCA) contamination, is one of the most common wine faults. It occurs when natural cork comes into contact with a compound called TCA, resulting in a musty, moldy aroma and an unpleasant taste. The presence of cork taint can make a wine undrinkable and is often described as the smell of wet cardboard or a damp basement. To identify cork taint, simply smell the wine before tasting. If you detect any off-putting aromas, such as those mentioned, it is likely that the wine is affected.

How to handle cork taint:

If you find that a bottle of wine is affected by cork taint, there is unfortunately no way to salvage the wine. The best course of action is to contact the winery or retailer and explain the issue. Many reputable establishments will offer a replacement or refund.

2. Oxidation:

Oxidation occurs when wine is exposed to too much air, causing it to lose its freshness and vibrant flavors. Overexposure to air can happen through a faulty closure, such as a loose cork, or improper storage conditions. Signs of oxidation include a brownish color, a flat or dull taste, and aromas reminiscent of vinegar or sherry. Oxidation can happen to both red and white wines, but it is more commonly associated with older wines.

How to handle oxidation:

If you suspect that a wine has been oxidized, there are a few steps you can take to salvage it if the level of oxidation is not too severe. Firstly, try decanting the wine into a clean container, allowing it to come into contact with more oxygen and potentially open up some of its flavors. If this does not improve the wine, it may be best to use it for cooking or discard it. Proper storage techniques, such as sealing opened bottles with a wine preservation system or using inert gas sprays, can help prevent oxidation in the future.

3. Sulfur Compounds:

Sulfur compounds are commonly used in winemaking as a preservative and to prevent spoilage. While sulfur dioxide is an important component in winemaking, excessive amounts or poor usage can result in unwanted sulfurous aromas. These can range from burnt matches or rotten egg smells to a more pungent onion or garlic-like odor. The presence of sulfur compounds can greatly diminish the wine’s appeal.

How to handle sulfur compounds:

In many cases, the initial smell of sulfur compounds may dissipate with time and aeration. Swirling the wine in the glass can help release some of the volatile compounds, allowing them to evaporate. If the issue persists, decanting the wine or exposing it to oxygen for a short period may help. However, if the smells are overpowering and do not improve, it is best to contact the winery or retailer for assistance.

4. Brettanomyces:

Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘Brett,’ is a yeast strain that can impart distinct aromas and flavors in wine. While some minimal levels of Brett can add complexity and character to certain styles of wine, an overgrowth can result in unpleasant aromas and flavors reminiscent of barnyard, medicinal, or Band-Aid-like notes. The perception of Brett is subjective, with some wine enthusiasts enjoying its presence, but many consider it a fault.

How to handle Brettanomyces:

When it comes to Brettanomyces, handling is a matter of personal preference. Some wine enthusiasts appreciate the complexities it can bring to certain wine styles, while others find it off-putting. If you detect the presence of Brett, it is best to taste the wine and assess if the levels are tolerable to your palate. If it is too pronounced, consider contacting the winery or retailer for guidance.

Conclusion:

Understanding the most common wine faults and how to identify and handle them is essential for every wine lover. By knowing what to look out for and how to address these flaws, you can ensure that you enjoy your wine to its fullest potential. Whether it is a faulty cork, oxidation, excessive sulfur, or the presence of Brettanomyces, being equipped with this knowledge allows you to navigate the world of wine faults confidently. Remember, a flawed wine doesn’t mean the end of the world; it simply requires a bit of understanding and proper handling to ensure your overall wine experience remains enjoyable.
