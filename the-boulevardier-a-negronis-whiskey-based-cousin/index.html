The Boulevardier: A Negroni's Whiskey-Based Cousin


If you’re a fan of classic cocktails, chances are you’ve heard of the Negroni. This iconic Italian drink, made with equal parts gin, Campari, and sweet vermouth, has been a favorite among cocktail enthusiasts for decades. But what if I told you there’s a whiskey-based cousin of the Negroni that’s equally delicious and deserving of your attention? Allow me to introduce you to the Boulevardier.

The Origins of the Boulevardier

The Boulevardier is said to have originated in the 1920s during the heyday of the American expatriate community in Paris. It was first introduced by Erskine Gwynne, an American writer and socialite who published a literary magazine called “The Boulevardier.” Gwynne, known for his love of whiskey, decided to create a cocktail that showcased this spirit, while paying homage to his publication.

The Boulevardier Recipe

To make a Boulevardier, you’ll need the following ingredients:

  • 1 ½ ounces of bourbon or rye whiskey
  • 1 ounce of Campari
  • 1 ounce of sweet vermouth
  • Orange twist, for garnish

Here’s how you can prepare this delightful cocktail:

  1. Fill a mixing glass with ice.
  2. Add the bourbon or rye whiskey, Campari, and sweet vermouth.
  3. Stir gently for about 30 seconds to ensure the ingredients are thoroughly mixed and chilled.
  4. Strain the mixture into a chilled coupe or rocks glass.
  5. Garnish with an orange twist.

The Taste of the Boulevardier

One of the things that sets the Boulevardier apart from its gin-based counterpart is its rich and robust flavor profile. The whiskey brings a smoothness and depth that perfectly complements the bitter sweetness of the Campari and the herbal complexity of the sweet vermouth. The result is a cocktail that is both sophisticated and approachable, with a perfect balance of flavors.

Tips for Enjoying the Boulevardier

To fully appreciate the Boulevardier, here are a few tips to keep in mind:

  • Experiment with different types of whiskey: While bourbon and rye are the traditional choices for this cocktail, don’t be afraid to try it with other whiskey varieties. Each will bring its own unique character to the drink.
  • Adjust the sweetness: If you prefer a sweeter cocktail, you can increase the amount of sweet vermouth. Conversely, if you prefer a more bitter drink, you can decrease the amount of sweet vermouth and increase the Campari.
  • Explore different garnishes: While an orange twist is the classic garnish for the Boulevardier, feel free to get creative and experiment with other citrus peels or even a Luxardo cherry.

Conclusion

The Boulevardier is a cocktail that deserves a place in every cocktail lover’s repertoire. Its whiskey base adds a depth and complexity that sets it apart from the Negroni, while still maintaining the perfect balance of flavors. Whether you’re a whiskey aficionado or simply looking to try something new, the Boulevardier is sure to impress. So, the next time you’re in the mood for a classic cocktail, give this Negroni’s whiskey-based cousin a try. Cheers!
