The Norse Valkyries: Choosers of the Slain


When we think of Norse mythology, one of the most intriguing and mysterious aspects is the presence of the Valkyries. These fierce and powerful female deities were tasked with selecting the slain warriors who would be taken to Valhalla, the hall of the slain in Asgard. With their unique blend of beauty and strength, the Valkyries have captivated the imaginations of countless people throughout history.

Originating from Old Norse, the word ‘Valkyrie’ translates to ‘chooser of the slain’ or ‘feminine spirits of the fallen.’ They were believed to be Odin’s (the chief god in Norse mythology) handmaidens, serving him by selecting the bravest warriors who died in battle. These chosen warriors would become einherjar, elite warriors who would fight alongside the gods during the events of Ragnarok, the battle that would ultimately lead to the end of the world.

Symbolizing both life and death, the Valkyries were often depicted as ethereal and otherworldly beings. They were often described as beautiful maidens with long flowing hair, adorned in armor and wielding spears or swords. Additionally, they were sometimes portrayed riding upon majestic winged horses or flying swans, serving as a symbol of their ability to transcend the mortal realm.

One of the most famous mentions of the Valkyries in Norse mythology is in the epic poem ‘Völuspá.’ In this poem, the Valkyries are described as descending from the skies, riding to the battlefield to select the fallen warriors. They were believed to swoop down onto the battleground, choosing only the bravest and most worthy of men. This notion further highlights the valiant and courageous nature of the warriors who caught the attention of these divine beings.

The Valkyries were not only known for their role in battle but also for their ability to shape the fate of mortals. It was said that they had the power to predict and influence the outcome of conflicts. Their presence on the battlefield was often seen as an omen, signaling either victory or defeat for those engaged in the fight. Their role as arbiters of fate highlights their connection to both the mortal realm and the realm of the gods.

While the Valkyries were often associated with battle and death, they were revered and respected by the Norse people. They were seen as protectors and chosen by the gods themselves, highlighting their importance within the mythology. Their role in selecting the slain warriors for Valhalla also provided a glimmer of hope for those facing uncertain times. The prospect of being chosen by a Valkyrie and spending eternity in the halls of the gods was a comforting thought for many Norse warriors.

In addition to their role as choosers of the slain, the Valkyries were also associated with love and passion. In some tales, they were believed to intervene in matters of the heart, playing matchmaker between mortal lovers. This aspect showcased their multifaceted nature, linking them not just to war and death but also to the joy and beauty of life.

Much like other figures in Norse mythology, the Valkyries have endured in popular culture. They continue to inspire art, literature, and even films. Richard Wagner’s opera ‘Die Walküre’ prominently features the Valkyries and their iconic ride to the battlefield. Marvel Comics has also introduced the character Valkyrie, a powerful Asgardian warrior based on the Norse mythological figures.

In conclusion, the Norse Valkyries remain a fascinating and important part of Norse mythology. Their beauty, strength, and role as choosers of the slain have captivated the imagination of people for centuries. As symbols of both death and transcendence, they serve as a reminder of the complexities of life and the eternal struggle between good and evil. Whether depicted in ancient sagas or modern adaptations, the Valkyries continue to inspire and intrigue, cementing their place in the rich tapestry of Norse mythology.
