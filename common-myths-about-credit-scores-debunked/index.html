Common Myths About Credit Scores Debunked


Are you curious about credit scores? You’re not alone. Many people find themselves mystified by the complexities of credit scoring, and as a result, myths and misconceptions abound. In this comprehensive guide, we’ll debunk some of the most common myths surrounding credit scores, empowering you to make informed decisions about your financial health.

Myth 1: Checking Your Credit Score Will Lower It

One of the most pervasive myths about credit scores is that simply checking your score will cause it to drop. In reality, there are two types of credit inquiries: hard inquiries and soft inquiries. Hard inquiries occur when a lender checks your credit as part of a credit application, such as applying for a loan or credit card. These inquiries can have a minor impact on your score, but the effect is typically minimal and temporary. On the other hand, soft inquiries, which include checking your own credit score or when a potential employer performs a background check, have no impact on your score whatsoever. So feel free to monitor your credit score regularly without fear of it taking a hit.

Myth 2: Closing Old Accounts Will Improve Your Credit Score

It’s a common misconception that closing old or unused credit accounts will improve your credit score. In reality, closing accounts can actually harm your score in several ways. First, it can decrease your overall available credit, which can increase your credit utilization ratio—the amount of credit you’re using compared to the total amount available to you. A higher credit utilization ratio can negatively impact your score. Additionally, closing old accounts can shorten the average age of your credit history, which is another factor used to calculate your score. Instead of closing old accounts, consider keeping them open and using them responsibly to maintain a positive credit history.

Myth 3: Paying Off Debt Will Immediately Improve Your Credit Score

While paying off debt is certainly a positive financial move, don’t expect your credit score to skyrocket overnight. Your credit score is based on a variety of factors, including your payment history, credit utilization, length of credit history, types of credit accounts, and new credit inquiries. While paying off debt can improve your credit utilization ratio and demonstrate responsible financial behavior, it may take some time for these changes to be reflected in your credit score. Additionally, negative marks such as late payments or collections accounts can stay on your credit report for several years, impacting your score even after the debt has been paid off. Patience and consistency are key when it comes to improving your credit score.

Myth 4: Closing Credit Cards With High Limits Will Improve Your Score

Another common misconception is that closing credit cards with high credit limits will improve your credit score. In reality, closing these accounts can actually have the opposite effect. As mentioned earlier, closing accounts can decrease your overall available credit, which can increase your credit utilization ratio and potentially lower your score. Additionally, closing accounts with high credit limits can shorten the average age of your credit history, which can also impact your score. Instead of closing these accounts, consider keeping them open and using them responsibly to maintain a healthy credit profile.

Myth 5: You Only Have One Credit Score

Contrary to popular belief, you actually have multiple credit scores, not just one. There are several different credit scoring models used by various lenders and credit bureaus, each with its own methodology for calculating scores. The most well-known credit scoring model is the FICO score, which ranges from 300 to 850 and is used by many lenders to assess creditworthiness. However, there are also alternative scoring models, such as VantageScore, that may be used by some lenders. Additionally, each of the three major credit bureaus—Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion—may generate its own credit report and score based on the information in its database. As a result, you may have multiple credit scores, each of which can vary slightly depending on the scoring model and credit bureau used.

Myth 6: You Need to Carry a Balance to Build Credit

Contrary to popular belief, you don’t need to carry a balance on your credit cards to build credit. In fact, carrying a balance can actually cost you money in the form of interest payments without providing any additional benefit to your credit score. Instead, focus on using your credit cards responsibly by paying your balance in full and on time each month. This demonstrates to lenders that you’re able to manage credit responsibly and can help you build a positive credit history. Additionally, keeping your credit utilization ratio low—ideally below 30%—can further improve your credit score. So don’t fall into the trap of carrying a balance thinking it will help your credit. Pay your bills on time, keep your balances low, and watch your credit score rise.

In conclusion, understanding the truth behind common credit score myths is essential for maintaining healthy credit and making informed financial decisions. By debunking these myths, you can empower yourself to take control of your financial future and achieve your goals. So the next time you hear someone perpetuating a credit score myth, set the record straight and share your newfound knowledge. Your credit score—and your wallet—will thank you.
