The Folklore of Food: Superstitions and Traditions


Have you ever wondered why certain foods are considered lucky or unlucky? Or why we follow specific food traditions during holidays and special occasions? Food has always been an integral part of human culture, and it’s no surprise that it comes with its own folklore, superstitions, and traditions. In this blog post, we will delve into the fascinating world of food folklore, exploring the stories behind some of our most beloved culinary beliefs.

1. Breaking the Wishbone: One popular food tradition is breaking the wishbone, often found in poultry like chicken or turkey. It is believed that if two people pull the bone apart and it breaks unevenly, the person left with the larger piece will have their wish granted. This tradition dates back to ancient times when people believed that the bone held magical powers.

2. Spilling Salt: We’ve all heard the saying, ‘Don’t spill salt, or you’ll have bad luck!’ This superstition originates from the belief that salt is a precious commodity and wasting it brings misfortune. To ward off bad luck, it is customary to throw a pinch of spilled salt over your left shoulder, symbolically blinding evil spirits lurking behind you.

3. Lucky Number 7: The number seven holds special significance in many cultures, and food is no exception. It is believed that consuming seven courses during a meal brings good luck and prosperity. This tradition can be traced back to ancient times when the number seven was considered sacred and symbolized completeness.

4. New Year’s Food Traditions: New Year’s Eve is a time for celebration and new beginnings, and many cultures have specific food traditions associated with this holiday. For example, in Spain, it is customary to eat 12 grapes at midnight, one for each stroke of the clock, symbolizing good luck for each month of the coming year. In the Southern United States, eating black-eyed peas on New Year’s Day is believed to bring good fortune and prosperity.

5. Wedding Cake Traditions: Wedding cakes have a long history of symbolism and superstition. The tradition of saving the top tier of the wedding cake for the couple’s first anniversary is believed to bring good luck and fertility. Additionally, the act of cutting the cake together represents the couple’s first joint task as newlyweds.

6. Lenten Traditions: During the Christian season of Lent, many people give up certain foods or habits as a form of sacrifice. In some cultures, it is believed that eating pancakes on Shrove Tuesday, also known as Pancake Day, will bring good luck and ensure a bountiful harvest for the coming year.

7. Birthday Candle Traditions: Blowing out birthday candles is a fun tradition, but did you know it has its roots in ancient superstition? It was believed that evil spirits were attracted to the smoke from extinguished candles. By blowing out the candles in one breath, the birthday person could ward off these spirits and ensure a year of good luck.

Food folklore is an intriguing aspect of our cultural heritage, connecting us to our ancestors and their beliefs. Whether it’s breaking wishbones, spilling salt, or following specific food traditions, these customs add depth and meaning to our culinary experiences. So the next time you encounter a food superstition or tradition, take a moment to appreciate the rich folklore behind it and enjoy the delicious connections to our past.
