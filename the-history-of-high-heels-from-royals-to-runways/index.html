The History of High Heels: From Royals to Runways


High heels have become synonymous with fashion and glamour, but their origins date back centuries. From their humble beginnings as functional footwear to their iconic status in the modern world, high heels have gone through a fascinating evolution. In this blog post, we will take a deep dive into the history of high heels, exploring their origins, their significance throughout history, and their enduring popularity in today’s fashion world.

The origins of high heels can be traced back to ancient civilizations. The Ancient Egyptians were one of the earliest cultures to don footwear with elevated heels. However, these early versions were not solely for fashion purposes but had practical uses. The elevated heels on Egyptian sandals were designed to elevate the wearer’s feet above the hot desert sands.

High heels also had a practical purpose in ancient Rome. Roman actors and those of high social status wore ‘buskins,’ a type of footwear with elevated soles, to signify their elevated status on stage or in society.

It was not until the 16th century that high heels truly made their mark in Europe, thanks to Catherine de’ Medici. The Italian-born queen of France, a fashion-forward trendsetter, introduced high heels to the French court. She was known for her petite stature, and high heels helped her appear taller and more regal. The trend quickly caught on with the French nobility, and high heels became a symbol of status and power.

During the 17th century, heels became even more fashionable, particularly among men. King Louis XIV of France, also known as the Sun King, was an avid wearer of high heels. He issued an edict that only members of his court were allowed to wear red heels, solidifying their association with royalty.

In the following centuries, high heels began to decline in popularity for men but surged in popularity among women. In the 18th century, French fashion designer Marie Antoinette popularized the high-heeled shoe as a symbol of femininity and wealth. The French Revolution saw a decline in extravagant dress, including high heels, but they made a comeback in the 19th century with the rise of Victorian fashion.

The 20th century saw high heels cement their status as a fashion staple. The iconic stiletto heel was introduced in the 1950s by designer Roger Vivier, and it quickly became a symbol of femininity and seduction. Audrey Hepburn and Marilyn Monroe were among the many iconic women who helped popularize high heels during this era.

Throughout the latter half of the 20th century, high heels continued to evolve and adapt to changing fashion trends. Platform heels became popular in the 1970s, while the 1980s brought about chunky heels and power dressing. Designers like Christian Louboutin and Manolo Blahnik became household names, creating luxurious, coveted high-heeled shoes that were prized by celebrities and fashionistas alike.

In the modern era, high heels have become more diverse than ever. From sky-high stilettos to comfortable block heels, the options are endless. High heels are no longer just for formal occasions; they are now embraced in both casual and professional settings.

Despite their enduring popularity, high heels have come under scrutiny for their potential negative health effects. Prolonged wear can lead to foot pain, instability, and even long-term damage. However, advancements in shoe technology and the rise of comfortable alternatives have made it possible for fashion-forward individuals to enjoy the beauty of high heels without sacrificing comfort.

In conclusion, the history of high heels is a testament to their enduring appeal and their ability to adapt to changing fashion trends. From their practical origins in ancient civilizations to their association with power and beauty in royal courts, high heels have continually evolved to remain at the forefront of fashion. They have become a symbol of femininity, power, and self-expression. Whether worn by royals, on the runways, or in everyday life, high heels continue to captivate our attention and elevate our style.
