Mathematics in Robotics: Kinematics and Control


Robotics is an interdisciplinary field that combines elements of computer science, engineering, and mathematics. Mathematics plays a critical role in various aspects of robotics, from designing robot structures to control and motion planning. In this blog post, we will focus on one specific area of robotics known as kinematics and control, exploring how mathematics enables robots to move and interact with their environment.

Kinematics in Robotics

Kinematics is the study of the motion of objects without considering the forces that cause the motion. In the context of robotics, kinematics deals with understanding and describing the movement of robot limbs, joint angles, and end-effectors. Mathematical representations of robot kinematics can help determine the position, velocity, and acceleration of robot components.

Forward Kinematics

Forward kinematics is the process of determining the position and orientation of the end-effector given the joint angles of a robot. This process involves using mathematical transformations such as rotations and translations to compute the position and orientation of each segment of the robot’s arm based on the joint angles.

The Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) convention is a mathematical framework commonly used for representing the kinematics of robotic systems. It provides a systematic way to assign coordinate systems to each joint and derive the transformation matrices between consecutive joint frames.

Inverse Kinematics

Inverse kinematics, on the other hand, involves finding the joint angles required for a robot’s end-effector to reach a desired position and orientation in space. It allows robots to perform tasks such as picking and placing objects, painting, or even performing intricate surgical procedures.

Inverse kinematics problems can be challenging because there may be multiple solutions or no solution at all. Mathematical techniques such as numerical optimization, geometric methods, and analytical solutions are used to solve these problems. Algorithms like the Jacobian matrix and the Newton-Raphson method are employed to compute the joint angles for desired end-effector poses.

Control in Robotics

Control is another crucial aspect of robotics, enabling precise and coordinated movements of robot actuators. Mathematics provides the foundation for control theory, which deals with designing algorithms and systems to command and regulate the behavior of robots.

Feedback Control

Feedback control uses measurements from sensors to continuously adjust and correct the robot’s motions. This method allows robots to adapt to changing environmental conditions and disturbances. It involves comparing desired robot states or trajectories with the actual states provided by sensors and using mathematical techniques to determine the required control inputs.

Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) control is a widely used feedback control strategy. It relies on error signals (the difference between desired and actual states) to adjust control inputs proportionally, based on past errors, and considering the rate of change of errors.

Trajectory Planning

Trajectory planning involves generating smooth and collision-free paths for the robot to follow. Mathematics comes into play when determining how the robot should move between waypoints or avoid obstacles.

Various mathematical algorithms such as the Rapidly-exploring Random Trees (RRT) and Artificial Potential Fields (APF) are used for trajectory planning. RRT helps explore the robot’s workspace efficiently, while APF assigns attractive and repulsive forces to drive the robot towards the goal and away from obstacles.

Conclusion

Mathematics is a fundamental tool in the field of robotics, particularly in kinematics and control. By leveraging mathematical concepts and algorithms, engineers and researchers are able to design robots that can efficiently perform tasks in a variety of scenarios. The study of kinematics allows for precise and accurate control of robot movements, while control theory provides the means for regulating and adapting robot behavior. This symbiotic relationship between mathematics and robotics ensures that robots can operate safely and effectively, unlocking a wide range of applications across industries.
