The Influence of Skin Contact in Winemaking: From White to Orange Wines


When it comes to winemaking, there are many variables that can influence the taste and quality of a wine. One of these variables is skin contact, which refers to the amount of time that grape skins remain in contact with the juice during fermentation. While this technique is commonly associated with red wines, it is also used in the production of white and orange wines, and can have a profound influence on the final product.

Skin contact is a winemaking technique that dates back thousands of years and is particularly important in the production of red wines. During the fermentation process, grape skins contain pigments, tannins, and aromas that can greatly impact the flavor and structure of the wine. By allowing the juice to ferment with the skins, winemakers are able to extract these compounds and create wines with greater complexity and depth.

In recent years, skin contact has also gained popularity in the production of white wines. Traditionally, white wines are made by quickly pressing the juice off the skins to minimize contact and preserve the wine’s delicate flavors and aromas. However, by allowing the juice to ferment with the skins, winemakers are able to create white wines with a richer mouthfeel and greater intensity of flavors. This technique is commonly used in the production of white wines known as ‘orange wines,’ which have a deep golden color and a unique flavor profile.

Orange wines are made from white grapes, but are produced using techniques typically associated with red winemaking. The grape skins and seeds are left in contact with the juice for an extended period of time, resulting in a wine with a deep amber or orange hue. This extended skin contact not only adds color to the wine, but also imparts tannins and flavors that are not typically found in white wines. Orange wines are known for their complexity, with flavors ranging from citrus and stone fruit to nuts and spice. They often have a distinct texture and can be quite savory, making them a fascinating and versatile addition to any wine lover’s collection.

The influence of skin contact on winemaking extends beyond just the flavor and color of the wine. It can also impact the wine’s texture, mouthfeel, and aging potential. Tannins, which are naturally present in grape skins, provide structure and can help wines age gracefully. By allowing the juice to ferment with the skins, winemakers are able to extract these tannins and create wines that have the potential to improve with time.

However, it is important to note that skin contact is not suitable for all grape varieties or winemaking styles. Some white grape varieties have very delicate flavors and aromas that can be easily overwhelmed by extended skin contact. Additionally, the amount of time that the skins are left in contact with the juice can greatly influence the final product. Shorter skin contact may result in a wine with subtle flavors and aromas, while longer skin contact can produce a wine with more pronounced characteristics.

In conclusion, skin contact is a winemaking technique that can greatly influence the taste and quality of a wine. From red wines with greater complexity and depth, to white wines with a richer mouthfeel and intensity of flavors, to the unique and intriguing orange wines, the amount of time that grape skins remain in contact with the juice can have a profound impact on the final product. It is an exciting technique that allows winemakers to create wines with a range of flavors and textures, and adds a new dimension to the world of wine. Whether you are a red wine lover, a white wine connoisseur, or simply curious about the world of orange wines, exploring the influence of skin contact in winemaking is sure to be a fascinating journey.
