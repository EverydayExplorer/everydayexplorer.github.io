Criminal Justice and Mental Health: A Broken System


The intersection of mental health and the criminal justice system is a complex and multifaceted issue with significant repercussions for individuals and society as a whole. While the criminal justice system is intended to ensure public safety and hold individuals accountable for their actions, the system is often poorly equipped to address and accommodate the mental illness. Instead, the system typically criminalizes those with mental health issues and exacerbates their conditions.

Mental health disorders, including depression, anxiety, bipolar, schizophrenia, and substance abuse disorders, affect millions of Americans. According to the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH), approximately one in five adults in the United States live with a mental illness. However, statistics show that people with mental health conditions are over-represented in the criminal justice system. It is estimated that up to 20% of inmates in jails and prisons have a serious mental illness.

The criminalization of mental illness predominantly occurs in two ways: through the criminalization of non-violent offenses and through prevalence in the homeless population. People with mental illness are often criminalized for non-violent offenses, such as disorderly conduct, trespassing, and loitering, which can result from symptoms of their mental illness. The criminalization of non-violent acts associated with mental illness perpetuates a cycle of stigma and discrimination for those with mental illness, all the while providing these individuals with insufficient care solutions.

The homeless population is particularly vulnerable to criminalization and incarceration due to its significant overlap with mental illness. According to a survey conducted by the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA), 26% of homeless adults in shelters live with serious mental illness. These individuals are often criminalized for “quality of life” offenses, such as sleeping on the street or engaging in public urination. The criminalization of homelessness and mental illness exacerbates these issues, resulting in further stigmatization, abuse, and in some cases, violence.

The criminal justice system is not equipped to handle mental illness. Correctional facilities have a strained relationship with mental health, with the prevalence of mental health concerns rising as they bear witness to a lack of resources and inadequate training given to correctional officers. Additionally, correctional facilities tend not to meet the needs of people with mental health disorders due to the way facilities are structured and managed. As a result, it is not uncommon for people with mental health concerns to be repeatedly incarcerated and given insufficient treatment.

Due to these limitations in the criminal justice system, mental health professionals are beginning to provide alternative solutions. The primary alternative is diversion programs that focus on community-based programs that can better serve people with mental health disorders than prisons. Diversion programs often work in conjunction with mental health services, providing individuals with services such as therapy, medication, and housing assistance. Diversion programs have been shown to result in a decrease in recidivism and an increase in community safety by providing participants with the care needed to better manage their conditions.

The intersection of mental health and the criminal justice system is a pressing issue that must be addressed. By addressing the root causes of the over-representation of mental health concerns in the criminal justice system, such as the criminalization of non-violent offenses and homelessness, community-based diversion programs can provide individuals with the help and support they need to manage their conditions effectively. As a result, we can reduce the stigma and discrimination experienced by people with mental illness, while also resulting in a safer and more cohesive society.
