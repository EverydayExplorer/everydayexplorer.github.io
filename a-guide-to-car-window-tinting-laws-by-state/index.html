A Guide to Car Window Tinting Laws by State


When it comes to car window tinting, it’s important to be aware of the laws in your state. Window tinting laws can vary greatly from state to state, and it’s crucial to comply with these regulations in order to avoid any legal issues.

Window tinting is a popular car modification that offers various benefits, such as protection from harmful UV rays, heat reduction, and enhanced privacy. However, it’s important to remember that excessive window tinting can be dangerous and may obstruct the driver’s vision, especially at night. This is why each state has its own regulations in place to ensure that window tints are within a safe and legal limit.

To help you navigate through the various window tinting laws across the United States, this article provides a comprehensive guide to car window tinting laws by state.

Alabama:
In Alabama, the front side windows must allow at least 32% of light to pass through. The back side windows and rear window can have any darkness level.

Alaska:
Alaska allows any window tint darkness for all windows, as long as the vehicle has both left and right side mirrors.

Arizona:
For Arizona, non-reflective tint is allowed above the manufacturer’s AS-1 line, which is the top 5 inches of the windshield. The front side windows must allow at least 33% of light to pass through, while the back side windows and rear window can have any darkness level.
