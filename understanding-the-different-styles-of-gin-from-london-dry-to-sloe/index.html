Understanding the Different Styles of Gin: From London Dry to Sloe


Gin has been a beloved spirit for centuries, known for its versatility and unique flavors. It’s an essential ingredient in classic cocktails like the Martini and the Negroni, and it has gained a loyal following among cocktail enthusiasts worldwide. But did you know that gin comes in various styles, each with its own distinct characteristics? From the iconic London Dry to the fruity Sloe, let’s take a journey through the different styles of gin and explore what sets them apart.

1. London Dry Gin:
London Dry is perhaps the most famous style of gin, known for its crisp and juniper-forward profile. Contrary to its name, this style of gin can be produced anywhere in the world, as long as it adheres to certain production methods. London Dry gin is made by redistilling a neutral grain spirit in the presence of botanicals, with juniper being the dominant flavor. The result is a dry and aromatic gin with a clean finish. London Dry gins often exhibit notes of citrus, coriander, and angelica root, making them perfect for classic gin cocktails.

2. Plymouth Gin:
Plymouth gin hails from the historic naval city of Plymouth in England. This style of gin is defined by its geographical indication, meaning it can only be produced in Plymouth. Plymouth gin is slightly sweeter and less juniper-focused than London Dry, with a smoother and more mellow character. It often displays earthy and herbal notes, with a hint of spice. Plymouth gin is a great choice for those who prefer a more balanced and approachable gin.

3. Old Tom Gin:
Old Tom gin is a style that bridges the gap between the classic London Dry and the sweeter Dutch genevers. It was popular during the 18th and 19th centuries, especially during the heyday of gin cocktails. Old Tom gin is slightly sweetened, providing a smoother and more flavorful experience. It is known for its rich mouthfeel and botanical complexity. Old Tom gin is a fantastic choice for cocktails like the Martinez and the Tom Collins, where its sweetness adds depth and balance.

4. Genever:
Genever is the ancestor of modern gin and originated in the Netherlands. Unlike the other styles mentioned so far, genever is made from a blend of malt spirit and neutral grain spirit, giving it a distinct maltiness. It has a more pronounced and robust flavor compared to London Dry, with a malty backbone and a hint of sweetness. Genever can be enjoyed neat or on the rocks, and it also adds a unique twist to classic cocktails.

5. Sloe Gin:
Sloe gin is a fruit-infused style that is often enjoyed as a liqueur. It starts with a base of gin, which is then steeped with sloe berries (a small, dark fruit related to plums) and sweetened with sugar. The result is a ruby-red spirit with a fruity and slightly tart taste. Sloe gin is often enjoyed on its own, over ice, or as an ingredient in cocktails. Its vibrant color and sweet flavor make it a favorite during the colder months.

6. Contemporary Gins:
In recent years, the gin industry has seen an explosion of contemporary gins that push the boundaries of traditional styles. These gins often experiment with unique botanicals, unconventional production methods, and creative flavor profiles. From floral gins infused with rose petals to citrus-forward gins bursting with tropical flavors, contemporary gins offer exciting and innovative options for gin enthusiasts.

In conclusion, gin is a spirit that comes in a wide variety of styles, each with its own characteristics and flavor profiles. From the classic London Dry to the fruity Sloe, there is a gin out there to suit every palate. So, whether you prefer a juniper-forward gin in your Martini or a sweeter option in your Tom Collins, take the time to explore and appreciate the diverse world of gin. Cheers!
