The Ethics of Volunteer Tourism


Volunteer tourism, more commonly known as “voluntourism”, is a booming industry that’s been gaining popularity in recent years. With increasing interest in sustainable travel and giving back to communities, it’s no surprise that more and more people are opting for voluntourism opportunities. However, with the rise of voluntourism, comes the need to reflect on its ethics.

Volunteer tourism refers to the practice of individuals traveling to another country for the purpose of volunteering their time and skills to assist in local communities. Voluntourism opportunities range from wildlife conservation and healthcare to education and infrastructure development. While it’s commendable for individuals to want to give back, there are several ethical considerations that need to be taken into account.

The “White Saviour” Complex

One of the most significant issues associated with voluntourism is the “white savior” complex. This term refers to the idea that voluntourists from Western countries are viewed as superior beings, and as such, they believe they’re “saving” the people they volunteer for. The issue with this is that it undermines the local community’s ability to assist themselves. By perpetuating this stereotype, voluntourists can create a dependency on Western assistance, which can lead to the disempowerment of the local community.

Short-term Impact

Another ethical concern with voluntourism is the impact of short-term volunteering. The majority of voluntourists only volunteer for a few weeks or months, which means that they don’t have sufficient time to make a significant difference. Additionally, the time taken to train volunteers and the disruption caused to the local community can have a negative impact. The lack of volunteers’ long-term commitment, combined with the cost of travel, can lead to a waste of resources and a negative impact on the local community.

The Cost of Voluntourism

Voluntourism can also be expensive. While some organizations offer affordable opportunities, many charge high fees for participants to take part in volunteer programs. This can exclude individuals from low-income backgrounds from volunteering, which can perpetuate the “white saviour” stereotype. Additionally, the money paid by voluntourists doesn’t always go directly to the local community, so it’s important to do research and support organizations that are transparent about where the funds go.

Skills and Experience

While voluntourism opportunities are open to anyone, it’s important to reflect on the skills and experience that you have to offer. In some cases, it may be more beneficial to donate money to an organization rather than volunteering your time. Similarly, before offering your services, it’s essential to research the local community’s needs and whether your skills align with what’s required.

In conclusion, while voluntourism can have positive impacts, it’s essential to think critically about its ethical implications. To ensure voluntourism has a positive impact, organizations and individuals must prioritize transparency, community empowerment, and long-term commitment. By recognizing the need for ethical practices in voluntourism, we can strive towards sustainable and meaningful volunteer opportunities that genuinely benefit the local community.
