Why Do We Cry? Unraveling the Science Behind Emotional Tears


Tears have a unique power to convey our deepest emotions - from joy and laughter to sadness and grief. But have you ever wondered why we cry? What is the science behind these emotional tears? In this blog post, we will delve into the fascinating world of crying and explore the reasons behind this universal human phenomenon.

Crying is a natural and instinctive response to various emotional stimuli. It is a complex physiological process that involves our brain, nervous system, and tear glands. While crying is often associated with sadness and pain, it can also be triggered by happiness, empathy, and even awe-inspiring moments.

One of the primary reasons we cry is to release and relieve emotional stress. When we experience intense emotions, such as sadness or frustration, our brain signals the release of stress hormones, including cortisol. These hormones can build up in our body and lead to physical and psychological discomfort. Crying helps to flush out these stress hormones, providing us with a sense of relief and catharsis.

Furthermore, tears contain natural painkillers and mood-enhancing substances, such as endorphins and oxytocin. These chemicals can help reduce pain and promote feelings of happiness and bonding. That’s why shedding tears during emotional moments can actually make us feel better and more connected to others.

Another interesting aspect of crying is its social function. Humans are social beings, and crying serves as a powerful means of communication. When we cry, we signal distress and vulnerability, eliciting empathy and support from those around us. This social response can foster a sense of closeness and strengthen interpersonal bonds.

But why do we cry emotional tears, while other animals do not? The answer lies in the evolution of our tear glands. Humans have developed specialized tear glands, called lacrimal glands, which produce emotional tears. These tears contain higher levels of proteins, hormones, and other substances that are absent in basal tears, which are produced to keep our eyes lubricated and free from debris.

While the exact reason for the evolution of emotional tears is still debated, scientists believe that it may have been a byproduct of our complex emotional and social lives. Our ability to cry emotional tears may have provided an evolutionary advantage by facilitating communication, enhancing social bonding, and promoting emotional well-being.

In conclusion, crying is an intricate and multifaceted process that goes far beyond a simple emotional response. It is a vital mechanism through which we release stress, experience emotional relief, and connect with others on a deeper level. The science behind crying sheds light on the intricate workings of our human nature and highlights the importance of embracing and understanding our emotions.

So, the next time you find yourself shedding tears, remember that it is a natural and healthy part of being human. Embrace the power of your tears and appreciate the beauty of this emotional phenomenon.
