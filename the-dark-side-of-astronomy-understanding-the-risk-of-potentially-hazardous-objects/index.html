The Dark Side of Astronomy: Understanding the Risk of Potentially Hazardous Objects


Astronomy is one of the oldest sciences in the world, and it has always been a source of fascination for humanity. From the stars in the sky to the discovery of new planets, astronomy has provided us with a glimpse into the vast universe which surrounds us.

However, there is a dark side to astronomy that many people are often unaware of. This dark side is the study of potentially hazardous objects (PHOs) which includes asteroids and comets. PHOs are celestial objects that have the potential to collide with the Earth and cause catastrophic damage.

It is believed that a large asteroid was the cause of the mass extinction that wiped out the dinosaurs over 65 million years ago. More recently, in 2013, a small asteroid exploded over the city of Chelyabinsk in Russia, injuring thousands of people and causing significant damage to buildings.

So what is being done to protect us from these potentially disastrous objects? Thankfully, a number of programs are in place to monitor and identify PHOs that may pose a risk to our planet.

One of these programs is the NASA Near-Earth Object Program (NEOP). This program monitors PHOs that come within 30 million miles of Earth and tracks their trajectories to determine if they pose a risk of impact. If a PHO is identified as a potential threat, NASA works with other space agencies to develop strategies to mitigate the risk.

Another program is the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS). This program consists of two telescopes that are able to track PHOs and determine their trajectory. ATLAS is designed to identify PHOs that are at least 30 meters in diameter and is capable of detecting objects several days before they come into contact with Earth.

Despite these programs, there is still a risk of a PHO colliding with our planet. It is impossible to predict when or where a PHO will collide with Earth, but the likelihood remains low. However, the consequences of such a collision would be catastrophic.

In conclusion, while there is a dark side to astronomy, it is important to understand the risks posed by PHOs. With continued research and monitoring, we can better protect our planet from potential impacts. So the best way to minimize the risk is to continue monitoring and investing in research in order to protect our planet.
