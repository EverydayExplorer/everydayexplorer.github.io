The Influence of Microorganisms on Winemaking: From Wild Fermentation to Brettanomyces


In the magical world of winemaking, the role of microorganisms is often underestimated. These tiny creatures play a significant role in shaping the flavors, aromas, and overall character of the wine. From wild fermentation to the notorious Brettanomyces, microorganisms have the power to make or break a wine. In this blog post, we will explore the fascinating influence of microorganisms on winemaking and how they contribute to the complex and diverse world of wine.

Let’s start with the basics. Microorganisms are microscopic organisms that exist all around us. In winemaking, they are primarily responsible for the fermentation process, where grape juice is transformed into wine. The most common microorganism involved in this process is yeast. Yeast consumes the sugars in grape juice and converts them into alcohol, producing carbon dioxide as a byproduct. This is known as alcoholic fermentation.

Traditionally, winemakers relied on wild fermentation, where the natural yeast present on the grape skins initiates the fermentation process. This method allows for a more spontaneous and unique expression of the wine. The flavors and aromas produced during wild fermentation can vary greatly depending on the specific strain of yeast present. This creates a sense of terroir, as the microorganisms indigenous to a particular vineyard contribute to the wine’s distinctive character.

However, the use of wild fermentation can be unpredictable and risky. Winemakers have little control over the fermentation process, which can result in off-flavors and spoilage. To mitigate these risks, many winemakers now prefer to use cultured yeast strains, which are specifically selected for their predictable fermentation characteristics. This ensures a more consistent quality and flavor profile in the final wine.

Now, let’s talk about Brettanomyces, a microorganism that has gained infamy in the wine world. Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘Brett,’ is a type of yeast that can cause significant changes in wine aroma and flavor. Some wine enthusiasts adore its earthy, spicy, and leathery aromas, considering it a desirable characteristic in certain styles of wine, such as Syrah and Mourvèdre. However, others perceive it as a flaw, as it can overpower the fruit and result in off-putting aromas, akin to band-aids or barnyards.

Brettanomyces can be quite tenacious and difficult to control. It can survive in the winery environment, even after thorough cleaning and sanitization. Winemakers use various techniques to prevent or manage Brettanomyces, such as monitoring sulfur levels, maintaining proper hygiene practices, and utilizing effective filtration methods. Some winemakers even embrace its presence and intentionally allow a small amount of Brettanomyces to add complexity to their wines.

In conclusion, microorganisms have a profound impact on winemaking, from wild fermentation to the influence of Brettanomyces. They contribute to the unique character and flavor profile of each wine, making the world of wine diverse and exciting. Whether it’s the spontaneous nature of wild fermentation or the controversial presence of Brettanomyces, microorganisms add an element of unpredictability and complexity to the winemaking process. So, the next time you enjoy a glass of wine, take a moment to appreciate the hidden influence of these tiny creatures. Cheers!
