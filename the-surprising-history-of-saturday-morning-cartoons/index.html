The Surprising History of Saturday Morning Cartoons


Saturday mornings have always held a special place in the hearts of children and adults alike. It’s a time when the weekend is in full swing, and there’s a sense of excitement in the air. And for many of us, that excitement was fueled by one thing - Saturday morning cartoons.

If you grew up in the 80s or 90s, you can probably still remember waking up early, grabbing a bowl of sugary cereal, and plopping down in front of the television to watch your favorite cartoons. But have you ever wondered how this tradition came to be? The history of Saturday morning cartoons is actually quite surprising.

Believe it or not, the concept of airing cartoons specifically on Saturday mornings began in 1962 as a result of a deal struck between major television networks and toy companies. At that time, television was still relatively new, and the Saturday morning time slot was considered a wasteland in terms of programming. However, toy companies saw a golden opportunity to promote their products directly to children.

The networks, on the other hand, were eager to fill this unused airtime. They saw the potential of capturing the attention of young viewers and advertisers, who were specifically targeting the children’s market. It was a win-win situation - the toy companies would finance the production of the cartoons, and the networks would have content to fill their Saturday morning slots.

The first wave of Saturday morning cartoons included classics like The Flintstones, The Jetsons, and Scooby-Doo. These shows were a hit with kids, and soon other networks joined in, creating a fierce competition for viewership. Saturday mornings became a battleground for networks and advertisers, as they vied for the attention of children and, by extension, their parents’ wallets.

As the popularity of Saturday morning cartoons grew, so did the pressure on networks to produce more content. This led to the creation of a phenomenon known as ‘cartoon blocks,’ where several cartoons would air consecutively within a two- to three-hour time frame. These blocks allowed the networks to showcase a wide variety of shows and appeal to different age groups.

Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, Saturday morning cartoons reached the height of their popularity. Iconic shows like Transformers, Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles, and Pokémon became cultural phenomena, capturing the hearts of millions of children across the world. The success of these shows led to the creation of merchandise, action figures, and even spin-off movies, further cementing their place in pop culture history.

However, as the new millennium approached, the landscape of television began to change. Cable and satellite television became more prevalent, offering children a plethora of channels with dedicated children’s programming. Couple this with the rise of the internet and on-demand streaming services, and the traditional Saturday morning cartoons began to lose their charm.

In the early 2000s, major networks started to shift away from Saturday morning cartoons, replacing them with news programming and educational shows. The decline of this once beloved tradition saddened many fans, marking the end of an era.

But even though Saturday morning cartoons may no longer be a staple of television programming, their legacy remains. The impact these shows had on popular culture, storytelling, and animation cannot be overstated. They shaped the childhoods of generations and left an indelible mark on the entertainment industry.

So, the next time you stumble upon an old episode of a Saturday morning cartoon, take a moment to appreciate the history behind it. It’s a reminder of a simpler time when Saturday mornings were filled with laughter, adventure, and a sense of wonder. And while the world may have changed, the memories of those Saturday mornings will continue to brighten our days.
