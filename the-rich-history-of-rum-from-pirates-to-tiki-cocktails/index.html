The Rich History of Rum: From Pirates to Tiki Cocktails


Rum, the luscious and versatile spirit made from sugarcane, has a history as rich and complex as its flavor profile. From the high seas to tropical beaches, rum has played a significant role in shaping the world’s culture, economy, and cocktail scene. In this blog post, we’ll embark on a journey through time to explore the fascinating history of rum, from its origins in the Caribbean to its role in iconic tiki cocktails.

Origins of Rum

Rum’s story begins in the Caribbean, where sugarcane was introduced by Christopher Columbus in the late 15th century. As sugarcane plantations flourished, so did the production of sugar, molasses, and eventually, rum. The earliest recorded distillation of rum took place in the 17th century on the sugarcane plantations of Barbados. Back then, it was a rough, unaged spirit known as ‘kill-devil’ due to its strong and fiery nature.

Pirates and Privateers

Rum quickly became a staple for sailors and pirates who roamed the high seas. It was a source of both sustenance and solace during long, treacherous journeys. Pirates like Captain Morgan and Blackbeard were known to enjoy their fair share of rum, and it often played a role in their raucous exploits. Privateers, government-sanctioned pirates of sorts, were also known to receive rum rations as part of their compensation.

The Triangle Trade

During the colonial era, rum played a central role in the infamous Triangle Trade. This triangular route involved the exchange of goods, including rum, between Europe, Africa, and the Americas. The molasses produced as a byproduct of sugar production in the Caribbean was sent to New England, where it was distilled into rum. This rum was then traded for enslaved Africans in West Africa. The cruel and inhumane Triangle Trade was driven, in part, by the demand for rum.

The American Revolution

Rum also played a role in the American Revolution. The British imposed heavy taxes on molasses and sugar, leading to a surge in smuggling and illicit distillation in the American colonies. The infamous Boston Tea Party of 1773 was fueled by anger over British taxation, which included the Sugar Act.
