The Mythology of Seasons: How Folklore Explains the Changing of the Year


The changing of the seasons has long fascinated humans throughout history. From the blistering heat of summer to the icy chill of winter, each season brings its own unique charm and wonder. But have you ever wondered how these transitions came to be? The answer lies in the rich tapestry of folklore and mythology that has been passed down through generations. In this blog post, we will delve into the captivating mythology of seasons and explore the fascinating stories behind the changing of the year.

1. The Origins of Spring:
Spring, the season of rebirth and renewal, has been celebrated in various mythologies across the world. In Greek mythology, the arrival of spring was closely associated with the goddess Persephone. According to the myth, Persephone was kidnapped by Hades, the god of the underworld, and taken to his realm during winter. Her mother, Demeter, the goddess of agriculture, mourned the loss of her daughter and caused the earth to become barren. However, when Persephone was allowed to return to the surface world, Demeter’s joy brought back the blooming of flowers and the return of fertility to the land.

2. The Power of Summer:
Summer, the season of warmth and abundance, holds a special place in many cultures’ mythology. In Norse mythology, the summer season was linked to the god Baldr, the epitome of light, beauty, and joy. Baldr’s presence brought forth long, sunny days and bountiful harvests. However, his tragic death at the hands of Loki, the trickster god, marked the beginning of the gradual decline of summer into autumn and eventually winter. This myth symbolizes the fleeting nature of summer’s blissful days.

3. The Harvest of Autumn:
Autumn, a season of harvest and preparation for the approaching winter, is filled with myths and legends. In Celtic mythology, the festival of Samhain marked the end of the harvest season and the beginning of winter. It was believed that during this time, the veil between the living and the dead was at its thinnest, allowing spirits to roam freely. This belief gave birth to the modern-day celebration of Halloween. The mythological significance of autumn reminds us of the cyclical nature of life and the importance of honoring our ancestors.

4. The Embrace of Winter:
Winter, a season of cold and darkness, has often been associated with tales of struggle and survival. In Japanese mythology, the deity Yuki-onna, or ‘Snow Woman,’ personifies the beauty and danger of winter. She is depicted as a ghostly figure with pale skin and flowing white hair. Yuki-onna is said to appear during snowstorms, luring unsuspecting travelers to their demise. This myth serves as a reminder of the harsh realities of winter and the need for caution and preparedness.

5. The Eternal Cycle:
In many mythologies, the changing of the seasons is seen as a cyclical process, representing the eternal cycle of life, death, and rebirth. From the ancient Egyptians’ belief in the sun god Ra’s daily journey across the sky to the Native American tribes’ reverence for the Great Spirit’s guidance of the seasons, these myths remind us of the interconnectedness of all living things and the inevitability of change.

As we reflect on the mythology of seasons, we gain a deeper appreciation for the natural world and our place within it. These ancient stories not only entertain and inspire us but also provide insights into the human experience and our relationship with the seasons. So the next time you witness the transition from one season to another, take a moment to ponder the rich mythology that underlies this constant dance of nature.
