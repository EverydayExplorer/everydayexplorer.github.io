Deer Hunting Tactics: Still Hunting vs. Stand Hunting


Deer hunting is an exciting and challenging sport that has been enjoyed by many people for generations. In the world of deer hunting, there are two main tactics used to successfully harvest a deer: still hunting and stand hunting.

Still Hunting

Still hunting involves moving slowly and quietly through the woods while actively searching for deer. This tactic is typically used by hunters who enjoy the challenge of stalking their prey and don’t mind putting in the extra effort needed to be successful.

When still hunting, the hunter must be extremely patient and observant. They must look for signs of deer such as tracks, droppings, and bedding areas. They must also constantly scan their surroundings for any movement that could be a deer. Many still hunters use binoculars to help them spot deer before they are spotted themselves.

One of the benefits of still hunting is that the hunter can cover a lot of ground and search multiple areas for deer. This makes it more likely that they will find a good location to set up and take a shot.

Stand Hunting

Stand hunting, also known as tree stand hunting, involves setting up in one location and waiting for a deer to come to you. This tactic is popular with hunters who prefer a more relaxed approach to hunting and want to maximize their chances of making a successful shot.

To be successful at stand hunting, hunters must first locate a good hunting spot. This includes looking for areas with high deer traffic such as game trails or near a food source. They then set up a stand, which can be a tree stand, ground blind, or natural cover.

Once the stand is set up, the hunter must be patient and wait for the deer to come to them. Many hunters use a deer call or scent attractant to help lure deer to their location. When a deer does approach, the hunter must be patient and wait for a good shot opportunity.

One of the benefits of stand hunting is that it requires less physical activity than still hunting and can be a good option for hunters who are not physically fit. It also allows the hunter to remain in one location and observe the wildlife around them, which can be a peaceful and enjoyable experience.

Which is Better?

Both still hunting and stand hunting can be effective tactics for harvesting deer. The decision to use one over the other often comes down to personal preference and skill level.

Still hunting requires a lot of patience, skill, and knowledge of the area being hunted. It can be a great option for experienced hunters who enjoy the challenge of the chase.

Stand hunting, on the other hand, requires less physical exertion and can be a good option for hunters who want to take a more relaxed approach to hunting. It can also be a good option for beginners who are just starting out.

In conclusion, both still hunting and stand hunting have their pros and cons. The key to success is choosing the tactic that best fits your skill level, preferences, and the hunting environment. Good luck out there, and happy hunting!
