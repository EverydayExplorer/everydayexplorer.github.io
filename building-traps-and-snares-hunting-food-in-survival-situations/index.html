Building Traps and Snares: Hunting Food in Survival Situations


When it comes to survival situations, hunting for food is an essential skill to have. Whether you find yourself lost in the wilderness or facing a natural disaster, knowing how to build traps and snares can make the difference between going hungry and sustaining yourself. In this blog, we will explore the art of building traps and snares for hunting food in survival situations.

The Importance of Traps and Snares

In a survival situation, finding food can be incredibly challenging. While learning how to hunt with weapons is important, it may not always be possible to have access to them. That’s where traps and snares come in. They provide a way to passively catch prey without needing constant surveillance or physical effort.

Basic Principles

Before we dive into specific trap designs, it’s important to understand some basic principles. When creating traps and snares, there are a few key factors to keep in mind:

Stealth

Animals are naturally wary, especially when they sense danger. To increase your chances of success, it is crucial to maintain stealth when building and setting traps. Avoid leaving signs of human presence and try to camouflage your traps as much as possible.

Location

Choosing the right location for your trap is essential. Observe animal trails, feeding areas, or places where animal tracks are concentrated. Additionally, understanding the habits and behaviors of your target animals can help you determine the best location to set your traps.

Simplicity

In a survival situation, you may not have access to specialized tools or materials. Keep your trap designs simple and use readily available resources from your surroundings. This will ensure that you can build traps quickly and effectively with what you have on hand.

Now that we’ve covered the basic principles, let’s take a look at some common traps and snares that you can build in a survival situation.

Deadfall Trap

The deadfall trap is one of the oldest and most reliable traps used for hunting. It consists of a large, heavy object that is balanced and propped up by sticks or a trigger mechanism. When an animal touches the trigger, it releases the support, causing the heavy object to fall and trap the prey beneath it.

To build a deadfall trap, start by finding a large rock or log that can serve as the heavy object. Look for two sturdy sticks that can act as a support structure. Prop up the heavy object using the sticks, making sure to leave enough space for your prey to pass under. Attach a trigger mechanism to the setup, which can be a bait or a stick propping up the structure. When the animal disturbs the trigger, the heavy object will fall, trapping the prey.

Snare Trap

A snare trap is another effective way to catch small to medium-sized animals. It works by using a looped cord or wire that tightens around the animal’s neck or body when it tries to pass through.

To build a snare trap, start by finding a flexible cord or wire. Create a noose by forming a loop in the cord and leaving a small opening. Place the noose in an area where you have observed animal activity, such as a game trail or feeding area. Make sure the noose is large enough for the animal’s head or body to fit through but tightens when pulled. Secure the cord to a tree or other stable object and set up camouflage to make it less visible to the animals.

Fish Trap

In many survival situations, water sources provide a valuable opportunity to catch food. Building a fish trap is an effective way to passively catch fish without needing to actively fish using a rod and hook.

To build a fish trap, start by finding a shallow area near a water source where fish are likely to swim. Gather rocks or sticks to form a fence-like structure underwater, leaving an opening for the fish to swim in. It’s essential to create a funnel effect, guiding the fish toward the opening and preventing them from easily swimming out.

Once you’ve constructed the fish trap, periodically check it to see if you’ve caught any fish. Remember to be patient, as fish may take some time to become comfortable with the trap and swim in.

Conclusion

In a survival situation, knowing how to build traps and snares can be a lifesaver. They provide a means of hunting food passively and can significantly increase your chances of survival. Remember to always prioritize safety, respect local regulations, and only use traps and snares when necessary for survival.

By understanding the basic principles, such as stealth, location, and simplicity, and by familiarizing yourself with the various trap designs, you’ll be better prepared to secure food in a survival situation.
