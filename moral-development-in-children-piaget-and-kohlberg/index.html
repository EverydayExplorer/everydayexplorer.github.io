Moral Development in Children: Piaget and Kohlberg


Moral development is an essential aspect of a child’s growth and development. It shapes their understanding of right and wrong, and lays the foundation for their moral reasoning as they grow into adulthood. Two prominent theorists who have made significant contributions to the field of moral development are Jean Piaget and Lawrence Kohlberg. In this blog post, we will explore their theories and how they can help parents and educators understand and support children’s moral growth.

Jean Piaget, a Swiss psychologist, proposed a theory of moral development based on cognitive development. According to Piaget, children go through distinct stages of cognitive development, and moral development is closely linked to these stages. Piaget believed that children’s moral reasoning evolves as they acquire new cognitive skills and structures.

Piaget identified two main stages of moral development: the heteronomous stage and the autonomous stage. In the heteronomous stage, which typically occurs in children aged 4 to 7, children view rules as fixed and unchangeable. They believe that rules are imposed by authorities and cannot be altered. Piaget referred to this stage as ‘morality of constraint’ because children at this stage are bound by external rules and regulations.

In the autonomous stage, which usually emerges around the age of 10, children begin to understand that rules are not absolute and can be modified through mutual agreement. They develop a sense of fairness and take into account the intentions of others when making moral judgments. Piaget referred to this stage as ‘morality of cooperation’ because children at this stage start to consider the needs and perspectives of others.

Lawrence Kohlberg, an American psychologist, expanded on Piaget’s work and proposed a more elaborate theory of moral development. Kohlberg’s theory consists of six sequential stages organized into three broad levels: preconventional, conventional, and postconventional.

In the preconventional level, which is typically observed in early childhood, children’s moral judgments are based on self-interest and the avoidance of punishment. They view actions as right or wrong based on the consequences they bring to themselves. At this stage, children are motivated by external factors rather than internalized principles.

In the conventional level, which emerges in late childhood and early adolescence, children’s moral reasoning becomes more influenced by societal norms and expectations. They focus on maintaining social order and gaining approval from others. Children at this level abide by the rules and laws set by society, as they value the idea of being a ‘good citizen.’

In the postconventional level, which is not reached by everyone, individuals develop their own moral principles that may transcend societal norms and laws. They engage in abstract moral reasoning and consider universal ethical principles such as justice, equality, and human rights. At this level, individuals are guided by internalized values and may even go against societal norms to uphold their principles.

Understanding Piaget’s and Kohlberg’s theories of moral development can greatly benefit parents and educators in supporting children’s moral growth. By recognizing that moral development is closely intertwined with cognitive development, parents and educators can design age-appropriate activities and discussions to foster moral reasoning skills in children.

For young children in the heteronomous stage, it is important to provide clear rules and explanations to help them understand the consequences of their actions. This can be done through simple discussions, storytelling, and role-playing activities. As children enter the autonomous stage, parents and educators can encourage them to engage in collaborative problem-solving, where they are encouraged to consider the perspectives of others and negotiate mutually beneficial solutions.

In the preconventional level, parents and educators can help children develop empathy and a sense of responsibility by highlighting the impact of their actions on others. It is essential to create a supportive and nurturing environment where children feel safe to express their thoughts and emotions without fear of judgment or punishment.

In the conventional level, parents and educators can promote moral reasoning by discussing real-life ethical dilemmas and encouraging children to consider multiple perspectives. Providing opportunities for volunteering and community service can also help children understand the importance of social responsibility and empathy towards others.

For individuals in the postconventional level, parents and educators can encourage reflection, critical thinking, and open dialogue about complex moral issues. This can be done through engaging in debates, ethics workshops, and encouraging independent research on topics related to moral dilemmas.

In conclusion, moral development plays a crucial role in shaping children’s understanding of right and wrong, and their ability to make ethical decisions. Piaget’s and Kohlberg’s theories provide valuable insights into the stages and processes involved in moral development. By understanding these theories, parents and educators can support children’s moral growth and help them become responsible, empathetic, and ethical individuals.
