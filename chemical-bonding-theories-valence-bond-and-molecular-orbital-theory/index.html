Chemical Bonding Theories: Valence Bond and Molecular Orbital Theory


Chemical bonding plays a crucial role in understanding the behavior and properties of molecules. Two prominent theories that explain the nature of chemical bonding are the Valence Bond (VB) theory and the Molecular Orbital (MO) theory. In this blog post, we will explore these theories and discuss their significance in the field of chemistry.

Valence Bond Theory:
The Valence Bond theory, proposed by Linus Pauling in the 1930s, focuses on the concept of overlap between atomic orbitals to form bonds. According to this theory, atoms form chemical bonds by sharing electrons in such a way that their atomic orbitals overlap. The strength of the bond is determined by the extent of this overlap.

In Valence Bond theory, the formation of a covalent bond is explained by the overlapping of two half-filled atomic orbitals. The overlapping orbitals can be of different types, such as s-s, s-p, p-p, etc. The shared electrons are represented by a bond, and the unshared electrons are represented by lone pairs.

This theory also explains the formation of multiple bonds, such as double and triple bonds. In these cases, the atomic orbitals involved in bonding overlap more than once, leading to the formation of multiple bonds between atoms.

Molecular Orbital Theory:
The Molecular Orbital theory, developed by Robert Mulliken and Friedrich Hund in the 1930s, takes a different approach to explain chemical bonding. According to this theory, atomic orbitals combine to form molecular orbitals that extend over the entire molecule. These molecular orbitals are delocalized, meaning they are not confined to a specific atom but are spread out over the entire molecule.

The Molecular Orbital theory describes the bonding and antibonding interactions between atomic orbitals. When atomic orbitals combine constructively, they form bonding molecular orbitals, which are lower in energy than the original atomic orbitals. Conversely, when atomic orbitals combine destructively, they form antibonding molecular orbitals, which are higher in energy.

This theory provides a more comprehensive understanding of the electronic structure of molecules. It explains phenomena such as molecular stability, bond length, and bond energy. It also accounts for the concept of electron delocalization and the existence of molecular orbitals in conjugated systems.

Comparison between Valence Bond and Molecular Orbital Theory:
Both Valence Bond and Molecular Orbital theories offer valuable insights into chemical bonding. While the Valence Bond theory focuses on the localized nature of bonding, the Molecular Orbital theory considers the delocalization of electrons throughout the molecule.

Valence Bond theory is more intuitive and easier to visualize, as it explains bonding in terms of overlapping atomic orbitals. On the other hand, the Molecular Orbital theory provides a more sophisticated understanding of molecular structure and electronic properties.

In conclusion, understanding chemical bonding is essential for unraveling the behavior of molecules. The Valence Bond and Molecular Orbital theories provide complementary perspectives on the nature of chemical bonds. While the Valence Bond theory emphasizes the concept of overlapping atomic orbitals, the Molecular Orbital theory highlights the delocalization of electrons. By combining these theories, scientists gain a comprehensive understanding of chemical bonding, enabling them to predict and explain various chemical phenomena.
