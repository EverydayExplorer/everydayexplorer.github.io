Wine Faults and How to Identify Them


Wine is a complex and sophisticated beverage that can be enjoyed by individuals with varying levels of wine knowledge. Whether you are a casual wine drinker or a seasoned connoisseur, it is important to understand the concept of wine faults and how to identify them. A wine fault refers to any undesirable characteristic or flaw that impacts the taste, aroma, or overall quality of a wine. In this blog post, we will explore some common wine faults, understand their causes, and learn how to identify them.

1. Cork Taint

One of the most well-known wine faults is cork taint, also known as corked wine. Cork taint is caused by a chemical compound called 2,4,6-trichloroanisole (TCA), which is typically found in natural corks. When a wine is affected by cork taint, it will exhibit a musty or moldy smell, reminiscent of wet cardboard or a damp basement. Additionally, the flavors of the wine will be muted and lack the vibrant fruitiness that is usually associated with the varietal.

To identify cork taint, swirl the wine in the glass and take a deep sniff. If you detect any off-putting aromas or a lack of fruitiness, it is likely that the wine is corked. Keep in mind that cork taint affects only a small percentage of wines, as the industry has made significant improvements in cork quality in recent years.

2. Oxidation

Oxidation is another common wine fault that occurs when a wine is exposed to too much oxygen. This can happen during the winemaking process, as well as during storage or aging. Oxidized wine will have a brownish color and a flat, dull taste. The aroma may also resemble sherry or bruised apple.

To identify oxidation, visually inspect the wine for a brownish tint. Next, take a small sip and pay attention to the flavors. If the wine tastes flat, lacking fruitiness or freshness, it is likely that it has been oxidized. Proper storage techniques like keeping the wine bottles upright and within a suitable temperature range can help prevent oxidation.

3. Reduction

On the opposite end of the spectrum from oxidation is a fault known as reduction. Reduction occurs when a wine has been made or stored in an environment with limited exposure to oxygen. This fault can result in the production of certain sulfur compounds, which give the wine a smell similar to rotten eggs or burnt rubber.

To identify reduction, gently swirl the wine in the glass to release any trapped odors. If you detect any unpleasant sulfurous aromas, it is likely that the wine has reduction. This fault is more common in wines that are high in sulfur dioxide, which is sometimes added as a preservative.

4. Volatile Acidity

Volatile acidity is a fault that refers to the presence of excessive amounts of acetic acid in a wine. Low levels of volatile acidity can actually contribute positively to the wine’s complexity and aroma, but when it exceeds a certain threshold, it becomes a fault. Wines with high volatile acidity may have a vinegar-like smell and taste, as well as an unpleasant sharpness on the palate.

To identify volatile acidity, sniff the wine and look for a strong vinegar aroma. Additionally, take a small sip and notice if there is a harsh, acidic sensation on your tongue. If these symptoms are present, it is likely that the wine has a high volatile acidity.

Conclusion

Understanding wine faults and being able to identify them are essential skills for anyone who appreciates wine. By being aware of the common faults like cork taint, oxidation, reduction, and volatile acidity, you can make informed decisions when purchasing and enjoying wines. Remember to trust your senses when evaluating a potential wine fault and seek guidance from knowledgeable experts or wine professionals when in doubt. With practice, you will become more adept at recognizing these faults and making informed decisions about the wines you consume. Cheers to enjoying fault-free wines!
