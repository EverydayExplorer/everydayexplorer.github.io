Comic Book Tropes We're Tired of: From Fridging to Retcons


Comic books have been a beloved form of entertainment for decades, captivating readers with their colorful illustrations and captivating storylines. However, like any medium, comic books have their fair share of tropes that can become tiresome and repetitive. In this blog post, we’ll explore some of the comic book tropes that we’re tired of, from fridging to retcons.

1. Fridging: One trope that has garnered significant criticism is the concept of ‘fridging.’ This term refers to the practice of killing off a female character solely to provide motivation for a male protagonist. It’s an overused plot device that diminishes the agency and importance of female characters, reducing them to mere plot devices. Readers are tired of seeing women being sacrificed for the sake of advancing a male character’s narrative.

2. Retcons: Another trope that has become tiresome is the constant use of retcons, or retroactive continuity. Retcons involve altering previously established storylines or character histories, often to fit new narratives or to undo unpopular plot developments. While retcons can sometimes inject new life into a series, they can also create confusion and undermine the integrity of the storytelling. Readers want consistency and a sense of continuity, rather than constant changes to the established canon.

3. Damsel in Distress: The damsel in distress trope is another cliche that has worn out its welcome. This trope often portrays female characters as helpless and in need of rescue, reinforcing outdated gender stereotypes. Readers are longing for more strong and independent female characters who can hold their own and contribute to the story in meaningful ways.

4. Token Diversity: While diversity and representation have become increasingly important in comic books, the token diversity trope can be frustrating. Token diversity refers to the inclusion of characters from marginalized backgrounds solely for the purpose of appearing inclusive, without giving them significant character development or meaningful storylines. Readers want authentic representation and well-rounded characters, not just token gestures.

5. Deus ex Machina: The overuse of deus ex machina, or a sudden and convenient resolution to a seemingly unsolvable problem, can undermine the tension and believability of a comic book story. Readers want to see characters facing genuine challenges and finding creative solutions, rather than relying on convenient plot devices to save the day.

In conclusion, while comic books have provided us with countless memorable stories and characters, it’s important to acknowledge and address the tropes that have become tiresome. By moving away from tired cliches like fridging, retcons, damsels in distress, token diversity, and deus ex machina, comic book creators can deliver fresh and engaging narratives that resonate with readers. Let’s embrace diversity, strong character development, and thoughtfully crafted storylines to keep the comic book medium thriving for years to come.
