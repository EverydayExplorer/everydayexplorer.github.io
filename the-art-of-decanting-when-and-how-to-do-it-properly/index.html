The Art of Decanting: When and How to Do It Properly


Decanting wine is an age-old practice that has been passed down through generations. It involves transferring the contents of a wine bottle into a decanter, allowing the wine to breathe and ultimately enhancing its flavors and aromas. While decanting may seem like a daunting task for many wine enthusiasts, it is a skill that can be learned and mastered with a little knowledge and practice. In this blog post, we will delve into the art of decanting and explore when and how to do it properly.

Why Decant Wine?

Decanting serves several purposes that can vastly improve the overall wine-tasting experience. Firstly, it helps to separate the sediment that may have formed in older wines. Sediment is a natural occurrence in aged wines and can negatively impact the taste and texture. By carefully pouring the wine into a decanter, you can leave the sediment behind, resulting in a clearer and more pleasant pour.

Secondly, decanting allows the wine to come into contact with oxygen. Oxygenation can soften the harsh tannins found in young red wines, making them more approachable and enjoyable to drink. Oxygen also helps to enhance the wine’s aromas, allowing you to fully appreciate the complex bouquet it has to offer.

When to Decant?

Decanting is not necessary for every bottle of wine. It is primarily reserved for older red wines and full-bodied young red wines. Older wines tend to develop sediment over time, which can affect their taste and texture. Decanting these wines helps to remove any sediment and bring out their true flavors. Full-bodied young red wines, on the other hand, can benefit from decanting as it allows them to open up and release their aromas more quickly.

White wines and light-bodied red wines usually do not require decanting. These wines are best enjoyed fresh and crisp, without the need for prolonged exposure to oxygen. However, there may be exceptions depending on the specific characteristics of the wine, so it is always a good idea to research the particular wine you are planning to decant.

How to Decant Properly?

Decanting wine may seem straightforward, but there are a few key steps to follow to ensure optimal results:

  1. Choose an appropriate decanter: Decanters come in various shapes and sizes, but the most important factor is to choose one with a wide base and a narrow neck. This design allows for maximum exposure to oxygen while minimizing the risk of spillage.

  2. Position the wine bottle: Before decanting, it is advisable to store the wine bottle upright for a few hours or overnight. This helps to settle any sediment at the bottom of the bottle.

  3. Remove the foil and cork: Carefully remove the foil and cork from the wine bottle, taking care not to disturb any sediment that may have settled.

  4. Pour slowly and steadily: Hold the decanter at a slight angle and pour the wine into it slowly and steadily. This helps to minimize the disturbance of any sediment. If you notice sediment approaching the neck of the bottle, stop pouring and discard the remainder.

  5. Let it breathe: Once the wine is decanted, let it sit for at least 30 minutes to an hour before serving. This allows the wine to fully oxygenate and develop its aromas and flavors.

In Conclusion

Decanting is an art that wine lovers can appreciate and utilize to enhance their wine-tasting experience. By decanting at the right time and following the proper steps, you can elevate the flavors, aromas, and overall enjoyment of your wine. Remember to choose an appropriate decanter, pour slowly and steadily, and allow the wine ample time to breathe. With practice, you will become more confident and skilled in the art of decanting, taking your appreciation of wine to new heights.
