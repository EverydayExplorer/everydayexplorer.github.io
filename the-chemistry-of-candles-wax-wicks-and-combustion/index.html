The Chemistry of Candles: Wax, Wicks, and Combustion


Candles have been an integral part of human civilization for thousands of years, serving various purposes from illumination to religious rituals and ambiance. While their flickering flames may appear simple, the chemistry behind candles is surprisingly complex. In this blog post, we’ll explore the intricate chemistry of candles, focusing on the components of wax, wicks, and combustion.

The Chemistry of Wax

Wax is the primary component of candles, typically derived from natural sources such as beeswax, soy wax, or paraffin wax. The chemical composition of wax varies depending on its source, but most waxes consist primarily of long-chain hydrocarbons.

  1. Paraffin Wax: Paraffin wax, derived from petroleum, is one of the most common types of candle wax. It consists primarily of saturated hydrocarbons, which are molecules composed of carbon and hydrogen atoms bonded together in long chains. These hydrocarbons provide paraffin wax with its characteristic properties, including low melting point and excellent burn stability.

  2. Beeswax: Beeswax, produced by honeybees, is another popular choice for candle-making. It contains a mixture of esters, fatty acids, and hydrocarbons, giving it a unique fragrance and color. Beeswax candles are prized for their natural aroma and clean-burning properties.

  3. Soy Wax: Soy wax is derived from soybean oil and is a renewable, biodegradable alternative to traditional waxes. It consists primarily of triglycerides, which are esters formed from glycerol and fatty acids. Soy wax candles are popular among environmentally-conscious consumers due to their sustainable nature.

The Role of Wicks

The wick is a crucial component of candles, responsible for delivering fuel to the flame and maintaining a steady burn. Most candle wicks are made of braided cotton fibers, which serve as a capillary system to draw liquid wax up from the candle’s reservoir to the flame.

  1. Capillary Action: Capillary action is the phenomenon by which liquids move through narrow spaces or porous materials, such as the fibers of a candle wick. As the wick absorbs liquid wax, capillary forces pull the wax upward, where it is vaporized and burned by the flame.

  2. Stabilizing Agents: Some candle wicks are treated with stabilizing agents, such as boric acid or salts, to improve their burn characteristics. These additives help prevent the wick from curling or mushrooming during combustion, ensuring a more consistent and efficient burn.

Combustion Chemistry

The combustion of candles is a chemical reaction that involves the oxidation of wax molecules in the presence of oxygen from the air. This process releases energy in the form of heat and light, producing the familiar flickering flame of a candle.

  1. Oxidation of Hydrocarbons: During combustion, the hydrocarbons in the wax molecules react with oxygen to form carbon dioxide (CO2) and water vapor (H2O). This exothermic reaction releases heat energy, which sustains the flame and keeps it burning.

  2. Soot Formation: Incomplete combustion of wax can result in the formation of soot, which consists of tiny carbon particles. Factors such as wick size, air flow, and wax composition can influence the amount of soot produced during burning. Trimmed wicks and well-ventilated environments can help minimize soot formation and prolong the life of candles.

Conclusion

The chemistry of candles is a fascinating subject that encompasses the properties of wax, the mechanics of wicks, and the intricacies of combustion. By understanding the chemical processes at play, we can appreciate the science behind these everyday objects and the warm glow they provide. Whether used for relaxation, celebration, or illumination, candles continue to enchant and inspire us with their timeless chemistry.
