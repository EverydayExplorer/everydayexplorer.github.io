Why Does Popcorn Pop? The Physics of Popping Corn


Have you ever wondered why popcorn pops? It’s a fascinating phenomenon that has intrigued scientists for centuries. In this blog post, we will delve into the physics behind the popping of corn and explore the science behind this beloved snack.

To understand why popcorn pops, we need to start by looking at the structure of a corn kernel. A corn kernel consists of three main parts: the outer hull, the starchy endosperm, and a small amount of water contained within the kernel. The endosperm is the part of the kernel that we eat as popcorn. It is made up of both hard and soft starches, with the hard starches being the key to popcorn’s explosive nature.

When a popcorn kernel is heated, the water inside the kernel turns into steam. As it heats up, the steam builds up pressure within the kernel, causing it to become pressurized. This pressure continues to increase until it reaches a critical point, at which the hull of the kernel can no longer contain it. The sudden release of pressure causes the kernel to burst open and, voila, we have fluffy popcorn!

But why does the pressure build up in the first place? This is where the hard starches come into play. When the kernel is heated, the moisture inside it turns into steam. However, the steam is trapped within the kernel by the hard starches. These hard starches act as a seal, preventing the steam from escaping. As the temperature increases, the moisture turns into steam and the pressure inside the kernel rises.

Interestingly, not all corn kernels pop. Only a specific type of corn, known as ‘popcorn’ or ‘popping corn,’ has the necessary combination of hard and soft starches to create the popping effect. Other types of corn, such as ‘sweet corn,’ do not have the same properties and will not pop when heated.

Now that we understand the basics of why popcorn pops, let’s dive deeper into the physics involved. When the pressure inside the kernel exceeds the strength of the hull, the kernel bursts open. This release of pressure creates a shock wave that travels through the air, producing the familiar popping sound. The sudden expansion of the starchy endosperm also causes it to take on a fluffy texture, making popcorn so deliciously light and airy.

But what about the temperature at which popcorn pops? The popping temperature of popcorn is around 190 to 200 degrees Celsius (375 to 400 degrees Fahrenheit). This is well above the boiling point of water (100 degrees Celsius or 212 degrees Fahrenheit). The high temperature is necessary to create the steam and pressure needed for popping. It’s important to note that if you heat the popcorn kernels too slowly, they won’t pop properly. This is because the moisture inside the kernels will escape gradually instead of turning into steam and building up pressure.

In conclusion, the physics of popping corn is a fascinating subject that combines heat transfer, pressure, and the unique properties of corn kernels. The combination of hard and soft starches in popcorn allows it to trap moisture and create the necessary pressure for popping. When heated to the right temperature, the pressure inside the kernel becomes too great for the hull to contain, resulting in a delightful explosion and the birth of fluffy, delicious popcorn.

So, the next time you prepare a bowl of popcorn, take a moment to appreciate the science behind it. And remember, popcorn is not just a tasty treat, but a testament to the wonders of physics!
