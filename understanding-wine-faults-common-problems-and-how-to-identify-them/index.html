Understanding Wine Faults: Common Problems and How to Identify Them


When it comes to enjoying a good glass of wine, there’s nothing more disappointing than discovering that it’s been spoiled by a fault. Wine faults can occur at any stage of the winemaking process, and they can affect the taste, aroma, and overall quality of the wine. In this blog post, we will explore some of the most common wine faults and provide tips on how to identify them.

1. Cork taint: One of the most well-known wine faults, cork taint is caused by the presence of a compound called TCA (trichloroanisole) in the cork. When a wine is affected by cork taint, it will have a distinct musty or moldy odor, often described as smelling like wet cardboard or a damp basement. If you detect this off-putting smell in your wine, it’s likely that the bottle has been tainted by a faulty cork.

2. Oxidation: Oxidation occurs when wine is exposed to too much oxygen, causing it to lose its freshness and vibrant flavors. Signs of oxidation include a brownish color in white wines or a brick-like color in red wines. Oxidized wines may also have a flat or dull taste, lacking the fruitiness and complexity that are characteristic of well-preserved wines.

3. Volatile acidity: Volatile acidity is the presence of excessive acetic acid in wine, which can give rise to a vinegar-like smell. Wines with high levels of volatile acidity may have a sharp or pungent aroma, reminiscent of nail polish remover or vinegar. Additionally, these wines can taste overly acidic and unpleasantly sharp on the palate.

4. Reduction: Reduction occurs when wine is exposed to too little oxygen, leading to the development of sulfurous aromas. Wines affected by reduction may have a distinct smell of rotten eggs, sewer gas, or burnt rubber. Reduction can also mute the flavors of a wine, resulting in a dull or uninteresting taste.

5. Brettanomyces: Brettanomyces, or simply ‘brett,’ is a yeast that can settle in wine barrels and tanks, causing a range of undesirable flavors and aromas. Wines affected by brett may have smoky, barnyard-like smells, often described as ‘horse blanket’ or ‘band-aid.’ Additionally, brett can produce compounds that contribute to a bitter aftertaste, disrupting the overall balance of the wine.

6. Sulfur dioxide: Sulfur dioxide, or SO2, is a common preservative used in winemaking. However, when used in excessive amounts, it can result in an unpleasant smell and taste. Wines with high levels of sulfur dioxide may have a pungent, burnt-match odor, similar to the smell of rotten eggs. Drinking wines with excessive levels of sulfur dioxide can cause headaches and allergic reactions in some individuals.

Now that we’ve covered some of the most common wine faults, let’s discuss how to identify them. The first step is to trust your senses. Before taking a sip, swirl the wine in the glass and observe its appearance and color. Next, give it a gentle sniff, paying attention to any off-putting aromas. Finally, take a small sip and evaluate the taste and texture on your palate.

If you suspect a wine fault, it’s always a good idea to compare it to a known, fault-free wine for confirmation. Keeping a reference wine on hand can help train your palate to detect and differentiate between various faults. Additionally, seeking advice from knowledgeable wine professionals or participating in blind tasting events can greatly enhance your ability to identify wine faults accurately.

In conclusion, understanding wine faults and knowing how to identify them is crucial for any wine enthusiast. By familiarizing yourself with these common wine faults, you can avoid disappointment and ensure that you enjoy every glass of wine to its fullest potential. So next time you open a bottle, don’t let a wine fault ruin your experience—trust your senses and savor the complexities of a well-made wine.
