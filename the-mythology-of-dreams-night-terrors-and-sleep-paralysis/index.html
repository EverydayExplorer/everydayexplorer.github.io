The Mythology of Dreams: Night Terrors and Sleep Paralysis


Have you ever woken up in the middle of the night, paralyzed with fear, convinced that there was something terrifying lurking in the shadows? Or experienced vivid nightmares that felt so real, it was as if you were trapped in a horror movie? If so, you may have encountered the phenomenon known as night terrors and sleep paralysis.

What are Night Terrors?

Night terrors, also known as sleep terrors, are intense episodes of fear or terror that occur during sleep. Unlike nightmares, which usually happen during REM sleep and can be recalled upon waking, night terrors occur during non-REM sleep and are often forgotten or only vaguely remembered.

During a night terror, a person may suddenly sit up in bed, scream, thrash around, or exhibit signs of intense fear. Their heart rate and breathing may increase, and they may appear wide-eyed and agitated. Night terrors can last anywhere from a few seconds to a few minutes, leaving the person feeling confused and disoriented upon waking.

The Origins of Night Terrors in Mythology:

Night terrors have been a subject of fascination and fear for centuries, with various cultures attributing them to supernatural entities or malevolent spirits. In Greek mythology, it was believed that night terrors were caused by the goddess of the night, Nyx, and her son, Hypnos, the god of sleep. They were said to send terrifying visions and nightmares to mortals as punishment or as a means of manipulation.

In Norse mythology, the concept of night terrors was associated with the creatures known as the Mara or the Nightmare. These malevolent beings were said to sit on the chests of sleeping people, causing them to experience a sense of suffocation and terror.

Understanding Sleep Paralysis:

Sleep paralysis is another perplexing phenomenon closely related to night terrors. It is a state of temporary paralysis that occurs immediately before falling asleep or upon waking up. During sleep paralysis, a person is fully aware of their surroundings but unable to move or speak. They may also experience hallucinations, often of shadowy figures or strange entities.

Sleep paralysis is thought to be a result of a disruption in the transition between sleep stages. When we sleep, our bodies go through different sleep cycles, including REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep and non-REM sleep. During REM sleep, our brains are highly active, and our muscles are temporarily paralyzed to prevent us from acting out our dreams. However, in cases of sleep paralysis, this paralysis continues even after waking up, causing a sense of helplessness and fear.

The Cultural Significance of Sleep Paralysis:

Sleep paralysis has long been associated with supernatural beings and otherworldly encounters in various cultures around the world. In many African and Caribbean traditions, sleep paralysis is attributed to the presence of a malevolent spirit known as the “Old Hag” or the “Night Witch.” This entity is believed to sit on the chest of the sleeping person, causing the paralysis and inducing terrifying hallucinations.

In Japanese folklore, sleep paralysis is known as “Kanashibari” or “Kanashibarazu.” It is believed to be caused by a vengeful spirit or demon that restrains the victim, making them unable to move or scream. These cultural interpretations reflect the universal human experience of sleep paralysis and its profound impact on our psyche.

Scientific Explanations and Coping Strategies:

While the mythology surrounding night terrors and sleep paralysis is fascinating, modern science provides us with a more rational explanation. Night terrors are believed to be caused by a disruption in the transition between sleep stages, particularly during non-REM sleep. Sleep paralysis, on the other hand, is attributed to a temporary malfunction in the brain’s sleep-wake mechanisms.

Fortunately, there are several coping strategies that can help individuals manage night terrors and sleep paralysis. Maintaining a regular sleep schedule, practicing relaxation techniques before bed, and creating a calming sleep environment can all contribute to a more peaceful night’s sleep. If these methods prove ineffective, seeking professional help from a sleep specialist or therapist may be beneficial.

In conclusion, the mythology surrounding night terrors and sleep paralysis reflects our enduring fascination with the mysterious realm of dreams. While the origins of these phenomena may be rooted in ancient beliefs and folklore, understanding the scientific explanations and implementing effective coping strategies can help alleviate the fear and distress that they can cause. So, the next time you find yourself trapped in the realm of night terrors or sleep paralysis, remember that there is a rational explanation behind the mythology, and you are not alone in your experience.
