Why Do Onions Make You Cry? Unraveling the Chemical Compounds and Eye Irritation


Are you familiar with the stinging sensation in your eyes when you cut open an onion? It’s no secret that onions have the ability to bring tears to your eyes, but have you ever wondered what causes this reaction? In this blog, we will delve into the chemical compounds found in onions and unravel the mystery behind the eye irritation.

The Culprit: Syn-Propanethial-S-oxide

When an onion is cut or chopped, it releases a volatile compound known as syn-propanethial-S-oxide (PSO). This compound is a sulfur-based gas that stimulates the tear glands in your eyes, leading to the production of tears.

PSO is synthesized by a specific enzyme called the lachrymatory factor synthase (LFS) when the onion’s cells are disrupted. The LFS enzyme converts a non-volatile precursor substance into PSO, which is then released into the air, reaching your eyes and causing irritation.

Unraveling the Chemical Reactions

To understand why onions make you cry, it’s important to delve into the chemical reactions that occur. When PSO comes into contact with the natural moisture in your eyes, it reacts with the water to form sulfuric acid. This acid is responsible for the stinging and burning sensation that you experience.

The sulfuric acid triggers your eyes’ reflex to produce tears as a protective mechanism. These tears help to dilute and wash away the irritant, aiding in the alleviation of the uncomfortable sensation.

The Role of Enzymes

Enzymes play a vital role in the production of PSO and the tear-inducing properties of onions. LFS, as mentioned earlier, is responsible for converting the precursor substance into PSO.

Interestingly, onions also contain other enzymes that interact with the sulfuric compounds present. One such enzyme is alliinase, which is responsible for converting a different precursor compound called alliin into another sulfur-based compound called sulfenic acid.

When an onion is undisturbed, these enzymes are kept separate in different parts of the cell. However, cutting or chopping the onion causes the release of these enzymes, resulting in a chain reaction that ultimately leads to the formation of PSO and other irritating compounds.

Variations in Onion Types and Tear Production

Not all onions are created equal when it comes to tear production. The intensity of eye irritation can vary depending on the specific type of onion you are working with. Some onions are naturally higher in LFS activity and sulfuric compound concentrations, making them more tear-inducing than others.

For example, the pungent red onions tend to have a higher concentration of these compounds compared to milder varieties like sweet onions. Cooking or sautéing onions can also reduce their tear-inducing properties, as heat denatures the enzymes responsible for the production of PSO.

Minimizing Onion-Induced Tears

While it may be impossible to completely avoid tearing up when cutting onions, there are several tricks you can try to minimize the eye irritation:

  1. Chill the onions: Refrigerating onions before cutting can help slow down the release of irritating compounds, thereby reducing tear production.
  2. Cut near water: Running water or chopping onions near a running faucet can help wash away released compounds and prevent them from reaching your eyes.
  3. Use a sharp knife: A sharp knife minimizes the damage to onion cells, reducing the amount of PSO released during cutting.
  4. Wear goggles: Wearing protective goggles or glasses creates a physical barrier between your eyes and the irritating compounds.

Conclusion

Onions contain chemical compounds, particularly syn-propanethial-S-oxide (PSO), that are responsible for the eye irritation and tears experienced when cutting or chopping them. Enzymes present in onions catalyze the production of these compounds, triggering a chain reaction that ultimately leads to the irritating effects.

While tears may be an inevitable part of the onion-cutting process, understanding the chemical reactions behind it can help us find ways to minimize the discomfort. So, the next time you find yourself reaching for an onion in the kitchen, remember to use sharp knives, chill the onion, or consider wearing goggles to make the experience tear-free!
