The Evolution of Artificial Intelligence in Literature: From Frankenstein to Ex Machina


Artificial intelligence (AI) has become an integral part of our lives, revolutionizing various industries such as healthcare, finance, and transportation. But did you know that AI has also made its mark in literature? From the classic novel ‘Frankenstein’ by Mary Shelley to the thought-provoking film ‘Ex Machina,’ the portrayal and exploration of AI have evolved significantly over time. In this blog post, we will delve into the fascinating journey of AI in literature and how it has shaped our perception of this groundbreaking technology.

The birth of AI in literature can be traced back to Mary Shelley’s iconic novel, ‘Frankenstein.’ Published in 1818, this gothic masterpiece tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a scientist who creates a creature using unconventional methods. While not explicitly an AI, the creature can be seen as a metaphorical representation of the potential dangers and ethical dilemmas associated with the pursuit of knowledge and science. Shelley’s exploration of the consequences of playing God and the moral responsibility of creators set the foundation for future AI narratives.

Fast forward to the 20th century, and we encounter Isaac Asimov, often referred to as the father of AI in science fiction. Asimov’s collection of short stories, ‘I, Robot’ (1950), introduced the concept of intelligent robots governed by the Three Laws of Robotics. These laws, designed to ensure the safety and well-being of humans, became a recurring theme in AI literature. Asimov’s work not only popularized the idea of AI, but it also raised important questions about the relationship between humans and machines, and the potential risks of relying on AI for our daily lives.

In the following decades, AI continued to captivate the literary world. In the 1980s, William Gibson’s ‘Neuromancer’ explored the concept of cyberspace and artificial intelligences. This groundbreaking novel introduced readers to a dystopian future where AI entities, known as ‘AIs,’ had significant influence over the world’s networks and information systems. Gibson’s work not only showcased the potential power of AI but also delved into the complexities of human-machine interactions and the blurring boundaries between virtual and physical realities.

Moving into the 21st century, we witness a shift in AI literature. One notable example is the 2014 film ‘Ex Machina,’ written and directed by Alex Garland. This thought-provoking film centers around a young programmer who is invited to administer the Turing Test to an intelligent humanoid robot named Ava. ‘Ex Machina’ explores themes of consciousness, identity, and the moral implications of creating AI entities. The film’s portrayal of AI as both fascinating and potentially dangerous reflects our contemporary anxieties about the rapid advancement of technology.

As AI continues to evolve in real life, so does its portrayal in literature. Contemporary authors are incorporating AI into their narratives, exploring the ethical, social, and philosophical implications of this technology. Works like ‘The Circle’ by Dave Eggers and ‘Machines Like Me’ by Ian McEwan offer fresh perspectives on AI in the context of privacy, surveillance, and the nature of humanity.

In conclusion, the evolution of AI in literature mirrors our changing perception of this technology. From the cautionary tales of ‘Frankenstein’ to the complex explorations of consciousness in ‘Ex Machina,’ AI narratives have evolved alongside our understanding of AI in the real world. As we continue to grapple with the implications of AI in our society, literature provides a valuable platform for critical reflection and exploration. So, the next time you pick up a book or watch a movie featuring AI, take a moment to ponder the deeper questions it raises about our relationship with technology and the future that awaits us.
