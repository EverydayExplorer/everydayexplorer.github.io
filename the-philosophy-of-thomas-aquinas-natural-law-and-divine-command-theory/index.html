The Philosophy of Thomas Aquinas: Natural Law and Divine Command Theory


Thomas Aquinas, a 13th-century theologian and philosopher, is widely regarded as one of the most influential figures in Western intellectual history. His philosophy seeks to reconcile faith and reason, drawing on both philosophical insights and theological teachings. One of the key aspects of Aquinas’s thought is his understanding of ethics, particularly his views on natural law and divine command theory. In this blog post, we will explore these two ethical theories and how Aquinas synthesizes them.

The Foundation of Aquinas’s Ethics

Aquinas’s ethics is grounded in his belief that God is the ultimate source of all goodness and truth. He argues that human beings are created in the image of God and, therefore, possess a natural inclination towards goodness and moral conduct. This natural inclination is reflected in the concept of natural law.

Natural Law Theory

According to Aquinas, natural law is an objective moral law that is derived from the nature of human beings and the world around us. It is a universal and unchanging set of moral principles that can be discovered through reason. Natural law theory holds that certain actions are inherently good or evil, regardless of cultural or individual beliefs.

Aquinas argues that human beings have a natural inclination towards certain goods, such as self-preservation, procreation, and social life. These goods are objectively good for human beings because they lead to the fulfillment of our natural inclinations. Consequently, actions that promote these goods are morally right, while actions that hinder or undermine them are morally wrong.

For example, the preservation of human life is considered a fundamental aspect of natural law. Therefore, murder is seen as morally wrong because it directly conflicts with the natural inclination towards self-preservation. Similarly, acts of charity and kindness are seen as morally good because they promote the well-being of others, aligning with our natural inclination towards social life.

Divine Command Theory

While Aquinas emphasizes the importance of reason in determining right and wrong through natural law, he also recognizes the role of divine revelation in guiding ethical conduct. Divine command theory posits that an action is morally right if God commands it, and morally wrong if God forbids it.

Aquinas argues that divine command theory complements natural law theory by providing additional guidance and clarification. Since God is the ultimate source of moral authority and goodness, his commands provide a higher level of certainty and guidance in ethical decision-making. However, Aquinas stresses that the moral principles established by divine revelation must be consistent with natural law. If a command contradicts the fundamental goods and inclinations inherent in human nature, it cannot be considered morally right.

Synthesizing Natural Law and Divine Command Theory

Aquinas takes a nuanced approach to the relationship between natural law and divine command theory. He argues that natural law offers a broad framework for ethical decision-making, while divine command theory provides specific guidance within that framework. In other words, natural law principles form the foundation of morality, and divine commands supplement and clarify those principles.

Furthermore, Aquinas argues that reason and revelation are ultimately compatible because they both emanate from the same source of truth – God. Reason can lead us to a general understanding of moral principles, while divine revelation provides specific commands that align with those principles. By synthesizing reason and revelation, Aquinas seeks to bridge the gap between faith and reason, demonstrating that both are essential for a complete understanding of ethics.

Conclusion

Thomas Aquinas’s philosophy of natural law and divine command theory continues to resonate with modern thinkers and ethical theorists. His synthesis of reason and revelation offers a comprehensive framework for ethical decision-making that acknowledges both our natural inclinations and divine guidance. Aquinas’s emphasis on the objective nature of moral principles and the role of God as the ultimate source of moral authority provides a solid foundation for understanding and evaluating ethical dilemmas. Whether one agrees with Aquinas’s religious beliefs or not, his ideas on natural law and divine command theory offer valuable insights into the nature of ethics and the relationship between faith and reason.
