The Joy of Small Game Trapping: Snares, Box Traps, and More


Trapping small game is an ancient skill that has been used by humans for thousands of years to procure food and fur. While modern hunting methods have largely replaced traditional trapping techniques, there is still a certain joy and satisfaction that comes from setting a trap and successfully catching a wild animal. In this guide, we’ll explore the art of small game trapping, including various types of traps and techniques for successful trapping.

The Basics of Small Game Trapping

Small game trapping involves setting traps to capture animals such as rabbits, squirrels, and raccoons. Traps are typically designed to either capture the animal alive for later release or to kill it quickly and humanely for food or fur.

Before setting out to trap small game, it’s important to check local regulations regarding trapping seasons, permitted trap types, and required licenses. Additionally, trappers should always prioritize ethical and humane trapping practices.

Types of Traps

There are several types of traps commonly used for small game trapping, each with its own advantages and disadvantages. Some of the most common types of traps include:

  1. Snares: Snares are simple traps consisting of a noose attached to a trigger mechanism. When an animal enters the snare and pulls on the noose, the trap tightens around its neck or body, capturing it alive or killing it instantly.

  2. Box Traps: Box traps are enclosed traps that capture animals alive. They consist of a box-like structure with a trigger mechanism that causes the door to close when the animal enters the trap.

  3. Deadfall Traps: Deadfall traps are primitive traps that use a heavy weight to crush the animal when triggered. They are typically used for killing small game quickly and humanely.

  4. Pit Traps: Pit traps are dug into the ground and covered with a thin layer of leaves or other debris. When an animal steps on the trap, it falls into the pit and is unable to escape.

  5. Foothold Traps: Foothold traps are designed to capture animals by the foot. They consist of a spring-loaded mechanism that closes around the animal’s foot when triggered.

Setting Traps

To set a trap, first, identify a suitable location with signs of small game activity such as tracks, droppings, or chewed vegetation. Clear any debris from the area and set the trap according to the manufacturer’s instructions or your own design.

It’s important to camouflage the trap with natural materials to make it blend in with the surroundings and minimize the chances of detection by the target animal. Additionally, be sure to check traps regularly to ensure that captured animals are treated humanely and according to local regulations.

The Joy of Small Game Trapping

For many trappers, the joy of small game trapping comes from the thrill of the hunt and the satisfaction of successfully capturing an elusive target. Trapping requires patience, skill, and a deep understanding of animal behavior, making it a rewarding and challenging pursuit.

Trappers also appreciate the opportunity to connect with nature and experience the outdoors in a unique and intimate way. Trapping allows individuals to observe wildlife up close and learn about animal habits and habitats.

Conclusion: A Time-Honored Tradition

In conclusion, small game trapping is a time-honored tradition that offers a unique and rewarding way to connect with nature and procure food or fur. By understanding the basics of trapping and employing ethical and humane practices, trappers can enjoy the thrill of the hunt while respecting the natural world around them. Whether you’re a seasoned trapper or new to the sport, small game trapping can provide a fulfilling and enriching outdoor experience.
