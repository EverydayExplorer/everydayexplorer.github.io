The Quantum Many-Worlds Interpretation: Parallel Universes and Schrdinger's Cat


Have you ever wondered about the mind-boggling concept of parallel universes? Well, buckle up, because we’re about to delve into the mind-bending world of the Quantum Many-Worlds Interpretation, a theory that challenges our fundamental understanding of reality. And what better way to explore this mind-bending theory than by diving into the famous thought experiment involving Schrödinger’s Cat?

First, let’s take a step back and understand the basics. The Quantum Many-Worlds Interpretation is a hypothesis in quantum mechanics proposed by physicist Hugh Everett III in the 1950s. According to this theory, every possible outcome of a quantum event actually occurs, but in separate parallel universes. This means that every decision you make, every quantum event that occurs, creates a branching pathway, leading to the existence of multiple universes.

Now, let’s get to the fun part – Schrödinger’s Cat. This famous thought experiment, conceptualized by Austrian physicist Erwin Schrödinger in 1935, was designed to illustrate the absurdity of the Copenhagen Interpretation of quantum mechanics. In this experiment, a cat is placed inside a box with a radioactive substance that has a 50% chance of decaying and releasing a lethal poison. According to the Copenhagen Interpretation, the cat exists in a superposition of both being alive and dead until the box is opened and observed. However, the Many-Worlds Interpretation takes this a step further.

According to the Many-Worlds Interpretation, as soon as the radioactive substance decays, two parallel universes are created – one where the cat is alive and another where the cat is dead. In each of these universes, a different observer sees a different outcome. This means that there is a universe where you observe a live cat and another where you observe a dead cat. Mind-blowing, right?
