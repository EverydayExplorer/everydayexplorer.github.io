How to Identify and Avoid Wine Faults


Are you a wine enthusiast eager to deepen your understanding of wine tasting? Perhaps you’re a budding sommelier or simply someone who enjoys a good glass of wine and wants to enhance your appreciation for it. In either case, understanding wine faults is crucial to recognizing quality and enjoying your wine to the fullest. In this comprehensive guide, we’ll delve into the world of wine faults, explore common issues that can affect wine quality, and learn how to identify and avoid them.

What Are Wine Faults?

Wine faults, also known as defects or flaws, are undesirable characteristics that can diminish the quality and enjoyment of a wine. These faults can occur at any stage of the winemaking process, from grape cultivation to bottling, and can result from various factors such as poor vineyard management, faulty equipment, or improper storage conditions.

While some wine faults are minor and easily overlooked, others can significantly impact the taste, aroma, and overall drinking experience. Learning to identify these faults can help you make informed decisions when selecting and enjoying wine.

Common Wine Faults and How to Recognize Them

  1. Cork Taint (TCA): One of the most well-known wine faults, cork taint is caused by the presence of 2,4,6-trichloroanisole (TCA) in natural cork stoppers. Wines affected by cork taint may exhibit musty or moldy aromas, often described as resembling wet cardboard or damp basement. To identify cork taint, simply sniff the wine before tasting. If you detect any unpleasant aromas, such as mold or mildew, the wine may be tainted.

  2. Oxidation: Oxidation occurs when wine is overexposed to oxygen, leading to the degradation of its flavors and aromas. Oxidized wines may appear brownish in color and exhibit aromas reminiscent of sherry, nuts, or dried fruit. Additionally, the wine may taste flat or dull on the palate. To detect oxidation, observe the wine’s color and aroma. If it appears darker than expected or smells stale, it may be oxidized.

  3. Sulfur Compounds: Sulfur compounds, such as hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and mercaptans, can impart off-putting aromas to wine, such as rotten eggs, burnt rubber, or garlic. These compounds can result from poor fermentation practices, excessive sulfur dioxide usage, or improper sanitation. To identify sulfur-related faults, swirl the wine in your glass and take a sniff. If you detect any unpleasant odors resembling sulfur or rotten eggs, the wine may be affected.

  4. Brettanomyces (Brett): Brettanomyces is a type of yeast that can produce volatile phenols, imparting earthy, barnyard-like aromas to wine. Wines affected by Brett may exhibit aromas of sweaty horse, band-aid, or damp hay. While some wine enthusiasts enjoy the complexity Brett can bring to certain styles of wine, excessive levels can overwhelm the fruit character and detract from the overall balance. To detect Brett character, swirl the wine in your glass and take a deep sniff. If you notice any funky or barnyard-like aromas, Brett may be present.

  5. Reduction: Reduction occurs when wine is exposed to limited oxygen, leading to the production of sulfur-containing compounds. Wines affected by reduction may exhibit aromas of rotten eggs, burnt rubber, or struck match. Additionally, the wine may taste flinty or austere on the palate. To identify reduction, swirl the wine in your glass and take a sniff. If you detect any sulfurous or pungent aromas, the wine may be reduced.

How to Avoid Wine Faults

While encountering wine faults is inevitable to some extent, there are steps you can take to minimize the risk and ensure the quality of the wines you purchase and enjoy:

  1. Choose Reliable Producers: Opt for wines from reputable producers known for their commitment to quality and attention to detail in the winemaking process.

  2. Inspect the Bottle: Before purchasing a bottle of wine, visually inspect the label, capsule, and cork for any signs of damage or contamination.

  3. Store Wine Properly: Store wine in a cool, dark place away from direct sunlight and temperature fluctuations to prevent premature aging and oxidation.

  4. Use Proper Glassware: When tasting wine, use clean, odor-free glassware to avoid any potential contamination or interference with the wine’s aromas and flavors.

  5. Trust Your Senses: When tasting wine, trust your senses and pay attention to any unusual aromas, flavors, or appearances that may indicate a fault.

By familiarizing yourself with common wine faults and practicing vigilant observation, you can become a more discerning wine enthusiast and enjoy a greater appreciation for the diverse world of wine. Cheers to happy and fault-free wine tasting experiences!
