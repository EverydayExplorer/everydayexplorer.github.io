The Voyage of Voyager: 40 Years of Exploring the Outer Solar System


On August 20, 1977, NASA launched the Voyager 2 spacecraft, followed by its twin, Voyager 1 on September 5, 1977. The mission of these two spacecraft was to study the outer solar system, including Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, and beyond. The Voyager missions were groundbreaking in their exploration of the outer solar system, providing us with important insights into the planets and moons that make up our cosmic neighborhood. As we celebrate the 40th anniversary of this historic mission, let’s take a closer look at the voyage of Voyager and its legacy.

Between the two spacecraft, the Voyager missions have explored all four giant gas planets in our solar system: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Voyager 1 performed a close flyby of Jupiter in March 1979, and Voyager 2 followed in July. They sent back the first ever images of the intricate clouds of Jupiter, revealing a vast and complex system of storms and weather patterns. The Voyagers also observed Jupiter’s four largest moons — Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto — in detail, discovering volcanic activity on Io and the possibility of liquid water oceans beneath the surface of Europa.

The spacecraft then journeyed on to Saturn, where Voyager 1 discovered the small moon Pan and the spokes that rim the planet’s Rings. Voyager 2 observed Saturn’s dynamic atmosphere and detected three new moons. Both spacecrafts also studied the famous Cassini Division in the planet’s iconic rings.

Voyager 2 was the first spacecraft to encounter Uranus. Its flyby in 1986 revealed a pale blue-green planet with a highly tilted magnetic field, an unusual feature that astronomers are still trying to understand. The spacecraft also discovered 11 new moons orbiting Uranus. Voyager 2 then continued on to Neptune, where it became the first spacecraft to fly by this far-off gas giant in 1989. The mission revealed Neptune’s great blue spot, similar to Jupiter’s great red spot, and discovered six new moons orbiting the planet.

Although the Voyager missions were only intended to explore the four outer gas planets, NASA chose to extend the mission’s plane and explore the outer limits of our solar system. Both Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 continue to explore the outer boundary of our solar system, known as the heliosphere. The Voyagers have provided valuable insights into the properties of the solar wind and the interstellar medium that envelopes our entire planetary system.

After 40 years in space, the Voyager spacecraft continue to operate and transmit data back to Earth. Although they are now billions of miles from our planet, the Voyagers’ instruments continue to send back crucial data about the heliosphere and beyond, informing our understanding of humanity’s place in the universe.

The Voyager missions represent one of the greatest achievements of space exploration, inspiring countless students, scientists and space enthusiasts. On September 5, 2017, Voyager 1 celebrated its 40th anniversary in space, marking an incredible milestone for space exploration and humanity as a whole.

In conclusion, the voyage of Voyager highlights the important role of space exploration in expanding our knowledge and understanding of the natural world. The stunning images and valuable scientific data captured by Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 have enriched our understanding of our own planet, our solar system, and the universe beyond. As we look forward to new missions and increased exploration of our cosmic neighborhood, the legacy of the Voyager missions continues to inspire and shape our understanding of our place in the universe.
