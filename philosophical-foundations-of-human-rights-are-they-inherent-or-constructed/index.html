Philosophical Foundations of Human Rights: Are They Inherent or Constructed?


Human rights have been a central topic of debate and discussion for centuries, and they continue to be a cornerstone of modern societies and international relations. These rights encompass a range of fundamental principles, such as the right to life, liberty, and equality, and are considered essential for ensuring the dignity and well-being of individuals around the world. However, the question of whether human rights are inherent or constructed remains a complex and contentious issue in the realm of philosophy and ethics.

In this blog post, we will delve into the philosophical foundations of human rights, exploring the contrasting perspectives that view them as either inherent or constructed. By examining these perspectives, we can gain a deeper understanding of the nature of human rights and their implications for our society.

The Inherent Perspective

One perspective argues that human rights are inherent, meaning that they exist as natural and intrinsic aspects of human existence. Proponents of this view often draw upon philosophical traditions, such as natural law theory, which posits that certain moral principles are universally applicable to all human beings by virtue of their humanity.

One of the key figures in the natural law tradition is Thomas Aquinas, who argued that there are objective moral truths grounded in the nature of human beings. According to Aquinas, human rights are not created by governments or societies but are instead discovered through reason and reflection. These rights are seen as inherent to human nature, and their existence is independent of human institutions or legal systems.

Furthermore, the inherent perspective often invokes the concept of human dignity. The idea is that every individual possesses an inherent dignity that must be respected and protected. This dignity is the foundation upon which human rights are built, and it is not contingent upon societal consensus or legal frameworks.

The Constructed Perspective

On the other side of the debate, there are those who argue that human rights are constructed, meaning that they are products of human societies and are contingent upon historical, cultural, and political factors. This perspective challenges the notion of inherent or natural rights and instead suggests that human rights are socially created and subject to change over time.

One prominent proponent of the constructed perspective is the philosopher John Stuart Mill, who believed that rights are the result of social contracts and agreements among individuals within a society. According to this view, human rights are not pre-existing but are established through collective deliberation and consensus.

The constructed perspective also emphasizes the role of cultural relativism. It acknowledges that different societies may have different conceptions of rights based on their unique cultural, historical, and political contexts. This perspective raises questions about the universality of human rights and whether they can be applied uniformly across diverse societies.

A Middle Ground: The Hybrid Perspective

While the inherent and constructed perspectives represent two contrasting views on the philosophical foundations of human rights, some philosophers advocate for a middle-ground approach. This hybrid perspective acknowledges that there may be some rights that are inherent to human nature, such as the right to life and bodily integrity, while also recognizing that many rights are constructed and subject to societal agreements.

Philosopher Martha Nussbaum, for example, argues for a list of ‘capabilities’ that are essential for human flourishing. These capabilities include the ability to lead a life of dignity, to have access to education and healthcare, and to participate in political processes. Nussbaum’s approach combines elements of both inherent and constructed perspectives by recognizing certain core capabilities as inherent while allowing for variation in the specific expression of these capabilities in different societies.

Implications for Human Rights Today

The debate between the inherent and constructed perspectives on human rights has significant implications for how we understand and protect these rights in contemporary society. If we lean more towards the inherent perspective, we might argue for the existence of a set of universal human rights that are inalienable and not subject to negotiation or compromise. This perspective tends to prioritize the protection of individual rights above all else.

Conversely, the constructed perspective invites us to consider the importance of cultural context and the need for ongoing dialogue and negotiation among diverse societies. It recognizes that the concept of human rights is not static and may evolve over time as societies change and develop.

In practice, many human rights advocates and organizations take a pragmatic approach that draws from both perspectives. They recognize the existence of certain fundamental rights that are inherent to human dignity while also acknowledging the need for cultural sensitivity and adaptation in the implementation of these rights.

Conclusion

The question of whether human rights are inherent or constructed is a deeply philosophical and ethical one that continues to shape discussions on human rights at both the national and international levels. While there may not be a definitive answer to this question, exploring these contrasting perspectives enriches our understanding of the nature and origins of human rights.

In the end, whether one believes that human rights are inherent, constructed, or a combination of both, the fundamental goal remains the same: to protect the dignity, well-being, and freedom of every individual, regardless of their background or circumstances. This shared commitment to upholding human rights is what unites us in the pursuit of a more just and equitable world.
