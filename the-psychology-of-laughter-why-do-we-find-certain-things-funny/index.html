The Psychology of Laughter: Why Do We Find Certain Things Funny?


Laughter is an intriguing human behavior that has captivated the attention of psychologists and researchers for centuries. We all enjoy a good laugh, but have you ever stopped to ponder why certain things tickle our funny bone? In this blog post, we will delve into the fascinating world of humor and explore the psychology behind why we find certain things funny.

1. Relief theory:
One of the earliest theories on humor, proposed by Sigmund Freud, suggests that laughter serves as a release of built-up tension or anxiety. According to this theory, we find things funny because they provide a temporary escape from serious or stressful situations. For example, a comedian cracking a joke about a common everyday mishap can make us laugh because it momentarily relieves us of our worries.

2. Incongruity theory:
Another prominent theory, proposed by Immanuel Kant and later expanded upon by Arthur Schopenhauer, posits that humor originates from the unexpected or incongruous elements within a situation. In other words, when something deviates from our expectations or violates established norms, it creates a sense of surprise or absurdity that elicits laughter. This theory explains why puns, slapstick comedy, and absurd jokes can often leave us in fits of laughter.

3. Superiority theory:
According to this theory, formulated by Thomas Hobbes, Henri Bergson, and later refined by Alfred Adler, humor arises from a sense of superiority over others. We find things funny when they highlight the flaws, mistakes, or eccentricities of others. By laughing at these shortcomings, we enhance our own self-esteem and reinforce our perceived superiority. Stand-up comedians often employ this type of humor by exaggerating common human follies or mocking societal absurdities.

4. Incongruity-resolution theory:
Building upon the incongruity theory, this hybrid theory proposed by Richard Shuster posits that we find things funny when we experience a moment of confusion or cognitive dissonance, followed by the resolution of the incongruity. When our brain is momentarily puzzled by a joke or a witty remark and then successfully resolves the incongruity, it rewards us with laughter. This theory helps explain why clever wordplay, double entendre, and punchlines can elicit such strong reactions.

5. Social bonding theory:
Laughter plays a crucial role in social interactions and group dynamics. As social creatures, we use humor to build connections, diffuse tension, and establish rapport with others. Sharing a humorous moment with our friends or colleagues enhances our sense of belonging and strengthens social bonds. Psychologist Robert Provine suggests that laughter is primarily a social behavior, serving as a means of communication and bonding within social groups.

6. Cognitive processing theory:
Humor often requires a certain level of cognitive processing and understanding. According to this theory, proposed by Peter McGraw, we find things funny when they challenge our cognitive schemas and require us to engage in a higher level of mental processing. Puns, wordplay, and clever jokes often require us to make connections between different ideas or concepts, activating our analytical thinking and rewarding us with laughter when we successfully decode the joke.

7. Emotional release theory:
Laughter has the power to release pent-up emotions and provide an outlet for expressing difficult or taboo thoughts and feelings. Jokes that touch upon sensitive topics or explore controversial themes can help individuals cope with complex emotions by allowing them to laugh at their own discomfort or fears. By finding humor in the face of adversity, we are able to gain a sense of control and resilience over difficult situations.

While these theories provide valuable insights into the psychology of laughter, it is important to note that humor is highly subjective and influenced by individual differences, cultural norms, and personal experiences. What one person finds funny, another may find offensive or unamusing. Nevertheless, understanding the underlying mechanisms of laughter can help us appreciate the intricacies of human psychology and the power of humor in our lives.

In conclusion, the psychology of laughter is a multifaceted topic that encompasses various theories and explanations. From the release of tension to the resolution of incongruity, our sense of humor reflects our complex cognitive processes, emotional needs, and social interactions. By unraveling the secrets of laughter, we gain a deeper understanding of ourselves and the role humor plays in our daily lives. So, the next time you find yourself in fits of laughter, take a moment to appreciate the fascinating psychology behind it.
