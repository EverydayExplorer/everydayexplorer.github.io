Understanding Wine Faults: Identifying and Resolving Common Issues


Wine is a complex and sophisticated beverage that can bring joy and delight to many. However, like any natural product, wine can sometimes have faults that affect its taste, aroma, and overall quality. Understanding these wine faults is essential for wine enthusiasts and professionals alike, as it allows for the identification and resolution of common issues.

In this blog post, we will explore some of the most common wine faults, how to identify them, and the best ways to resolve these issues. Whether you’re a wine connoisseur or just a casual wine drinker, learning about wine faults can enhance your overall wine experience and help you make educated choices when selecting and enjoying wines.

Common Wine Faults

  1. Cork Taint: This is perhaps the most well-known wine fault. It occurs when a wine comes into contact with a contaminated cork during the aging process. The compound responsible for this fault is called TCA (2,4,6-Trichloroanisole), which imparts a musty, damp cardboard odor to the wine. If you detect a musty smell, reminiscent of a wet basement or old newspaper, it is likely that the wine is affected by cork taint.

  2. Oxidation: Oxidation occurs when wine is exposed to excessive amounts of oxygen. This commonly happens when a bottle is left open for an extended period or when the closure is faulty. Oxidized wine often exhibits a brownish color, a flat or stale taste, and a loss of fruity aromas. To prevent oxidation, ensure proper storage and use an airtight closure when re-corking opened bottles.

  3. Reduction: Wine can also be affected by reduction, which happens when inadequate exposure to oxygen causes a buildup of sulfur compounds. Reduction can manifest as a rotten egg or burnt rubber smell and can also result in a reductive character on the palate, with flavors resembling garlic or onion. To alleviate reduction, decanting the wine or allowing it to breathe for some time before serving can help dissipate the unwanted aromas.

  4. Heat Damage: Excessive exposure to heat can cause wine to spoil. When wine is exposed to high temperatures, it can become ‘cooked,’ resulting in a stewed, overripe fruit aroma. Heat can also cause the expansion of the wine, pushing the cork out slightly and allowing oxygen to enter the bottle. To prevent heat damage, store wine in a cool, dark place, away from direct sunlight or heat sources.

  5. MLF (Malolactic Fermentation) Spoilage: Malolactic fermentation is a natural process that converts malic acid to lactic acid, contributing to the smoothness and creamy mouthfeel of many wines. However, if this process occurs unintentionally or uncontrolled, it can lead to spoilage. MLF spoilage typically presents as a buttery or buttermilk-like aroma, masking the wine’s other attributes. Winemakers meticulously monitor and control MLF to avoid spoiling the wine.

Identifying and Resolving Wine Faults

Identifying a wine fault is the first step in resolving the issue. Here are some tips on how to identify common wine faults:

  • Use your senses: Pay attention to the wine’s appearance, aroma, and taste. Visual cues such as unusual coloration or excessive sediments can indicate a fault. An off-putting aroma or taste that does not correspond to the wine’s characteristics is also a red flag.

  • Compare with a reference: If you suspect a wine fault, compare it with a similar wine that you know is not flawed. This comparison will help you identify any significant discrepancies in aroma, taste, or appearance.

Once you have identified a wine fault, here are some possible ways to resolve the issue:

  1. Contact the retailer: If you purchased the wine from a reputable store or winery, reach out to them and explain the fault you have encountered. They may offer a replacement or refund, depending on their policies and the specific fault.

  2. Seek advice from a wine professional: If you’re unsure about the fault or its severity, consult a wine professional or sommelier. They can provide guidance and recommend possible solutions based on their experience and knowledge.

  3. Learn from the experience: Analyzing and understanding a wine fault can be a valuable learning opportunity. Reflect on the fault and use it as a way to expand your wine knowledge and improve your future wine selections.

In Conclusion

Understanding wine faults and knowing how to identify and resolve common issues is crucial for every wine lover. By familiarizing yourself with these faults, you can make informed choices when purchasing and enjoy your wine journey to the fullest. Remember to trust your senses, seek professional advice when needed, and embrace the learning experiences that wine faults can provide. Cheers to a world of great wines, free from faults and full of delight!
