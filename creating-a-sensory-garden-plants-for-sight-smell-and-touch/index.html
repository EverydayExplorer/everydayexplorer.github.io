Creating a Sensory Garden: Plants for Sight, Smell, and Touch


Imagine strolling through a garden where every step delights the senses. The vibrant colors of blooming flowers catch your eye, while the sweet fragrance of herbs perfumes the air. With each gentle caress, you feel the softness of delicate petals and the rough texture of aromatic foliage beneath your fingertips. Welcome to the world of sensory gardening, where plants are carefully selected and arranged to engage sight, smell, and touch, creating an immersive experience that awakens the senses and nurtures the soul.

What is a Sensory Garden?

A sensory garden is a specially designed outdoor space that stimulates the senses through the use of plants, textures, colors, fragrances, and other sensory elements. These gardens are thoughtfully planned to provide a multisensory experience, offering visitors the opportunity to connect with nature in a deeply personal and meaningful way.

Plants for Sight

When planning a sensory garden, visual appeal plays a crucial role. Here are some plants known for their striking colors, interesting foliage, and captivating blooms:

  1. Daylilies (Hemerocallis spp.): These versatile perennials come in a wide range of colors and flower shapes, adding splashes of vivid hues to the garden landscape.

  2. Lavender (Lavandula spp.): With its fragrant purple spikes and silvery foliage, lavender not only delights the sense of smell but also adds visual interest to garden borders and pathways.

  3. Japanese Maple (Acer palmatum): prized for its stunning foliage colors, Japanese maple varieties offer a feast for the eyes with shades of red, orange, yellow, and green.

  4. Sunflowers (Helianthus annuus): These cheerful annuals brighten any garden with their towering stems and large, golden-yellow flowers that follow the sun’s path throughout the day.

  5. Coleus (Plectranthus scutellarioides): prized for their vibrant foliage patterns and colors, coleus plants thrive in partial shade and bring a pop of color to shady garden spots.

Plants for Smell

Fragrant plants add an extra dimension to the sensory garden, filling the air with their delightful scents. Here are some aromatic favorites to consider:

  1. Roses (Rosa spp.): Known for their timeless beauty and intoxicating fragrance, roses come in a variety of scents, from sweet and floral to spicy and citrusy.

  2. Lilies (Lilium spp.): These elegant flowers emit a heady fragrance that perfumes the air, making them a favorite choice for bouquets and garden borders alike.

  3. Mint (Mentha spp.): Whether it’s peppermint, spearmint, or chocolate mint, these aromatic herbs release a refreshing scent when brushed against or crushed, making them perfect for sensory gardens.

  4. Jasmine (Jasminum spp.): prized for its sweet, floral fragrance, jasmine vines add a touch of exotic beauty to the garden while filling the air with their intoxicating scent.

  5. Lavender (Lavandula spp.): In addition to its visual appeal, lavender also offers a soothing fragrance that calms the mind and uplifts the spirit, making it a must-have for any sensory garden.

Plants for Touch

In a sensory garden, tactile sensations are just as important as visual and olfactory experiences. Here are some plants with interesting textures that beg to be touched:

  1. Lamb’s Ear (Stachys byzantina): Named for its soft, fuzzy leaves that resemble a lamb’s ear, this perennial ground cover is a favorite among children and adults alike.

  2. Sundew (Drosera spp.): With its sticky, glandular hairs that trap insects, sundew plants provide a fascinating tactile experience for curious gardeners.

  3. Sempervivum (Hens and Chicks): These succulent plants are known for their rosette-shaped foliage and unique texture, making them a popular choice for rock gardens and containers.

  4. Velvet Plant (Gynura aurantiaca): With its velvety purple leaves that feel like soft suede to the touch, velvet plant adds a touch of luxury to the sensory garden.

  5. Lemon Verbena (Aloysia citrodora): prized for its lemon-scented foliage, lemon verbena leaves have a slightly rough texture that releases its aromatic oils when rubbed, providing a multisensory experience.

Designing Your Sensory Garden

When designing a sensory garden, consider the needs and preferences of your intended audience, whether it’s children, adults, or individuals with sensory impairments. Create designated areas for different sensory experiences, such as a fragrant herb garden, a touch-and-feel zone with tactile plants, and a colorful flowerbed for visual delight. Incorporate features such as winding pathways, seating areas, and water features to enhance the overall sensory experience and encourage exploration.

In conclusion, creating a sensory garden is a rewarding endeavor that allows you to connect with nature on a deeper level while providing a sanctuary for relaxation and reflection. By carefully selecting plants for sight, smell, and touch, you can design a garden that engages the senses and fosters a sense of well-being for all who visit. So roll up your sleeves, dig in the dirt, and let your senses guide you as you create a sensory garden that delights the soul and inspires the imagination.
