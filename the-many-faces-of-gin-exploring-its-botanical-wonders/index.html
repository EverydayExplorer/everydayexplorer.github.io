The Many Faces of Gin: Exploring Its Botanical Wonders


Gin is a spirit with a rich history dating back to the 17th century. It has become a staple in many bars and households around the world, thanks to its versatility and unique flavor profile. Many people associate gin with a simple cocktail like a gin and tonic, but there is so much more to this spirit than meets the eye. In this blog, we will explore the many faces of gin and the botanical wonders that make it such a beloved spirit. One of the most fascinating aspects of gin is its botanical base. Gin is made from a neutral spirit that is then infused with a variety of botanicals, including juniper berries, coriander, angelica root, and citrus peel. The combination of these botanicals is what gives gin its unique flavor profile and makes it so versatile. Depending on the botanicals used and the distillation process, gin can have a range of flavors, from light and floral to bold and spicy. Juniper berries are the most important botanical in gin and are what give gin its distinctive piney flavor. Juniper berries grow on small shrubs that are found throughout the Northern Hemisphere and have been used for medicinal purposes for centuries. The berries are harvested in the autumn and then dried before being used in the distillation process. Coriander is another important botanical in gin and is used to balance the strong flavor of the juniper berries. Coriander seeds come from the coriander plant, which is a member of the parsley family. The seeds have a slightly sweet and spicy flavor and are often used in Middle Eastern and Indian cuisine. Angelica root is used in gin to add a subtle earthy flavor and to help bind the other botanicals together. The root comes from the Angelica plant, which is a member of the carrot family. The plant is native to Europe and Asia and has been used for medicinal purposes for centuries. Citrus peel is used in gin to add a bright and fresh flavor. The peel can come from a variety of citrus fruits, including lemon, lime, and grapefruit. The oils in the peel give gin a zesty and refreshing taste. In addition to these main botanicals, many distillers use other botanicals to add complexity and depth to their gins. Some of these additional botanicals include orris root, cardamom, cassia bark, and lavender. Now that we have explored the botanical wonders of gin, let’s take a closer look at the different types of gin available. There are four main types of gin: London Dry Gin, Old Tom Gin, Genever, and New Western Dry Gin. London Dry Gin is the most common type of gin and is the style that most people are familiar with. It is made with a neutral spirit and infused with botanicals, with no added sugar or flavorings. The name \‘London Dry\’ does not refer to the location of production but rather to the style of gin. Old Tom Gin is a slightly sweeter and more full-bodied gin that was popular in the 18th and 19th centuries. It is made with a neutral spirit that is sweetened with sugar or honey before being infused with botanicals. Old Tom Gin is often used in classic cocktails like the Tom Collins and the Martinez. Genever is a Dutch style of gin that is made with a malted grain spirit and infused with botanicals. It is a more full-bodied and malty gin that is often enjoyed neat or in a cocktail. New Western Dry Gin is a more modern style of gin that is characterized by the use of non-traditional botanicals and a more pronounced flavor profile. This style of gin is often associated with American craft distillers and is known for its bold and unique flavors. In conclusion, gin is a versatile and complex spirit that is beloved by many. Its botanical base is what makes it so unique and allows for a wide range of flavors and styles. Whether you prefer a classic gin and tonic or a more adventurous cocktail, there is a gin out there for everyone. So the next time you order a gin cocktail, take a moment to appreciate the botanical wonders that make it so special.
