The Mathematics Behind Fractals: From Koch Snowflake to Mandelbrot Set


Fractals are fascinating mathematical objects that have gained widespread attention for their intricate and beautiful patterns. They are a graphical representation of complex mathematical formulas that repeat infinitely at different scales. Fractals exhibit self-similarity, meaning that when you zoom in on a small section of the fractal, you will see a similar pattern to the whole.

In this blog post, we will explore two famous examples of fractals: the Koch Snowflake and the Mandelbrot Set. We will delve into the mathematical concepts behind these fractals and understand how they are generated.

The Koch Snowflake

The Koch Snowflake is a fractal curve that is constructed by repeatedly adding smaller segments to an initial equilateral triangle. The construction of the Koch Snowflake is based on an iterative process that involves dividing each line segment into three equal parts and replacing the middle third with an equilateral triangle.

Mathematically, the length of each segment after the nth iteration can be calculated using the formula:

Length of segment = Initial length / 3^n

As n approaches infinity, the length of the segments approaches zero, but the total length of the curve remains infinite. This property of the Koch Snowflake is what gives it its intricate self-similar pattern.

Visually, the Koch Snowflake resembles a snowflake with infinitely many jagged edges. It is a classic example of a fractal that exhibits self-similarity, no matter how deeply you zoom into it.

The Mandelbrot Set

The Mandelbrot Set is one of the most famous and visually stunning fractals. It is named after Benoit Mandelbrot, the mathematician who discovered and popularized it. The Mandelbrot Set is defined in the complex plane by an iterative formula:

Znew = Zold^2 + C

Here, Znew is the new value after each iteration, Zold is the previous value, and C is a constant. The values of Znew are computed repeatedly, and if at any point the magnitude of Znew exceeds a certain threshold value, the iteration is stopped. The points in the complex plane where the iteration remains bounded constitute the Mandelbrot Set.

The Mandelbrot Set is generated by performing this iterative calculation for each point in the complex plane. The color of each point in the generated fractal corresponds to the number of iterations required to determine whether it belongs to the set or not. The visually striking patterns of the Mandelbrot Set emerge from the intricate boundaries between points that are inside and outside the set.

One of the most intriguing features of the Mandelbrot Set is its self-similarity. Zooming into any part of the fractal reveals smaller versions of the whole set, with intricate details and patterns repeating infinitely. The deeper you zoom, the more details you uncover, revealing an astonishing level of complexity.
