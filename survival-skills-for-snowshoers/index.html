Survival Skills for Snowshoers


Are you a nature lover who can’t get enough of the great outdoors, even in the winter? Do you enjoy the challenge and solitude that comes with snowshoeing through snowy landscapes? If so, then you’re not alone. Snowshoeing has gained popularity as a fun and accessible winter activity that allows outdoor enthusiasts to explore areas that would otherwise be difficult to access during the colder months.

While snowshoeing can be a thrilling experience, it’s important to remember that winter conditions can be unpredictable and potentially dangerous. Therefore, it’s crucial to be prepared and equipped with the necessary survival skills to ensure your safety while out on the trails. In this blog post, we’ll discuss some essential survival skills and precautions that every snowshoer should be aware of.

1. Be Weather Wise
One of the most important aspects of snowshoeing safely is to be aware of the weather conditions before you head out. Winter weather can change rapidly, so it’s crucial to check the forecast and plan your trip accordingly. Keep an eye out for potential snowstorms or extreme drops in temperature, as these can present serious hazards to snowshoers.

2. Dress for Success
Proper clothing is key when it comes to winter sports, and snowshoeing is no exception. Layering your clothing is essential to stay warm and dry while on the trails. Start with a moisture-wicking base layer to keep sweat away from your body, add an insulating layer for warmth, and finish with a waterproof and breathable outer layer to protect against wind, snow, and moisture.

3. Know the Terrain
Before embarking on a snowshoeing adventure, take the time to research and familiarize yourself with the terrain you’ll be navigating. Some trails may have steeper sections, icy patches, or even avalanche-prone areas. Understanding the topography of the trail will allow you to plan your route more effectively and avoid potentially dangerous situations.

4. Carry Essential Gear
Having the right gear can make all the difference when it comes to survival in the winter wilderness. Along with the basics such as snowshoes, trekking poles, and a backpack to carry your essentials, make sure you bring a map, compass, and a reliable GPS device to navigate your way. It’s also crucial to carry a headlamp, extra batteries, a first aid kit, a fire starter, and a multitool. These items can be lifesavers in emergency situations.

5. Practice Navigation Skills
Snow-covered landscapes can be disorienting, and it’s easy to lose your sense of direction. Being proficient in navigation skills is essential in order to stay on track and ensure a safe return. Brush up on your map reading skills, learn how to use a compass, and practice using a GPS device before venturing out into the winter wilderness.

6. Build a Snow Shelter
In the event of an unexpected turn of events or an emergency, knowing how to build a snow shelter can literally save your life. There are several types of snow shelters you can build, from simple snow caves to more elaborate snow trenches. Educate yourself on the various techniques, practice building shelters before your trip, and carry the necessary tools to construct one.

7. Be Avalanche Aware
Snowshoers should always be mindful of the risk of avalanches, especially if you’re venturing into backcountry or mountainous regions. Check the avalanche forecast and terrain ratings for the area you plan to explore. Avoid steep slopes and areas prone to avalanches, and always travel with a companion or let someone know your planned route and estimated time of return.

8. Carry Extra Food and Water
Snowshoeing can be physically demanding, and it’s crucial to fuel your body adequately to stay warm and energized. Pack extra food high in calories and nutrients, as well as plenty of water or warm beverages, to prevent dehydration and keep your energy levels up during your snowshoeing adventure.

9. Stay Hydrated and Protect Against Frostbite
It’s easy to overlook hydration during winter activities, as we tend to think of it as mostly a concern in hot weather. However, staying properly hydrated is just as important when snowshoeing. Dehydration can increase the risk of hypothermia and frostbite. Make sure to drink plenty of fluids throughout the day, even if you don’t feel particularly thirsty.

Additionally, take precautions to protect yourself from frostbite by wearing warm and waterproof boots, thick socks, and insulated gloves. Keep an eye on exposed skin and cover up with appropriate gear if you start feeling any discomfort or numbness.

10. Leave No Trace
Lastly, but just as importantly, always remember to leave no trace. As snowshoers, it’s our responsibility to minimize our impact on the environment. Be mindful of where you walk and try to stick to established trails or areas where your presence won’t leave a lasting impact. Respect wildlife and their habitats, and always carry out any trash or waste that you generate during your adventure.

In conclusion, snowshoeing is a fantastic way to experience the winter wonderland while enjoying the thrills of outdoor exploration. However, it’s crucial to have the necessary survival skills and knowledge to stay safe and make the most of your journey. By being weather-wise, dressing appropriately, knowing the terrain, carrying essential gear, and practicing navigation skills, you can ensure a safe and enjoyable snowshoeing experience. Remember to always be prepared, be cautious, and respect nature. Stay safe and enjoy your winter adventures!
