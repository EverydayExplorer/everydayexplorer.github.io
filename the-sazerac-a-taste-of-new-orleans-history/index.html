The Sazerac: A Taste of New Orleans History


If you’re a fan of classic cocktails, then you’ve likely heard of the Sazerac. This iconic drink is not only a staple of New Orleans, but it also has a history as rich and complex as its flavor profile. In this blog, we’ll dive into the origins of the Sazerac and explore how it has become a symbol of New Orleans culture.

The Sazerac is believed to be the oldest known American cocktail, originating in the early 19th century. It was first concocted by a Creole apothecary named Antoine Amedie Peychaud in the famous French Quarter of New Orleans. Peychaud created his own brand of bitters, which he mixed with cognac and various other ingredients to create what would later become known as the Sazerac.

The original recipe called for the use of Sazerac de Forge et Fils brandy as the base spirit, hence the name of the cocktail. However, as the popularity of the Sazerac grew, the recipe evolved to include rye whiskey as a substitute for brandy. This change in ingredients was mainly due to the Phylloxera epidemic, which devastated vineyards in France and caused a scarcity of brandy.

The Sazerac is traditionally prepared by rinsing a glass with absinthe, which imparts a subtle anise flavor to the drink. The glass is then filled with a mixture of whiskey, simple syrup or sugar, and a few dashes of Peychaud’s bitters. The cocktail is stirred with ice and strained into the absinthe-rinsed glass, and finally garnished with a lemon twist.

One of the key elements that sets the Sazerac apart from other cocktails is the use of bitters. Peychaud’s bitters, with its distinctive flavors of anise, citrus, and herbs, provides a unique complexity to the drink. Today, Peychaud’s bitters are still used in the classic Sazerac recipe, ensuring that the traditions of the past are preserved.

The Sazerac quickly gained popularity and became a favorite among locals and visitors alike. It became the official cocktail of New Orleans in 2008, a testament to its significance in the city’s culinary and cultural landscape. The Sazerac has even inspired the creation of an annual festival called the ‘Sazerac Cocktail Week,’ where bartenders from all over the world showcase their skills in crafting this timeless libation.

While the classic Sazerac recipe remains a beloved favorite, variations of the cocktail have emerged over time. Some bartenders experiment with different types of whiskey, such as rye or bourbon, to add their own twist to the classic flavor profile. Others may opt for different types of bitters or even incorporate local ingredients to create a more unique experience.

As you can see, the Sazerac is much more than just a drink – it’s a piece of New Orleans history. It has withstood the test of time and remains a symbol of the city’s vibrant cocktail culture. So the next time you find yourself in the Big Easy, be sure to order a Sazerac and raise a glass to the rich heritage and traditions that have shaped this iconic cocktail. Cheers!
