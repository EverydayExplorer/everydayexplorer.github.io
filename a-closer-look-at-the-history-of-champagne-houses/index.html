A Closer Look at the History of Champagne Houses


Champagne, the sparkling wine renowned for its elegance and effervescence, is a symbol of celebration and luxury. The name ‘Champagne’ is synonymous with sophistication and fine taste. But have you ever wondered about the history of the Champagne houses that produce these exquisite bottles of bubbly? Join us on a journey as we take a closer look at the fascinating history of Champagne houses.

Birth of Champagne

The story of Champagne dates back several centuries. It all began in the region of Champagne, located in northeastern France. In the early days, the region of Champagne was known for its still wines rather than the sparkling variety we are familiar with today. It was during the mid-17th century that the creation of sparkling Champagne was discovered by accident.

Legend has it that the cold winters in Champagne halted the fermentation process of the wine, only for it to restart during the warmer months. The discovery of secondary fermentation in the bottle led to the creation of the iconic bubbles that now define Champagne.

The Rise of Champagne Houses

As the popularity of Champagne grew, so did the number of Champagne houses. These houses, also known as ‘Maisons de Champagne,’ played a pivotal role in shaping the Champagne industry. Some of the oldest Champagne houses date back to the 18th century, while others emerged in the 19th century.

These houses were often founded by families with a long-standing history in winemaking, who ventured into producing Champagne. They established their own vineyards, crafted their unique blends, and developed the art of aging Champagne to perfection. Many of these houses are still in operation today and continue to be renowned for their exceptional quality.

Prominent Champagne Houses

Over the centuries, certain Champagne houses have risen to prominence, becoming synonymous with excellence and luxury. Let’s take a closer look at a few of these notable Champagne houses:

  1. Moët & Chandon: Founded in 1743, Moët & Chandon is one of the most renowned and largest Champagne houses in the world. Their iconic brand and luxurious image have made them a favorite among celebrities and Champagne enthusiasts alike.

  2. Taittinger: Established in 1734, Taittinger is known for its commitment to tradition and high-quality production. The family-owned Champagne house is famed for its elegant, refined style, and their vineyards are mostly situated in the grand cru areas of Champagne.

  3. Veuve Clicquot: Founded in 1772 by a widow named Madame Clicquot, Veuve Clicquot is celebrated for its bold and innovative approach. The house is credited with creating the first known vintage Champagne and was instrumental in popularizing Champagne in the Russian market.

  4. Bollinger: Dating back to 1829, Bollinger is recognized for its dedication to craftsmanship and exceptional vineyard sources. With a reputation for producing robust and richly flavored Champagnes, Bollinger has remained a favorite among connoisseurs for generations.

The Art of Champagne Making

Champagne houses have perfected the art of Champagne making through meticulous attention to detail and craftsmanship. From the careful selection of grapes to the blending of different vintages, the goal is to create a harmonious and consistent taste profile.

One crucial aspect unique to Champagne is the process of aging the wine on its lees, also known as ‘sur lie.’ This deliberate maturation gives Champagne its distinctive complexity and character. The length of this aging period varies according to the desired style, ranging from a minimum of 15 months to several years.

Conclusion

The history of Champagne houses offers a captivating insight into the evolution of Champagne as a symbol of luxury and celebration. From accidental discoveries to deliberate craftsmanship, the journey of Champagne houses has contributed to the unique allure and prestige surrounding this legendary sparkling wine.

Next time you raise a glass of Champagne to toast a special occasion, take a moment to appreciate the heritage and passion that goes into each bottle. The Champagne houses continue to be the custodians of this centuries-old tradition, ensuring that Champagne remains the drink of choice for celebrations around the world.
