The Mythological Influences in Shakespearean Plays


Shakespearean plays have long been hailed as literary masterpieces, filled with rich characters, complex plots, and timeless themes. One of the key elements that adds depth and meaning to these plays is the mythological influences at play. Shakespeare drew heavily from Greek and Roman mythology, intertwining these ancient stories with his own genius to create unforgettable narratives.

From the tragic tale of Macbeth to the romantic escapades of A Midsummer Night’s Dream, mythology serves as a powerful backdrop, providing insights into human nature and the universal human experience. In this blog, we will explore some of the prominent mythological influences found in Shakespearean plays and how they contribute to the overall resonance and appeal of his works.

1. The Influence of Greek Tragedies

Greek tragedies, such as those written by Sophocles and Euripides, explored the grand themes of fate, hubris, and the fall of great men. Shakespeare weaved these themes into his plays, incorporating elements of Greek tragedy to enhance the dramatic tension and impact of his stories.

One notable example is the play Macbeth, which draws inspiration from the Greek myth of Oedipus. Like Oedipus, Macbeth is a tragic character whose ambition ultimately leads to his downfall. Additionally, the themes of fate and prophecy, so prevalent in Greek mythology, are evident in Macbeth through the witches’ prophecies and the protagonist’s relentless pursuit of power.

2. The Influence of Roman Mythology

While Greek mythology heavily influenced Shakespeare, Roman mythology also played a significant role in shaping his works. Roman mythology focused on themes of power, politics, and the clash between order and chaos—a perfect fit for Shakespeare’s exploration of kingship, betrayal, and the complexity of human desires.

In Julius Caesar, for example, Shakespeare draws upon the historical events surrounding the assassination of Julius Caesar and the subsequent power struggle among his followers. The character of Brutus, driven by his idealistic principles and manipulated by others, reflects the tragic hero archetype found in both Greek and Roman mythology.

3. The Influence of Mythological Creatures

Mythological creatures and supernatural beings also find their place in Shakespeare’s plays, adding an otherworldly element to the stories. Fairies, witches, and spirits populate A Midsummer Night’s Dream, Macbeth, and The Tempest, respectively, infusing the narratives with a sense of magic and enchantment.

In A Midsummer Night’s Dream, the mischievous Puck serves as a playful intermediary between the mortal and fairy worlds, while the three witches in Macbeth heighten the suspense and foreboding atmosphere. These mythological creatures bring a sense of wonder and mystery to the plays, creating a heightened sense of theatricality.

4. The Influence of Classical Literature

Beyond Greek and Roman mythology, Shakespeare also drew inspiration from classical literature, including the works of Ovid and Virgil. Ovid’s Metamorphoses, a collection of myths and legends, provided Shakespeare with a rich tapestry of stories, characters, and themes to incorporate into his plays.

For instance, the story of Pyramus and Thisbe from Ovid’s Metamorphoses is famously reimagined in A Midsummer Night’s Dream as the tragicomic play-within-a-play performed by the laboring Mechanicals. This interplay between classical literature and Shakespeare’s works demonstrates his ability to adapt and transform ancient myths into contemporary theater.

In conclusion, the mythological influences in Shakespearean plays are not only a testament to the enduring power of ancient stories, but also a testament to Shakespeare’s ability to infuse his works with timeless themes and universal human experiences. By drawing on the world of gods, heroes, and mythical creatures, Shakespeare created an intricate tapestry of drama, tragedy, and comedy. His plays continue to captivate audiences today, highlighting the enduring relevance and fascination with mythological influences in literature.
