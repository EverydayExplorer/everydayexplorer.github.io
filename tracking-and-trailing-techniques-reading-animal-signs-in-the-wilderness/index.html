Tracking and Trailing Techniques: Reading Animal Signs in the Wilderness


When exploring the great outdoors, there is something truly captivating about being able to track and trail animals. The ability to decipher the signs left behind by these creatures can provide a wealth of information and deepen our understanding of the wilderness. Tracking is not only a valuable skill for nature enthusiasts and photographers, but it is also an essential tool for wildlife biologists and conservationists. In this article, we will delve into the world of tracking and trailing techniques and learn how to read animal signs in the wilderness.

Understanding Animal Tracks

One of the first and most important aspects of tracking is learning to identify and interpret animal tracks. Animal tracks are impressions left in the ground by an animal’s foot or paw. They can reveal crucial information such as the type of animal, its size, and even its behavior. To accurately identify animal tracks, it’s important to consider various factors such as the number of toes, the overall shape of the track, and any unique features like claws or pads.

To start, look for patterns in the tracks. Some animals, like cats, have retractable claws, which means their tracks won’t show claw marks. Others, like canines, have non-retractable claws, leaving distinct markings behind. By observing the shape and size of the track, you can make educated guesses about the animal that made it.

Scat and Other Animal Droppings

Another valuable clue left behind by animals is their scat or droppings. These can provide insights into an animal’s diet, health, and even recent activities. For example, the presence of berries in the scat indicates a herbivorous diet, while the presence of fur or bones suggests a carnivorous diet. Fresh scat may indicate that the animal is nearby, while older scat might suggest it has moved on.

When examining scat, be cautious of potential diseases or parasites that may be present. It’s best to use gloves and avoid directly touching the scat to prevent any possible infections or contamination.

Animal Trailing Techniques

Trailing is the art of following animal tracks to gain a deeper understanding of their behavior and movements. It requires patience, keen observation skills, and the ability to interpret the signs left along the trail. Here are some techniques to help you become a skilled animal trailer:

1. Track Patterns: Pay attention to the spacing between tracks, as it can reveal an animal’s speed and gait. A slower pace might suggest the animal was grazing or searching for food, while larger strides may indicate it was running or being chased.

2. Disturbances: Look for broken branches, disturbed vegetation, or trampled grass to determine an animal’s route. These signs can indicate where an animal has paused, changed direction, or encountered an obstacle.

3. Food Sources: Familiarize yourself with the plants and vegetation that serve as food sources for the animals you are tracking. By identifying browse lines or partially eaten plants, you can gain insights into an animal’s eating habits and preferred food sources.

4. Bedding Areas: Animals often have preferred bedding sites where they rest and sleep. Look for areas where grass or foliage is flattened or disturbed, indicating previous occupancy.

5. Scents and Sounds: Developing your senses is crucial when trailing animals. Pay attention to any unique scents or sounds that may suggest an animal’s presence. Experienced trackers may even be able to differentiate between different animal scents.

The Ethics of Wildlife Tracking

While tracking and trailing animals can be a fascinating and educational experience, it’s important to approach it ethically and responsibly. Here are a few guidelines to follow:

1. Respect Wildlife: Do not disturb or harass animals in their natural habitat. Keep a safe distance and observe from afar to avoid causing stress or interfering with their behavior.

2. Stick to Established Trails: When tracking animals, it’s essential to minimize your impact on the environment. Stick to established trails to avoid trampling vegetation or damaging fragile ecosystems.

3. Leave No Trace: Pack out what you pack in. Make sure to leave the environment as you found it, without leaving any waste or evidence of your presence.

4. Obtain Permits: In some areas, tracking and trailing wildlife may require permits or permissions. Make sure to research and comply with any regulations before venturing into protected areas.

Tracking and trailing techniques provide a window into the secret lives of wildlife. By learning to read animal signs in the wilderness, we can gain a deeper appreciation for the natural world and become better stewards of the environment. Whether you’re an avid hiker, a wildlife photographer, or a researcher, honing your tracking skills will undoubtedly enrich your outdoor experiences. So, the next time you find yourself exploring the wilderness, take a moment to slow down, look closely, and follow the clues left behind by the animals that call it home.
