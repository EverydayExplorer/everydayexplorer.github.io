Survival Skills: Building and Using Tourniquets and Splints


In a world where unforeseen accidents and emergencies can occur at any moment, having basic survival skills can make all the difference. One such skill that everyone should have in their arsenal is the ability to build and use tourniquets and splints. These simple yet effective tools can be lifesaving in situations where medical help is not immediately available. In this blog post, we will explore the importance of tourniquets and splints in survival situations, as well as how to build and use them effectively.

Tourniquets are devices used to control severe bleeding in a limb. When a major artery is damaged, blood loss can be rapid and life-threatening. By applying a tourniquet above the injury site, blood flow to the limb is restricted, effectively stopping the bleeding. It is crucial to understand that tourniquets should only be used as a last resort when direct pressure and elevation are not enough to control the bleeding. When applying a tourniquet, it is essential to place it at least two inches above the injury site, ensuring it is tight enough to stop the bleeding but not so tight that it causes further damage. Remember to always mark the time the tourniquet was applied, as prolonged use can lead to tissue damage and other complications.

Splints, on the other hand, are devices used to immobilize and support injured limbs. In survival situations, where medical help may be hours or even days away, splints can provide temporary stability, preventing further damage and reducing pain. Splints can be made from various materials, such as sticks, branches, or even clothing. The key is to create a rigid structure that supports the injured limb, preventing any movement that could exacerbate the injury. When applying a splint, it is crucial to immobilize both the joint above and below the injury site, ensuring that the limb remains stable.

Now that we understand the importance of tourniquets and splints let’s discuss how to build and use them effectively. When constructing a tourniquet, it is essential to use a wide and sturdy material that can withstand the pressure required to stop the bleeding. Some suitable materials include belts, ropes, or even strips of clothing. To apply the tourniquet, wrap it around the limb above the injury site, creating a loop. Then, using a stick or other rigid object, twist the loop until the bleeding stops. Secure the tourniquet in place and remember to seek medical help as soon as possible.

When it comes to splints, improvisation is key. Look for materials in your surroundings that can be used to create a supportive structure. Sticks, branches, or even rolled-up clothing can serve as excellent splinting materials. Carefully position the injured limb in a neutral position, ensuring that it remains straight and stable. Place the splint on both sides of the limb, securing it in place with bandages or any available fabric. By immobilizing the limb, you can prevent any further damage and provide some relief to the injured person.

In conclusion, learning survival skills such as building and using tourniquets and splints can be invaluable in emergency situations. These simple techniques can help control severe bleeding and provide temporary stability to injured limbs until professional medical help can be reached. Remember, practice these skills in a controlled environment before you find yourself in a real-life emergency. By being prepared and equipped with these survival skills, you can increase your chances of successfully managing injuries and potentially saving lives. Stay safe and be prepared!
