The Science of Sneezing: The Physiology Behind the Achoo!


Sneezing is a natural bodily function that often catches us by surprise. The sudden, explosive release of air through the nose and mouth can be attributed to various triggers, from allergies to illness. However, have you ever wondered about the science behind this involuntary action? In this comprehensive blog post, we will delve into the fascinating physiology behind sneezing, uncovering the intricate mechanisms that govern this seemingly simple bodily reflex.

Anatomy of a Sneeze

The act of sneezing, or sternutation, begins with a stimulus that activates the trigeminal nerve in the nasal cavity. This nerve sends signals to the brainstem, which then initiates a series of physiological responses. The first stage involves a deep inhalation, during which the chest expands and the glottis (the opening between the vocal cords) closes. As the lungs fill with air, pressure builds up in the chest.

The Trigger: Allergens, Irritants, and More

Sneezing can be triggered by a variety of factors, including allergens such as pollen, dust, pet dander, and mold. When these substances enter the nasal passages, they can irritate the sensitive mucous membranes, prompting the body to initiate a sneeze in an attempt to expel the intruders. Additionally, viral or bacterial infections can also stimulate the sneeze reflex as the body works to rid itself of harmful pathogens.

The Build-Up and Release

Once the pressure in the chest reaches a certain point, the glottis suddenly opens and the breath, along with the irritants or allergens, is forcefully expelled at speeds of up to 100 miles per hour. This forceful expulsion is what we perceive as a sneeze, and it serves the crucial function of clearing the nasal passages and protecting the respiratory system from potentially harmful substances.

The Contagious Nature of Sneezing

It’s no secret that sneezing is highly contagious, and the reason behind this lies in the brain’s response to social cues. Research has shown that the sound of a sneeze triggers an automatic response in others, causing them to mimic the action. This phenomenon, known as ‘reflexive contagion,’ is a testament to the power of social influence on our physiological responses.

The Aftermath: Post-Sneeze Sensations

After a sneeze, many people experience a sense of relief, as the nasal passages are cleared and the irritants are expelled. This feeling of relief is accompanied by a momentary pause in breathing, known as the ‘sneeze reflex pause.’ During this brief interval, the heart rate and blood pressure may temporarily decrease, contributing to the overall sensation of release.

The Myth of Sneeze Suppression

While it may be tempting to suppress a sneeze, doing so can actually be harmful. The pressure that builds up in the chest during a suppressed sneeze can lead to various complications, such as ruptured blood vessels in the eyes, ears, or nose, as well as potential damage to the eardrums. Therefore, it’s best to allow a sneeze to occur naturally, as it is the body’s way of protecting itself from potential harm.

In Conclusion

Sneezing is a remarkable physiological process that plays a crucial role in maintaining the health and well-being of the respiratory system. From its intricate neural pathways to the powerful expulsion of air and irritants, the science of sneezing offers a fascinating glimpse into the inner workings of the human body. By understanding the physiology behind the ‘achoo,’ we can develop a deeper appreciation for this common yet extraordinary bodily function.
