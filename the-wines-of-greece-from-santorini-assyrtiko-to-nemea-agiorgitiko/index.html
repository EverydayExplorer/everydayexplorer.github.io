The Wines of Greece: From Santorini Assyrtiko to Nemea Agiorgitiko


Greece is not only known for its stunning architecture, beautiful islands, and rich history. It is also gaining recognition for its exceptional wines. From the ancient times, the Greeks have been growing grapes and producing wine, making it one of the oldest wine-producing regions in the world. Today, Greek wines are making a comeback, delighting wine enthusiasts with their unique characteristics and distinct flavors. In this blog post, we will explore the wines of Greece, from the famous Santorini Assyrtiko to the bold Nemea Agiorgitiko.

Santorini, a picturesque island in the Aegean Sea, is famous for producing one of Greece’s most celebrated white wines - Assyrtiko. The volcanic soil of the island, combined with the hot and dry climate, creates the perfect conditions for growing grapes with exceptional acidity and mineral character. Assyrtiko wines from Santorini are known for their crispness, citrus flavors, and distinct saline notes. These wines pair beautifully with seafood, as well as Mediterranean dishes like grilled octopus and feta cheese.

Moving on to the red wines of Greece, Nemea in the Peloponnese region is renowned for its Agiorgitiko grape variety. Often referred to as the ‘blood of Hercules,’ Agiorgitiko produces bold and complex red wines that can rival some of the best international varietals. Nemea’s warm climate and well-drained clay soils create the perfect conditions for the grape to thrive, resulting in wines with rich black fruit flavors, velvety tannins, and a touch of spice. Agiorgitiko wines pair wonderfully with grilled meats, hearty stews, and strong cheeses.

Another grape variety worth mentioning is Xinomavro, which is primarily grown in the wine regions of Naoussa and Amyndeon. Often compared to Italy’s Nebbiolo, Xinomavro produces wines with high acidity, firm tannins, and intense flavors of red fruits, spices, and earth. These wines are known for their aging potential and can develop complex flavors and aromas over time. Xinomavro pairs well with roasted meats, game dishes, and aged cheeses.

Besides Assyrtiko, Agiorgitiko, and Xinomavro, Greece boasts a wide variety of indigenous grape varieties. Moschofilero, grown in the high-altitude vineyards of Mantinia, is a fragrant white wine with floral aromas, crisp acidity, and flavors of citrus and stone fruits. Mavrodaphne, primarily grown in the Patras region, produces dark, sweet, and fortified dessert wines with flavors of dried fruits and spice.

While Greek wines may be lesser-known globally compared to their European counterparts, they are gaining popularity for their exceptional quality, unique flavors, and excellent value. Greek winemakers have been investing in modern winemaking techniques, cultivating ancient vineyards, and experimenting with different winemaking styles. As a result, the reputation of Greek wines is on the rise, and they are regularly receiving international accolades.

In recent years, Greek wines have gained recognition in the export market, with wineries successfully exporting to various countries, especially the United States, Canada, and the United Kingdom. This growth in exports can partially be attributed to the wine industry’s efforts to improve quality and appeal to international tastes. Additionally, Greek wine tourism is booming, with visitors from around the world flocking to the country’s stunning vineyards, participating in wine tastings, and learning about the fascinating history and culture of winemaking in Greece.

In conclusion, the wines of Greece offer a delightful and diverse range of options for wine lovers to explore and enjoy. From the crisp and mineral-driven Assyrtiko of Santorini to the bold and complex Agiorgitiko of Nemea, Greece’s wines encapsulate the country’s rich history, unique terroir, and vibrant culture. Whether you are a seasoned wine enthusiast or a curious beginner, be sure to seek out these exceptional Greek wines and experience the taste of Greece in every sip.
