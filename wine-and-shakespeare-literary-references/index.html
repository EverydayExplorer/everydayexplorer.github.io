Wine and Shakespeare: Literary References


In the world of literature, few names hold as much significance and reverence as William Shakespeare. His plays and sonnets continue to captivate audiences centuries after they were written. Shakespeare’s works are renowned for their timeless themes, memorable characters, and beautiful language. But did you know that wine plays a significant role in many of his works? Indeed, Shakespeare frequently used wine as a symbol, a narrative device, and a metaphor to explore human emotions, social hierarchies, and the human condition itself.

One of the most iconic references to wine in Shakespeare’s plays can be found in ‘Macbeth.’ In Act II, Scene II, Lady Macbeth famously declares, ‘That which hath made them drunk hath made me bold.’ She uses the metaphor of alcohol to describe the courage she derives from the murder of King Duncan. This line not only showcases Lady Macbeth’s psychological state but also highlights the transformative power of wine. It demonstrates how alcohol can affect one’s behavior and alter perceptions.

In ‘Othello,’ Shakespeare once again employs wine as a symbol of manipulation and deceit. Iago, the play’s villain, strategically utilizes Cassio’s vulnerability to alcohol to further his malevolent plans. By getting Cassio drunk, Iago creates a situation where Cassio loses control and engages in a brawl. This incident leads to his dismissal from military service and sets in motion the tragic events of the play. Shakespeare cleverly uses wine to depict how seemingly harmless indulgence can have devastating consequences.

Another play where wine holds great significance is ‘Romeo and Juliet.’ In Act II, Scene II, Romeo compares Juliet to a sparkling wine when he exclaims, ‘It is the east, and Juliet is the sun!’ This metaphor emphasizes Juliet’s beauty and radiance, comparing her to a heavenly body and highlighting her role as Romeo’s guiding light. The association of wine with love and passion underscores the intensity of their relationship.

Furthermore, Shakespeare draws on the social aspects of wine in his comedies. In ‘Twelfth Night,’ for instance, Sir Toby Belch and Sir Andrew Aguecheek engage in raucous drinking sessions. These scenes serve as comic relief and spark humorous situations. The use of wine in this context satirizes the excessive behavior and revelry that can occur when people consume alcohol in a social setting.

Beyond the plays themselves, Shakespeare’s sonnets also contain references to wine. Sonnet 73, for example, compares the aging process to the waning of a day and the depletion of wine from a goblet. This analogy emphasizes the transient nature of life and the inevitability of mortality. Wine serves as a reminder of the passing of time, enriching the sonnet’s poignant message.

The intertwining of wine and Shakespeare’s works is emblematic of the human experience. Wine, like literature, has the power to evoke emotions, transport us to different worlds, and enhance our understanding of ourselves and others. It acts as a conduit for expressing joy, sorrow, love, and longing – all themes that Shakespeare masterfully explored.

From Lady Macbeth’s boldness fueled by wine to Romeo’s declaration of love for Juliet, Shakespeare’s references to wine are both captivating and thought-provoking. They remind us of the multi-faceted nature of this beloved beverage and its ability to reflect the complexities of human existence.

In conclusion, the literary references to wine in Shakespeare’s works showcase his mastery of language and his understanding of the human condition. Wine serves as a powerful metaphor, symbol, and narrative tool, enabling Shakespeare to convey profound emotions and ideas to his audience. It adds depth, richness, and nuance to his already extraordinary body of work. So, the next time you read or watch a Shakespearean play, pay attention to the presence of wine, and you’ll discover a world of hidden meanings and unforgettable moments.
