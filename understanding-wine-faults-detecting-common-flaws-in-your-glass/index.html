Understanding Wine Faults: Detecting Common Flaws in Your Glass


As wine enthusiasts, we all strive to enjoy the perfect glass of wine. Whether it’s a crisp white or a full-bodied red, the aromas, flavors, and textures of wine can transport us to different worlds. However, sometimes we encounter a glass that doesn’t quite meet our expectations. This could be due to a fault in the wine, which can greatly impact our enjoyment. In this blog, we will explore some common wine faults and how to detect them, helping you become a more discerning wine connoisseur.

1. Cork Taint

One of the most well-known wine faults is cork taint, also known as ‘corked’ wine. This occurs when a chemical compound called TCA (2,4,6-trichloroanisole) contaminates the cork and subsequently the wine. The result is an unpleasant musty odor and a muted flavor profile. If you detect a damp basement or wet cardboard smell in your wine, chances are it is corked.

2. Oxidation

Oxidation is another common wine fault that can occur when a wine is exposed to too much oxygen. This can happen if the wine bottle is not properly sealed or stored. Oxidized wine often has a brownish color, a flat taste, and a smell reminiscent of vinegar or sherry. Pay attention to the wine’s color and aroma to identify if oxidation has occurred.

3. Volatile Acidity

Volatile acidity refers to the presence of acetic acid in wine, which is responsible for the vinegary smell and taste. While a small amount of volatile acidity can contribute to a wine’s complexity, excessive amounts can be a fault. If your wine smells like nail polish remover or vinegar, it may be a sign of volatile acidity.

4. Reduction

On the opposite end of the spectrum, reduction occurs when wine lacks exposure to enough oxygen. This can result in a wine that smells like rotten eggs, burnt rubber, or even sewage. Reduction can be remedied by allowing the wine to breathe or decanting it before consumption.

5. Sulfur Compounds

Sulfur compounds are often used in winemaking as a preservative, but excessive use can result in unpleasant smells and tastes. A wine with too much sulfur can have aromas resembling burnt matches or rotten eggs. These compounds can dissipate with time, so giving the wine a chance to breathe or aerate can help mitigate the fault.

6. Heat Damage

When wine is exposed to high temperatures, it can become ‘cooked,’ resulting in a loss of freshness and vibrancy. Heat-damaged wine may taste flat and lack complexity. Proper storage and transportation are crucial in avoiding this fault.

7. Bacterial Spoilage

Bacterial spoilage can occur when specific bacteria, such as Brettanomyces, infect the wine. This can result in off-putting aromas like barnyard, horse sweat, or even band-aids. While some people enjoy a touch of these aromas, excessive bacterial spoilage can be considered a fault.

By familiarizing yourself with these common wine faults, you’ll be better equipped to identify them when they arise. Remember, wine faults are not a reflection of your taste or knowledge; they are simply part of the complex world of wine. Learning to detect these flaws will help you appreciate the truly exceptional wines that come your way and avoid disappointment.

Next time you pour yourself a glass, take a moment to evaluate its aroma, color, and taste. By honing your senses and being aware of wine faults, you’ll elevate your wine drinking experience and become a more discerning wine lover. Cheers to exploring the fascinating world of wine!
