The Renaissance of Vermouth: From Forgotten Mixer to Craft Darling


Vermouth, once a forgotten mixer in the world of cocktails, has experienced a renaissance in recent years. This versatile and aromatic fortified wine has seen a revival in popularity, taking center stage in craft cocktail bars and home mixology enthusiasts’ collections.

## A Brief History

Vermouth has a rich history dating back to ancient times. Its origins can be traced to the medicinal wines of ancient Greece, where herbs and spices were added to wine for their perceived health benefits. The word ‘vermouth’ itself is derived from the German word ‘wermut,’ meaning wormwood, a key ingredient in many classic vermouth recipes.

However, it wasn’t until the 18th century that vermouth as we know it today started to take shape. In Turin, Italy, Antonio Benedetto Carpano created the first commercial recipe for vermouth in 1786, blending wormwood, herbs, and spices with fortified wine. This laid the foundation for the development of various styles of vermouth, including the sweet and dry variants that we are familiar with today.

## The Decline and Rebirth

Despite its early success, vermouth experienced a decline in popularity over the years. The rise of other spirits and cocktails, such as gin and vodka, in the mid-20th century overshadowed vermouth’s role as a core ingredient in classic cocktails like the Martini and the Manhattan. Consequently, it became largely relegated to the back of the liquor cabinet, collecting dust alongside other forgotten mixers.

However, the craft cocktail revival of the past decade brought vermouth back into the spotlight. Bartenders and mixologists sought to revive classic recipes and introduce new creative concoctions, putting vermouth front and center. As consumers developed a taste for unique and complex flavors, the demand for high-quality vermouth skyrocketed.

## The Rise of Craft Vermouth

Craft distilleries and winemakers recognized the growing demand for vermouth and seized the opportunity to create their own artisanal expressions of this venerable spirit. They experimented with unique botanical blends, aging techniques, and production methods to create vermouths that showcased the depth and complexity of flavors.

Craft vermouths are now made with a range of wine bases—white, red, and rosé—enabling producers to explore different flavor profiles. While classic recipes use neutral spirits for fortification, some craft producers have started using brandy or even gin distillates to add depth and character to their vermouths.

The botanicals used in craft vermouth production have become a point of pride for many producers. Traditional ingredients, such as wormwood, gentian, and citrus peel, are combined with a variety of herbs, spices, and fruits to create unique flavor profiles. Craft producers often source locally grown or wild-harvested botanicals, further adding to the distinction and character of their vermouths.

## The Versatility of Vermouth

One of the reasons for vermouth’s newfound popularity is its versatility in mixology. While it is a key component in classic cocktails like the Negroni and the Martinez, it can also be enjoyed on its own. The trend of serving vermouth over ice, sometimes with a splash of soda water and a twist of citrus, has gained traction, allowing vermouth to be appreciated as a standalone aperitif.

Additionally, vermouth variants, such as bianco (white) and rosso (red) vermouth, offer different flavor profiles and pairings. Bianco vermouth tends to be sweeter and pairs well with citrusy and herbal flavors, while rosso vermouth is typically drier and pairs well with richer and spicier flavors.

## Incorporating Vermouth into Everyday Life

Vermouth’s revival goes beyond the world of cocktails. Chefs and home cooks have also started incorporating vermouth into their culinary creations, adding depth and complexity to dishes. From deglazing pans to braising meats, vermouth’s herbal and aromatic qualities lend a distinctive touch to a wide range of recipes.

Beyond the kitchen, vermouth tastings and vermouth-focused events have become increasingly popular. Inspired by the resurgence of craft beer and artisanal spirits, vermouth enthusiasts gather to sample different vermouth styles, learn about the production process, and appreciate the craftsmanship behind this revitalized spirit.

## Conclusion

The revival of vermouth in recent years has been nothing short of remarkable. From being a forgotten mixer to becoming a craft darling, vermouth has experienced a resurgence in popularity thanks to the creativity of bartenders, the ingenuity of craft producers, and the evolving tastes of consumers.

As we continue to embrace the world of craft cocktails and celebrate the rich history of spirits and mixers, let us raise a glass to vermouth—a true testament to the power of reinvention and the enduring appeal of classic flavors.
