The Myth of Atlantis in Sci-Fi and Fantasy


In the vast realm of science fiction and fantasy, there is one enduring myth that continues to captivate the imagination of both writers and readers alike - the myth of Atlantis. This legendary lost city, submerged beneath the depths of the ocean, has been a source of inspiration for countless stories, novels, and movies in the genre. Its allure lies in the tantalizing possibility of an advanced civilization that mysteriously vanished, leaving behind only fragments of its existence.

The myth of Atlantis can be traced back to the ancient Greek philosopher Plato, who described the city in his dialogues ‘Timaeus’ and ‘Critias.’ According to Plato’s account, Atlantis was a powerful and prosperous city-state located beyond the Pillars of Hercules, which were thought to be the modern-day Strait of Gibraltar. He portrayed Atlantis as an ideal society with advanced technology and a highly organized political structure.

However, the Atlantis myth really took off in the 19th century, thanks to the works of authors like Jules Verne and H.P. Lovecraft. Verne’s novel ‘Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea’ introduced the concept of a hidden underwater civilization, which many believe was inspired by the legend of Atlantis. Lovecraft, on the other hand, incorporated elements of the myth into his Cthulhu Mythos, creating a dark, cosmic horror around the lost city.

Since then, Atlantis has become a staple in science fiction and fantasy literature. In the works of authors like Edgar Rice Burroughs and Arthur C. Clarke, Atlantis became a gateway to other worlds or dimensions. It represented a place of wonder, where ancient technologies and mystical forces could be harnessed. This notion of Atlantis as a source of hidden knowledge and power has influenced countless stories in the genre.

One of the most famous examples of Atlantis in science fiction is the city of Rapture from the video game ‘BioShock.’ Set in an alternate version of the 1960s, Rapture is a sprawling underwater metropolis built by a visionary industrialist. The game explores themes of unchecked ambition, societal collapse, and the consequences of tampering with forces beyond our control. Rapture serves as a cautionary tale of the dangers of pursuing an Atlantis-like utopia.

In recent years, the myth of Atlantis has also found its way into the realm of superhero stories. In the Marvel Universe, the character of Namor the Sub-Mariner hails from Atlantis. He is depicted as a conflicted anti-hero torn between his loyalty to his underwater kingdom and his alliances with surface-dwelling superheroes. The inclusion of Atlantis in the Marvel Universe adds depth and complexity to Namor’s character, as well as providing a fantastical setting for epic conflicts.

The enduring popularity of Atlantis in science fiction and fantasy is a testament to the myth’s timeless allure. It represents a world of possibilities, where ancient mysteries collide with futuristic technologies. The concept of a lost civilization holds a universal fascination, as it taps into our deep-seated desire for discovery and adventure.

From its origins in Plato’s works to its modern interpretations in literature, movies, and video games, Atlantis continues to captivate us with its promise of hidden knowledge and untold wonders. It serves as a reminder that even in the realm of speculative fiction, there are still mysteries waiting to be unraveled.

So, the next time you dive into a science fiction or fantasy story, keep an eye out for the myth of Atlantis. You never know what secrets and wonders lie beneath the surface, just waiting to be explored.
