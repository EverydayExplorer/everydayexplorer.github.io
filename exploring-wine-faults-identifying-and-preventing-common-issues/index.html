Exploring Wine Faults: Identifying and Preventing Common Issues


Wine, often considered a complex and sophisticated beverage, has been enjoyed by connoisseurs and enthusiasts for centuries. It has a rich history, a multitude of flavors, and the ability to complement a wide range of cuisines. However, like any other agricultural product, wine is not immune to imperfections. In this blog, we will delve into the fascinating world of wine faults, understanding what they are, how to identify them, and, most importantly, how to prevent them from occurring.

What are Wine Faults?

Wine faults, as the name suggests, are undesirable characteristics or defects in wine that can impact its taste, aroma, appearance, and overall quality. These faults can occur at various stages of winemaking, from grape cultivation and harvesting to fermentation, aging, and bottling. While some wine faults may be subtle and hardly noticeable, others can render a wine undrinkable.

Common Wine Faults

  1. Cork Taint (TCA): One of the most well-known wine faults, cork taint is caused by a compound called 2,4,6-trichloroanisole (TCA). It can impart musty, moldy, or damp cardboard-like aromas to the wine. This fault is typically associated with natural cork closures, although it can also occur in synthetic corks and screw caps.

  2. Oxidation: Oxygen is a wine’s enemy once it’s in the bottle. Oxidation occurs when wine is exposed to too much air, leading to a loss of freshness and fruity aromas. Signs of oxidation include a brownish color, a sherry-like aroma, and a flat taste.

  3. Reduction: On the flip side of oxidation, reduction occurs when wine is deprived of oxygen. This can lead to off-putting sulfurous or rotten egg-like odors. To prevent reduction, winemakers need to carefully manage sulfur dioxide levels during production.

  4. Brettanomyces (Brett): Brettanomyces is a yeast strain that can introduce barnyard, sweaty saddle, or band-aid-like aromas into wine. It’s a persistent fault that can be difficult to eliminate once it takes hold in a winery.

  5. Volatility: Volatile acidity (VA) results from the presence of acetic acid in wine, giving it a vinegary smell and a sharp, unpleasant taste. Small amounts of VA are acceptable in some wines, but excessive levels can be a fault.

  6. Sulfur Compounds: Hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and mercaptans are sulfur compounds that can create unpleasant odors reminiscent of rotten eggs, garlic, or burnt rubber.

Identifying Wine Faults

Identifying wine faults can be a challenging task, even for experienced tasters. It often requires a keen sense of smell and taste, as well as knowledge of what specific faults smell and taste like. Here are some tips for identifying common wine faults:

  1. Cork Taint (TCA): The musty, wet cardboard aroma is a dead giveaway. If you encounter this smell in a wine, it’s likely corked.

  2. Oxidation: Look for color changes, particularly in white wines turning brownish. An overly flat or sherry-like aroma and taste are also indicative of oxidation.

  3. Reduction: Rotten egg or sulfurous odors are key indicators of reduction. Swirling the wine in the glass can sometimes help dissipate these odors.

  4. Brettanomyces (Brett): Barnyard, sweaty, or medicinal notes in wine may suggest the presence of Brettanomyces.

  5. Volatility: A vinegary aroma and a sharp, acidic taste are signs of excessive volatile acidity.

  6. Sulfur Compounds: Rotten egg, garlic, or burnt rubber odors can signal the presence of sulfur compounds.

Preventing Wine Faults

Preventing wine faults is crucial for winemakers and wine producers who aim to deliver high-quality products consistently. Here are some strategies to prevent common wine faults:

  1. Quality Control: Start with high-quality grapes and maintain strict quality control throughout the winemaking process. Healthy grapes are less likely to lead to faulty wine.

  2. Sanitation: Ensure that all winemaking equipment is thoroughly cleaned and sanitized to prevent microbial contamination.

  3. Proper Storage: Store wine in a cool, dark, and stable environment to minimize the risk of oxidation and other faults caused by temperature fluctuations and light exposure.

  4. Sulfur Dioxide Management: Use sulfur dioxide (SO2) judiciously as an antioxidant and preservative but avoid overuse, which can lead to sulfur-related faults.

  5. Closure Selection: Choose closures, such as screw caps or synthetic corks, that minimize the risk of cork taint.

  6. Yeast and Microbial Control: Monitor and control the yeast strains and microorganisms used in fermentation to prevent unwanted microbial activity.

  7. Hygiene and Cleanliness: Maintain strict hygiene standards in the winery to prevent contamination by spoilage organisms.
