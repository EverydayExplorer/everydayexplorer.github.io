The Impact of Malolactic Fermentation: Transforming Tartness to Creaminess


Malolactic fermentation, also known as MLF, is a secondary fermentation process that occurs in some wines. It is a transformational process that has a significant impact on the sensory characteristics and overall quality of wine. In this blog post, we will explore the impact of malolactic fermentation, specifically how it transforms tartness to creaminess in wine.

But first, let’s understand what malolactic fermentation is. It is a process where tart-tasting malic acid is converted into softer-tasting lactic acid. This fermentation is carried out by lactic acid bacteria, mainly Oenococcus oeni. These bacteria consume the malic acid present in the wine and produce lactic acid as a byproduct, along with carbon dioxide.

So, what does malolactic fermentation do to the wine? The transformation of malic acid to lactic acid brings about several changes in the wine’s sensory profile. One of the key changes is the reduction in perceived acidity. The tartness associated with high levels of malic acid is softened, making the wine more palatable and less sharp on the palate.

Additionally, malolactic fermentation can contribute to a creamy or buttery texture in the wine. This is due to the production of diacetyl, a compound that gives the wine a buttery aroma and flavor. The presence of diacetyl is often associated with wines that have undergone malolactic fermentation, particularly those made with certain grape varieties, such as Chardonnay.

The transformational impact of malolactic fermentation extends beyond acidity and texture. It can also influence the wine’s aromatics. During the fermentation process, the lactic acid bacteria release volatile aroma compounds, which can enhance the wine’s bouquet. These compounds can add complexity and depth to the wine’s aroma, making it more enticing to the senses.

Moreover, malolactic fermentation can play a role in the stability and aging potential of wine. By reducing the levels of malic acid, which is more prone to microbial spoilage than lactic acid, the fermentation process helps to protect the wine from potential microbial contamination. This contributes to the wine’s stability and longevity, allowing it to age gracefully.

Now that we understand the impact of malolactic fermentation on wine, let’s delve into how winemakers facilitate and control this process. Malolactic fermentation can occur spontaneously, meaning that the lactic acid bacteria present in the wine can initiate the fermentation on their own. However, winemakers often choose to inoculate the wine with specific lactic acid bacteria strains to ensure a controlled and predictable fermentation process.

The timing of malolactic fermentation is another crucial consideration for winemakers. It can either be conducted shortly after primary fermentation or delayed until after the wine has undergone aging in oak barrels. The choice depends on the desired style of the wine and the winemaker’s preference.

In terms of winemaking regions, malolactic fermentation is widely practiced in cool-climate regions, where high levels of malic acid are common in grapes. The process is particularly prevalent in the production of Chardonnay, as it helps to balance the varietal’s naturally high acidity and enhance its creamy, buttery characteristics.

In conclusion, malolactic fermentation is a transformative process that significantly impacts the sensory profile and quality of wine. By converting tart malic acid into softer lactic acid, this fermentation process reduces acidity, adds a creamy texture, enhances aromatics, and contributes to the wine’s stability and aging potential. Winemakers play a pivotal role in facilitating and controlling this fermentation process to achieve the desired style of wine. So, the next time you enjoy a glass of Chardonnay or any wine that boasts creaminess and complexity, remember the impact of malolactic fermentation. Cheers!
