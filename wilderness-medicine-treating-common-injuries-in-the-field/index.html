Wilderness Medicine: Treating Common Injuries in the Field


When venturing into the great outdoors, it’s essential to be prepared for the unexpected. While immersing oneself in nature can be a rejuvenating and soul-enriching experience, accidents and injuries can still occur with little warning. In a remote wilderness setting, access to immediate medical attention may be limited, which is why having a basic understanding of wilderness medicine becomes crucial.

Wilderness medicine, also known as expedition medicine, is a specialized field of medicine that focuses on providing medical care in austere and remote environments. It encompasses a broad range of medical knowledge and skills to assess, stabilize, and treat injuries and illnesses that may occur in the backcountry. In this blog post, we will explore some of the most common injuries encountered in the field and discuss how to provide effective treatment in wilderness settings.

1. Sprains and Strains:
One of the most prevalent injuries in the wilderness is sprains and strains, usually caused by slips, falls, or strenuous activities. When treating sprains or strains, the primary approach is the RICE method - Rest, Ice, Compression, and Elevation. Resting the injured limb, applying ice to reduce swelling, using compression bandages, and elevating the limb can help alleviate pain and promote healing. It’s also important to immobilize the injury by using a splint or wrap to prevent further damage.

2. Cuts and Abrasions:
Open wounds are another common occurrence in the wilderness, especially when dealing with sharp objects, rough terrains, or encounters with wildlife. To clean a wound in the field, start by irrigating it with clean water to remove any debris or dirt. Use antiseptic wipes or betadine solution to prevent infection. Apply a sterile dressing or bandage to keep the wound clean and protected. For deeper wounds that may require stitches, it’s crucial to carry a first aid kit with sterile sutures or adhesive wound closure strips.

3. Burns:
Accidental burns can happen when camping, cooking over a campfire, or using portable stoves. For minor burns, immediately cool the affected area with cold water to dissipate the heat and alleviate pain. Applying a sterile non-stick burn dressing can protect the burn from infection and further damage. Aloe vera gel or a burn ointment can also offer soothing relief. If the burn is severe, it may be necessary to evacuate the injured person to seek advanced medical help.

4. Fractures and Dislocations:
Broken bones and joint dislocations can occur during falls, accidents, or while engaging in high-intensity activities. In the wilderness, it’s important to immobilize the injured limb or joint to prevent further damage and reduce pain. Improvised splints and slings made from available materials can be used to provide support and stability until professional medical help can be reached. Padding the splint with clothing or soft materials can help cushion the injured area and relieve discomfort.

5. Hypothermia and Heat-related Illnesses:
Exposure to extreme temperatures can lead to dangerous conditions such as hypothermia or heat exhaustion. When treating hypothermia, it’s crucial to move the affected person to a warmer environment and remove wet clothing. Gradually warm them using blankets or warm fluids, focusing on the core of the body. In cases of heat-related illnesses, move the person to a cooler place, remove excess clothing, and provide fluids and electrolyte-rich drinks to prevent dehydration.

In conclusion, when venturing into the wilderness, it’s essential to equip oneself with basic knowledge and skills in wilderness medicine. Understanding how to treat common injuries that can occur in remote locations can make a significant difference in the outcome of an emergency situation. Relating to sprains and strains, cuts and abrasions, burns, fractures and dislocations, and hypothermia or heat-related illnesses requires carrying essential first aid supplies and practicing preparedness. By doing so, adventurers can fully enjoy the beauty of nature while also safeguarding their well-being in the face of unexpected circumstances.
