Wilderness Survival Skills for Families: Teaching Children to Thrive in Nature


As families increasingly seek ways to disconnect from screens and reconnect with nature, the importance of wilderness survival skills for children becomes more evident. In an age dominated by technology, ensuring that our children have the knowledge and abilities to thrive in the great outdoors is crucial not only for their physical well-being but also for their mental and emotional development. In this comprehensive guide, we’ll explore the essential wilderness survival skills that families can teach their children, transforming outings into opportunities for learning, bonding, and adventure.

  1. Navigation and Orienteering: One of the fundamental skills for wilderness survival is the ability to navigate through unknown terrain. Teach your children how to read a map, use a compass, and identify landmarks. Encourage them to pay attention to their surroundings and develop a sense of direction. Practicing these skills on hikes or nature walks will not only build confidence but also instill a deeper appreciation for the natural world.

  2. Shelter Building: Knowing how to construct a shelter is essential for staying safe and comfortable in the wilderness. Teach your children different shelter-building techniques using natural materials such as branches, leaves, and moss. Start with simple structures like lean-tos and progress to more advanced designs as their skills improve. Building shelters can be a fun and creative activity for the whole family, fostering teamwork and problem-solving abilities.

  3. Fire Starting: Fire provides warmth, light, and the means to cook food in the wilderness. Teach your children how to safely build and maintain a fire using various methods such as friction, flint and steel, or a magnifying glass. Emphasize the importance of fire safety and responsible stewardship of the environment. Practice fire-starting skills in different weather conditions to prepare for real-life scenarios.

  4. Water Procurement and Purification: In a survival situation, access to clean water is paramount. Teach your children how to find water sources in the wilderness and methods for purifying water to make it safe for drinking. Demonstrate techniques such as boiling, using water purification tablets, or constructing a simple filtration system using natural materials. Encourage them to stay hydrated and to prioritize water conservation.

  5. Wilderness First Aid: Accidents and injuries can happen in any outdoor setting, so it’s essential to equip your children with basic first aid skills. Teach them how to assess and treat common injuries such as cuts, sprains, and insect bites. Consider enrolling in a wilderness first aid course as a family to learn more advanced techniques and gain confidence in handling emergencies.

  6. Foraging and Wild Edibles: Teach your children how to identify edible plants and berries in the wild, as well as how to distinguish them from toxic species. Start with a few easy-to-recognize plants like dandelions, blackberries, and wild strawberries, and gradually expand their knowledge as they become more proficient. Foraging for wild edibles not only provides a valuable survival skill but also connects children to the natural abundance of the environment.

  7. Wildlife Awareness and Safety: Encourage your children to develop a respect for wildlife and to learn how to coexist safely with animals in their natural habitats. Teach them how to recognize signs of potentially dangerous wildlife and what to do in encounters with animals such as bears, snakes, or insects. Emphasize the importance of maintaining a safe distance and avoiding confrontations whenever possible.

By equipping your children with these essential wilderness survival skills, you empower them to explore and enjoy the natural world with confidence and resilience. Beyond the practical benefits, teaching children to thrive in nature fosters a sense of stewardship and reverence for the environment, instilling values that will serve them well throughout their lives. So, pack your bags, lace up your boots, and embark on adventures together as a family, knowing that you’re not just surviving in the wilderness – you’re thriving.
