The history of music in commercials


Music has always played a significant role in commercials. It has the power to evoke emotions, create connections, and leave a lasting impact on viewers. From catchy jingles to famous songs, the history of music in commercials is a fascinating journey that mirrors the evolution of advertising and popular culture.

In the early days of commercials, music was often used in the form of jingles. These short, catchy tunes were specifically composed to promote a product or brand. They usually featured simple lyrics and a memorable melody that would stick in people’s minds. These jingles became an essential part of advertising, helping to build brand awareness and foster a sense of familiarity among consumers.

One of the earliest examples of music in commercials dates back to the 1920s when radio advertisements became popular. Back then, advertisers realized the power of music to draw attention, so they began using jingles and catchy tunes in their radio spots. These jingles were often performed by talented singers or vocal groups, adding another layer of entertainment to the commercial experience.

As television became the dominant medium for advertising in the 1950s, music in commercials evolved to accommodate the new visual format. Advertisers started incorporating popular songs from well-known artists into their commercials. By using familiar tunes, they aimed to tap into the emotional connection people had with the music, hoping it would translate into a positive perception of the product being promoted.

In the 1960s and 1970s, the use of music in commercials became more diverse. Advertisers started experimenting with different genres and styles to target specific demographics. Rock music, for example, was frequently used to appeal to younger audiences, while softer melodies and ballads were chosen to create a more sentimental atmosphere. This era witnessed iconic commercials featuring songs like Coca-Cola’s “I’d Like to Buy the World a Coke” and the unforgettable jingle for McDonald’s – “Two all-beef patties, special sauce, lettuce, cheese, pickles, onions on a sesame seed bun.”

The 1980s and 1990s marked a significant shift in the use of music in commercials. Advertisers began licensing hit songs from popular artists to enhance their commercials’ appeal. This approach allowed companies to tap into the existing emotional connection people had with the songs, instantly evoking nostalgia or positive associations. Some examples from this era include the use of The Beatles’ “Revolution” in a Nike commercial or the famous Levi’s jeans commercial featuring the song “Inside” by Stiltskin.

With the rise of the internet and digital platforms in the 2000s, music in commercials took on a new dimension. Advertisers not only included popular songs but also utilized independent artists’ music to create a unique and authentic feel. This approach aligned with the shift towards more personalized and targeted advertising, as well as the growing awareness of supporting emerging artists. Companies like Apple used this strategy effectively, featuring up-and-coming artists like Feist and Jet in their iPod commercials.

Today, music in commercials has become more diverse and versatile than ever. Advertisers incorporate music from various genres and styles to cater to specific demographics and evoke desired emotions. The emergence of streaming platforms and social media has given brands more opportunities to connect with consumers through music, using platforms like TikTok and YouTube to launch viral campaigns.

In conclusion, the history of music in commercials is a testament to the power of music as a tool in advertising. From jingles in radio spots to hit songs licensed for TV commercials, music has played a crucial role in shaping the advertising landscape. As technology and consumer behavior continue to evolve, we can expect music in commercials to adapt and innovate, offering new ways for brands to engage with their audiences.
