Wilderness Medicine: Basic First Aid for Survivalists


In the vast and untamed wilderness, accidents and emergencies can happen. Whether you find yourself deep in the mountains, exploring dense forests, or trekking through remote deserts, being equipped with basic first aid knowledge is crucial for survivalists.

Wilderness medicine focuses on providing medical care in outdoor environments where resources may be limited, and help may be far away. In this blog post, we will delve into the essentials of wilderness medicine, particularly basic first aid techniques that every survivalist should know.

Assessing the Situation

When an emergency strikes in the wilderness, the first step is to assess the situation. Is the scene safe? Are there any hazards that could pose a threat to you or the injured person? Take a moment to evaluate the surroundings before proceeding further.

Prioritizing Care

In wilderness medicine, it’s important to prioritize care based on the severity of injuries. Follow the ABCDE approach:

  1. Airway: Ensure the injured person’s airway is clear and open. If there are any obstructions, such as vomit or foreign objects, they need to be removed.

  2. Breathing: Check for normal breathing. If the person is not breathing, provide rescue breaths by performing mouth-to-mouth or mouth-to-nose resuscitation.

  3. Circulation: Assess for signs of circulation by checking the pulse. If there is no pulse, initiate cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) immediately.

  4. Disability: Evaluate the person’s level of consciousness and assess for head injuries or spinal cord injuries that could impede proper functioning.

  5. Exposure: Keep the injured person warm and protect them from further harm by covering them with blankets or using a makeshift shelter.

Treating Injuries and Conditions

Now that you’ve assessed the situation and prioritized care, let’s delve into some common injuries and conditions that survivalists may encounter in the wilderness and how to manage them:

  1. Cuts and Wounds: Clean the wound with clean water, if available, or use a sterile saline solution. Apply direct pressure to control bleeding, and dress the wound using clean materials.

  2. Fractures and Sprains: Immobilize the injured limb using makeshift splints or other available resources. Apply ice or cold packs wrapped in cloth to reduce swelling.

  3. Snake Bites: Keep the person calm and still to slow the spread of venom. Remove any tight clothing or jewelry from the affected limb and immobilize it. Seek medical help as soon as possible.

  4. Heat Exhaustion/Heat Stroke: Move the person to a cool, shaded area and remove excess clothing. Cool their body by applying wet towels or soaking them in cold water. Offer small sips of water if they are conscious.

  5. Insect Bites and Stings: Remove the stinger, if present, using a credit card or similar object. Wash the affected area with soap and water, and apply a cold compress to reduce swelling.

  6. Hypothermia: Remove wet clothing and replace it with dry layers. Warm the person gradually by providing them with warm drinks and covering them with blankets. Seek medical assistance if necessary.

Building a Wilderness First Aid Kit

To be prepared for emergencies, it’s important to pack a well-stocked wilderness first aid kit. Here are some essential items to include:

  1. Adhesive bandages of various sizes
  2. Sterile gauze pads
  3. Medical tape
  4. Antiseptic wipes or solution
  5. Tweezers and scissors
  6. Instant ice packs
  7. Disposable gloves
  8. CPR mask
  9. Pain relievers and antihistamines
  10. Emergency blanket

Seeking Professional Help

While basic first aid skills are essential for wilderness survival, it’s important to remember that they are not a substitute for professional medical care. In the event of a serious or life-threatening emergency, seek professional help as soon as possible.

It’s also worth considering taking a wilderness medicine course or obtaining certification to further enhance your skills. These courses provide in-depth knowledge on treating wilderness-specific injuries and conditions, and can better prepare you for unexpected situations.

Conclusion

Wilderness medicine is a unique discipline that requires survivalists to be well-versed in basic first aid techniques. By assessing the situation, prioritizing care, and treating common injuries and conditions, survivalists can be better equipped to handle emergencies in remote outdoor environments.

Remember to always be prepared by packing a well-stocked first aid kit and seeking professional help when necessary. By doing so, you can ensure your safety and the safety of those around you in the great outdoors.
