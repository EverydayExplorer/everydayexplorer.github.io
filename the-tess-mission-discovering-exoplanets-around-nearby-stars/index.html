The TESS Mission: Discovering Exoplanets Around Nearby Stars


In the vast expanse of the universe, there are countless mysteries waiting to be unraveled. One such mystery revolves around the existence of exoplanets - planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. These distant celestial bodies hold the potential to unlock the secrets of our own origins and provide insights into the possibility of life beyond Earth. To embark on this extraordinary quest, NASA launched the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) mission.

TESS, the successor to the Kepler Space Telescope, was launched on April 18, 2018, with the primary goal of discovering exoplanets orbiting the brightest stars near our solar system. Equipped with four wide-field cameras, TESS scans the sky, dividing it into 26 sectors and observing each sector for about 27 days. This comprehensive survey allows TESS to observe over 200,000 stars, searching for the minute dips in brightness that occur when an exoplanet transits or passes in front of its host star.

The transit method employed by TESS is a powerful tool for detecting exoplanets. As a planet crosses in front of its star, it blocks a small fraction of the star’s light, resulting in a detectable decrease in brightness. By carefully monitoring these subtle changes, TESS can identify potential exoplanet candidates. Once a candidate is identified, ground-based telescopes confirm its existence and determine its characteristics, such as size, mass, and orbit.

TESS focuses primarily on stars that are closer and brighter than those observed by the Kepler mission. This selection criterion makes follow-up observations much easier, enabling scientists to study the atmospheres of these exoplanets using other advanced instruments, such as the James Webb Space Telescope, slated for launch in 2021.

One of the most exciting aspects of the TESS mission is the potential for discovering Earth-sized exoplanets within the habitable zone of their host stars. The habitable zone, also known as the ‘Goldilocks zone,’ is the region around a star where conditions may be just right for liquid water to exist on a planet’s surface. Finding exoplanets within this zone is crucial in the search for potentially habitable worlds and the potential for extraterrestrial life.

Since its launch, TESS has made significant contributions to the field of exoplanet research. In its first year of operation, TESS discovered over 1,000 exoplanet candidates, including some intriguing finds, such as the ‘missing link’ exoplanet, HD 21749b, and the ‘hot Neptune,’ LHS 3844b. These discoveries have provided valuable insights into the diversity of exoplanetary systems and have opened up new avenues for further exploration.

Apart from its primary mission of exoplanet discovery, TESS has also contributed to other areas of astrophysics. It has observed numerous variable stars, including cataclysmic variables, pulsating stars, and eclipsing binaries. Additionally, TESS has been instrumental in detecting supernovae and studying active galactic nuclei, enhancing our understanding of the universe’s dynamic nature.

In conclusion, the TESS mission represents a significant leap forward in our quest to unravel the mysteries of the universe. By surveying nearby stars and scanning the sky for transiting exoplanets, TESS has already made remarkable discoveries and promises to continue expanding our knowledge of these distant worlds. With each new find, we edge closer to answering the age-old question: Are we alone in the universe? The TESS mission reminds us that the possibilities are infinite, and the next groundbreaking discovery may be just around the corner.
