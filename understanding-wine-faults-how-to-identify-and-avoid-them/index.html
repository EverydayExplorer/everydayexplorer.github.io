Understanding Wine Faults: How to Identify and Avoid Them


Wine enthusiasts understand the excitement of opening a bottle of wine and savoring its complex aromas and flavors. However, just like any other natural product, wine can develop faults and deviations from its intended qualities. These faults can greatly affect the overall enjoyment and experience of your wine. In this blog post, we will explore some common wine faults, how to identify them, and most importantly, how to avoid them.

What are Wine Faults?

Wine faults refer to undesirable characteristics that can develop during the winemaking process, storage, or even due to natural factors. Faults can arise from many sources, including vineyard problems, improper fermentation, and storage conditions.

Types of Wine Faults

  1. Cork Taint: One of the most well-known wine faults is cork taint, also known as corked wine. It occurs when a wine comes into contact with a cork that contains a compound called trichloroanisole (TCA). Cork taint can cause the wine to have a musty, wet cardboard-like aroma and taste. Thankfully, the incidence of cork taint has decreased with the use of alternative closures like screw caps and synthetic corks.

  2. Oxidation: Exposure to oxygen can lead to oxidation, which can have a significant impact on the flavor and aroma of the wine. Oxidized wine often takes on a brownish color and has a nutty, caramel-like taste. Proper storage and handling of wine, especially after opening the bottle, can help prevent oxidation.

  3. Sulfur Compounds: Sulfur dioxide is commonly added to wine as a preservative, but excessive amounts can cause faults. The presence of sulfur compounds can result in a strong, unpleasant smell resembling burnt rubber or rotten eggs. Winemakers carefully regulate sulfur additions to avoid these faults.

  4. Volatile Acidity: Acetic acid, also known as vinegar, is produced during the fermentation process. However, excessive acetic acid can result in a fault known as volatile acidity. Wines with high levels of volatile acidity have a sharp, vinegar-like smell and taste. Proper hygiene and control of fermentation conditions can help minimize this fault.

  5. Brettanomyces: Brettanomyces, or ‘brett,’ is a yeast strain that can produce aromas and flavors reminiscent of band-aid, barnyard, or sweaty horse. While some wine enthusiasts appreciate the complexity it can bring to certain styles of wine, excessive levels of brett can be considered a fault. Strict sanitation practices and monitoring are essential to prevent its growth.

Identifying Wine Faults

Recognizing wine faults requires a keen sense of smell and taste. Here are some signs to look out for:

  • Unpleasant aromas: Unusual and off-putting smells like wet cardboard, vinegar, or rotten eggs indicate the presence of faults.
  • Sour or spoiled taste: If the wine tastes overly sour, vinegary, or spoiled, it may have a fault.
  • Unusual appearance: Noticeable discoloration, excessive cloudiness, or significant effervescence can be signs of a fault.

Remember that faults can present themselves differently depending on the type of wine and the level of severity. Developing your palate and becoming familiar with the characteristics of different wine styles is crucial for identifying faults.

Avoiding Wine Faults

While some faults may occur naturally, winemakers employ various techniques to minimize their occurrence. As a consumer, there are steps you can take to avoid wine faults and ensure a quality experience:

  1. Choose reputable producers: Opt for wines from well-established and reputable wineries that prioritize quality and follow good winemaking practices.

  2. Inspect the bottle: Examine the bottle for any signs of damage, such as leaking or moldy labels. These could be indicators of underlying faults.

  3. Store wine properly: Proper storage conditions, including temperature, humidity, and light exposure, are vital in preventing wine faults. Keep your wine in a cool, dark place, away from strong odors.

  4. Pay attention to the cork: When opening a bottle with a natural cork closure, check for any signs of mold or moisture on the cork. While it doesn’t guarantee a fault-free wine, it can give you an idea of the cork’s condition.

  5. Trust your senses: Smell and taste the wine before consuming. If something seems amiss, trust your instincts and consult a knowledgeable wine professional or wine merchant.

Understanding wine faults and being able to effectively identify and avoid them can greatly enhance your wine-drinking experience. By following these tips, you can minimize the risk of opening a bottle of wine with noticeable faults and maximize your enjoyment of this beloved beverage. Cheers!
