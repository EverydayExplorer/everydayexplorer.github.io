Wine and Terpenes: Aromatic Compounds Explained


Wine is often celebrated for its complex flavors and aromas. When you take a sip of your favorite red or white wine, you may notice a range of scents and tastes that make the experience so enjoyable. These sensory elements are a result of various compounds found in wine, with one group in particular playing a significant role in shaping wine’s aromatic profile: terpenes.

Terpenes are a diverse group of organic compounds that are naturally occurring in many plants, including grapes. They are responsible for the wide array of scents and flavors we associate with wine, from the fruity and floral notes in a crisp Riesling to the herbal and spicy tones of a bold Cabernet Sauvignon. In this blog post, we’ll dive deep into the world of wine and terpenes, exploring what they are, where they come from, and how they influence the flavors and aromas in your glass.

What Are Terpenes?

Terpenes are a class of hydrocarbons produced by a variety of plants, including grapes. These compounds are what give fruits, flowers, and herbs their distinctive aromas and flavors. They are found in the essential oils of these plants and are responsible for the scents we associate with them. In wine, terpenes are primarily present in the grape skins, as well as in smaller quantities in the grape pulp and seeds.

The chemical structure of terpenes consists of isoprene units, which are five-carbon building blocks. Various combinations of these units result in the numerous terpene compounds found in nature. This diversity is what leads to the wide range of aromas and flavors in wines, as different grape varieties and growing conditions can produce distinct terpene profiles.

Common Terpenes Found in Wine

While there are hundreds of different terpenes that can be found in wine, some are more commonly encountered than others. Here are a few of the key terpenes and the aromas and flavors they contribute to wine:

  2. Myrcene: Myrcene is found in grapes and is responsible for the herbal and fruity aromas in wines like Sauvignon Blanc and Muscat. It’s also a prominent terpene in hops, contributing to the aroma of beer.

  3. Limonene: Limonene imparts a zesty, citrusy aroma and flavor to wines, often found in varieties like Pinot Grigio and Viognier. It’s also prevalent in the peels of citrus fruits.

  4. Terpinolene: This terpene offers a complex profile of pine, floral, and herbal notes. It can be found in a variety of wines, including Cabernet Sauvignon and Syrah.

  5. Geraniol: Geraniol contributes to the sweet, floral, and rosy aromas found in some wines, such as Merlot and Grenache. It’s also found in geraniums.

  6. Caryophyllene: Caryophyllene is known for its spicy and woody notes and is commonly found in red wines like Zinfandel and Cabernet Franc. It’s also present in black pepper.

Understanding these terpenes and their associated aromas can enhance your wine-tasting experience. When you can identify the specific terpenes in a wine, you’ll have a better appreciation of its complexity and a more nuanced vocabulary for describing its attributes.

Terpenes in the Vineyard and Winemaking

Terpenes don’t just magically appear in wine; their presence is influenced by several factors, from grape variety to winemaking techniques. Here’s how terpenes come into play throughout the winemaking process:

  1. Grape Variety: Different grape varieties naturally contain varying levels and types of terpenes. This is why a Riesling from Germany may exhibit more pronounced floral notes compared to a Chardonnay from California.

  2. Growth Conditions: The terpene content in grapes can also be affected by factors such as climate, soil type, and vineyard altitude. Cooler climates, for instance, tend to produce grapes with higher terpene concentrations.

  3. Ripeness: The stage of grape ripeness at harvest is crucial. Overripe grapes can lose some of their terpene content, leading to a less aromatic wine.

  4. Winemaking Techniques: Winemakers can influence terpene extraction during the winemaking process. For instance, the use of skin contact during fermentation can result in greater terpene concentration in the final wine.

The Terroir Connection

Terroir, the concept that the environment in which grapes are grown imparts unique characteristics to the wine, plays a significant role in terpene expression. Terroir encompasses soil type, climate, topography, and even the local flora and fauna. All these elements can influence the presence and concentration of terpenes in grapes.

For example, a vineyard surrounded by fragrant lavender fields may produce wines with subtle lavender notes due to the influence of nearby terpene-rich plants. Similarly, a vineyard with rocky, mineral-rich soil may contribute to wines with unique mineral and earthy terpene undertones.

Terpenes and Wine Pairing

Understanding the terpenes in wine can also enhance your wine pairing experiences. Just as certain terpenes complement each other in nature, they can also complement and enhance the flavors in food. Here are some general guidelines for pairing wine and food based on terpenes:

  1. Citrusy Terpenes: Wines with citrusy terpenes like limonene pair well with seafood, salads, and dishes that feature lemon or lime accents.

  2. Herbal Terpenes: Wines with herbal terpenes like myrcene go beautifully with dishes containing fresh herbs, such as basil or thyme.

  3. Floral Terpenes: Floral wines with geraniol or linalool terpenes can complement dishes featuring edible flowers, light cheeses, and aromatic herbs.

  4. Spicy Terpenes: Wines with spicy terpenes like caryophyllene are great partners for bold, spicy foods, such as Mexican or Indian cuisine.

The Future of Terpenes in Wine

As wine enthusiasts and researchers continue to explore the world of terpenes, we can expect to see more focus on terroir-driven winemaking and the cultivation of grape varieties with unique terpene profiles. Winemakers will continue to experiment with terpene extraction methods to create wines that push the boundaries of aroma and flavor.

Additionally, wine education programs may increasingly incorporate terpene analysis into their curriculum, empowering wine lovers to deepen their appreciation for the complexities of wine. This knowledge can help consumers make more informed choices when selecting wines to suit their preferences.

In conclusion, terpenes are a fascinating aspect of wine that greatly influence its aromatic and flavor profiles. These organic compounds, found naturally in grapes and influenced by various factors, give each wine its unique personality.
