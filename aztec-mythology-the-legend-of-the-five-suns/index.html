Aztec Mythology: The Legend of the Five Suns


In the vast tapestry of human mythology, few tales are as captivating and enigmatic as the legend of the Five Suns in Aztec mythology. This ancient Mesoamerican belief system weaves a complex narrative that explores the creation and destruction of the world through a series of cosmic cycles. Join me on a journey through time and space as we delve into the depths of Aztec mythology and unravel the captivating tale of the Five Suns.

According to Aztec cosmology, the world has undergone several cycles of creation and destruction, each heralded by the emergence and extinction of a sun. The Aztecs believed that the universe had previously been destroyed and recreated four times before the present era, known as the Fifth Sun.

The First Sun, known as Nahui-Ocelotl, was the age of the jaguar. It was a time of darkness and chaos, where the world was inhabited by monstrous beings. The gods Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl, in an act of divine sacrifice, assumed the form of two great serpents and tore the world apart, bringing an end to the First Sun.

The Second Sun, Nahui-Ehécatl, was the age of the wind. The world was populated by giants who were ultimately blown away by powerful hurricanes. The gods Quetzalcoatl and Tezcatlipoca, once again assuming the form of serpents, brought about the end of the Second Sun.

The Third Sun, Nahui-Quiahuitl, was the age of rain. The world was inhabited by humans who were eventually destroyed by a torrential rain of fire. The gods Tlaloc and Chalchiuhtlicue, the deities of rain and water, orchestrated the end of the Third Sun.

The Fourth Sun, Nahui-Atl, was the age of water. The world was inhabited by fish-like creatures who were consumed by a great flood. The gods Tlaloc and Chalchiuhtlicue once again played a crucial role in bringing about the end of the Fourth Sun.

Finally, we arrive at the current era, the Fifth Sun, known as Nahui-Ollin. This age is believed to have begun on August 13, 3114 BCE, and it is said to be governed by the god Tonatiuh, the sun god. The Aztecs believed that this era would also come to a cataclysmic end, much like the previous four suns, but the exact nature of this event remains shrouded in mystery.

The legend of the Five Suns serves as a powerful metaphor for the cyclical nature of existence and the impermanence of the physical world. It reflects the Aztecs’ deep reverence for the forces of nature and their understanding of the delicate balance between creation and destruction.

As we explore the rich tapestry of Aztec mythology, we uncover a profound understanding of the human experience and our place in the universe. The legend of the Five Suns not only offers a captivating narrative but also invites us to contemplate the ever-changing nature of our world and the eternal cycles that shape our lives.

In conclusion, the legend of the Five Suns in Aztec mythology is a captivating tale that explores the creation and destruction of the world through a series of cosmic cycles. This ancient belief system offers a unique perspective on the cyclical nature of existence and invites us to contemplate the profound mysteries of our universe. As we delve into the depths of Aztec mythology, we unlock a treasure trove of wisdom and insight that continues to inspire and fascinate us to this day.
