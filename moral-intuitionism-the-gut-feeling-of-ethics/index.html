Moral Intuitionism: The Gut Feeling of Ethics


As human beings, we often rely on our intuition to guide our actions and decisions. We have all experienced moments where we just had a gut feeling about what was morally right or wrong. This form of ethical decision-making is known as moral intuitionism.

Moral intuitionism suggests that our moral judgments are in fact intuitive and immediate. They arise from a deep-seated instinct within us, a gut feeling that tells us what is right or wrong. It implies that our moral knowledge is not derived from reason or empirical evidence, but rather from an inner sense, an internal compass that guides our moral judgment.

Intuitionism has a long history in philosophy, dating back to thinkers like Plato and Aristotle who recognized the importance of intuition in ethics. But modern proponents of moral intuitionism, such as David Hume and W.D. Ross, have further developed this concept and provided a deeper understanding of how our moral intuitions shape our ethical judgments.

One of the key aspects of moral intuitionism is its emphasis on the immediacy and emotional nature of our moral intuitions. When faced with a moral dilemma, we often experience an emotional response, a gut feeling that tells us what is right or wrong. This emotional response is not rational but is based on our innate sense of morality.

For example, imagine you witness someone stealing from a charity. You may immediately feel a sense of disgust or outrage, a gut feeling that tells you this action is morally wrong. This emotional response arises from your innate moral intuition, which recognizes the inherent wrongness of stealing from those in need.

Moral intuitionism also suggests that our moral intuitions are not infallible. While our gut feelings can provide us with invaluable guidance, they are not always reliable indicators of moral truth. Our intuitions can be shaped by a variety of factors, such as cultural norms, personal biases, or emotional states. Therefore, it is important to critically reflect on our moral intuitions and consider other perspectives before making ethical decisions.

Critics of moral intuitionism argue that relying solely on gut feelings can lead to inconsistencies and subjectivity in ethical judgments. They advocate for a more rational and evidence-based approach to morality, emphasizing the importance of reason and empirical evidence in ethical decision-making.

However, moral intuitionism does not discount the role of reason and evidence in ethics. Rather, it suggests that our moral intuitions should be considered alongside rational reflection and empirical data. Our intuitions can serve as a starting point for ethical deliberation, providing us with initial insights that can be further analyzed and evaluated.

In fact, recent research in psychology and neuroscience supports the idea of moral intuitionism. Studies have shown that our moral judgments are often made intuitively, within milliseconds, before we even have a chance to engage in conscious reasoning. These findings suggest that our intuition plays a significant role in shaping our ethical judgments.

In conclusion, moral intuitionism highlights the significance of our gut feelings in ethical decision-making. Our moral intuitions provide us with immediate insights into what is morally right or wrong. While not infallible, they serve as a valuable starting point for ethical deliberation. By considering our moral intuitions alongside rational reflection and empirical evidence, we can make more informed and ethically sound decisions. So the next time you have a gut feeling about what is right or wrong, trust your moral intuition and let it guide your ethical choices.
