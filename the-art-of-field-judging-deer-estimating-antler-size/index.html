The Art of Field Judging Deer: Estimating Antler Size


Deer hunting is a popular sport throughout the United States. Many hunters would like to harvest large bucks with big antlers. However, estimating antler size can be difficult, especially for less experienced hunters. The art of field judging deer is a crucial skill that can help hunters make informed decisions when deciding which deer to harvest. This article will provide tips on how to estimate antler size while field judging deer.

1. Knowing the Difference Between Points and Tines

The first step in estimating antler size is to understand the difference between points and tines. Points are the antler’s main beams, while tines are the smaller prongs that grow from the main beams. Most hunters count the main beams, including all the points and tines that are an inch or longer. A buck with eight points has four tines on each antler, but a buck with ten points may only have two tines but two extra brow tines on each antler.

2. Estimating Beam Length

Once you have counted the points and tines, you can start estimating the beam length. The length of the beams will give you an idea of the buck’s size. Long beams are usually an indication of an older buck with bigger antlers. You can estimate the beam length by comparing the antlers to the deer’s body size. A buck with antlers that extend beyond the ears is likely to have a larger spread than one that doesn’t.

3. Measuring the Spread

The spread is another essential factor when it comes to estimating antler size. The spread is the measurement between the tips of the antlers. The wider the spread, the bigger the buck’s antlers. When field judging deer, it’s essential to measure the spread from the outside edge of one antler to the outside edge of the other, rather than from the tips of the antlers, as some bucks may have unevenly distributed mass and this will give a better indication of the buck’s true spread.

4. Judging Mass

Mass is the thickness of the antlers at different points. Mass is essential for estimating antler size and for determining trophy potential. Bucks with more mass are usually older and have bigger antlers. You can estimate the mass of the antlers by looking at the girth of the main beams and the tines. Bucks with thick bases will usually have more mass than those with thin bases.

5. Evaluating Symmetry

Symmetry is another crucial factor when it comes to estimating antler size. Symmetry refers to the balance between the two antlers. A buck with symmetrical antlers is likely to have a bigger rack than one with uneven antlers. To evaluate symmetry, simply compare the antlers and look for any noticeable differences between them. These could include differences in tine length, beam thickness or the number of points.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the art of field judging deer is a critical skill that can help hunters make informed decisions when deciding which deer to harvest. Estimating antler size is a significant part of field judging deer, and by following the tips outlined above, hunters can increase their chances of harvesting a trophy buck. Remember that it’s not just about the size of the antlers, but also the age and overall health of the deer. By putting all of these factors together, hunters can have a better understanding of what they’re looking at when assessing potential trophy deer.
