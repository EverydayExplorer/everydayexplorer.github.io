The Art of Decanting: Why and How it Impacts the Taste of Wine


Picture this: you’re celebrating a special occasion and you’ve just opened a bottle of fine wine. The anticipation is high as you pour the first glass. But as you take your first sip, you can’t help but feel a little underwhelmed. The flavors aren’t as vibrant and the wine seems a bit closed off. What went wrong?

This is where the art of decanting comes into play. Decanting is the process of pouring wine from its original bottle into a separate vessel, typically a decanter, before serving. While it may seem like an unnecessary step, decanting can significantly impact the taste of wine, enhancing its aromas and flavors, and ultimately improving your overall wine-drinking experience.

One of the main reasons for decanting is to allow the wine to breathe. When a bottle of wine is first opened, it often has a tight or closed off character due to being trapped in a sealed environment. By pouring the wine into a decanter, you are exposing it to oxygen, which helps to open up the aromas and flavors.

This process allows the wine to release volatile compounds that have been trapped inside the bottle. These compounds, known as esters, are responsible for the bouquet and aroma of the wine. By allowing them to evaporate, you are able to fully appreciate and enjoy the true essence of the wine.

Additionally, decanting can help to remove any sediment that may have formed in the bottle. Sediment is a natural occurrence in older wines, particularly red wines, and is made up of tannins and other solids that have precipitated out over time. When you decant the wine, you can carefully pour it off, leaving the sediment behind in the original bottle. This ensures that the wine you are serving is clear and free from any unwanted particles.

So, now that you understand why decanting is important, let’s talk about how to do it properly.

First, choose the right decanter. The shape and size of the decanter can have an impact on the decanting process. A wide-bottomed decanter with a large surface area allows for greater exposure to oxygen, facilitating a faster and more effective aeration process. However, any decanter will do the job as long as it has enough capacity to hold the entire bottle of wine.

Once you have selected your decanter, carefully remove the cork or cap from the bottle of wine. Slowly pour the wine into the decanter, being cautious not to disturb any sediment at the bottom of the bottle. You can use a funnel or a decanting device with a built-in strainer to ensure a clean pour.

After the wine is in the decanter, let it sit and breathe for an appropriate amount of time. Generally, younger wines require less time to aerate, while older wines may benefit from a longer exposure to oxygen. For younger wines, 15-30 minutes should suffice, while older wines may need up to two hours or more.

During this waiting period, you can swirl the wine gently in the decanter to help expedite the aeration process. This movement helps to introduce more oxygen to the wine, further enhancing the flavors and aromas.

Once the wine has had time to breathe, it is ready to be served. Pour it into clean glasses, taking care to avoid any sediment that may have settled in the bottom of the decanter. The wine is now primed to be enjoyed to its fullest potential.

Decanting is not reserved for only rare or expensive wines. Even everyday bottles can benefit from this simple yet effective technique. The art of decanting allows you to take control of the wine-drinking experience and transform an ordinary moment into something extraordinary.

In conclusion, decanting is a powerful tool that can elevate your wine-drinking experience. By allowing the wine to breathe and removing any sediment, decanting helps to unleash the full potential of the wine’s aromas and flavors. So, the next time you open a bottle of wine, take a moment to decant it, and prepare to be amazed by the difference it can make. Cheers to the art of decanting!
