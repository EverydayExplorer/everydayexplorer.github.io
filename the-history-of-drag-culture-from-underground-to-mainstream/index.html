The History of Drag Culture: From Underground to Mainstream


Drag culture has undeniably made its mark on mainstream entertainment in recent years, with shows like RuPaul’s Drag Race achieving international success and recognition. But where did this vibrant and captivating art form originate? How has it evolved over time to become the mainstream phenomenon we see today? In this blog post, we will take a deep dive into the history of drag culture, tracing its roots from underground performances to its current position in pop culture.

Drag, as a form of performance, dates back centuries. The term ‘drag’ is believed to have originated from the phrase ‘dressed as a girl,’ as men would often dress up in women’s clothing for comedic or theatrical purposes. This tradition can be seen in various cultures throughout history, including ancient Greece and Shakespearean England. These performances often served as a means of entertainment, mocking societal norms and challenging gender roles. However, drag in these early stages was largely confined to underground or subversive spaces, as it was generally considered taboo or even criminal in many societies.

It wasn’t until the 20th century that drag culture began to gain more visibility and recognition. In the early 1900s, drag balls started to emerge as a prominent feature of LGBTQ+ culture. These balls provided a safe space for LGBTQ+ individuals to express themselves and engage in flamboyant performances. Iconic drag queens like Gladys Bentley and Bert Savoy became popular figures during this time, showcasing their talents on stage and pushing societal boundaries.

The rise of drag culture continued throughout the mid-19th century and into the 1960s, with the emergence of drag bars and cabarets. These establishments became havens for the LGBTQ+ community, offering a place to celebrate and showcase their identities. One notable example was The Stonewall Inn, a bar in New York City, which played a pivotal role in the LGBTQ+ rights movement after a police raid sparked a series of protests. Drag queens were at the forefront of these demonstrations, bravely standing up for their rights and demanding equality.

However, it was in the 1990s when drag culture truly started to make its mark on mainstream culture. Leigh Bowery, a British performance artist and drag queen, gained international recognition for his extravagant and avant-garde performances. Bowery’s unique style and boundary-pushing creativity inspired many drag performers to push the limits of their art. At the same time, the emergence of drag queen RuPaul brought drag into the mainstream consciousness with the release of his hit song ‘Supermodel (You Better Work)’ and the launch of his groundbreaking reality show, RuPaul’s Drag Race.

Since its inception in 2009, RuPaul’s Drag Race has become a global phenomenon, winning numerous awards and gaining millions of fans worldwide. The show follows a competition format, where drag queens from all walks of life compete for the crown by showcasing their talents in a series of challenges. Drag Race has not only raised the profile of drag culture but has also provided a platform for LGBTQ+ voices and issues. The show has brought drag queens into the homes of mainstream audiences, breaking down barriers and challenging preconceived notions of gender and identity.

With the rise of social media and the internet, drag culture has become more accessible and influential than ever before. Drag queens now have the ability to connect with audiences around the world through platforms like Instagram, YouTube, and TikTok. These digital spaces have allowed performers to reach larger and more diverse audiences, expanding the reach and impact of drag culture.

Today, drag culture continues to evolve and push boundaries. Drag queens are celebrated not only for their glamorous performances but also for their activism and ability to use their platforms to advocate for social change. They have become powerful symbols of self-expression, resilience, and empowerment.

In conclusion, the history of drag culture is one of resilience, creativity, and pushing boundaries. From its origins in underground performances to its current position in mainstream entertainment, drag has captivated audiences and challenged societal norms. Its evolution and rise to mainstream prominence are a testament to the power of art in shaping culture and creating positive change. Drag culture serves as a reminder that self-expression knows no limits and that we can all benefit from embracing our true selves, regardless of gender or identity. So, next time you see a drag queen sashaying across the stage or on your television screen, take a moment to appreciate their incredible journey and the rich history they represent.
