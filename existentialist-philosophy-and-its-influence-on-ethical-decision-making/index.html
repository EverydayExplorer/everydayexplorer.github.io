Existentialist Philosophy and Its Influence on Ethical Decision Making


In the realm of philosophy, few schools of thought have captivated the human mind quite like existentialism. Stemming from the works of influential thinkers such as Soren Kierkegaard, Friedrich Nietzsche, and Jean-Paul Sartre, existentialism delves into the fundamental questions of human existence and the search for meaning in a seemingly chaotic world.

At its core, existentialism challenges traditional notions of morality and ethics, urging individuals to take responsibility for their choices and actions. It emphasizes the individual’s freedom and autonomy, emphasizing that one’s existence precedes their essence. In other words, individuals are not born with a predetermined purpose or moral code; rather, they must construct their own values and meanings in life.

Existentialist philosophy has profound implications for ethical decision making. It encourages individuals to confront the inherent moral dilemmas and uncertainties of life head-on, rather than seeking refuge in external authorities or established moral frameworks. Instead of relying on religious or societal norms, existentialists argue that ethical choices should stem from an individual’s authentic self and personal experiences.

One of the key concepts in existentialist ethics is the idea of authenticity. Authenticity refers to the individual’s commitment to living in accordance with their true values and beliefs, rather than conforming to societal expectations or external pressures. When faced with ethical decisions, existentialists advocate for individuals to reflect on their own values, desires, and emotions, and choose the course of action that aligns with their authentic self.

Existentialist thought also emphasizes the concept of personal responsibility. It posits that individuals are fully responsible for the consequences of their choices and actions, and that they must bear the weight of their decisions. This philosophy rejects the notion of blaming external factors or circumstances for one’s actions, emphasizing instead the importance of personal agency and accountability.

Furthermore, existentialism recognizes the inherent anxiety and anguish that arise from the freedom and responsibility of making ethical decisions. Existentialists argue that this anxiety is an essential part of the human condition and should not be avoided or suppressed. Instead, individuals are encouraged to embrace the existential angst and use it as a catalyst for self-reflection and growth.

The influence of existentialist philosophy on ethical decision making extends beyond the realm of personal choices. It has also shaped broader ethical frameworks, such as existentialist ethics in healthcare, business, and politics. In healthcare, for example, existentialist ethics emphasize the importance of respecting patients’ autonomy and allowing them to make decisions based on their own values and beliefs.

In conclusion, existentialist philosophy offers a unique perspective on ethical decision making. By emphasizing personal responsibility, authenticity, and the inherent uncertainties of existence, it challenges individuals to confront moral dilemmas with courage and introspection. Whether applied to personal choices or larger ethical frameworks, existentialism encourages individuals to navigate the complexities of life with integrity and self-awareness. By embracing the principles of existentialism, we can strive for a more meaningful and ethically conscious existence.
