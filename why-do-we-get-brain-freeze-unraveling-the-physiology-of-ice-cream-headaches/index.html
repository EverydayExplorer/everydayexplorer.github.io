Why Do We Get Brain Freeze? Unraveling the Physiology of Ice Cream Headaches


As summer approaches and temperatures begin to rise, many of us indulge in cold treats to beat the heat. One of the most popular summertime delights is ice cream - creamy, smooth, and delicious. However, if you’ve ever experienced that sudden and intense pain in your head after indulging in a cold treat too quickly, you’ve experienced what is commonly known as a brain freeze.

Brain freeze, also referred to as ice cream headaches or sphenopalatine ganglioneuralgia (SPG), is a temporary but excruciating headache that occurs after consuming cold foods or beverages. It is estimated that about one-third of the population experiences brain freeze at some point in their lives.

But why does this seemingly harmless act of enjoying ice cream cause such intense pain? To unravel the physiology behind brain freeze, we need to understand the intricate workings of our bodies.

The sensation of brain freeze occurs when the cold food or beverage touches the roof of our mouths. More specifically, the rapid consumption of the cold treat causes the blood vessels in our palate to constrict. This constriction is a natural response triggered by the sudden temperature change.

Interestingly, this constriction of blood vessels is our body’s way of protecting itself. When exposed to extreme cold, the blood vessels narrow to reduce blood flow in the area. This helps prevent heat loss and keep our core body temperature stable. However, this sudden constriction can lead to a rapid increase in blood pressure.

As blood vessels constrict and blood pressure rises, our body initiates a unique response to counteract this phenomenon. It sends signals to the brain, activating the trigeminal nerve. The trigeminal nerve is responsible for carrying sensations from the face to the brain, and brain freeze is a result of these signals being sent.

The trigeminal nerve consists of three branches, and one of these branches, known as the ophthalmic branch, supplies the roof of our mouth, forehead, and sinuses. When triggered by the rapid temperature change, the ophthalmic branch sends signals to the brain, leading to the sensation of pain.

The brain interprets these signals as coming from the forehead, which is why we feel the intense pain there during a brain freeze. Interestingly, if we place our tongue on the roof of our mouth or press our thumb against the roof of our mouth, we can alleviate brain freeze by warming the area and dilating the blood vessels.

It is important to note that brain freeze typically lasts for only a short duration, usually no more than a minute or two. However, the intensity of the pain can vary from person to person. Some individuals may experience mild discomfort, while others may find it debilitating.

If you frequently experience brain freeze and want to prevent it, there are a few strategies you can try. One method is to consume cold treats slowly, allowing your body to acclimate to the temperature change gradually. Another approach is to take small bites or sips and warm them in your mouth before swallowing.

While brain freeze is a temporary and harmless phenomenon, it serves as a reminder of the intricate workings of our bodies. Understanding the physiology behind brain freeze can help us appreciate the complexity of our nervous system and how it responds to various stimuli.

So, the next time you indulge in a frosty treat on a hot summer day, savor each bite and take your time. Appreciate the wonder of our bodies and the fascinating phenomenon of brain freeze. And if the pain strikes, remember, it’s just a temporary sensation that will soon pass, leaving you free to enjoy your frozen delight.
