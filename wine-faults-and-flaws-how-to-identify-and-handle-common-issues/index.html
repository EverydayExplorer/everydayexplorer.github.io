Wine Faults and Flaws: How to Identify and Handle Common Issues


When it comes to enjoying a glass of wine, nothing can ruin the experience faster than encountering a wine fault or flaw. These issues can range from subtle to overpowering, and can greatly impact the taste, aroma, and overall quality of the wine. As a wine enthusiast, it’s important to be able to identify these faults and flaws and understand how to handle them. In this blog post, we will explore some of the most common wine faults and flaws, and provide tips on how to identify and handle them like a pro.

1. Cork Taint (TCA)
One of the most well-known wine faults is cork taint, also known as TCA (2,4,6-Trichloroanisole). This issue arises when a wine comes into contact with a contaminated cork, resulting in a musty or wet cardboard aroma and taste. If you notice these characteristics in your wine, it’s likely that it is suffering from cork taint. In this case, the best course of action is to simply ask for a replacement bottle, as cork taint cannot be fixed.

2. Oxidation
Oxidation occurs when a wine is exposed to too much air, causing it to lose its freshness and develop flavors of sherry, vinegar, or even wet cardboard. Signs of oxidation may include a flat or dull taste, browning of the wine, or an aroma reminiscent of bruised apples or nuts. To prevent oxidation, ensure that opened bottles are properly stored with minimal exposure to air. If you encounter an oxidized wine, try adding some citrus zest or a splash of sparkling water to help mask the off flavors.

3. Volatile Acidity (VA)
Volatile Acidity refers to the presence of acetic acid in a wine, which gives off a strong vinegary smell. While a small amount of VA can add complexity to a wine, excessive amounts can be off-putting and indicate a flaw. High levels of VA can result from poor winemaking practices or bacterial contamination. If you detect a strong vinegar aroma in your wine, it is likely suffering from volatile acidity. Unfortunately, there is no remedy for this issue, so it’s best to seek a replacement bottle.

4. Reduction
Reduction occurs when a wine lacks oxygen during its winemaking process or post-bottling. This can result in a variety of off-putting smells, such as rotten eggs or burnt rubber. Reduction can be remedied by decanting the wine or allowing it to breathe for some time. Swirling the glass vigorously can also help introduce oxygen and alleviate the reduction. However, if the wine does not improve after these steps, it’s best to seek a replacement bottle.

5. Brettanomyces (Brett)
Brettanomyces, also known as Brett, is a type of yeast that can cause off-putting aromas and flavors in wine. While some wine enthusiasts appreciate a slight hint of Brett, excessive levels can lead to barnyard, medicinal, or band-aid-like aromas. The presence of Brett is often more prevalent in old world wines, such as those from France or Italy. If you detect Brett in your wine and find it overwhelming, opt for wines produced in regions with lower Brett presence, such as cool-climate regions.

6. Sulfur Dioxide (SO2)
Sulfur dioxide is a preservative that is commonly used in winemaking to prevent spoilage and oxidation. However, excessive amounts of SO2 can lead to unwanted aromas and flavors, such as burnt matches or rotten eggs. While low levels of SO2 are normal, high levels can indicate poor winemaking practices or an issue with the storage of the wine. If you detect a strong sulfur aroma, it’s best to let the wine breathe for some time or aerate it using a decanter to help dissipate the sulfur compounds.

In conclusion, being able to identify and handle wine faults and flaws is crucial for any wine enthusiast. By understanding the common issues and their causes, you can confidently navigate the world of wine and make informed decisions when encountering a flawed bottle. Remember to trust your senses and, when in doubt, consult with a sommelier or wine professional for guidance. Cheers to enjoying flawless wines!
