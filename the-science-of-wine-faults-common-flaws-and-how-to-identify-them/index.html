The Science of Wine Faults: Common Flaws and How to Identify Them


Wine is a beloved beverage enjoyed by many around the world. With its rich history and complex flavors, wine has the power to captivate our senses and transport us to different parts of the world. However, not all wines are created equal, and sometimes, flaws can emerge that can alter the taste and overall experience. In this blog post, we will delve into the science behind wine faults, explore common flaws, and learn how to identify them.

Before we dive into the specifics, it’s important to understand that wine faults are not necessarily a sign of poor quality. Even the finest wines can sometimes develop flaws due to various factors such as winemaking techniques, storage conditions, or even the grapes themselves. The key is to be able to recognize these faults and understand their impact on the wine’s overall character.

One of the most common wine faults is cork taint, often caused by a compound called trichloroanisole (TCA). When a wine is affected by cork taint, it can exhibit musty, moldy, or damp cardboard-like aromas and flavors. This fault can be easily identified by its distinctive smell, which can overpower the natural fruity and floral notes of the wine.

Another common wine flaw is oxidation. Exposure to oxygen can cause a wine to lose its freshness and vibrant flavors, resulting in a flat or dull taste. Oxidation can be identified by the presence of brownish hues in white wines or browning at the edges of red wines. Additionally, an oxidized wine may have aromas reminiscent of sherry or vinegar.

Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘Brett,’ is a yeast strain that can lead to another wine fault. In small quantities, Brett can add complexity and desirable aromas to certain wines. However, when it becomes too dominant, it can produce off-putting aromas of barnyard, band-aid, or wet dog. Identifying Brett can be subjective, as some wine enthusiasts actually enjoy these earthy and funky notes. Nevertheless, excessive Brett presence can detract from the overall quality of the wine.

Sulfur compounds, commonly associated with the smell of rotten eggs, can also contribute to wine faults. These compounds can arise from the use of sulfur dioxide during winemaking, which serves as a preservative and antioxidant. However, when used in excess or during fermentation, it can result in off-putting aromas and flavors. Recognizing sulfur faults is relatively straightforward, as the smell is distinct and unpleasant.

One final wine fault worth mentioning is volatile acidity (VA). In small amounts, VA can contribute to the wine’s complexity and aromatic profile. However, excessive levels can result in a vinegary smell and taste, often described as nail polish remover or acetone. High levels of VA can be indicative of poor winemaking practices or bacterial contamination.

Identifying wine faults requires a keen sense of smell and taste. It’s important to trust your senses and be aware of any deviations from the expected aromas and flavors. If you suspect a wine may be faulty, it’s always helpful to compare it to a known good bottle for reference.

In conclusion, the science of wine faults is a fascinating topic that sheds light on the complexities of winemaking and the potential challenges that can arise. By understanding common flaws and how to identify them, wine enthusiasts can enhance their appreciation and enjoyment of this timeless beverage. So, the next time you uncork a bottle, take a moment to assess its character and explore the nuances that make each wine unique. Cheers to the science behind wine faults and the journey of exploration it invites us on!
