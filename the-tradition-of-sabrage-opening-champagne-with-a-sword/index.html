The Tradition of Sabrage: Opening Champagne with a Sword


If you’ve ever attended a classy event or celebrated a special occasion, you may have witnessed someone opening a champagne bottle with a dramatic flourish, using nothing but a sword. This impressive show of skill and tradition is known as sabrage. The art of sabrage dates back to the days of Napoleon, and it continues to fascinate and captivate champagne lovers around the world.

The History of Sabrage

The origins of the sabrage tradition can be traced back to the French Revolution and the Napoleonic era. During this time, Napoleon’s army would celebrate their victories with champagne. Legend has it that the tradition of sabrage began when Napoleon’s soldiers, who were always on horseback, found themselves without a proper tool to open their bottles of champagne after a victorious battle.

In a display of ingenuity, these soldiers would use their swords to quickly and efficiently open the bottles. This not only saved them time but also added an element of grandeur and celebration to the moment. The tradition was born, and it quickly spread throughout the ranks of Napoleon’s army.

The Technique

Opening a champagne bottle with a sword may seem like a daunting task, but with practice and the right technique, it can be mastered by anyone willing to give it a try. Here’s a step-by-step guide on how to perform sabrage:

  1. Choose the Right Bottle: It’s important to select a bottle of champagne that has a seam running along the side, from the bottom to the lip. This seam is the weakest point in the bottle and will make it easier to break.

  2. Chill the Champagne: Before attempting sabrage, make sure the bottle is properly chilled. This will help ensure a clean break when the blade strikes the bottle.

  3. Remove the Foil and Wire Cage: Carefully remove the foil and wire cage from the neck of the bottle, exposing the cork.

  4. Find the Seam: Locate the seam running along the side of the bottle. This is where you will aim your saber.

  5. Position the Saber: Hold the sword firmly in your dominant hand, with the blade pointing away from you. Position the back of the blade against the seam, at a 45-degree angle.

  6. Slide the Sword: With a swift and confident motion, slide the saber quickly up the bottle towards the cork. Apply enough force to break the glass, but not so much that it shatters.

  7. Follow Through: As you slide the sword up the bottle, continue your motion until the saber strikes the lip of the bottle. This will ensure a clean break.

  8. Enjoy the Celebration: Once the top of the bottle pops off, pour the champagne into glasses and enjoy the fruits of your sabrage skills.

Safety Considerations

While sabrage can be a thrilling and impressive way to open a bottle of champagne, it’s essential to exercise caution and prioritize safety. Here are a few tips to keep in mind:

  • Practice Makes Perfect: Before attempting sabrage in front of an audience, practice on a bottle with a dull blade or even a plastic sword.

  • Choose the Right Sword: It’s important to select a sword that is specifically designed for sabrage. Opt for a dull blade with no cutting edge, as this will help minimize the risk of accidents.

  • Mind Your Surroundings: Ensure that you have enough space and a clear area to perform the sabrage. Move away from valuable or fragile objects to avoid any accidents.

  • Protect Yourself: Wear protective eyewear and gloves to protect yourself from flying glass and potential accidents.

The Celebration Continues

The tradition of sabrage adds an extra touch of elegance and excitement to any special occasion. Whether it’s a wedding, anniversary, or a New Year’s Eve celebration, sabring a champagne bottle with a sword is sure to impress your guests and create a memorable moment.

While it may take some practice to perfect the technique, the thrill and sense of achievement that come with successfully opening a bottle of champagne with a sword is well worth the effort. So, the next time you’re ready to celebrate, why not channel your inner Napoleon and give sabrage a try? Cheers to tradition, celebration, and the joy of champagne!

Note: Sabrage should only be attempted by individuals of legal drinking age who are comfortable handling sharp objects. Exercise caution and drink responsibly.
