Discovering Indigenous Voices: Books by Native Authors


In today’s increasingly diverse and interconnected world, it is essential that we make space for marginalized voices to be heard. One group whose narratives have often been overlooked or overshadowed is the Indigenous community. However, in recent years, there has been an inspiring surge in books written by Native authors that offer a unique and valuable perspective on history, culture, and contemporary issues. This blog post aims to celebrate and highlight some of the most noteworthy works in this burgeoning area of literature.

One of the reasons why reading books by Native authors is so important is that it allows us to dismantle stereotypes and challenge misconceptions. For too long, Indigenous peoples have been subjected to harmful stereotypes perpetuated by mainstream media and literature. By reading books written by Native authors, we can gain a more nuanced understanding of their diverse cultures, traditions, and experiences. These books provide us with an opportunity to break down barriers and foster empathy and understanding.

One of the most celebrated authors in the Indigenous literary world is Sherman Alexie. His novel ‘The Absolutely True Diary of a Part-Time Indian’ is a coming-of-age story that provides a powerful and authentic portrayal of life on a Native American reservation. Through the eyes of the protagonist, Junior, we witness the challenges he faces as he navigates the complexities of identity, poverty, and discrimination. Alexie’s writing brilliantly captures the humor and resilience of Indigenous communities, while also shedding light on the harsh realities they endure.

Another remarkable Native author is Louise Erdrich, a member of the Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians. Erdrich has written an extensive body of work that delves into the complexities of Native American life. Her novel ‘The Round House’ won the National Book Award for Fiction in 2012, and it is not hard to see why. Set on a North Dakota reservation, the book follows a young boy named Joe as he seeks justice for his mother, who was violently assaulted. Through Joe’s perspective, Erdrich explores themes of sovereignty, loss, and the intergenerational effects of trauma.

A book that offers a glimpse into the historical struggles faced by Indigenous communities is ‘There There’ by Tommy Orange. This multilayered novel brings together the stories of twelve characters, all preparing to attend the Big Oakland Powwow. Through Orange’s lyrical prose, we gain insight into the urban Native American experience and the ways in which colonization and cultural erasure have shaped their lives. ‘There There’ is a powerful and thought-provoking work that addresses the complexities of identity, family, and the ongoing fight for self-determination.

These are just a few examples of the incredible books by Native authors that deserve our attention. By actively seeking out and reading these works, we can amplify Indigenous voices and support the development of a more inclusive literary landscape. Reading books by Native authors is not only a way to learn and grow personally but also an act of solidarity and allyship.

In conclusion, the rise of books written by Native authors is a pivotal moment in the literary world. These books have the power to challenge stereotypes, foster understanding, and contribute to the decolonization of literature. Discovering Indigenous voices is an essential step towards creating a more equitable society and celebrating the rich cultural heritage of the Indigenous communities. So next time you’re looking for a new book to read, consider picking up a title by a Native author. You might just find your perspective expanded and your empathy deepened.
