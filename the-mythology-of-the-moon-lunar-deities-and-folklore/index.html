The Mythology of the Moon: Lunar Deities and Folklore


The moon, with its gentle glow and mysterious presence, has captivated humans for centuries. Throughout history, various cultures around the world have developed rich mythologies and folklore surrounding the moon. In this blog post, we will delve into the enchanting world of lunar deities and explore the diverse tales and beliefs associated with the moon.

The Moon in Ancient Civilizations

Ancient civilizations such as the Mesopotamians, Egyptians, and Greeks revered the moon as a powerful celestial being. In Mesopotamian mythology, the moon god Nanna, also known as Sin, was highly regarded as the father of all gods. The Egyptians worshipped Thoth, the ibis-headed god of the moon, who was believed to have created the calendar and bestowed wisdom upon humankind. The Greeks, on the other hand, worshipped Selene, the goddess of the moon, who rode her silver chariot across the night sky.

Lunar Deities and Their Stories

  1. Artemis (Greek) - Known as the goddess of the hunt and the moon, Artemis was a formidable figure. She was often depicted with a silver bow and arrow, roaming through the forests accompanied by a pack of hunting dogs. Artemis was also associated with childbirth and protected young girls as they transitioned into womanhood.

  2. Chang’e (Chinese) - A beloved figure in Chinese folklore, Chang’e was a beautiful woman who drank an elixir of immortality and ascended to the moon. Legend has it that she resides there with her companion, the Jade Rabbit, and is worshipped during the Mid-Autumn Festival.

  3. Tsukuyomi (Japanese) - In Japanese mythology, Tsukuyomi is the god of the moon and brother of Amaterasu, the sun goddess. Tsukuyomi’s actions were often associated with the phases of the moon, and he was believed to have the power to control time. His story is intertwined with Amaterasu’s, with both playing significant roles in the creation myth of Japan.

  4. Ibis (Mayan) - The Mayans had their own moon goddess known as Ix Chel or Ibis. She was associated with fertility, weaving, and medicine. Ibis was often depicted with a crescent moon headdress and was believed to have the power to control rainfall and storms.

Folklore and Moon Beliefs

Apart from deities, various cultures have developed folklore and beliefs surrounding the moon. Some of these fascinating tales include:

  • The Man in the Moon - Many cultures have seen the face of a man or a rabbit etched onto the moon’s surface. These interpretations have given rise to countless stories and legends.

  • Lunar Eclipses - Throughout history, lunar eclipses have been regarded as significant celestial events. In various cultures, these events were believed to be the result of a celestial battle or the moon being consumed by a mythical creature.

  • Moon as a Symbol of Transformation - The moon’s waxing and waning phases have often been associated with the cycle of life and death. Many cultures believe that the moon holds the power to transform and influence human emotions and behaviors.

The Moon’s Influence Today

While our understanding of the moon has evolved through scientific advancements, the moon’s allure and symbolism continue to captivate us. From influencing art and literature to shaping cultural festivities, the moon remains an integral part of human existence.

Next time you gaze up at the night sky and catch a glimpse of the moon’s radiant glow, remember the ancient tales and beliefs that have woven their magic into its celestial presence.
