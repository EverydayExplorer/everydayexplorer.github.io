Exploring the Wines of Hungary: Tokaji and More


Hungarian wines have gained international recognition in recent years, thanks to their unique flavors and rich history. One of the most celebrated Hungarian wines is Tokaji, a sweet dessert wine that has been enjoyed by wine enthusiasts for centuries. However, Hungary offers more than just Tokaji. The country is also home to a wide variety of other delicious wines that are worth exploring.

Tokaji, also known as Tokay, is named after the region it comes from – the Tokaj region in northeastern Hungary. The tradition of winemaking in this area dates back to the 16th century, making it one of the oldest wine regions in the world. The volcanic soil and microclimate of the region play a crucial role in producing the unique flavors of Tokaji wines.

What sets Tokaji apart from other dessert wines is the process of noble rot, or botrytis cinerea. Noble rot is a beneficial fungus that affects the grapes, allowing them to shrivel and concentrate their sugars. This gives Tokaji its distinct sweetness and complex aroma. The aszú grapes, affected by noble rot, are picked individually and crushed into a paste, which is then added to a base wine produced from healthy grapes. The sweet nectar that comes from this process creates a truly exceptional wine.

Traditionally, Tokaji wines are classified by the number of aszú grapes used in the production. The classification ranges from 3 to 6 Puttonyos, with higher numbers indicating higher sugar levels and greater complexity. Tokaji Aszú 6 Puttonyos is considered the pinnacle of Tokaji wines, while the 3 Puttonyos is a lighter and more approachable style. Both offer an explosion of flavors, including notes of apricot, honey, orange peel, and marmalade.

However, Tokaji is not the only wine that Hungary has to offer. The country boasts several other wine regions that produce high-quality wines. One of these regions is Eger, located in the northeastern part of the country. Eger is known for its red wines, with the flagship grape being Kékfrankos, also known as Blaufränkisch. Eger’s red wines are known for their elegance, complexity, and aging potential. The region also produces white wines, such as Olaszrizling and Chardonnay, which showcase the diversity of Eger’s winemaking.

Another wine region worth exploring is Villány, located in southern Hungary. Villány is famous for its full-bodied red wines, particularly those made from the Bordeaux grape varieties, such as Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Cabernet Franc. The region’s warm climate, coupled with the rich red clay soil, contributes to the production of intense and robust wines. Villány reds often exhibit flavors of dark fruit, chocolate, and spices, with a long and lingering finish.

In the west of Hungary, near the Austrian border, lies the Burgenland region. Here, you will find the Sopron wine region, renowned for its flagship grape variety, Kékfrankos. Sopron is known for producing elegant and refined red wines that showcase the unique character of the grape. The region’s cool climate and limestone-rich soil contribute to the development of wines with high acidity, vibrant fruit flavors, and smooth tannins.

If you’re a fan of sparkling wines, be sure to explore Hungary’s sparkling wine region, located in the northeastern part of the country. The region produces a wide variety of sparkling wines, including traditional method sparkling wines made using the Champagne method. The cool climate and volcanic soil of the region contribute to the production of sparkling wines with high acidity, fine bubbles, and complex flavors.

When it comes to exploring Hungarian wines, Tokaji is often the first name that comes to mind. However, Hungary has much more to offer than just this sweet dessert wine. From the elegant reds of Eger to the robust wines of Villány and the refined sparkling wines of the northeast, Hungary’s wine regions are waiting to be discovered. So pour yourself a glass of Hungarian wine and embark on a journey through the diverse and delightful world of Hungarian winemaking.
