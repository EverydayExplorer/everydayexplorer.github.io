The Kepler-90 System: A Star with Eight Planets


The universe is full of wonders, and one of the most fascinating discoveries in recent years is the Kepler-90 system. This extraordinary star system, located approximately 2,545 light-years away from Earth in the constellation Draco, has captured the attention of astronomers and space enthusiasts alike. What makes Kepler-90 so remarkable is the fact that it is home to not just one or two, but eight planets!

A Brief Introduction to the Kepler-90 System

Named after the Kepler space telescope, which detected it in 2013, the Kepler-90 system revolves around a star similar to our own sun. However, unlike our solar system, where we have a mere eight planets, Kepler-90 boasts an impressive total of eight planets orbiting around its central star.

The Planets of Kepler-90

Let’s take a closer look at the eight planets that make up the Kepler-90 system:

  1. Kepler-90b: The innermost planet in the system, Kepler-90b is a rocky planet with a scorching surface temperature. It completes an orbit around its star in just 7.5 days.

  2. Kepler-90c: Slightly larger than Earth, Kepler-90c is also a rocky planet. It orbits its star in 9.6 days and is located just outside the orbit of Kepler-90b.

  3. Kepler-90d: Moving further out, we find Kepler-90d. This planet is a gas giant, similar in size to Saturn. It takes about 14.4 days to complete one orbit around its star.

  4. Kepler-90e: Continuing our journey, we come across Kepler-90e. Another rocky planet, it is slightly smaller than Earth and has a shorter orbital period of 17.3 days.

  5. Kepler-90f: Kepler-90f is a larger gas giant, comparable to Jupiter in size. It completes an orbit around the star in approximately 29.7 days.

  6. Kepler-90g: This planet is the closest in size to Earth and has captured the imagination of many. Kepler-90g orbits its star in 59.7 days, making it one of the most intriguing planets in the system.

  7. Kepler-90h: Moving further out, we encounter Kepler-90h. This gas giant is roughly twice the size of Earth and takes around 91.9 days to complete one orbit.

  8. Kepler-90i: Finally, we have Kepler-90i, the outermost planet in the system. This gas giant is about three times the size of Earth and takes a staggering 125.4 days to complete its orbit.

The Significance of Kepler-90

The discovery of the Kepler-90 system has profound implications for our understanding of planetary systems in the universe. By finding a star with a similar arrangement of planets as our own solar system, scientists are gaining valuable insights into the formation and evolution of planetary systems.

The Kepler-90 system also raises questions about the possibility of habitable planets and the potential for extraterrestrial life. While none of the planets in this system are currently considered habitable, the diversity of planetary types and sizes suggests that there may be other star systems out there that could harbor life.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the Kepler-90 system is a stellar marvel, with its eight planets orbiting a star in a delicate cosmic dance. This discovery not only expands our understanding of the universe but also ignites our imagination about the possibility of other habitable worlds beyond our own solar system. As we continue to explore the depths of space, the Kepler-90 system stands as a testament to the wonders that await us in the vast expanse of the cosmos.

So, let us gaze toward the stars, inspired by the Kepler-90 system, and continue our quest to unravel the mysteries of the universe.
