Books That Will Make You Question the Meaning of Life: Existential Reads


In a world filled with distractions and superficialities, sometimes we find ourselves longing for a deeper understanding of life and our place in it. We seek answers to the age-old questions that have plagued humanity since the dawn of time. What is the meaning of life? Do we have a purpose? Is there such a thing as free will? These existential inquiries have captivated philosophers, theologians, and thinkers throughout history, and the quest for answers continues today.

If you find yourself pondering these profound questions, you’re not alone. Many great minds have delved into the depths of existentialism, grappling with the complexities of human existence. In this blog post, we will explore a selection of books that will not only make you question the meaning of life but also offer insights, perspectives, and thought-provoking ideas that might just change the way you see the world.

1. ‘Man’s Search for Meaning’ by Viktor E. Frankl:
In this timeless classic, Viktor Frankl shares his experiences as a Holocaust survivor and explores the search for meaning in life. Through his personal story and reflections, Frankl argues that the pursuit of meaning is the ultimate driving force behind human existence.

2. ‘The Stranger’ by Albert Camus:
Camus’s absurdist novel follows the story of Meursault, a detached and indifferent protagonist who becomes embroiled in a murder case. As the narrative unfolds, Meursault’s existential journey challenges societal norms and forces the reader to confront the absurdity of life.

3. ‘The Myth of Sisyphus’ by Albert Camus:
In this philosophical essay, Camus explores the concept of the absurd and confronts the ultimate question of suicide. He argues that life is essentially meaningless, yet we are compelled to find our own meaning and embrace the absurdity of our existence.

4. ‘Thus Spoke Zarathustra’ by Friedrich Nietzsche:
Nietzsche’s magnum opus delves into the concept of the ‘Ubermensch’ or the ‘Superman.’ Through a series of parables and proclamations, Nietzsche challenges traditional morality and advocates for a new way of living, free from societal constraints.

5. ‘The Unbearable Lightness of Being’ by Milan Kundera:
Kundera’s novel explores the complexities of love, sex, and existence. Set against the backdrop of the political turmoil in Czechoslovakia, the story follows four main characters as they navigate the weight of their choices and the seemingly insignificant nature of life.

6. ‘The Stranger in the Woods’ by Michael Finkel:
This non-fiction account tells the extraordinary story of Christopher Knight, who lived alone in the Maine woods for 27 years, completely detached from society. Through Knight’s tale, Finkel raises questions about the nature of solitude, human connection, and the meaning of freedom.

7. ‘The Trial’ by Franz Kafka:
Kafka’s novel follows the protagonist Josef K. as he is arrested and put on trial for a crime that is never revealed to him. Through the absurd and surreal events that unfold, Kafka explores themes of guilt, bureaucracy, and the inherent absurdity of the human condition.

These books are just a starting point on your existential journey. Each one offers a unique perspective and invites you to question the meaning of life. Whether you find solace, confusion, or enlightenment within their pages, they are sure to provoke deep thought and contemplation.

Remember, the meaning of life is a deeply personal and subjective matter. These books are not intended to provide definitive answers, but rather to encourage introspection and self-discovery. So, dive into these existential reads, embrace the questions they raise, and embark on a journey of exploration and enlightenment.
