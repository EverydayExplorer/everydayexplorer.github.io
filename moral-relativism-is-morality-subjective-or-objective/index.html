Moral Relativism: Is Morality Subjective or Objective?


One of the oldest and most debated issues in philosophy is the question of whether morality is subjective or objective. This question lies at the core of moral relativism, a philosophical stance that argues that moral judgments are not universally true but rather depend on the individual or society that holds them. In contrast, proponents of moral objectivism argue that morality is independent of personal opinions and beliefs. In this blog post, we will explore the concepts of moral relativism and moral objectivism, examining their strengths and weaknesses and considering the implications they have on our understanding of ethics.

Understanding Moral Relativism

Moral relativism posits that what is morally right or wrong can vary from person to person or from society to society. According to this view, there are no objective or absolute moral standards that can be applied universally; instead, moral judgments are shaped by cultural norms, individual values, and personal experiences. For the moral relativist, morality is subjective and contingent upon cultural relativism.

Cultural Relativism

Cultural relativism is a specific version of moral relativism that argues that moral values are determined by the cultural context in which they arise. Under this perspective, what may be considered morally acceptable in one culture may be viewed as morally wrong in another. For example, the practice of polygamy may be embraced within certain cultures, while it is widely condemned in others. Cultural relativism challenges the notion of an objective moral truth, asserting that morality is a product of culture and societal norms.

Subjective Relativism

Subjective relativism is another variant of moral relativism that focuses on the individual’s perspective. According to this view, moral judgments are entirely subjective and dependent on personal beliefs and preferences. Each individual is free to shape their own moral code based on their own values, experiences, and beliefs. Subjective relativism suggests that there are no universal moral principles that apply to everyone; instead, individuals should respect the moral perspectives of others.

Critiques of Moral Relativism

While moral relativism offers a flexible and inclusive approach to ethics, it is not without criticism. One of the most common criticisms of moral relativism is that it ultimately leads to moral skepticism â€“ the belief that there are no objective moral truths. Critics argue that this can have dangerous consequences, as it undermines the ability to condemn acts that are universally considered immoral, such as genocide or torture.

Moreover, the idea that morality is entirely subjective raises serious questions about moral progress. If morality is simply a matter of personal or cultural opinion, how can we ever argue for moral improvement or challenge immoral practices? Moral relativism can also lead to a sense of moral relativism, where individuals may become indifferent to ethical issues, as there are no objective standards to guide them.

Moral Objectivism: An Alternative Approach

In contrast to moral relativism, moral objectivism posits that there are objective moral truths that exist independently of personal opinions or cultural norms. Objectivists argue that certain moral principles are universally valid and binding on all individuals irrespective of cultural differences or personal beliefs. These moral principles are presumed to have an objective reality that can be discovered through reason and reflection.

Metaethical Objectivism

Metaethical objectivism is a philosophical position that argues for the existence of objective moral truths. It suggests that objective moral facts can be discovered and understood through rational inquiry. Proponents of metaethical objectivism argue that moral principles are grounded in objective facts about the nature of the world or human beings. For example, the principle of not causing unnecessary harm can be grounded in the inherent value of human life and the desire to minimize suffering.

Normative Objectivism

Normative objectivism takes a further step and argues that there are specific moral principles that are objectively true. Normative objectivists believe that moral truths exist independently of individual perspectives and cultural norms. They argue that individuals have a moral obligation to act in accordance with these objective moral principles, regardless of personal feelings or societal customs.

Conclusion

The debate between moral relativism and moral objectivism has been ongoing for centuries, and both perspectives offer unique insights into the nature of morality. Moral relativism highlights the role of cultural and personal subjectivity in shaping moral judgments, while moral objectivism emphasizes the existence of objective moral truths that can be rationally discerned.

Ultimately, whether morality is subjective or objective is a complex and multifaceted question that may never receive a definitive answer. However, the exploration of these contrasting viewpoints allows us to better understand the diverse ways in which individuals and societies conceive and approach ethical issues. By engaging in thoughtful discussions and critical reflection, we can continue to deepen our understanding of morality and its implications on our lives and society as a whole.
