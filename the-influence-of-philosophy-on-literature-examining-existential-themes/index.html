The Influence of Philosophy on Literature: Examining Existential Themes


Philosophy and literature have always been deeply intertwined, influencing each other in myriad ways. One of the most impactful philosophical movements on literature is existentialism, a school of thought that explores the meaning of human existence and the individual’s experience in the world. Existential themes can be found in a wide range of literary works, shaping characters, plots, and narratives in significant ways.

Existentialism emerged in the 19th and 20th centuries as a response to the growing sense of disillusionment and alienation in modern society. Philosophers like Søren Kierkegaard, Friedrich Nietzsche, and Jean-Paul Sartre delved into questions of existence, freedom, and the nature of reality, asserting that individuals must create their own meaning and values in a seemingly indifferent universe. This philosophy had a profound impact on literature, particularly in the genres of novel, short story, and drama.

One of the key existential themes that permeates literature is the idea of the absurdity of life. Existentialist writers often depict characters who grapple with feelings of meaninglessness, isolation, and despair in a world that can seem chaotic and senseless. In works like Albert Camus’ ‘The Stranger’ and Franz Kafka’s ‘The Metamorphosis,’ protagonists confront the absurdity of their existence and the arbitrary nature of human experience.

Existentialism also explores the concept of freedom and responsibility. Existentialist writers frequently depict characters who are faced with difficult choices and must take ownership of their actions and decisions. This theme is central to Jean-Paul Sartre’s play ‘No Exit,’ in which three characters are trapped in a room together for eternity, forced to confront their own choices and the consequences of their actions.

Another key existential theme in literature is the search for authenticity and individuality. Existentialist writers often explore the struggle to find one’s true self in a world that can feel stifling and conformist. In works like Virginia Woolf’s ‘To the Lighthouse’ and J.D. Salinger’s ‘The Catcher in the Rye,’ characters grapple with questions of identity and authenticity, seeking to live a life that is true to their own values and beliefs.

Existential themes also manifest in the portrayal of relationships and the dynamics of human connection. Existentialist writers explore how individuals navigate the complexities of love, friendship, and family in a world that can feel alienating and uncertain. In works like Fyodor Dostoevsky’s ‘Notes from Underground’ and Samuel Beckett’s ‘Waiting for Godot,’ characters confront the challenges of relating to others and forming meaningful connections in a seemingly indifferent universe.

Overall, the influence of philosophy on literature, particularly existential themes, has enriched the literary landscape and deepened our understanding of the human experience. By exploring questions of existence, freedom, authenticity, and relationships, existentialist writers have created works that resonate with readers and continue to provoke thought and reflection. In a world that is often characterized by uncertainty and ambiguity, literature infused with existential ideas offers a unique perspective on the complexities of being human.
