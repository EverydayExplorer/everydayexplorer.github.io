The Problem of Evil: Exploring theodicies and Responses


The age-old philosophical conundrum known as the ‘Problem of Evil’ has puzzled thinkers for centuries. It revolves around the apparent contradiction between the existence of evil and the existence of a benevolent, all-powerful God. This complex and deeply thought-provoking issue has spurred a myriad of theodicies and responses aimed at reconciling the coexistence of evil and a loving deity. In this blog, we will delve into the heart of this philosophical dilemma, exploring the various theodicies and responses that have been posited throughout history.

The Problem of Evil

The Problem of Evil arises from the observation of the existence of suffering and malevolence in the world, juxtaposed with the belief in an omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent deity. How can such a deity allow the existence of evil and suffering? This question has troubled theologians, philosophers, and laypeople alike, prompting the development of numerous theodicies—philosophical attempts to justify the ways of God in the face of evil.

Theodicies and Responses

One of the most well-known theodicies is the Free Will Defense, which posits that God, in granting humans free will, also allows the possibility of evil choices and actions. This theodicy argues that genuine moral agency necessitates the ability to choose between good and evil, and that the existence of evil is a consequence of this essential freedom.

Another approach to the Problem of Evil is the Soul-Making Theodicy, which suggests that the presence of evil and suffering in the world serves as a means for the development and maturation of the human soul. According to this view, adversity and hardship are essential for the cultivation of virtues such as compassion, empathy, and resilience.

Additionally, the concept of ‘divine hiddenness’ has been proposed as a response to the Problem of Evil. This viewpoint maintains that God’s apparent absence or silence in the face of evil is a deliberate aspect of divine interaction, intended to foster genuine relationships and moral growth in individuals.

Exploring further, the Existentialist response to the Problem of Evil emphasizes the inherent absurdity and irrationality of human existence, positing that the human condition inherently involves confronting the reality of suffering and malevolence. This perspective calls for an acceptance of the ambiguity and uncertainty of life, rather than seeking definitive answers to the problem of evil.
