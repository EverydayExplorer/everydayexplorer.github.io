Basics of Trapping and Snaring in Wilderness Survival


When it comes to wilderness survival, one of the most essential skills to possess is the ability to find food. In a survival situation, finding and catching food can be challenging, especially if you don’t have the necessary tools. That’s where trapping and snaring techniques come into play. Trapping and snaring are age-old methods of catching animals for sustenance and have been used by humans for thousands of years. In this blog post, we are going to explore the basics of trapping and snaring in wilderness survival.

Why trap or snare?

Trapping and snaring can be effective methods of catching wild game for several reasons. Firstly, they require minimal tools and equipment, making it an ideal option in a survival situation where resources are limited. You can create effective traps and snares using basic materials found in the wilderness such as sticks, ropes, and natural cordage.

Secondly, trapping and snaring are passive methods of hunting. Unlike actively stalking and pursuing game, traps and snares can work while you focus on other essential survival tasks like shelter building or water collection. This allows you to maximize your efforts and conserve energy.

Lastly, traps and snares have the potential to catch a wide range of animal species, depending on the size and type of trap used. This versatility increases your chances of a successful catch and provides a diverse source of food.

Different types of traps and snares

There are a variety of traps and snares that can be employed in wilderness survival, each designed to target a specific type of animal. Let’s explore some of the most common ones:

  1. Deadfall trap: This trap is designed to crush the animal by using a heavy object that is triggered to fall when the animal touches the bait or disturbs the trigger mechanism.

  2. Pitfall trap: A pitfall trap involves digging a hole in the ground and covering it with foliage or other natural materials. When an animal unknowingly steps on the covering, it falls into the pit, making it an effective trap for creatures that primarily move on the ground.

  3. Snares: Snares are simple loops made of cordage or wire that are strategically placed along animal trails or near bait. When the animal walks through the snare, it tightens around their neck, effectively trapping them.

  4. Spring snare: Similar to regular snares, spring snares are triggered by a loaded spring mechanism that tightens the loop when the animal disturbs the bait.

  5. Fish traps: Fish traps are designed to catch fish in bodies of water. They can be made using baskets, nets, or even carved-out logs, and are placed in strategic areas to intercept fish as they swim.

These are just a few examples of traps and snares that can be used in wilderness survival. The effectiveness of each trap depends on several factors such as bait selection, trap placement, and knowledge of animal behavior.

Considerations and ethical aspects

While trapping and snaring can be effective methods of catching food in a survival situation, it is important to consider the ethical aspects. It is crucial to remember that these techniques are meant for survival purposes only, not for recreational hunting or indiscriminate trapping. Respect for wildlife and the environment should always be prioritized.

Additionally, it is vital to familiarize yourself with local regulations and laws regarding trapping and snaring. Some areas may require permits or have specific rules in place to protect certain species. Always prioritize conservation efforts and ensure your actions are legal and environmentally responsible.

Conclusion

In conclusion, trapping and snaring are valuable skills to learn for wilderness survival. These techniques can provide you with a passive means of catching food, allowing you to conserve energy and focus on other important survival tasks. By understanding the different types of traps and snares available, as well as considering ethical aspects, you can add an effective tool to your survival skill set. Remember to always prioritize the well-being of wildlife and practice responsible trapping techniques. With knowledge, practice, and respect, trapping and snaring can be valuable additions to your wilderness survival repertoire.
