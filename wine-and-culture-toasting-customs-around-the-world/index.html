Wine and Culture: Toasting Customs Around the World


In many cultures around the world, toasting with a glass of wine is a tradition that signifies celebration, camaraderie, and unity. From ancient rituals to modern-day practices, toasting customs vary greatly from one country to another, offering a glimpse into the diverse cultures and histories that surround the enjoyment of wine.

Let’s take a tour around the world and explore some of the most fascinating and unique toasting traditions:

1. France: The birthplace of wine, France has a long-standing tradition of toasting called ’trinquer.’ When raising a glass, it is common to make direct eye contact with everyone at the table and say ‘santé’ or ‘à votre santé,’ meaning ’to your health.’ In French culture, toasting is seen as a way to show respect and goodwill.

2. Italy: Italian toasting customs are deeply rooted in the country’s rich history. Italians often clink their glasses together while saying ‘salute’ (pronounced sah-LOOT-eh), meaning ‘health.’ It is important to maintain eye contact during the toast as a sign of sincerity and friendship.

3. Germany: Germans take their toasting seriously, and it is customary to look each person in the eye as you clink your glasses together. To say ‘cheers’ in German, you would say ‘prost!’ Short and simple, ‘prost’ is often accompanied by a firm handshake or a friendly embrace.

4. Spain: In Spain, the art of toasting is called ‘brindis’ and is an integral part of social gatherings. The Spanish saying ‘¡Salud!’ is used to toast to good health and is often followed by a second toast with the phrase ‘¡Arriba, abajo, al centro, pa’ dentro!’ which means ‘Up, down, to the center, inside!’

5. China: In Chinese culture, toasting is an important gesture of respect and honor. When toasting, it is customary to hold your glass with both hands as a sign of respect for the person you are toasting to. The host usually initiates the toasts, and it is considered impolite to decline a toast.

6. Japan: In Japan, toasting is called ‘kanpai’ and is often done with sake, a traditional Japanese rice wine. Kanpai is a significant part of Japanese culture and is usually accompanied by a slight bow. It is polite to wait for the host to initiate the toast before taking your first sip.

7. Russia: Russian toasting is a grand affair that involves long speeches and heartfelt wishes. When toasting, it is common for everyone to stand up, clink glasses, and say ‘za zdorovye,’ which means ’to health.’ Russians believe that the more toasts, the better the celebration.

8. United States: In the United States, toasting customs are diverse and can vary depending on the occasion and the region. A common toast is ‘cheers,’ with clinking glasses and looking each person in the eye. Americans often use toasts as an opportunity to celebrate milestones or express gratitude.

These are just a few examples of the many toasting customs that exist around the world. So, the next time you raise a glass, take a moment to appreciate the cultural significance behind the act of toasting. Whether you’re in France, Italy, Germany, or any other part of the world, the act of raising a glass of wine is a universal symbol of celebration and unity.

Not only do these toasting customs showcase the diverse traditions of different cultures, but they also remind us of the importance of connection and community. So, let us raise our glasses and toast to the richness of wine and culture that brings people together from all corners of the globe.

Cheers!
