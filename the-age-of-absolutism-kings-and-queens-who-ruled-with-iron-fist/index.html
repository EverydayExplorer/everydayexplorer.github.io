The Age of Absolutism: Kings and Queens Who Ruled with Iron Fist


The Age of Absolutism was a period in European history that lasted roughly from 1550 to 1800. However, it is important to note that absolute monarchs have ruled outside of Europe and during different periods in human history. For example, the Egyptian Pharaohs ruled with absolute control and under the belief in a divine right [1].

During the Age of Absolutism, several monarchs reigned with an iron fist, exercising absolute control over their realms. These rulers held immense power and authority, often claiming their right to rule as a divine mandate. Let’s explore some of the notable kings and queens who ruled during this era.

Louis XIV of France

One of the most famous absolute monarchs of the Age of Absolutism was Louis XIV of France. He reigned from 1643 to 1715 and is often referred to as the ‘Sun King.’ Louis XIV centralized power in his own hands, establishing an elaborate court at the Palace of Versailles. He believed in the divine right of kings and famously declared, ‘L’état, c’est moi’ (I am the state). Louis XIV’s reign was characterized by his efforts to consolidate royal power, expand French territory, and promote French culture and arts [4].

Peter the Great of Russia

Peter the Great was another influential absolute monarch who ruled during the Age of Absolutism. He reigned as the Tsar of Russia from 1682 to 1725. Peter the Great implemented sweeping reforms to modernize Russia and transform it into a major European power. He centralized authority, modernized the military, promoted education and industry, and expanded Russia’s borders. Peter the Great’s reign marked a significant turning point in Russian history, as he sought to bring Russia into the modern age [1].

Frederick the Great of Prussia

Frederick the Great, also known as Frederick II, was the King of Prussia from 1740 to 1786. He is often regarded as one of the most enlightened absolute monarchs. Frederick the Great implemented administrative, legal, and educational reforms, promoting religious tolerance and supporting the arts and sciences. He also expanded Prussia’s territory through military conquests. Frederick the Great’s reign exemplified the concept of ’enlightened absolutism,’ where rulers aimed to improve the well-being of their subjects while maintaining absolute power [4].

Maria Theresa of Austria

Maria Theresa was the Archduchess of Austria and Queen of Hungary and Bohemia from 1740 to 1780. She faced significant challenges during her reign, including the War of Austrian Succession and the Seven Years’ War. Maria Theresa implemented administrative and economic reforms, strengthening the central government and promoting agricultural and industrial development. She also focused on education and welfare reforms. Maria Theresa’s reign marked a period of stability and progress for the Habsburg Empire [1].

Elizabeth I of England

Although not traditionally associated with the Age of Absolutism, Elizabeth I of England ruled with a firm hand during her reign from 1558 to 1603. She was known as the ‘Virgin Queen’ and is considered one of England’s greatest monarchs. Elizabeth I maintained a strong central authority, navigated religious conflicts, and oversaw a period of cultural and economic growth known as the Elizabethan era. Her reign was marked by exploration, the defeat of the Spanish Armada, and the flourishing of English literature and theater [1].

Conclusion

The Age of Absolutism witnessed the rule of kings and queens who exercised absolute control over their realms. Monarchs like Louis XIV, Peter the Great, Frederick the Great, Maria Theresa, and Elizabeth I left a lasting impact on their respective countries and shaped the course of European history. While their methods of governance varied, these rulers all sought to consolidate power and promote the interests of their nations. The Age of Absolutism represents a fascinating period in history, showcasing the complexities of political authority and the ambitions of monarchs who ruled with an iron fist.
