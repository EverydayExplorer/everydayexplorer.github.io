Surviving a Snakebite: What to Do in the Wilderness


When it comes to spending time in the great outdoors, it’s important to be prepared for any situation, including the possibility of a snakebite. While encountering a snake in the wilderness may be rare, it’s crucial to know how to handle such an emergency as it can be a matter of life and death. In this blog post, we will discuss the steps you need to take to survive a snakebite in the wilderness, providing practical tips and advice that could save your life.

1. Stay calm and assess the situation

The first and most important thing to do when you or someone you’re with gets bitten by a snake is to remain calm. Panicking will only worsen the situation and increase your heart rate, which can cause the venom to circulate more quickly. Take a moment to assess the snake and try to remember its appearance, as this information will be useful for medical professionals.

2. Call for help and seek medical assistance

If you have cell service, call for emergency help immediately. Inform the dispatcher that you or someone else has been bitten by a snake and provide them with your exact location. If you don’t have cell service, it’s essential to seek help as soon as possible. If you’re with someone, send them to get help while you stay with the injured person.

3. Keep the bite victim still and immobilize the wound

It is crucial to keep the bite victim as still as possible to prevent the venom from spreading quickly throughout their body. Encourage them to lie down and immobilize the bitten limb using a sling or any other makeshift device you can find. Keeping the wound below the level of the heart can also help slow the venom circulation.

4. Remove any tight clothing or jewelry

If the bite occurred on an extremity, such as an arm or leg, it’s necessary to remove any tight clothing or jewelry around the area immediately. This is to ensure that if swelling occurs, it won’t be constricted, causing additional complications and pain.

5. Clean the wound

While it may be tempting to try to suck out the venom, this is not recommended as it can lead to additional infections. Instead, clean the wound using soap and water if available. If not, wiping it with a clean cloth or bandage will suffice.

6. Apply a pressure immobilization bandage

To slow the venom from spreading further, apply a pressure immobilization bandage (PIB) over the wound. Start a few inches above the site of the bite and wrap the bandage firmly, but not too tightly, up the affected limb. The bandage should be applied similar to how you would wrap a sprain. This technique helps prevent the venom from reaching vital organs, thus buying more time until medical help arrives.

7. Monitor vital signs

While waiting for medical assistance, it’s crucial to monitor the bite victim’s vital signs, including their breathing, pulse, and level of consciousness. Note any changes and be prepared to perform CPR or rescue breaths if necessary.

8. Do not use ice or apply a tourniquet

Contrary to popular belief, ice and tourniquets should not be used in the case of a snakebite. They can do more harm than good by causing tissue damage or limiting blood flow to the affected area, aggravating the situation.

Conclusion

Encountering a snakebite in the wilderness can be a frightening experience, but with the right knowledge and preparedness, survival is possible. It’s essential to stay calm, seek immediate medical assistance, and immobilize the wounded limb. Remember, many snakebites in the wilderness are non-venomous, but it’s crucial to treat all snakebites seriously and follow the appropriate steps. By being prepared and knowing what to do, you increase your chances of survival and a safe return from your outdoor adventures.

Remember, the information provided here is for informational purposes only and should not replace professional medical advice.
