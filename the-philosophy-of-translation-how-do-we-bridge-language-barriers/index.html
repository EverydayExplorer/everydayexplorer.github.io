The Philosophy of Translation: How Do We Bridge Language Barriers?


Translation is something that has been around for centuries, but it is only now that it is growing in importance. Today, translation can make or break any business operations. From multinational corporations to small startups, effective communication with international clients is crucial. But, the question remains: how do we bridge the language barriers? This is a philosophy of translation.

The Philosophy of Translation is all about the concept of “bracketing”. Bracketing refers to the suspension of judgments and one’s preconceptions about the text that needs to be translated. In simple words, it means to put aside our own interpretations and to enter into the author’s world of meaning. This approach is essential in creating accurate translations of the text that truly convey the author’s intended meaning to the reader.

Translation has always been a point of contention between purists who believe in literal word-for-word translations and those who prefer a more subjective approach. In the past, the main aim of translation was just to convey the meaning of the text without necessarily respecting the style, cultural context, or the language nuances of the original text.

However, the modern approach towards translation has shifted towards keeping the original author’s style, cultural context, and language nuances intact. This delves into the theory of cultural translation where it is essential to maintain the cultural context of the author and provide a better understanding to the reader by replacing culturally exclusive references with ones that are more relatable to the target audience.

Furthermore, the process of translation involves going beyond the mere interpretation of language and entering into the text’s culture, social function, and meaning. Understanding and analyzing the source text are important in order to create a translation that is faithful to the author’s original work. The translator’s task is to comprehend not just the language but also the cultural significance behind the text.

Translation is more than just transferring words from one language to another. In fact, translation is an art in itself, with the ability to extract the essence of one language and express it in another. The primary goal of translation is to convey the original author’s message to the target audience accurately while maintaining the original content’s essence. This process involves a deep understanding of both languages involved and an intimate knowledge of the culture.

In conclusion, we can say that the philosophy of translation occupies a significant position in our contemporary world of globalization. Though translation is primarily about changing words from one language to another, it is also bridging the gap between cultures and fostering better communication between individuals from different societies. The importance of correct translations is indescribable in today’s rapidly changing global landscape, and it underestimates the goal of helping to foster better communication and collaboration between cultures.
