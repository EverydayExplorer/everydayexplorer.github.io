Restoring Voting Rights for Felons


One of the fundamental principles of a democratic society is that every citizen has the right to vote. It is the cornerstone of our political system, ensuring that every voice is heard and that every opinion counts. However, this right is not extended to all citizens, particularly those who have been convicted of a felony. According to the Sentencing Project, an estimated 6.1 million people are unable to vote because of felony convictions. The disenfranchisement of such a large population has serious implications for the health and inclusiveness of our democracy.

Voting is a fundamental right that should not be taken away from anyone, regardless of their past actions, especially since many individuals who have been incarcerated are often from marginalized and disenfranchised communities. Felons are disproportionately from low-income neighborhoods, have limited access to education and employment opportunities, and are often people of color. They are also more likely to be impacted by policies such as mass incarceration, which have significantly contributed to the disenfranchisement of this population.

This issue is becoming increasingly important as the United States faces a pivotal moment in its history. There is a growing awareness and recognition of the systemic racism that continues to exist in our society. While it is essential to address larger structural issues, restoring voting rights to felons is a crucial step towards a more inclusive and equitable democracy. It is a measure that acknowledges the need to rebuild trust and civic engagement in marginalized communities and move towards the realization of our democracy’s ideals.

Fortunately, there are signs of progress in this area. In recent years, several states have made significant strides towards restoring the right to vote for felons. For example, in Florida, a ballot initiative passed in 2018 that allowed felons to vote after completing their sentences. In Maryland, the state legislature passed a bill that restored voting rights to felons. Maine and Vermont have never disenfranchised convicted felons. These states have recognized the importance of restoring voting rights to this population.

However, there is still widespread resistance to this idea. Many politicians and policymakers view restoring voting rights to felons as a political issue instead of a fundamental right issue. Some argue that felons are not deserving of such rights and that restoring their right to vote is rewarding them for bad behavior. Others claim that felons are more likely to vote for Democrats and, therefore, restoring their right to vote is a partisan issue that will benefit one political party over another.

Yet, denying felons the right to vote has proven to have adverse effects on the individual and the community. By denying convicted felons the right to vote, we are removing their voice from our democracy and further marginalizing them from society. Research has shown that the restoration of voting rights to felons is associated with lower rates of reoffending, increased civic engagement, and social integration.

The restoration of voting rights to felons is also in line with international human rights standards. The United Nations has recognized the importance of restoring voting rights to felons as a way to promote human dignity, social justice, and equality. The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights issued a report in 2015 calling on the United States to restore voting rights to felons at all levels of government.

In conclusion, restoring voting rights to felons is not only a fundamental right issue but also a critical step towards building a more inclusive democracy. Disenfranchisement of this population has an adverse effect on individual and community well-being and hinders the promotion of human rights and social justice. As a society, it is our duty to recognize the importance of this issue, put aside political differences, and take steps towards restoring the right to vote for felons. Such measures would promote a healthy and strong democracy that includes all voices, irrespective of their past histories.
