The Mythology of the Aztec Calendar: Time, Prophecies, and Sacred Symbols


From the ancient civilization of the Aztecs comes a remarkable calendar system that not only served as a means to measure time but also held deep spiritual and mythological significance. The Aztec calendar, also known as the Sun Stone or the Aztec Sun Stone, provides us with a fascinating insight into the beliefs and prophecies of this Mesoamerican culture.

The Aztec Calendar System

The Aztec calendar system was incredibly complex and intricate, reflecting the importance of time and its relationship with the divine. At the core of this system were two separate calendars: the xiuhpōhualli (the ‘year count’) and the tonalpōhualli (the ‘day count’).

The xiuhpōhualli, composed of 365 days, was the calendar used to measure agricultural cycles and determine important religious events. It consisted of 18 months, each lasting 20 days, with the remaining 5 days regarded as unlucky and devoid of gods. These 5 unlucky days, known as nemōntemi, were believed to be a time of potential chaos and danger.

On the other hand, the tonalpōhualli comprised 260 days and was intricately linked to the concept of destiny. Each day of this calendar system had its own energy, represented by a combination of one of the 20 named days and one of the 13 numbers. This created a cycle of unique day energies that repeated every 260 days.

It is worth noting that the Aztec calendar was not limited to tracking time but also served as a divination tool. Priests and scholars would interpret the signs and symbols associated with each day to gain insight into the past, present, and future.

The Aztec Creation Myth

At the heart of the mythology surrounding the Aztec calendar is the creation myth, which explains the origin of time and the importance of sacrifice. According to Aztec belief, the universe went through multiple cycles of creation and destruction, and we are currently living in the Fifth Sun, the fifth epoch of this cosmic cycle.

In this creation myth, the gods Quetzalcoatl and Tezcatlipoca led the creation of the world. The gods decided to create a new cosmic era after the previous Four Suns were destroyed. The new era, known as the Fifth Sun, would be marked by a significant event called the ‘Binding of the Years.’

During this event, the gods decide to offer their own blood as sacrifice to create the sun. They gathered around a ceremonial fire and, using a blade made of obsidian, each god pierced his own flesh to let his blood drip into the fire. Quetzalcoatl, however, demanded a sacrifice from the gods themselves. He broke off his foot and threw it into the fire, symbolizing the sacrifice that would bring about the Fifth Sun.

The Aztec Calendar Stone: The Sun Stone

One of the most iconic representations of the Aztec calendar is the Aztec Calendar Stone, also known as the Sun Stone. This massive circular stone, measuring over 3 meters in diameter, was discovered in 1790 beneath the Zócalo, the main square of Mexico City.

The Sun Stone is beautifully carved with intricate symbols and figures that represent various mythological elements. At the center of the stone is the face of Tonatiuh, the sun god, with its tongue protruding and a solar disk on its headdress. Surrounding the central figure are concentric rings filled with complex glyphs and images.

The outermost ring of the Sun Stone represents the 20 days of the Aztec month, with each day symbolized by its own distinctive glyph. The next ring shows various deities and significant symbols related to agriculture, war, and sacrifice. The intricate carvings found on the Sun Stone provide us with valuable insights into the beliefs and rituals of the Aztec civilization.

Prophecies and Calendar Endings

One of the most captivating elements associated with the Aztec calendar is the prophecies and predictions it was believed to hold. According to Aztec mythology, every 52 years, a cosmic cycle called the ‘binding of the years’ would come to an end. This was considered a time of great uncertainty and fear, as it marked a time when the universe stood at the brink of destruction.

To prevent this catastrophe, the Aztecs believed it was necessary to conduct various rituals and sacrifices to ensure the continuity of life. During these times of transition, the Aztecs would extinguish all fires, and people would climb to a high point to observe the recreation of the universe. Once the ritual was completed, the fires would be reignited, symbolizing the survival of humanity and the renewal of life.

These prophecies and the symbolism of the Aztec calendar gained renewed interest in modern times, particularly in relation to the end of the Mayan Long Count calendar in 2012. Many saw this as a parallel to the Aztec belief in periodic cycles of creation and destruction. However, it is essential to note that contemporary interpretations often misinterpreted the significance of the end date as a doomsday prediction.

The Legacy of the Aztec Calendar

The Aztec calendar continues to captivate and intrigue people worldwide, as it offers a fascinating glimpse into the rich mythology and spiritual beliefs of this ancient civilization. Its complex and symbolic nature reflects the Aztec worldview, where time, sacrifice, and the divine were intricately interconnected.

Today, the Aztec calendar is celebrated as a symbol of Mexican cultural heritage and has become an iconic representation of the Aztec civilization. Its images and symbols can be found in Mexican art, architecture, and even popular culture, highlighting its enduring cultural significance.

In conclusion, the Aztec calendar stands as a testament to the ingenuity and complexity of ancient civilizations. Its profound mythological roots, prophecies, and sacred symbols provide us with invaluable knowledge about the beliefs, rituals, and cosmology of the Aztec people. The study of the Aztec calendar opens up a whole new avenue of understanding their sophisticated civilization and the importance they placed on timekeeping and spiritual connection.
