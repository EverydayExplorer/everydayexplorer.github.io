Creating Memorable Characters: Lessons from Classic Comedy Sketches


Creating memorable characters is key to writing successful comedy sketches. The most successful sketches have characters that are unique, memorable, and relatable to the audience. Classic comedy sketches have been used to create some of the most memorable characters in pop culture. In this blog post, we’ll examine some of the best comedy sketches and the memorable characters that were created from them.

1. The Not Ready for Prime Time Players (Saturday Night Live)

In 1975, Saturday Night Live debuted and became an instant hit. One of the reasons for its success was the creation of the Not Ready for Prime Time Players. This cast included some of the biggest names in comedy, including John Belushi, Dan Aykroyd, and Gilda Radner. Each of these actors created memorable characters that helped make Saturday Night Live a cultural phenomenon.

John Belushi’s character, Samurai Futaba, was a prime example of a memorable character. The Samurai was a parody of martial arts movies and featured Belushi as a bumbling warrior who could barely wield his sword. The Samurai was funny because he was out of place in the modern world. His outdated views and mannerisms made him a perfect target for satire.

Dan Aykroyd’s character, Elwood Blues, was also a classic. Elwood was one half of the musical duo, The Blues Brothers, and a devout Catholic. His devotion to religion provided the perfect foil for the bad boy persona of his partner, Jake Blues. Aykroyd’s deadpan delivery and straight-laced personality made him a perfect choice for the character.

2. The Coneheads (Saturday Night Live)

Another classic example of memorable characters from Saturday Night Live are The Coneheads. The Coneheads were a family of aliens who had landed on Earth and were trying to blend in with the human population. The family consisted of Beldar, Prymaat, and their daughter Connie. The Coneheads were memorable because of their distinctive look and their unique way of talking.

The Coneheads were a parody of the idea that aliens were trying to infiltrate human society. The creators of the sketch capitalized on the fear that some people had of the unknown and turned it into comedy. The Coneheads were so popular that they made appearances on multiple episodes of Saturday Night Live, as well as their own movie.

3. The Two Ronnies (The Two Ronnies)

The Two Ronnies was a British comedy sketch show that aired from 1971 to 1987. The show featured two comedians, Ronnie Barker and Ronnie Corbett, who each played multiple characters in each sketch. The Two Ronnies was famous for creating memorable characters that were both funny and relatable.

One of the most memorable characters from The Two Ronnies was Charley Farley, a character played by Ronnie Barker. Charley was a traveling salesman who could sell anything from brushes to wallpaper. His charisma and charm made him a beloved character and his catchphrase, “I know my place,” became a cultural phenomenon.

4. The Honeymooners (The Honeymooners)

The Honeymooners was a classic television show that aired from 1955 to 1956. The show featured Jackie Gleason as Ralph Kramden, a New York City bus driver, and his wife Alice, played by Audrey Meadows. The show was famous for creating memorable characters and for being one of the first sitcoms to feature working-class characters.

One of the most memorable characters from The Honeymooners was Ralph Kramden. Jackie Gleason created a character that was both funny and relatable. Ralph was a hardworking man who was always trying to make ends meet. His temper and his schemes to get rich quick were the perfect target for comedy.

In conclusion, creating memorable characters is key to writing successful comedy sketches. The characters that come from classic comedy sketches are some of the most beloved and enduring characters in popular culture. By studying these sketches and the characters that were created from them, writers can learn valuable lessons about creating characters that are funny, relatable, and unforgettable.
