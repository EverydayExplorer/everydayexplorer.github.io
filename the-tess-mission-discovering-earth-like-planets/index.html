The TESS Mission: Discovering Earth-like Planets


With hundreds of billions of stars within our Milky Way galaxy, it is not surprising that many of these could have planets orbiting them. The question of whether there are other habitable planets, similar to Earth, has intrigued scientists and astronomers for decades. And now, with the launch of the TESS mission, we are one step closer to finding the answer.

The Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) is a NASA mission designed to discover thousands of exoplanets beyond our solar system. Launched in April 2018, TESS is equipped with four wide-field cameras that scan the sky for signs of planets transiting or passing in front of their host stars. This method, known as the transit method, allows astronomers to detect tiny dips in the brightness of the stars, indicating the presence of an exoplanet.

One of the main goals of the TESS mission is to search for Earth-sized planets in the habitable zone of their star, also known as the Goldilocks zone. This is the region around a star where conditions are just right for liquid water to exist on the planet’s surface, which is considered a vital ingredient for the development of life as we know it. By studying these Earth-like planets, scientists hope to gain insights into the potential for life elsewhere in the universe.

TESS will survey the entire sky, divided into 26 sectors, over a two-year period. Each sector is approximately 24 degrees by 96 degrees in size, covering a total area that is 400 times larger than what was observed by the famous Kepler mission. The wide coverage and sensitivity of TESS allow it to search for exoplanets around a variety of stars, from small red dwarfs to large bright stars.

The TESS mission has already provided exciting discoveries in its first year of operation. One of the most notable findings includes the discovery of TOI 700 d, an Earth-sized exoplanet orbiting within the habitable zone of a small, cool M dwarf star. This discovery has sparked great interest within the scientific community as it represents one of the best candidates for a potentially habitable exoplanet. Additionally, TESS has also identified an intriguing object known as ‘Planet Nine’, which could potentially be a massive planet lurking in the outer edges of our own solar system.

But the TESS mission is not just about discovering exoplanets. It also paves the way for future missions and ground-based observations. Once TESS identifies potential exoplanets, follow-up observations can be conducted by other telescopes, such as the upcoming James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), to study the atmospheres of these planets and determine their compositions. This vital information will help scientists assess the potential habitability of these distant worlds.

Furthermore, TESS data is available to the public, allowing citizen scientists and amateur astronomers to contribute to the mission’s success. By combing through the vast amount of data collected by TESS, individuals from all walks of life can make their own discoveries and contribute to the ever-expanding knowledge of exoplanets.

In conclusion, the TESS mission is revolutionizing our understanding of exoplanets and the potential for life beyond Earth. By scanning the sky and detecting tiny fluctuations in star brightness, TESS has already uncovered numerous exoplanets, some of which are Earth-like and within the habitable zone. These discoveries not only provide insights into the cosmic diversity of planets but also raise intriguing questions about the existence of extraterrestrial life. With more data yet to be analyzed and new missions on the horizon, the TESS mission is undoubtedly an exciting step forward in our quest to discover Earth-like planets and unlock the mysteries of our universe.
