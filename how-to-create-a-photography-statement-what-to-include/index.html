How to Create a Photography Statement: What to Include


Creating a photography statement is an essential part of defining your artistic vision and communicating it effectively to your audience. Whether you are an emerging photographer or an established artist, a well-crafted photography statement can enhance your portfolio, improve your chances of being selected for exhibitions, and help potential clients understand your work better. This guide will walk you through the main components of a photography statement and offer tips on how to make it engaging and meaningful.

Understanding the Purpose of a Photography Statement

Before diving into the specifics of what to include in your photography statement, it’s crucial to understand its purpose. A photography statement serves multiple functions, including:

  • Articulating Your Vision: It provides a platform for you to express your artistic vision, influences, and creative process.
  • Connecting with Your Audience: A well-written statement can help viewers connect with your work on a deeper level, fostering a more emotional response.
  • Enhancing Your Brand: A photography statement can serve as part of your personal brand, helping to differentiate you from other photographers.
  • Facilitating Opportunities: Curators, galleries, and potential clients often look for statements to understand an artist’s intention and the context behind their work.

Elements to Include in Your Photography Statement

1. Introduction: Who You Are

Start your photography statement with a brief introduction about yourself. This section should include:

  • Your Name: Clearly state your name, or the name you go by professionally.
  • Your Background: Mention your education, training, or any relevant experience in photography. This could include workshops, mentorships, or self-taught journeys.
  • Your Location: If relevant, include where you are based, as this can influence your work.

Example:

“I am Jane Doe, a photographer based in Brooklyn, New York, with a degree in Fine Arts from XYZ University. My journey into photography began as a child, capturing moments in my neighborhood, which has shaped my artistic voice today.”

2. Artistic Vision: What Drives You

Next, delve into your artistic vision. This section should explain what inspires your photography and the themes you explore. Consider addressing:

  • Your Influences: Mention other artists, photographers, or movements that have inspired your work.
  • Themes and Subjects: Describe the primary themes, subjects, or concepts you focus on in your photography.
  • Your Unique Perspective: What makes your approach distinct? Share how your background or experiences shape the way you see the world.

Example:

“My work is inspired by the daily lives of urban dwellers, exploring themes of isolation and connection in a bustling city. Influenced by the likes of Henri Cartier-Bresson and Vivian Maier, I aim to capture candid moments that reflect the complexity of human emotions.”

3. Creative Process: How You Work

In this section, you should outline your creative process. This includes:

  • Your Techniques: Discuss any specific techniques or styles you utilize in your photography. Are you drawn to film or digital? Do you prefer staged compositions or candid shots?
  • Your Equipment: Briefly mention the equipment you use, but keep it focused on its relevance to your artistic vision.
  • Your Workflow: Describe how you approach a shoot, from concept to execution, and how you select and edit your images.

Example:

“I often work with natural light, preferring the organic quality it brings to my photographs. Using a Canon DSLR, I focus on spontaneous moments, shooting primarily in black and white to emphasize emotion over distraction. My editing process is minimal, aiming to preserve the authenticity of the captured moment.”

4. Goals: What You Hope to Achieve

Articulate your goals as a photographer. This could include:

  • Personal Goals: What do you hope to achieve in your artistic journey? This might be personal growth or exploring new themes.
  • Audience Connection: Describe what you want your audience to feel or understand when they view your work.
  • Future Aspirations: Mention any future projects, exhibitions, or collaborations you are looking forward to.

Example:

“My goal is to evoke a sense of empathy in my viewers, inviting them to reflect on their own experiences of connection and solitude. I am currently working on a series that explores urban landscapes and their relationship to human presence, with hopes of exhibiting it in local galleries.”

5. Conclusion: A Call to Action

Wrap up your photography statement with a strong conclusion that encourages further engagement. This could include:

  • Invitation to View Your Work: Encourage readers to explore your portfolio or follow your social media for updates.
  • Openness for Dialogue: Express your willingness to connect with viewers, other artists, or potential collaborators.

Example:

“I invite you to explore my portfolio and join me on this journey of visual storytelling. I am always open to dialogue and collaboration, so feel free to reach out to discuss art, photography, or creative ideas.”

Tips for Crafting an Engaging Photography Statement

1. Be Authentic

Your photography statement should reflect your true voice and artistic vision. Avoid jargon or overly complex language. Write as you would speak, allowing your personality to shine through.

2. Keep It Concise

While it’s essential to include all the necessary elements, try to keep your statement concise—preferably between 300 to 500 words. This encourages readers to engage without losing interest.

3. Edit and Revise

Once you’ve drafted your statement, take the time to edit and revise it. Look for clarity, coherence, and flow. Consider asking for feedback from trusted peers or mentors.

4. Update Regularly

As your work evolves, so should your photography statement. Regularly revisit and update it to reflect new themes, techniques, or projects.

5. Use Visual Language

While you are writing, remember that photography is a visual medium. Use descriptive language that evokes imagery and emotions, helping readers visualize your work even without seeing it.

Final Thoughts

Creating a photography statement is a valuable exercise in self-reflection and artistic expression. By articulating who you are, what drives you, your creative process, and your goals, you not only enrich your own understanding of your work but also enhance the experience for your audience. Remember, your photography statement is a living document—embrace the journey of refining and evolving it as you grow as an artist.

Now that you understand the structure and elements of a photography statement, take the time to draft your own. Engage deeply with your artistic vision, and let your statement resonate with those who encounter your work. Your photography statement can be the bridge that connects your vision with the world, so craft it thoughtfully and passionately.
