Reimagining Classic Tales: 10 Retellings That Give New Perspectives


Classics tales have captured the hearts and imaginations of readers for centuries. Stories like Cinderella, Romeo and Juliet, and Alice in Wonderland have become timeless classics, providing us with a sense of magic, wonder, and nostalgia. However, in recent years, there has been a trend in literature to reimagine these classic tales, offering readers a fresh perspective and a new way of looking at these beloved stories. From changing the setting to exploring different characters, these retellings breathe new life into familiar tales. In this blog post, we will explore 10 retellings that give new perspectives, showcasing the power of reimagining classic tales.

1. ‘Cinder’ by Marissa Meyer

In this futuristic retelling of Cinderella, Marissa Meyer takes readers to New Beijing, where a cyborg mechanic named Cinderella must navigate a world filled with prejudice and discrimination. By incorporating elements of science fiction and dystopian societies, Meyer offers a unique twist to the classic fairy tale, highlighting important themes such as identity, self-acceptance, and breaking societal norms.

2. ‘Wicked’ by Gregory Maguire

Gregory Maguire’s ‘Wicked’ takes readers on a journey to the Land of Oz, but this time, the story doesn’t focus on Dorothy and her red slippers. Instead, Maguire delves into the backstory of the Wicked Witch of the West, Elphaba, offering readers a nuanced and complex character study. Through Elphaba’s perspective, we gain a deeper understanding of her motivations, struggles, and the events that led to her infamous role as the villain in the original tale.

3. ‘The Lunar Chronicles’ by Marissa Meyer

Marissa Meyer strikes again with her captivating retelling of classic fairy tales in ‘The Lunar Chronicles’ series. Each book focuses on a different fairy tale, interweaving the stories of Cinderella, Little Red Riding Hood, Rapunzel, and Snow White. Set in a futuristic world where cyborgs, androids, and moon-dwelling Lunars exist, Meyer reimagines these tales and introduces strong, independent heroines who embark on epic adventures.

4. ‘Circe’ by Madeline Miller

Madeline Miller’s ‘Circe’ takes readers on a mythological journey, retelling the story of the infamous sorceress from Greek mythology. In this book, Circe’s character is given depth, complexity, and agency, challenging the perception of her as a one-dimensional villain. Miller explores themes of power, self-discovery, and feminism, offering a fresh perspective on a character who is often relegated to the sidelines in other myths.

5. ‘West’ by Edith Pattou

‘West’ is a reimagining of the classic fairy tale ‘East of the Sun, West of the Moon.’ Edith Pattou takes readers on an enchanted journey to the icy North, where a young girl named Rose sets out on a quest to rescue her beloved from an enchanted polar bear. Pattou’s retelling offers a beautifully written narrative with rich descriptions and a strong emphasis on the value of love, sacrifice, and the importance of following one’s heart.

6. ‘Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead’ by Tom Stoppard

Tom Stoppard’s play ‘Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead’ offers a fresh perspective on Shakespeare’s ‘Hamlet.’ The play follows the story of two minor characters from the original play, Rosencrantz and Guildenstern. Stoppard’s retelling explores the themes of fate, identity, and the search for meaning, while also providing a humorous and thought-provoking commentary on the nature of reality and existence.

7. ‘Wide Sargasso Sea’ by Jean Rhys

‘Wide Sargasso Sea’ is a prequel to Charlotte Brontë’s classic novel ‘Jane Eyre.’ Jean Rhys explores the backstory of the character Bertha Mason, the first wife of Mr. Rochester. Rhys’s retelling offers a feminist critique of the original novel, shedding light on the colonial and racial dynamics at play in the story. Through Bertha’s perspective, the novel explores themes of identity, madness, and the consequences of societal oppression.

8. ‘The Penelopiad’ by Margaret Atwood

Margaret Atwood’s ‘The Penelopiad’ reimagines the story of ‘The Odyssey’ from the perspective of Penelope, Odysseus’s wife. With her signature wit and insight, Atwood explores the forgotten voices of the women in the original tale and offers a feminist critique of the patriarchal society depicted in Greek mythology. Through Penelope’s retelling, readers gain a deeper understanding of her strength, resilience, and the sacrifices she made during Odysseus’s absence.

9. ‘A Thousand Acres’ by Jane Smiley

Jane Smiley’s ‘A Thousand Acres’ is a modern retelling of Shakespeare’s ‘King Lear.’ Set in the American Midwest, the novel follows the lives of three sisters and their complex relationship with their father. Smiley explores themes such as family, betrayal, and the destructive nature of unchecked power. By transplanting the story into a modern setting, Smiley brings new relevance and resonance to the timeless tale.

10. ‘Boy, Snow, Bird’ by Helen Oyeyemi

Helen Oyeyemi’s ‘Boy, Snow, Bird’ reimagines the story of Snow White, offering a mesmerizing exploration of race, identity, and self-discovery. Set in 1950s America, the novel follows the lives of three women - Boy Novak, Snow Whitman, and Bird - as they navigate issues of beauty, belonging, and the consequences of societal expectations. Oyeyemi’s retelling challenges traditional notions of beauty and explores the complexities of racial identity in an engaging and thought-provoking way.

In conclusion, the reimagining of classic tales provides readers with a fresh perspective and offers an opportunity to delve deeper into the themes and characters that have captured our hearts for generations. Whether it’s through science fiction, mythology, or shifting perspectives, these retellings breathe new life into familiar stories and remind us of the power of storytelling. So why not pick up one of these retellings and embark on a journey to discover a classic tale from a whole new perspective?
