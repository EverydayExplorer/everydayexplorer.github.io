The Ethics of Nuclear Weapons


The development of nuclear weapons began in the early half of the 20th century. Scientists, working on the Manhattan Project, created the first nuclear weapon that was used to bomb Hiroshima and Nagasaki, killing over 200,000 people and leaving behind long-lasting destruction that affected future generations. Since then, nuclear weapons have become one of the most powerful and controversial arms available to nation-states in the modern world. The power and potential destruction of such weapons have raised several questions and speculations on their morality, ethics, and legality. This essay will discuss the ethics of nuclear weapons, including the ethical considerations of its development, storage, and use, as well as the implications of nuclear weapons in international relations.

One of the central ethical issues regarding nuclear weapons is their development. Historically, the race to create nuclear weapons was prompted by the imminent threats posed by rival powers during the Cold War era, with the United States, Russia, and other countries investing heavily in their development. The creation of such weapons was driven by both practical and ideological reasons, as well as the fear of a future war that utilized new high-tech warfare tools. One argument against the development of nuclear weapons is the ethical concern over the use of resources for nuclear programs, while other humanitarian priorities remain unaddressed. Indeed, despite their sheer destructive power, nuclear weapons have little to no practical benefit to mankind, aside from the capability to deter other countries from attacking by threatening to retaliate with nuclear strikes.

Another crucial ethical debate around nuclear weapons concerns their possession. While several states have pursued the development of nuclear weapons by investing significant resources for the technology, many international treaties and organizations prohibit the acquisition of such weapons. Such treaties include the Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT), which aims to reduce the spread of nuclear weapons, discourages new countries from pursuing the development of such weapons, and urges the superpowers to reduce their nuclear arsenal. Nevertheless, certain countries, including North Korea, India, and Pakistan, continue to develop their nuclear arsenals in defiance of international treaties, leading to heightened tensions in their respective regions. Such activity of nuclear proliferation remains a significant ethical challenge due to the risks of accidental nuclear war, and the likelihood of rogue states using it for extremist and catastrophic purposes.

Additionally, another ethical issue regarding nuclear weapons is their storage, which includes the safekeeping and accessibility of such weapons by state-authorized personnel. The storage of nuclear weapons must be done under extreme caution and regulations to prevent unauthorized use and ensure their safety. The risk of losing track of nuclear material or its accidental detonation is catastrophic to the world, preventing the proliferation of nuclear weapons is a continuous ethical challenge. The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) monitors and inspects nuclear weapon storage sites to ensure compliance with regulations and promote transparency in the international nuclear security regime.

Finally, the central ethical challenge raised by nuclear weapons concerns their use in war. Given their enormous destructive power, some philosophers argue that the use of nuclear weapons is against humanity and is redundant to the just war theory and international humanitarian law. In other words, weapons of mass destruction, including nuclear weapons, should never be used in warfare because of the indiscriminate death and destruction they inflict on non-combatants and infrastructure. The use of nuclear weapons in any war would violate the fundamental humanitarian principles of humanity, proportionality, and discrimination.

In conclusion, the ethics of nuclear weapons presents a considerable ethical challenge that the international community must address. The development, possession, storage, and use of nuclear weapons have far-reaching moral and ethical implications, affecting not only international relations but the entire global community. While the non-proliferation regime has successfully prevented the spread of nuclear weapons to some extent, the risks of the use of available nuclear weapons technology by rogue states or terrorist organizations are increasing. It is vital for policymakers, scientists, and others to work toward a safe and peaceful world free from nuclear threats through a range of approaches, including nonproliferation, disarmament, transparency, and verification methods.
