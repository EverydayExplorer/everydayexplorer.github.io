The Chemistry of Scented Candles: Fragrance and Combustion


Scented candles have become a popular addition to our homes, creating a cozy and inviting atmosphere. But have you ever wondered about the chemistry behind these delightful fragrances? In this blog post, we will explore the fascinating world of scented candles, focusing on the chemistry of fragrance and combustion.

The Science of Fragrance

The beautiful aroma emitted by scented candles is a result of carefully selected fragrance oils. These oils, often derived from natural sources such as plants or fruits, contain aromatic compounds that give off pleasant smells. Each fragrance oil is a complex mixture of different molecules, each contributing to the overall scent.

One important group of compounds found in fragrance oils is known as esters. Esters are formed by the reaction between an alcohol and an acid, resulting in a pleasant and fruity aroma. For example, the ester ethyl acetate gives off a sweet and fruity smell reminiscent of bananas. Other compounds, such as aldehydes and ketones, also contribute to the overall fragrance of scented candles.

The science of fragrance goes even further with the concept of top, middle, and base notes. These refer to the different levels of volatility of aroma compounds. Top notes are the initial scents that you smell when you light a candle. They are light and volatile, and often include citrus or floral scents. Middle notes are the body of the fragrance and tend to be warmer and spicier. Finally, base notes are the heavy and long-lasting scents that linger even after the candle has been extinguished.

The Chemistry of Combustion

When you light a scented candle, the process of combustion begins. Combustion is a chemical reaction that occurs when a fuel (in this case, the wax) combines with oxygen from the air, producing heat, light, water vapor, and carbon dioxide.

The wax used in candles is typically a combination of different types of hydrocarbons. Hydrocarbons are compounds made up of hydrogen and carbon atoms and are commonly found in natural materials such as plant oils or animal fats. The most commonly used wax in scented candles is paraffin wax, which is derived from petroleum or crude oil.

During combustion, the hydrocarbons in the wax are broken down into simpler molecules. The heat from the flame vaporizes the wax, which then reacts with oxygen in the air. This reaction is exothermic, meaning it releases heat. The oxygen atoms combine with carbon and hydrogen atoms from the wax to form carbon dioxide and water vapor.

In addition to producing heat and light, the combustion of scented candles also releases the aromatic compounds from the fragrance oils. As the wax vaporizes, these compounds are carried along with the rising air currents and released into the surrounding environment. This is what gives scented candles their delightful aroma.

Considerations for Safety and Health

While scented candles can create a delightful atmosphere, it’s important to be mindful of safety and potential health concerns. When burning scented candles, it is recommended to keep the room well-ventilated to minimize the accumulation of potentially harmful combustion byproducts.

Some fragrance oils used in scented candles may contain volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that can contribute to indoor air pollution. To minimize exposure, choose candles made from natural and non-toxic ingredients. Be sure to trim the candle wick regularly to prevent excessive smoke and soot.

In conclusion, scented candles offer not only a cozy ambiance but also a fascinating look into the chemistry of fragrance and combustion. The careful selection of fragrance oils and the science behind combustion contribute to the delightful aromas that fill our homes. By understanding the chemistry behind scented candles, we can better appreciate the science that goes into creating these everyday luxuries.
