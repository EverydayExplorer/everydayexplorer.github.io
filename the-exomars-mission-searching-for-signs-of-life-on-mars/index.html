The ExoMars Mission: Searching for Signs of Life on Mars


The quest to explore the mysteries of the universe has always fascinated humanity. Over the years, numerous space missions have been conducted to unveil the secrets of distant planets and celestial bodies. One such mission that has captured the attention of scientists and space enthusiasts alike is the ExoMars mission - a joint endeavor by the European Space Agency (ESA) and the Russian Space Agency (Roscosmos). The primary aim of the ExoMars mission is to search for signs of past or present life on the red planet - Mars.

Mars, often referred to as Earth’s closest neighbor, has been a subject of immense curiosity for scientists for centuries. With its similarities to Earth and the potential for hosting life, it has become an ideal candidate for further exploration. The ExoMars mission stands at the forefront of the quest to uncover the secrets of the red planet.

The mission comprises two phases. The first phase, named ExoMars 2016, was launched on March 14, 2016. This phase consisted of the Trace Gas Orbiter (TGO) and a landing demonstration module known as Schiaparelli. The TGO is designed to study the Mars atmosphere and search for traces of gases that could indicate the presence of biological or geological activity. Schiaparelli, on the other hand, was a technology demonstrator for landing and entry capabilities.

Though the Schiaparelli lander unfortunately crash-landed during its descent, the ExoMars 2016 mission was able to successfully achieve its primary objective of entering Mars’ orbit. The TGO has been meticulously collecting data, providing valuable insights into the composition of Mars’ atmosphere. By studying trace gases such as methane, the TGO aims to identify potential sources that could be linked to active geological or biological processes.

Building upon the success of ExoMars 2016, the second phase of the mission, ExoMars 2022, is currently under development. Set to launch in 2022, this ambitious phase will focus on the delivery of the Rosalind Franklin rover to the Martian surface. Named after the renowned scientist Rosalind Franklin, who played a crucial role in the discovery of the DNA double-helix structure, this six-wheeled robotic explorer will be equipped with a suite of advanced scientific instruments.

The primary objective of the Rosalind Franklin rover is to search for signs of past or present life on Mars. It will analyze the composition of the Martian soil and rocks, as well as drill beneath the surface to collect samples for further analysis. The rover’s state-of-the-art instruments, including a subsurface drill, a life-searching laboratory, and a panoramic camera, will be pivotal in determining the habitability of Mars and its potential for hosting life.

One of the key aspects of the ExoMars mission is to prioritize planetary protection. As scientists delve deeper into the exploration of Mars, it is crucial to ensure that no terrestrial contamination is introduced to the red planet. Stringent measures are taken to sterilize spacecraft and instruments to prevent unwanted biological contamination. This ensures that any potential signs of life detected on Mars are genuinely Martian in origin, preserving the integrity of future discoveries.

The ExoMars mission also aims to pave the way for future human exploration of Mars. By understanding the planet’s atmospheric composition, geological history, and potential habitability, crucial insights will be gained for planning future manned missions. The data collected by ExoMars will assist in identifying suitable landing sites and developing technologies to support human presence on the red planet.

In conclusion, the ExoMars mission represents an ambitious endeavor to search for signs of life on Mars. With its two-phase approach and advanced instruments, this collaborative effort between ESA and Roscosmos is at the forefront of Mars exploration. By studying the composition of the Martian atmosphere and the potential for habitability, invaluable insights are being gained that could reshape our understanding of the universe and pave the way for future human exploration. As we eagerly await the launch of the ExoMars 2022 phase, the possibilities that lie ahead are truly awe-inspiring.
