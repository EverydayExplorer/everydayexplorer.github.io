The Role of Brettanomyces in Wine: The Funky Side of Fermentation


When it comes to wine, there’s a whole world of flavors and aromas waiting to be explored. While most people are familiar with the traditional characteristics of wine, such as fruity or oaky notes, there’s a lesser-known element that adds a unique and funky twist to some bottles: Brettanomyces.

Brettanomyces, often referred to as ‘Brett’ for short, is a wild yeast strain that can be found naturally in vineyards and wineries. It plays a crucial role in the fermentation process and can have a significant impact on the final product. In this blog post, we’ll dive into the fascinating world of Brettanomyces and explore its influence on wine.

First and foremost, it’s important to understand that Brettanomyces is considered a wine fault by many winemakers. Its presence can lead to a range of undesirable characteristics, including off-putting aromas and flavors. However, there’s a growing number of wine enthusiasts who appreciate the unique and complex profile that Brett can bring to a bottle.

One of the most distinctive traits associated with Brettanomyces is its ability to produce compounds known as ‘brettanomyces-derived aromas.’ These aromas can range from barnyard-like funkiness to sweaty horse blankets, and even Band-Aid-like medicinal notes. While this may sound unappealing to some, those who appreciate the complexity of wine often find these aromas intriguing and captivating.

But how does Brettanomyces end up in wine in the first place? It can enter the winemaking process through various means, such as contaminated barrels, equipment, or even on the surface of grapes. Once it finds its way into the fermenting juice, it can take hold and start to work its magic.

Brettanomyces thrives in a specific environment that includes low levels of sulfur dioxide, low pH, and moderate levels of alcohol. These conditions allow the yeast to survive and flourish, resulting in the production of its characteristic flavors and aromas. However, winemakers have developed various methods to control or eliminate Brett, such as using higher levels of sulfur dioxide or sterile filtration.

While Brettanomyces is often associated with red wines, it can also make an appearance in white wines, particularly those aged in oak barrels. The interaction between Brett and oak can result in even more complex and intriguing flavors and aromas.

It’s worth noting that the presence of Brettanomyces in wine is a delicate balance. In small amounts, it can enhance the overall complexity and add layers of character. However, when left unchecked, it can overpower the wine, making it undrinkable.

In conclusion, Brettanomyces is a fascinating and controversial element in winemaking. Whether you love it or hate it, there’s no denying its impact on the sensory experience of wine. So, the next time you open a bottle and detect a hint of funkiness, don’t be too quick to dismiss it. Embrace the funky side of fermentation and let Brettanomyces take you on a wild and adventurous journey through the world of wine.
