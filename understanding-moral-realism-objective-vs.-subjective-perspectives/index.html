Understanding Moral Realism: Objective vs. Subjective Perspectives


Morality is a fundamental aspect of human society, governing our actions, decisions, and relationships with others. It guides us in distinguishing between right and wrong, good and bad, and forms the basis of our ethical beliefs. When it comes to understanding morality, two contrasting perspectives often arise: objective moral realism and subjective moral relativism. In this article, we will delve into these two perspectives and explore the implications they have on our understanding of morality.

Objective moral realism posits that moral truths exist independently of human beliefs or opinions. According to this view, moral values and principles are objective and universal, transcending individual preferences and cultural variations. Objective moral realists argue that certain actions are inherently right or wrong, regardless of societal norms or personal feelings. They claim that moral facts exist in the same way as scientific facts, and can be discovered through rational inquiry and objective analysis.

Proponents of objective moral realism often cite moral intuition as evidence for their stance. They argue that our moral intuitions, the immediate gut feelings we have about ethical dilemmas, point towards the existence of objective moral truths. For example, the intuitive belief that torturing innocent people is morally wrong is considered by objectivists as indicative of an objective moral fact.

On the other hand, subjective moral relativism argues that moral values are not universally true, but rather depend on individual or cultural perspectives. According to this view, there are no objective moral standards applicable to all people at all times. Subjective moral relativists assert that moral judgments are purely subjective and vary from person to person or culture to culture. They emphasize the importance of cultural diversity and personal autonomy in shaping moral beliefs.

Relativists often point to the wide range of moral beliefs across different cultures as evidence for their position. They argue that what is considered morally acceptable in one culture may be regarded as immoral or taboo in another. For example, the acceptance of polygamy in certain societies while being condemned in others demonstrates the subjective nature of moral values.

While objective and subjective perspectives on morality present contrasting viewpoints, it is important to note that these positions are not necessarily mutually exclusive. Some philosophers propose a middle ground known as intersubjective moral realism. Intersubjectivists argue that although moral truths are not completely objective, they are based on shared values and collective agreement within a community or society. They consider moral values to be objective in a limited sense, as they arise from a consensus reached through rational discourse and mutual understanding.

Understanding the distinction between objective and subjective moral perspectives has significant implications for ethical debates and policy-making. Objective moral realism provides a foundation for universal moral principles and a framework for evaluating the rightness or wrongness of actions. It suggests the existence of moral obligations that apply to all individuals, regardless of personal beliefs or cultural context.

Subjective moral relativism, on the other hand, highlights the importance of cultural diversity and individual autonomy in shaping moral frameworks. It encourages tolerance and respect for differing opinions and emphasizes the need to understand and engage with alternative moral perspectives.

Ultimately, the question of whether morality is objective or subjective remains a complex and ongoing philosophical debate. While both perspectives offer valuable insights into our understanding of morality, finding common ground and fostering open dialogue between different viewpoints is crucial for ethical progress and a more inclusive society.

In conclusion, the objective vs. subjective perspectives on morality provide contrasting views on the nature of moral values and principles. Objective moral realism asserts the existence of objective moral truths independent of human beliefs, while subjective moral relativism argues for the subjective and culturally dependent nature of morality. The intersubjective moral realism attempts to bridge the gap between these perspectives by emphasizing consensus and shared values within a community. Understanding these perspectives can deepen our understanding of ethics and inform our approach to moral decision-making.
