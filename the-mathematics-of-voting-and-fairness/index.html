The Mathematics of Voting and Fairness


In any democratic society, a fundamental aspect of governance is the act of voting. From electing government officials to making decisions in organizations, voting is a mechanism through which individuals collectively express their preferences. However, have you ever wondered if the voting system we use is actually fair? Are there mathematical principles we can use to evaluate the fairness of different voting methods? In this blog post, we will explore the mathematics of voting and delve into the concept of fairness.

The Need for Fair Voting Systems

The concept of fairness in voting is crucial because it ensures that the outcome accurately represents the will of the voters. Fairness guarantees the legitimacy of the voting process and maintains public trust in democratic institutions. To achieve fairness, we need to analyze and understand the mathematical properties of different voting systems.

The Basic Principles of Voting Systems

Before delving into the mathematical details, let’s discuss the foundational principles of voting systems. First and foremost, a voting system should aim to satisfy the principle of ‘one person, one vote.’ This means that every eligible voter has an equal say in the outcome, and no individual’s vote carries more weight than another’s.

Another important principle is the ‘Majority Criterion’. According to this principle, if there is a candidate who has received a majority of first-choice votes, they should be declared the winner. This helps ensure that candidates with broad support among the voters are elected.

Mathematical Models for Voting Systems

To evaluate the fairness of different voting systems, mathematicians have developed several mathematical models. One of the most prominent models is called the ‘Arrow’s Impossibility Theorem,’ named after the Nobel Prize-winning economist Kenneth Arrow.

Arrow’s Impossibility Theorem states that it is impossible to design a voting system that satisfies all desirable criteria simultaneously. In other words, there will always be scenarios where a certain voting system fails to meet our criteria for fairness. This theorem highlights the inherent complexity of voting systems and the trade-offs that must be made.

Fairness Criteria for Voting Systems

To determine the fairness of a voting system, mathematicians have identified several key criteria. These criteria include:

  1. Majority Criterion: As mentioned earlier, if a candidate receives a majority of first-choice votes, they should be declared the winner.
  2. Condorcet Criterion: A voting system should elect the candidate who would defeat all other candidates in a head-to-head matchup.
  3. Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives: The ranking of candidates should not be affected by the inclusion or removal of irrelevant alternatives.
  4. Monotonicity Criterion: If a candidate improves in the rankings, their chances of winning should not decrease.
  5. Pareto Criterion: If every voter prefers candidate A over candidate B, then candidate A should not be ranked lower than candidate B.

Different Voting Systems

Now let’s explore some well-known voting systems and evaluate their fairness based on the criteria mentioned above.

  1. Plurality Voting: This is the simplest form of voting system, where voters choose a single candidate, and the candidate with the most votes wins. However, Plurality Voting fails to satisfy the majority criterion since the winner may not have received a majority of votes.

  2. Instant Runoff Voting: This system allows voters to rank the candidates in order of preference. If no candidate receives a majority of first-choice votes, the candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated, and their votes are redistributed to the remaining candidates. This process continues until a candidate receives a majority of votes. Instant Runoff Voting satisfies the majority criterion but fails to meet the Condorcet criterion.

  3. Borda Count: In this system, voters rank the candidates, and each rank receives a certain number of points. The candidate with the highest total score wins. Borda Count fails to satisfy the Condorcet criterion and can be susceptible to strategic voting.

  4. Approval Voting: In Approval Voting, voters can select multiple candidates they approve of, and the candidate with the most approvals wins. This method does not account for voter preferences beyond approval/disapproval and does not satisfy the Condorcet criterion.

Conclusion

The mathematics of voting provides us with a framework to assess the fairness of different voting systems. While no voting system can meet all the criteria simultaneously, understanding the mathematical principles behind voting helps us to identify the strengths and weaknesses of each system.

It is essential for individuals to be aware of these mathematical concepts to actively participate in discussions about voting reform. By considering the mathematical properties of voting systems, we can strive towards creating fairer systems that accurately reflect the will of the people.

Remember, fairness in voting is not just a desirable ideal; it is a fundamental principle that underpins the functioning of democracies worldwide.
