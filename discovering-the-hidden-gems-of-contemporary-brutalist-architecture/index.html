Discovering the Hidden Gems of Contemporary Brutalist Architecture


When it comes to architectural styles, there are few as polarizing as Brutalism. With its raw concrete facades and imposing structures, Brutalist architecture has often been met with mixed reactions. However, hidden amongst the concrete monoliths, there are hidden gems waiting to be discovered. In this blog post, we will take a closer look at contemporary Brutalist architecture and explore some of its lesser-known treasures.

1. National Theatre of Ghana, Accra, Ghana:
Located in Accra, the National Theatre of Ghana is a striking example of contemporary Brutalist architecture. Designed by the renowned Ghanaian architect, Joseph Hanson Kwabena Nketia, the theater features a unique concrete facade adorned with intricate geometric patterns. Inside, the theater showcases a blend of traditional Ghanaian and modern design elements, making it a must-visit for architecture enthusiasts.

2. Trellick Tower, London, United Kingdom:
Designed by renowned architect Ernő Goldfinger, the Trellick Tower in London is a prime example of Brutalist architecture. Completed in 1972, this residential building stands tall at 31 stories and features a distinctive stepped design that maximizes natural light and provides stunning views of the city. Despite its controversial past, the Trellick Tower has become an iconic landmark and a symbol of the city’s architectural heritage.

3. Metropol Parasol, Seville, Spain:
In the heart of Seville, the Metropol Parasol stands as a testament to the versatility of Brutalist architecture. Designed by German architect Jürgen Mayer, this massive wooden structure resembles a series of giant mushrooms, creating a unique and captivating visual experience. Visitors can explore the rooftop terrace, take in panoramic views of the city, and even visit an archaeological site located underneath the structure.

4. SESC Pompéia, São Paulo, Brazil:
Originally an abandoned factory, the SESC Pompéia in São Paulo has been transformed into a vibrant cultural center. Designed by renowned Brazilian architect Lina Bo Bardi, this Brutalist masterpiece showcases the adaptive reuse of industrial architecture. The building features exposed concrete, open spaces, and a unique integration of indoor and outdoor spaces. Today, it houses various cultural activities, including theaters, galleries, and sports facilities.

5. Museu de Arte de São Paulo (MASP), São Paulo, Brazil:
Designed by Italian-Brazilian architect Lina Bo Bardi, the Museu de Arte de São Paulo (MASP) is an architectural gem that stands out in the city’s skyline. The museum’s suspended structure, supported by two massive concrete pillars, creates a sense of lightness and transparency. Inside, visitors can admire an extensive collection of art, including works by renowned artists such as Van Gogh, Picasso, and Rembrandt.

These examples are just a glimpse into the world of contemporary Brutalist architecture. While often overshadowed by more traditional styles, these hidden gems offer a unique and thought-provoking experience for architecture enthusiasts. So, next time you’re exploring a new city, be sure to keep an eye out for these hidden treasures and discover the beauty and ingenuity of contemporary Brutalist architecture.
