The Evolution of Parental Guidance in Cartoon Ratings

3 min · 530 words

As parents, it’s essential to carefully monitor what our children watch on television, particularly in cartoons. Cartoons have been around for over a century, providing entertainment and educational value to young audiences. However, as the years have gone by, our perception and understanding of what’s appropriate for children have changed.

In the early days of cartoons, there were no real guidelines or regulations on what was considered suitable for kids. Cartoons often contained scenes of violence, racial stereotypes, and inappropriate content that we would never find in modern-day television.

As the 1960s rolled around, the film industry introduced the Motion Picture Production Code, which laid out a strict set of guidelines for all movies produced in the United States. This code eventually evolved into the Motion Picture Association of America (MPAA) rating system that we know today, which assigns ratings based on the content included in the film.

Following suit, the television industry soon realized the need for a similar rating system for all TV shows. In 1996, the TV Parental Guidelines were introduced and included categories such as TV-Y (appropriate for all ages), TV-PG (parental guidance suggested), TV-14 (parents strongly cautioned), and TV-MA (mature audiences only).

However, even with these guidelines in place, many parents still struggle with what is appropriate for their children. Some feel that certain shows rated as TV-Y may include mild violence or suggestive themes that they don’t want their young children exposed to. On the other hand, some parents feel that their older children can handle more mature content than what is deemed acceptable with a TV-14 rating.

That being said, it’s important to recognize that parental guidance is not only about the content shown on the screen but also about how parents interact with their children when watching media together. By discussing what is appropriate and why certain content may be upsetting or inappropriate, parents can help their children develop critical thinking skills and media literacy.

In recent years, streaming services like Netflix and Amazon Prime have started to create their own ratings systems, with unique categories such as ‘TV-Y7-FV’ (appropriate for ages seven and up, fantasy violence) and ‘TV-MA-LSV’ (for mature audiences, includes strong language, sex, and violence).

As the TV and film industry continues to evolve and change, so too will the rating systems used to guide parents in making informed decisions about what their children watch. It’s important to stay informed, discuss with other parents, and use both the TV ratings and your own judgment to ensure that your children are watching media that aligns with your values and beliefs.
