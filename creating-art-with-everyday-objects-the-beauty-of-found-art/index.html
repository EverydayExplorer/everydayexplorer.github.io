Creating Art with Everyday Objects: The Beauty of Found Art


In the world of art, creativity knows no bounds. Artists have always pushed the boundaries of traditional art materials and techniques, constantly seeking new ways to express their ideas and visions. One such movement that has gained popularity in recent years is the creation of art with everyday objects, commonly referred to as found art.

Found art, also known as ready-made or objet trouvé, is a form of artistic expression that involves using ordinary, often discarded objects as the medium for one’s artwork. These objects can range from everyday items like bottle caps, cardboard boxes, and old magazines to more unconventional materials such as scrap metal, bicycle parts, and even trash found on the streets.

What makes found art unique and captivating is its ability to transform mundane objects into something extraordinary. By repurposing these items and presenting them in a different context, artists are able to challenge our perceptions of value, aesthetics, and meaning. They invite us to see the beauty in the ordinary, to appreciate the overlooked and often discarded remnants of our daily lives.

One of the pioneers of found art is the legendary artist Marcel Duchamp. In 1917, Duchamp shocked the art world with his infamous artwork ‘Fountain,’ a simple urinal turned upside down and signed with the pseudonym ‘R. Mutt.’ Duchamp challenged the notion of what could be considered art, questioning the idea that skill or craftsmanship were prerequisites for artistic expression.

Since then, a long line of artists has followed in Duchamp’s footsteps, exploring the possibilities of found art. Some notable contemporary artists creating art with everyday objects include Tracy Emin, who incorporates personal belongings into her artwork, and Ai Weiwei, who uses found materials to highlight political and social issues.

So, why do artists choose to create art with everyday objects? For many, it is a way to break free from the confines of traditional art materials and techniques. It allows them to explore new avenues of expression and challenge the notion of what art can and should be. By using found objects, artists can tap into a wealth of cultural, historical, and personal associations that these items bring with them. They can evoke nostalgia, provoke thought, and tell stories that resonate with viewers on a deep and emotional level.

Furthermore, creating art with everyday objects is a sustainable and environmentally-friendly practice. By repurposing discarded or unwanted items, artists can reduce waste and give these objects a new lease on life. In doing so, they raise awareness about our consumption-driven society and the importance of reusing and recycling.

The process of creating art with everyday objects can be both liberating and challenging for artists. It requires a keen eye for aesthetics, as well as a deep understanding of the inherent qualities and possibilities of the chosen materials. Artists must carefully select and manipulate the objects, considering their textures, colors, shapes, and cultural connotations. They must also consider how these objects interact with each other and the space in which the artwork will be displayed.

From a viewer’s perspective, encountering art created with everyday objects can be a transformative experience. It invites us to question our preconceived notions of what is considered valuable or beautiful. It challenges us to look beyond the surface and immerse ourselves in the stories and emotions that these objects hold. It encourages us to see the potential for art in our own surroundings, reminding us that creativity can be found in the most unexpected places.

In conclusion, the beauty of found art lies in its ability to transform the ordinary into the extraordinary. By recontextualizing everyday objects, artists can create thought-provoking and visually stunning artwork that challenges our perceptions and expands our understanding of what it means to create art. Found art not only encourages sustainability and raises awareness about our consumption-driven society, but it also offers a fresh perspective on the world around us. So next time you come across a discarded object, take a moment to see it not just as trash, but as a potential canvas for your own creative expression.
