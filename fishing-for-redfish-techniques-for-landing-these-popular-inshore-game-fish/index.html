Fishing for Redfish: Techniques for Landing These Popular Inshore Game Fish


Redfish, also known as red drum, are a popular game fish found in coastal waters and estuaries along the Atlantic coast and Gulf of Mexico. With their beautiful coppery-red color and powerful fighting ability, redfish are highly sought after by anglers of all levels of experience. In this blog post, we will discuss some effective techniques for landing these elusive and exciting fish.

1. Choosing the Right Tackle

When targeting redfish, it is important to choose the right tackle that can handle their size and power. Medium-heavy to heavy spinning or baitcasting rods in the 7 to 8-foot range are ideal for casting and fighting these fish. Pair your rod with a reel that has a high line capacity and smooth drag system.

In terms of line, a 20 to 30-pound braided line with a fluorocarbon leader of 20 to 30 pounds will provide the necessary strength and stealth when fishing for redfish. The braided line offers good sensitivity and allows you to detect subtle strikes, while the fluorocarbon leader provides invisibility in clear water.

2. Locating Redfish

To increase your chances of catching redfish, you need to find their feeding grounds. They are commonly found around grass flats, oyster bars, mangrove shorelines, and pilings. Look for signs of baitfish activity such as schools of mullet, jumping shrimp, or diving birds. Redfish are opportunistic feeders and will often patrol these areas in search of an easy meal.

3. Understanding Redfish Behavior

Redfish are a schooling fish, and where you find one, you will likely find more. They are known to move in schools of various sizes, so if you catch one, it is worth casting back into the same area as there may be more fish nearby. Redfish will typically feed during the incoming and outgoing tides when baitfish are brought into their range.

4. Using the Right Lures and Baits

There are various lures and baits that are effective for targeting redfish. Soft plastic baits such as paddle tails, shrimp imitations, and jerk baits work well when retrieved slowly along the bottom. Use a jig head that matches the weight of the bait to ensure proper presentation. Natural baits like live shrimp, pinfish, or finger mullet are also productive when rigged on a Carolina or slip cork rig.

5. Practicing Stealth and Patience

When fishing for redfish, it is important to be stealthy and avoid causing excessive disturbance in the water. Redfish have excellent eyesight and can be easily spooked. Approach your fishing spot quietly and make accurate casts to avoid alarming the fish.

Additionally, patience is key when targeting redfish. They may not always strike immediately, so it’s important to work your bait or lure slowly and thoroughly cover the area. Redfish are notorious for ‘picking up’ baits or lures, meaning they may initially bite softly and then swallow the bait or lure a few moments later. Give them time to fully commit before setting the hook.

6. Practicing Proper Catch and Release Techniques

As responsible anglers, it is important to practice proper catch and release techniques to ensure the survival of redfish populations. When handling redfish, wet your hands before touching them to avoid removing their protective slime layer. Use a fish grip or lip gripper to hold onto the lower jaw without causing harm. Take a quick photo, if desired, and release the fish back into the water gently.

In conclusion, fishing for redfish can be a thrilling experience, and with the right techniques and strategies, you can improve your chances of landing these popular inshore game fish. Remember to choose the right tackle, locate their feeding grounds, understand their behavior, use the right lures and baits, practice stealth and patience, and practice proper catch and release techniques. Follow these tips, and you’ll be well on your way to hooking into some trophy-sized redfish!
