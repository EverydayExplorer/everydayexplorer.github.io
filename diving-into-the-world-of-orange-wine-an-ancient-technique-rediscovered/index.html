Diving into the World of Orange Wine: An Ancient Technique Rediscovered


Orange wine is a captivating trend that has taken the wine world by storm in recent years. With its distinct color, unique taste, and rich history, this wine style has become a favorite among wine enthusiasts and adventurers alike. In this blog post, we will dive into the world of orange wine, exploring its origins, production process, and the qualities that make it so special.

To fully understand orange wine, we must first explore its ancient roots. Contrary to popular belief, orange wine is not a new creation. In fact, this winemaking technique dates back thousands of years to the Caucasus region (modern-day Georgia, Armenia, and Iran). Traditionally made in clay vessels known as qvevri, orange wine was fermented with grape skins, seeds, and stems over an extended period of time. This method allowed for prolonged contact between the grape juice and the skins, resulting in a wine with an amber or orange hue.

While orange wine never completely disappeared from the winemaking scene, it did fade into obscurity for several centuries. However, in recent decades, a resurgence of interest in ancient winemaking techniques has brought orange wine back into the spotlight. Winemakers around the world are now experimenting with this method, leading to a diverse range of orange wines that showcase the versatility of this style.

So, what sets orange wine apart from its red, white, and rosé counterparts? One of the defining characteristics of orange wine is its tannin structure. Unlike most white wines, which are typically low in tannins, orange wine can have a notably grippy texture due to the extended skin contact during fermentation. This tannin presence gives orange wine a fuller body and a slightly more complex flavor profile.

In terms of flavor, orange wine is often described as having a unique combination of characteristics from both red and white wines. It can exhibit the fruitiness and acidity of a white wine, but also display the earthiness and structure of a red wine. The exact flavor profile will vary depending on the grape variety, terroir, and winemaking techniques used, making each bottle of orange wine a unique sensory experience.

The production process of orange wine is labor-intensive and requires the winemaker’s utmost care and attention. After harvesting the grapes, they are typically destemmed and placed in a vessel for fermentation. The length of skin contact can vary from a few days to several months, depending on the desired style and the grape variety. During this time, the juice absorbs the tannins, pigments, and other compounds present in the grape skins, resulting in the distinctive color and flavor profile of orange wine.

Once fermentation is complete, the wine is transferred to another vessel for aging. Some winemakers choose to age orange wine in oak barrels, while others prefer the use of clay amphorae. This decision can significantly influence the wine’s final flavor and texture. After aging, the wine is bottled, often without filtration or fining, preserving its natural character and integrity.

The surge in popularity of orange wine can be attributed to its versatility and food pairing potential. Due to its fuller body and pronounced flavors, orange wine can stand up to bold and savory dishes. It pairs exceptionally well with a range of foods, including cheese, charcuterie, roasted vegetables, grilled meats, and even spicy cuisine. Its acidity and tannins help cut through rich and fatty flavors, making it an excellent choice for adventurous food lovers.

The rise of orange wine has sparked the curiosity of wine enthusiasts worldwide, driving them to seek out unique flavors and experiences. As a result, more and more winemakers are embracing this ancient winemaking technique, leading to a resurgence of interest in forgotten grape varieties and traditional methods. Orange wine has become a symbol of innovation and exploration in the world of wine, showcasing the beauty and diversity that can be found within a single grape.

In conclusion, diving into the world of orange wine is a journey into the past, present, and future of winemaking. This ancient technique, rediscovered and reimagined, offers a rich sensory experience and a glimpse into the artistry and craftsmanship of winemakers around the world. Whether you’re a seasoned wine connoisseur or a curious beginner, orange wine is a delightful adventure awaiting your palate. So, raise a glass and embark on a voyage into the captivating world of orange wine.
