Space Debris: The Lurking Threat to Spacecraft


Space exploration has been an integral part of modern science and technology. It has opened up new dimensions of knowledge and has helped us understand our universe better. The advancements in science and technology have enabled us to launch several spacecraft into space, facilitating scientific exploration, communication, and navigation. While space exploration has come a long way, there is a lurking threat that poses a significant challenge – space debris.

The space around our planet is cluttered with debris, which poses a threat to spacecraft that venture into space. The debris is usually the result of human activities such as launching satellites, conducting space experiments, and testing spacecraft, among others. The debris ranges from small particles to large fragments that can damage or destroy a spacecraft. In this article, we will discuss the threat posed by space debris to spacecraft.

What is Space Debris?

Space debris refers to the material that orbits the Earth and has no useful purpose. The debris ranges from small particles, such as paint flecks, to huge fragments, such as derelict satellites and spent rocket stages. According to the European Space Agency (ESA), there are over 34,000 objects larger than 10 cm, 900,000 objects between 1 and 10 cm, and over 128 million objects smaller than 1 cm in Earth’s orbit.

The debris is formed due to several activities such as satellite launches, collisions between spacecraft, and explosions. The debris also arises from natural phenomena, such as micrometeoroids colliding with spacecraft, which release small particles.

The Threat Posed by Space Debris:

The space debris poses a significant threat to spacecraft venturing into space. The debris moves at high speeds, up to 28,000 km/h, and can cause severe damage to a spacecraft upon impact. The debris can disable or destroy a spacecraft’s important components, such as its solar panels, communication antennae, and propulsion systems.

The debris creates a danger to spacecraft as it can cause a cascading effect of collisions known as the Kessler Syndrome. The Kessler Syndrome occurs when there are enough debris particles in orbit to cause a chain reaction of collisions. The collisions result in the creation of more debris, leading to more collisions, and increasing the chance of further collisions. A scenario where the Kessler Syndrome occurs could render space exploration impossible.

The debris can also pose a threat to life on Earth. In 2018, the Chinese space station Tiangong-1 re-entered the Earth’s atmosphere, and its debris fragments landed in a remote part of the Pacific Ocean. However, in 1978, the Soviet satellite Cosmos 954 crashed in northern Canada, spreading radioactive debris over a large area.

The Mitigation Efforts to Combat Space Debris:

Several mitigation efforts are underway to reduce the risk posed by space debris. One of the mitigation efforts is to limit the production of debris by designing spacecraft that are less likely to leave debris. This can be achieved by reducing the number of stages used to launch a spacecraft, designing spacecraft with smaller parts, and avoiding unnecessary explosions of spent rocket stages.

Another mitigation effort is to improve tracking and monitoring of debris. The ESA and the United States Space Surveillance Network track and catalog the debris in Earth’s orbit. The tracking and monitoring help spacecraft operators plan their maneuvers to avoid the debris and reduce the risk of collisions.

The third mitigation effort is to remove the debris from orbit. Several methods to remove debris have been proposed, such as using lasers to slow down debris particles, using electrodynamic tethers to de-orbit debris, and actively pushing debris out of orbit using robotic arms.

Conclusion:

Space debris is a significant threat to spacecraft and poses a challenge to space exploration. Without proper mitigation efforts, the risk of spacecraft collisions will continue to increase, making space exploration impossible. The efforts to reduce the production of debris, tracking and monitoring the debris, and removing the debris are essential in ensuring the safety of spacecraft. Space exploration has come far, and it is essential to take the necessary steps to mitigate the risk posed by space debris to ensure that it continues to thrive.
