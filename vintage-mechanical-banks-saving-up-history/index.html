Vintage Mechanical Banks: Saving Up History


Saving money is a habit that has been long inculcated in humans as we transitioned from a barter system to a currency system. Over the years, money has not only become a means of exchange, but it has also become a symbol of power and success. For children, it is a tool to learn discipline and the importance of saving. This is why vintage mechanical banks have been an essential part of homes since the 19th century, and have been helping people save up history.

A mechanical bank is a money-saving device consisting of several moving parts. They were popular in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, and were known for their ingenuity and ability to entertain while saving money. The first mechanical banks were made of cast iron, and they were hand-painted and decorated with elaborate designs. The banks were designed to encourage children to save their pocket money and become financially responsible adults.

The history of mechanical banks dates back to ancient times when people used terracotta jars to save their money. In Europe, the first mechanical banks were designed and sold in Germany in the late 19th century. The banks were made of cast iron and were often designed to resemble animals or everyday objects. They became popular in the United States in the early 20th century, and many American companies began producing them. Some of the most famous manufacturers of mechanical banks included J & E Stevens, Kyser and Rex, and Shepard Hardware.

Mechanical banks came in many different shapes and sizes, and each one had a unique mechanism for saving money. Some banks required the user to insert a coin, while others required the user to press a lever or turn a crank. When the user operated the bank, a mechanism inside the bank would move, causing the coin to drop into a designated slot. The mechanism was often intricate and cleverly designed, making it both educational and entertaining for children.

One of the most popular types of mechanical banks was the “Speaking Dog” bank, which was made by J & E Stevens in the late 1800s. The bank featured a dog sitting on top of a house, and when a coin was inserted, the dog would bark and the coin would drop into the chimney. Other popular banks included the “Trick Dog” bank, which featured a dog that would jump through a hoop to deposit a coin, and the “Bad Accident” bank, which showed a boy being knocked over by a car.

Today, vintage mechanical banks are highly collectible and sought after by collectors. They are a unique piece of history that captures the spirit of a bygone era. Many collectors appreciate the intricate designs and mechanisms of the banks, as well as the nostalgia they evoke. Vintage mechanical banks can be found at antique stores, online auctions, and collectors’ shows.

In conclusion, vintage mechanical banks are more than just a tool to save money, they are a reminder of our past and a testament to our ingenuity. These banks were designed to teach children financial responsibility and entertain them at the same time. Today, they serve as a cherished collector’s item that has stood the test of time. If you are looking for a unique piece of history that captures the spirit of a bygone era, then a vintage mechanical bank is an excellent option to consider. So next time you come across a mechanical bank, appreciate its intricacies and remember that you are saving up history.
