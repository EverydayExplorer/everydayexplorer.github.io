Creating Effective Error Pages: Best Practices and Examples


Welcome to our blog post about creating effective error pages! We understand that encountering an error can be frustrating for both website visitors and the website owners. However, by implementing well-designed error pages, you can turn a negative experience into a positive one. In this blog post, we will discuss the best practices for creating error pages and provide you with some inspiring examples.

1. Keep it User-Friendly:
When a user encounters an error, it’s important to ensure that they feel supported and guided. Your error page should be clear, concise, and easy to understand. Avoid using technical jargon and provide a simple explanation of what went wrong.

2. Apologize and Take Responsibility:
Take responsibility for the error and show empathy towards the user’s experience. A sincere apology can go a long way in maintaining a positive relationship with your visitors.

3. Offer Assistance:
Instead of leaving your visitors feeling lost, provide them with helpful information or links to navigate back to the homepage, search for content, or contact customer support. This will help them find what they were looking for and prevent them from leaving your website.

4. Maintain Brand Consistency:
Design your error pages to align with your brand identity. Use your logo, brand colors, and typography to maintain consistency across your website. This will help users recognize that they are still on your website, despite encountering an error.

5. Inject Humor (When Appropriate):
Adding a touch of humor to your error pages can help lighten the mood and lessen user frustration. However, it’s important to use humor sparingly and ensure it aligns with your brand’s tone and values. Remember that what’s funny to one person may not be amusing to another, so tread carefully.

Now that we’ve covered the best practices, let’s take a look at some inspiring examples of effective error pages:

1. GitHub:
GitHub’s error page features a cute image of a sad octocat with a broken server. The page provides a brief explanation of the error and offers a button to return to the homepage. This simple yet visually appealing design effectively conveys the error while maintaining the brand’s friendly and approachable tone.

2. Slack:
Slack’s error page makes use of humor to lighten the frustration of encountering an error. It features an amusing message along with a cute illustration. The page provides options to contact support or return to the previous page. This error page effectively maintains the brand’s playful and friendly personality.

3. Mailchimp:
Mailchimp’s error page engages users with a creative 404 error message. The page features a funky illustration, a witty message, and a search bar to help users find what they were looking for. This error page effectively reflects Mailchimp’s brand personality while assisting users in finding their desired content.

In conclusion, creating effective error pages is crucial for maintaining a positive user experience on your website. By following the best practices we discussed and drawing inspiration from successful examples, you can turn a frustrating error into an opportunity to engage with your visitors. Remember to keep it user-friendly, apologize and take responsibility, offer assistance, maintain brand consistency, and inject appropriate humor. Happy designing!
