The Mythology of the Mayans: Creation Myths and Prophecies


The ancient Mayan civilization is often remembered for its impressive architectural sites, advanced mathematical systems, and intricate calendar system. However, one aspect of Mayan culture that is often overlooked is their rich mythology. The Mayans had a complex belief system that revolved around various gods and celestial beings. In this blog post, we will explore the fascinating world of Mayan mythology, focusing specifically on their creation myths and prophecies.

Creation Myths

The Mayans had several creation myths, each telling a different story about how the world and humans came into existence. One of the most prominent creation myths is the Popol Vuh. According to this myth, the gods first created the earth and the sky, followed by the animals. Unsatisfied with their creations, the gods then attempted to create humans. They first molded figures out of mud, but these figures dissolved in water. Next, they tried creating humans out of wood, but these beings lacked intelligence and had no souls. Finally, the gods created humans out of maize dough, which symbolized purity and strength. These humans were known as the ‘people of corn’ and became the ancestors of the Mayan people.

Another creation myth, known as the Chilam Balam, tells a different story. According to this myth, the gods created the world and humans in a series of cycles. The first cycle saw the creation of the earth and the sky, followed by the creation of animals and plants. In the second cycle, the gods created humans out of mud, but these beings were destroyed by a great flood. The third cycle saw the creation of humans out of wood, but they were destroyed by a volcanic eruption. Finally, in the fourth cycle, the gods created humans out of maize dough, who are the ancestors of the present-day Mayan people.

Prophecies

The Mayans were known for their exceptional astronomical knowledge, which allowed them to create a highly accurate calendar system. This astronomical knowledge also shaped their notions of prophesy and the future. The Mayans believed that time was cyclical, with periods of creation and destruction. They believed that each cycle was governed by a particular deity, and the end of a cycle would bring about significant changes.

One of the most famous Mayan prophecies is the ‘2012 phenomenon.’ According to the Mayan calendar, December 21, 2012, marked the end of a major cycle known as the Long Count. Many people misinterpreted this as the end of the world. However, for the Mayans, it was seen as a transition point, a time of renewal and rebirth. The end of the Long Count cycle was considered an opportunity for personal and societal transformation.

The Mayans also believed in the cyclical nature of time through the concept of the ‘Calendar Round.’ This calendar system combined two independent cycles: the 365-day solar calendar and the 260-day sacred calendar. The combination of these two cycles resulted in a cycle with a duration of 52 years. At the end of each Calendar Round, the Mayans believed that major changes would occur, and the world would be transformed.

Conclusion

The mythology of the Mayans provides us with a glimpse into the rich spiritual beliefs of this ancient civilization. Through their creation myths and prophecies, we can see how the Mayans perceived the origin of the world and the cycle of life. Their belief in cyclical time and the significance of transitions offers us a different perspective on our own existence. By studying Mayan mythology, we can gain a deeper understanding of ourselves and our place in the grand scheme of things.
