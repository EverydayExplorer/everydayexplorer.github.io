Why Do Apples Turn Brown? Unraveling the Science Behind Oxidation


We’ve all experienced the frustration of cutting into a crisp apple, only to find it quickly turning brown before our eyes. But why does this happen? What causes apples to oxidize so rapidly, resulting in the unappealing brown color? In this blog post, we will delve into the science behind apple browning and explain the process of oxidation in detail.

The Anatomy of an Apple

To understand why apples turn brown, let’s first take a closer look at the anatomy of an apple. Apples are composed of mostly water, with approximately 85% water content. The remaining 15% consists of various compounds, including sugars, acids, and enzymes. These components play a role in browning when the apple is exposed to oxygen.

The Role of Enzymes

One of the key players in the browning process is an enzyme called polyphenol oxidase (PPO), also known as tyrosinase. PPO is present in all apples and is responsible for catalyzing the oxidation of certain compounds found in the fruit. When the apple is cut or bruised, the cell walls are damaged, allowing PPO to come into contact with these compounds.

The Oxidation Process

Oxidation is a chemical reaction that occurs when a substance reacts with oxygen. In the case of apples, the PPO enzyme reacts with polyphenols, a type of compound found in the fruit’s cells. When these polyphenols are exposed to oxygen, they undergo a series of reactions that result in the formation of brown pigments known as melanins.

This process is commonly known as enzymatic browning and is responsible for the color change observed when an apple is exposed to oxygen. The reaction is facilitated by the presence of oxygen in the air and is accelerated with increased exposure time.

The Impact of pH and Temperature

pH and temperature also influence the browning of apples. Polyphenols are more stable in an acidic environment, while PPO functions optimally at a slightly acidic pH. This is why adding lemon juice, which is acidic, to cut apples can slow down the browning process. Heat, on the other hand, can denature the PPO enzyme, effectively halting the enzymatic reaction.

The Role of Antioxidants

To counteract the browning process, apples contain natural antioxidants, such as vitamin C and phenolic compounds. These antioxidants help prevent or slow down oxidation by scavenging free radicals and inhibiting the activity of the PPO enzyme. However, these antioxidants can be depleted over time or through cooking, leading to increased browning.

Preventing Apple Browning

There are several methods to prevent apple browning and extend the fruit’s visual appeal. One common method is to treat cut apples with an acidic solution, such as lemon juice or vinegar. The acid lowers the pH, inhibiting the PPO enzyme’s activity and slowing down the browning process.

Another approach is to use an antioxidant solution to coat the apple slices, creating a barrier between the fruit and the surrounding air. This method is often used in the food industry to preserve the appearance of sliced apples in products like salads or sandwiches.

The Implications Beyond Apples

While we’ve focused on apples in this blog post, it’s important to note that enzymatic browning is not unique to this fruit. Many other fruits and vegetables undergo a similar reaction when exposed to air. Bananas, pears, and potatoes, for example, are also prone to enzymatic browning.

Understanding the science behind apple browning can help us appreciate the factors at play and take steps to prevent or slow down the process. Whether it’s enjoying a fresh apple snack or preparing a meal with apple slices, we now know why apples turn brown and have solutions to preserve their visual appeal.

In conclusion, the browning of apples is a natural process triggered by enzymatic oxidation. The PPO enzyme interacts with polyphenol compounds found in the fruit, resulting in the formation of brown pigments. pH, temperature, and antioxidants all influence the rate of browning, and there are various methods available to prevent or slow down the process. So, the next time you bite into an apple, you’ll have a deeper understanding of why it turns brown.
