Why do objects appear larger or smaller in water? The science behind it


Have you ever wondered why objects appear larger or smaller when submerged in water? The phenomenon of objects appearing distorted in water is a fascinating optical effect that can be explained by the science of light and refraction. In this blog post, we will explore the reasons behind this phenomenon and uncover the scientific principles that govern it.

To understand why objects appear larger or smaller in water, we need to first understand the concept of refraction. Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another. When light enters water from air, it slows down and changes direction. This change in speed and direction causes the light waves to bend, leading to the distortion of the objects viewed through water.

The change in direction of light when it passes between two different mediums is determined by the refractive index of each medium. The refractive index is a measure of how much a material can bend light. Water has a higher refractive index than air, which means that light bends more when it passes from air to water.

When an object is submerged in water, the light rays that enter the water from the object are bent towards the normal (an imaginary line perpendicular to the surface of the water). This bending of light causes the rays to converge before they reach our eyes, making the object appear closer than it actually is. As a result, the object appears larger in size.

On the other hand, when we view an object above the water surface, the light rays are refracted away from the normal as they leave the water and enter the air. This diverging of light rays makes the object appear farther away from us than it actually is, causing it to appear smaller in size.

The degree to which objects appear larger or smaller in water depends on the angle at which they are viewed and the difference in refractive indices between air and water. If an object is viewed at a steep angle, the bending of light will be more pronounced, resulting in a more significant distortion of the object’s size.

It is also important to note that the distortion of object size in water is not only limited to the vertical dimension but extends to the horizontal dimension as well. This is due to the combination of refraction and the curved shape of water surface known as meniscus. The curved shape of the water surface acts as a lens, contributing to further distortions in size and shape.

The phenomenon of objects appearing larger or smaller in water has practical applications in various fields. One application is in the field of microscopy, where the refractive properties of water are utilized to achieve higher magnification and resolution. By immersing specimens in water, researchers can enhance the visibility of microscopic details.

In conclusion, the apparent enlargement or reduction in the size of objects when viewed through water is a result of the bending of light caused by refraction. The difference in refractive indices between air and water causes the light rays to bend, making objects appear closer or farther away than they actually are. The degree of distortion depends on the viewing angle and the refractive properties of the materials involved. Understanding this phenomenon not only satisfies our curiosity but also has practical applications in various scientific fields.
