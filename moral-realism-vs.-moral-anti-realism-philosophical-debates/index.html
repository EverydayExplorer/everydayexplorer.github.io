Moral Realism vs. Moral Anti-Realism: Philosophical Debates


Are moral values objective or subjective? Do they exist independently of human beliefs and opinions, or are they merely social constructs? These questions lie at the heart of the philosophical debates surrounding moral realism and moral anti-realism. In this blog post, we will explore these contrasting views, their implications, and the ongoing discourse within the field of ethics.

Moral Realism: The Objective View

Moral realism posits that moral properties, such as goodness or badness, exist objectively in the world. According to this view, moral facts are independent of human perception and belief. In other words, certain actions or states of affairs are inherently good or bad, regardless of our subjective opinions.

Proponents of moral realism argue that moral truths are discoverable through reason and empirical observation. They believe that moral judgments can be objectively true or false, and that moral principles hold universal validity. For example, the statement “murder is wrong” is seen as objectively true, regardless of cultural or individual perspectives.

Moral Anti-Realism: The Subjective View

On the other hand, moral anti-realism rejects the existence of objective moral truths. Advocates of moral anti-realism argue that moral judgments are subjective and dependent on human beliefs, attitudes, and cultural norms. According to this view, moral values are not inherent in the world, but rather constructed by individuals or societies.

Moral anti-realists often adopt a relativistic stance, claiming that moral principles vary across different cultures and historical periods. They argue that what is considered morally right or wrong is contingent upon social, historical, and psychological factors. For example, they might argue that the moral permissibility of same-sex marriage is a matter of social and cultural norms, rather than an objective truth.

The Debate and Its Implications

The debate between moral realism and moral anti-realism has far-reaching implications for ethical theories and the understanding of moral responsibility.

From a moral realist perspective, ethical theories such as consequentialism or deontological ethics can be seen as attempts to discover and apply objective moral principles. Moral realism supports the idea that individuals have moral duties and obligations that transcend personal preferences and desires.

On the other hand, moral anti-realism challenges the notion of moral objectivity and emphasizes the role of subjectivity in ethical decision-making. Anti-realists argue that moral judgments are ultimately expressions of personal values and preferences rather than reflections of an objective moral reality.

The Ongoing Discourse

The debate between moral realism and moral anti-realism continues to be an active area of research and discussion in the field of ethics. Philosophers engage in rigorous debate, presenting new arguments and counterarguments to support their respective positions.

Some philosophers seek to reconcile these opposing views by proposing hybrid theories, such as constructivism or expressivism, which aim to capture both the subjective and objective aspects of morality.

In conclusion, the philosophical debates surrounding moral realism and moral anti-realism raise fundamental questions about the nature of moral values and the foundations of ethical theories. By exploring these contrasting viewpoints, we gain a deeper understanding of the complexities of moral philosophy and the diverse perspectives within the field. Whether one supports the objectivity of moral values or embraces their subjectivity, engaging with these debates enriches our understanding of ethics and helps shape our own moral frameworks.
