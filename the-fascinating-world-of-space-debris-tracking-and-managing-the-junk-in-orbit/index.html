The Fascinating World of Space Debris: Tracking and Managing the Junk in Orbit


Space debris is a man-made issue that has plagued the space industry for years. With each space mission that we launch, we increase the amount of debris that circles around our planet, contributing to the problem. However, it’s not just the problem that’s fascinating- the process of tracking and managing space debris is equally thrilling.

Firstly, what is space debris? Space debris, also known as space junk, is any human-made object orbiting the Earth that no longer serves any useful purpose. This includes everything from abandoned spacecraft, discarded rocket stages, and even fragments of debris that are left behind from past space missions.

Understanding the gravity of the situation, NASA started a program to track space debris in 1978. The space agency now tracks more than 500,000 pieces of debris that are larger than half an inch in size, at speeds of around 17,500 miles an hour. Prioritizing space safety, NASA’s Orbital Debris Program (ODP) continually studies this evolution of the space debris environment.

NASA’s approach when tracking space debris is twofold. The first one involves the use of radar, which emits radio waves that bounce off objects orbiting around the Earth. The second is based on observation- from ground telescopes or the ISS (International Space Station). Millions of observations provide data that is then compiled in computer models to understand the behavioral patterns of debris and how to predict their movement.

But tracking space debris is just half the battle. Once debris has been identified, managing it becomes an issue. NASA and other space agencies around the world have several methods to combat this problem. One method is space debris mitigation, which involves designing spacecraft that will minimize the production of space debris during their operating lifetime and plan for their disposal at the end of their operational life.

Another approach is active debris removal, which entails the use of a range of technologies to capture larger pieces of debris and remove them from orbit. Harpoon nets, grappling arms, and space-based lasers are merely a few systems that have been tested.

However, it’s vital that removing debris does not create even more space debris. Therefore, space agencies have to be extremely careful about the methods that they use. Made in Space, a company that specializes in 3D printing technology in space, has developed a method that may prove to be more effective. Their “Archinaut” system is a set of robots that are capable of using 3D printing technology to print ‘structural spacecraft components,’ turning space debris into raw material for new spacecraft equipment.

However, until more innovative systems are put in place, the issue of space debris remains a pressing problem. In 2007, China infamously destroyed a weather satellite in space, creating over 3,000 pieces of debris and putting other spacecraft at risk of collisions. This single act increased the amount of space debris by 25% in a single day.

Space debris is a pressing issue that requires innovative solutions. While space agencies around the world have put various methods in place to track and manage space debris, the problem continues to grow. That said, it is essential to keep this issue in mind and continue to support and fund experiments that will aid in the development of new technologies and systems. Ultimately much like protecting our planet’s environment, it is the responsibility of the space industry to ensure the safety of its orbit.
