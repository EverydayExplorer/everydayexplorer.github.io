The Mythology of Horses and Mounts


Horses have long been a symbol of power, beauty, and freedom in mythologies around the world. These magnificent creatures have captivated the human imagination for centuries, just as they have served as loyal companions and reliable means of transportation. From the mythical Pegasus to the sacred horses of ancient gods, the mythology surrounding horses and mounts is as diverse as it is fascinating.

In Greek mythology, one of the most iconic horses is Pegasus. Born from Medusa’s severed head, Pegasus was a winged horse known for his speed and grace. According to the legends, Pegasus aided the hero Perseus in his battles against various monstrous creatures. Pegasus became the trusted steed of the Muses, the goddesses of inspiration in Greek mythology, and was often depicted in artwork alongside them. Pegasus has come to symbolize the power of flight and imagination, his image gracing countless paintings and sculptures over the years.

Another famous horse from Greek mythology is Arion, a divine creature gifted with the ability to converse with humans. Arion was known for his incredible speed and was said to have surpassed all other horses of the time. His story is intertwined with that of the poet and musician, Orpheus. It is said that Arion carried Orpheus safely through the perilous journeys of the Underworld, making him a beloved companion and a symbol of hope.

In Norse mythology, Sleipnir, an eight-legged horse, occupies a special place. Sleipnir was the offspring of Loki, the trickster god, who transformed into a mare to distract a giant’s horse, resulting in their divine offspring. Sleipnir became the steed of Odin, the All-Father and ruler of Asgard. With his eight legs, Sleipnir possessed incredible speed and was able to traverse the nine realms effortlessly. This mythic horse has become synonymous with Odin himself and is often depicted alongside him in Norse artwork and tales.

Moving to Celtic mythology, we find the goddess Macha and her infamous chariot horses. Macha was a powerful deity associated with sovereignty and war. According to legend, she raced against the king’s horses and won, despite being heavily pregnant. As a result, she cursed the men of Ulster with a debilitative condition during childbirth. Macha’s horses were said to be fierce, swift, and unstoppable in battle, showcasing their divine qualities and the fearsome nature of their owner.

In Hindu mythology, there are several significant horses and divine mounts. One such mount is Uchchaihshravas, a seven-headed white horse believed to be the king of horses. Uchchaihshravas emerged during the churning of the cosmic ocean and was claimed by Indra, the king of gods, as his mount. This celestial horse symbolizes power and purity and is believed to be the source of all horses on Earth according to Hindu mythology.

The mythology of horses and mounts extends beyond just these examples. Horses have been associated with deities, heroes, and various cultural symbols in mythologies all over the world. They often represent freedom, strength, and the untamed spirit of nature. These majestic creatures have been essential companions in quests, aiding heroes and guiding them through treacherous journeys.

As these mythological stories demonstrate, horses and mounts have held a special place in human imagination since ancient times. The bond between humans and these noble creatures has transcended time and culture, leaving an indelible mark on our collective consciousness. Whether it is the mythical Pegasus inspiring our dreams of flight or the fierce chariot horses of Macha symbolizing power and determination, the mythology of horses and mounts continues to captivate and inspire us today.
