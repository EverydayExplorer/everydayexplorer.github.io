Why Do We Get Motion Sickness?


Motion sickness, also known as travel sickness or kinetosis, is a common condition that affects many people. Whether it’s during a car ride, boat trip, or even while watching a 3D movie, the symptoms of motion sickness can be unpleasant and disruptive. But have you ever wondered why some people are more susceptible to motion sickness than others? In this blog post, we will explore the causes of motion sickness and uncover some interesting facts about this intriguing phenomenon.

What is Motion Sickness?

Motion sickness is a condition that occurs when there is a disconnect between the motion our body perceives and the motion our eyes perceive. Our inner ear, specifically the vestibular system, plays a crucial role in maintaining our sense of balance and spatial orientation. When we are in motion, our inner ear senses the movement and sends signals to our brain to adjust our body’s posture and balance. However, when there is a conflict between what our inner ear is sensing and what our eyes are seeing, motion sickness can ensue.

Causes of Motion Sickness

  1. Conflicting Sensory Signals: As mentioned earlier, one of the primary causes of motion sickness is a mismatch between the information our inner ear provides and what our eyes perceive. For example, when we are inside a moving car and reading a book, our inner ear senses the motion, but our eyes mainly focus on the static pages of the book. This contradiction leads to sensory conflict and triggers symptoms of motion sickness.

  2. Visual Cues: Visual cues can significantly influence our susceptibility to motion sickness. When we are inside a moving vehicle or on a boat, looking outside and focusing on the horizon can help synchronize the signals received by our inner ear and our eyes. On the other hand, when we focus on objects inside the vehicle, such as reading a book or staring at a screen, the sensory conflict intensifies, increasing the chances of experiencing motion sickness.

  3. Individual Sensitivity: It is important to note that not everyone is equally susceptible to motion sickness. Some individuals may experience symptoms more severely and frequently, while others may hardly experience them at all. This individual sensitivity can be attributed to various factors such as genetics, age, and previous experiences with motion sickness.

  4. Imbalance in the Autonomic Nervous System: The autonomic nervous system is responsible for regulating involuntary body functions, including digestion, heart rate, and breathing. Motion sickness is thought to occur due to an imbalance in the autonomic nervous system, particularly affecting the part known as the parasympathetic nervous system. This imbalance can lead to symptoms like nausea, dizziness, and vomiting.

Tips to Prevent Motion Sickness

While motion sickness can be a frustrating condition, there are several strategies you can employ to help prevent or alleviate its symptoms:

  1. Choose the Right Seat: When traveling by car, sit in the front seat or in a spot where you have a clear view of the road. In airplanes or boats, choose a seat located near the wings or in the middle of the vessel where the motion is less pronounced.

  2. Focus on the Horizon: Looking at a stable reference point, such as the horizon or a distant object, can help reduce sensory conflict and minimize the chances of developing motion sickness.

  3. Avoid Heavy Meals: Eating heavy or greasy meals before traveling can worsen motion sickness symptoms. Opt for light, non-greasy snacks instead and stay hydrated.

  4. Fresh Air and Ventilation: Opening windows or using air vents to get fresh air can help alleviate symptoms of motion sickness. Avoid strong odors or stuffy environments that can worsen the condition.

  5. Medications: Over-the-counter medications, such as antihistamines or specific motion sickness medications, can be effective in preventing or reducing symptoms. However, it is essential to consult with a healthcare professional before taking any medication.

Conclusion

Motion sickness is a complex condition that affects many individuals to varying degrees. The mismatch between what our inner ear senses and what our eyes see can lead to feelings of nausea, dizziness, and discomfort. Understanding the causes of motion sickness, such as conflicting sensory signals and individual sensitivity, can help us employ preventive measures and cope with the symptoms more effectively. By following the tips mentioned above and being mindful of our body’s signals, we can enjoy our travels and activities without the interference of motion sickness.
