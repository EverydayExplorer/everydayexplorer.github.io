Why do we get brain freeze when eating something cold?


Have you ever experienced the sudden and intense pain in your forehead when eating something cold too quickly? If so, you are familiar with the phenomenon known as brain freeze, also called an ‘ice cream headache.’ Brain freeze is a common occurrence that has puzzled scientists and ice cream lovers alike for years. In this blog post, we will delve into the science behind brain freeze and why it happens when we consume cold treats.

What is brain freeze?

Brain freeze is a temporary headache that occurs when one consumes cold foods or beverages rapidly. It typically lasts for a few seconds to a couple of minutes but can be quite painful in some cases. The pain is usually felt in the forehead area but can also extend to the temples and the back of the head.

The science behind brain freeze

Despite its name, brain freeze doesn’t actually originate in the brain. It is a result of the blood vessels in the roof of your mouth and the back of your throat constricting and then rapidly dilating. The rapid constriction and subsequent dilation of these blood vessels trigger pain signals that are perceived as a headache.

When we eat something cold, the coldness comes into contact with the warm tissues in our mouth and throat. This sudden temperature change causes blood vessels in those areas to constrict, reducing blood flow. The body responds to this by sending more blood to the area, causing the blood vessels to rapidly dilate. It is this dilation that triggers the pain signals and leads to the brain freeze.

Why does brain freeze happen?

While the exact cause of brain freeze is not fully understood, researchers have proposed a few theories. One theory suggests that brain freeze is a protective mechanism to prevent our brains from getting too cold. By experiencing pain, we are prompted to slow down or stop consuming cold foods or beverages, preventing our brains from cooling down excessively.

Another theory suggests that brain freeze is a result of referred pain. Referred pain occurs when pain is felt in an area of the body that is not the source of the pain stimulus. In the case of brain freeze, the pain is felt in the forehead, although the stimulus is the cold sensation in the mouth and throat.

How to prevent brain freeze

Fortunately, there are several ways to prevent or at least minimize the occurrence of brain freeze:

  1. Slow down: One of the most effective ways to prevent brain freeze is to eat or drink cold substances slowly. By taking small, slow bites or sips, you allow your mouth and throat to adjust to the cold temperature gradually.

  2. Warm your palate: If you feel a brain freeze coming on, pressing your tongue to the roof of your mouth may help warm up the area and alleviate the pain.

  3. Avoid cold substances altogether: If you are particularly prone to brain freeze, it may be best to avoid consuming extremely cold foods or beverages. Opting for slightly warmer options can help reduce the likelihood of experiencing a brain freeze.

  4. Keep hydrated: Staying hydrated can help prevent brain freeze as dehydration can make you more susceptible to experiencing it.

Conclusion

Brain freeze, though temporary, can be quite uncomfortable and even painful. Understanding the science behind it can help us prevent or manage its occurrence. By eating cold foods slowly, pressing our tongues to the roof of our mouths, and staying hydrated, we can enjoy our favorite frozen treats without the dreaded brain freeze. So next time you indulge in an ice cream cone or sip on an icy beverage, remember to take it slow and savor the experience, minus the brain freeze!
