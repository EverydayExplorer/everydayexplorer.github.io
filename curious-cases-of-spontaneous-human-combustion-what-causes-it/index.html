Curious Cases of Spontaneous Human Combustion: What Causes It?


Spontaneous Human Combustion (SHC) has long been a subject of fascination and intrigue for scientists and the general public alike. The phenomenon, where a human body inexplicably catches fire and burns, leaving minimal damage to the surrounding environment, has been documented in a number of cases throughout history. Although rare, these cases have sparked numerous theories about the potential causes of SHC.

One of the earliest recorded cases of SHC dates back to the 16th century, when a Danish anatomist named Thomas Bartholin described the death of an Italian knight named Polonus Vorstius. According to Bartholin, Vorstius ‘went up in ashes and smoke’ while sitting in his chair, leaving only his legs and a skull intact. This case, along with many others that followed, led to the mystery and speculation surrounding SHC.

Before we dive into the potential causes of spontaneous human combustion, it’s important to note that SHC remains a controversial subject within the scientific community. Many experts debate the existence of SHC altogether, attributing the reported cases to more conventional explanations such as faulty electrical wiring, accidental fires, or even intentional acts of arson.

However, for those who believe in the phenomenon, several theories have been proposed to explain what causes SHC. Let’s explore some of these theories in more detail:

1. The Wick Effect: This theory posits that SHC is the result of a chain reaction known as the ‘wick effect.’ According to this theory, a flammable substance, such as alcohol or body fat, acts as a fuel source. Once ignited by an external heat source, the heat produced liquefies the body fat, which is then drawn into the victim’s clothing, effectively acting as a wick. The clothing then continues to burn, intensifying the fire and leading to the eventual destruction of the body. This theory has gained significant traction within the scientific community, with several documented cases showing evidence of the wick effect.

2. Spontaneous Ignition: Another theory suggests that SHC is caused by a build-up of internal heat within the body, leading to spontaneous ignition. Proponents of this theory argue that certain individuals may possess a higher concentration of flammable substances in their bodies, such as acetone or methane, which can fuel an internal fire. Additionally, factors like poor ventilation or alcohol consumption may contribute to the build-up of heat, making certain individuals more susceptible to spontaneous combustion.

3. External Factors: Some researchers believe that external factors, such as static electricity or unusual atmospheric conditions, may play a role in triggering SHC. For example, individuals who regularly work with flammable materials, such as oil or chemicals, may be at a higher risk due to their exposure to potential ignition sources. Similarly, lightning strikes or other electrical discharges could potentially ignite a person, resulting in spontaneous combustion.

4. Metabolic Disorders: Certain metabolic disorders, such as diabetes or alcoholism, have also been suggested as potential causes of SHC. These conditions can lead to an accumulation of flammable substances in the body, making individuals more prone to combustion. Additionally, the impaired circulation and nerve damage associated with these disorders may increase the likelihood of SHC by reducing the body’s ability to detect and respond to extremes in temperature.

It’s important to note that, despite numerous theories, no conclusive evidence has been found to definitively explain the causes of spontaneous human combustion. The rarity of cases and the difficulties in studying and replicating the phenomenon make it a challenging subject for scientific investigation.

As the scientific community continues to explore the curious cases of spontaneous human combustion, it’s vital to approach the subject with a skeptical yet open mind. While some theories may seem plausible, the lack of concrete evidence makes it challenging to draw definitive conclusions.

Whether you believe in the phenomenon or remain skeptical, there’s no denying the enduring mystery of spontaneous human combustion. As long as cases continue to be reported and theories continue to be proposed, the fascination with SHC and its exploration will persist, keeping this enigma alive in the realm of the unexplained.
