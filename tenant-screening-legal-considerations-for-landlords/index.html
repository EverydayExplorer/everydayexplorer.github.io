Tenant Screening: Legal Considerations for Landlords


As a landlord, finding reliable and responsible tenants is crucial for the success of your rental property. Tenant screening plays a vital role in ensuring that you are selecting tenants who will not only pay their rent on time but also maintain the property properly. However, it is essential to understand that there are legal considerations you must take into account when conducting tenant screening. This blog post will explore those considerations, ensuring that you stay within the boundaries of the law while choosing the best tenants for your property.

Fair Housing Laws

Fair housing laws are in place to prevent discrimination and ensure equal housing opportunities for everyone. As a landlord, it is imperative to comply with these laws during the tenant screening process. Discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, or disability is strictly prohibited.

When conducting tenant screening, you must treat all applicants equally and fairly, without any bias or prejudice. This means that you cannot reject a potential tenant solely based on their membership in a protected class. You must evaluate each applicant based on their individual qualifications and suitability as a tenant.

To ensure compliance with fair housing laws, it is recommended to develop a consistent set of screening criteria that focuses on objective factors such as income, credit history, rental history, and criminal background checks.

Written Tenant Screening Policy

Having a written tenant screening policy is essential for maintaining transparency and avoiding any potential legal disputes. Your screening policy should clearly outline the criteria you use to evaluate applicants and the process you follow when conducting tenant screening.

Include the following information in your policy:

  1. Screening criteria: Clearly define the criteria you use to evaluate prospective tenants. This may include factors such as income requirements, credit score threshold, rental history, and criminal background checks.
  2. Application process: Detail the steps involved in the application process, including the completion of an application form, submission of supporting documents, and any fees associated with the screening process.
  3. Timeframe: Establish a timeframe within which you will review applications and make a decision. This ensures that you do not inadvertently delay the process and violate fair housing laws.
  4. Written notice: Specify whether you will provide written notice to applicants regarding the status of their application, and if so, the format and timing of such notice.
  5. Adherence to fair housing laws: Emphasize that you adhere to fair housing laws and do not discriminate against any protected class.

By having a written tenant screening policy, you create a consistent and transparent process that protects both you as a landlord and the applicants.

Financial Considerations

Financial considerations are an essential part of tenant screening. While you want to ensure that your tenants have the financial capability to pay their rent on time, it is vital to tread carefully to avoid violating any laws.

Assessing an applicant’s financial situation can be done by evaluating their income, credit history, and references. However, keep the following legal considerations in mind:

Income Requirements

Setting income requirements is common practice among landlords to ensure that tenants can afford the rent. However, it is crucial to establish reasonable income requirements that are not discriminatory.

Avoid setting income requirements that disproportionately impact certain protected classes. For example, setting an income requirement that excludes applicants receiving government assistance may be considered discriminatory. Instead, focus on a reasonable income-to-rent ratio that ensures the applicant can comfortably afford the rent.

Credit History

Reviewing an applicant’s credit history is an essential step in tenant screening. However, it is important to understand the legal limitations imposed on landlords regarding credit checks.

Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), landlords must obtain written consent from applicants before obtaining their credit reports. Additionally, landlords must provide applicants with an adverse action notice if they decide to deny their application based on their credit history.

Ensure that you follow the proper procedures outlined in the FCRA to protect yourself from any potential legal issues related to credit checks.

Rental History

Contacting previous landlords for references is a common practice in tenant screening. However, it is essential to be mindful of how you obtain and use this information.

When contacting previous landlords, focus on gathering relevant information related to the applicant’s rental behavior, such as timely rent payments, property maintenance, and compliance with lease terms. Avoid asking questions that may indirectly discriminate, such as inquiring about the applicant’s family status or race.

Criminal Background Checks

Conducting criminal background checks is another crucial aspect of tenant screening. However, it is important to navigate this area carefully to comply with fair housing laws and avoid potential legal complications.

When conducting criminal background checks, consider the following:

  1. Uniform application of screening criteria: Apply the same screening criteria to all applicants without singling out specific individuals or protected classes. This ensures that you maintain consistency and fairness in the screening process.
  2. Relevance to rental property: Ensure that the criminal records you consider are relevant to the safety and well-being of the rental property and its occupants. Focus on convictions related to violent crimes, drug offenses, or property damage.
  3. Individual assessment: Instead of outright rejecting an applicant based on their criminal record, consider an individualized assessment. This involves reviewing the nature and severity of the offense, the time elapsed since the offense, and the rehabilitation efforts of the individual.
  4. Adverse action notice: If you decide to deny an applicant based on their criminal background, provide them with an adverse action notice. This notice should include the specific criminal conviction or record that influenced your decision and any opportunity for the applicant to dispute inaccuracies.

Conclusion

Tenant screening is a critical process for landlords to ensure that they select responsible and reliable tenants. However, it is important to conduct tenant screening within the boundaries of the law. By adhering to fair housing laws, developing a written tenant screening policy, considering financial factors carefully, and conducting criminal background checks appropriately, you can navigate the tenant screening process successfully while minimizing legal risks. Remember, treating all applicants equally and fairly is the key to avoiding discrimination and maintaining a harmonious landlord-tenant relationship.
