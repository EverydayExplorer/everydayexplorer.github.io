The Fascinating History of High Heels: From Function to Fashion


Through the centuries, high heels have become an iconic fashion statement and a symbol of power and femininity. From their humble beginnings as footwear for soldiers to their status as a staple in every fashionista’s wardrobe, the history of high heels is truly fascinating. In this blog post, we will delve into the evolution of high heels, from their functional roots to their transformation into a must-have fashion accessory.

Let’s start at the beginning. The origins of high heels can be traced back to ancient times, around 4000 BC. It is believed that the first high-heeled shoes were worn in Egypt by butchers to avoid stepping in the blood of animals. These early heels were made of wood and had a practical purpose rather than a fashionable one.

Fast forward to ancient Rome, and we see high heels taking on a different role. Both men and women wore high-heeled shoes called ‘kothorni’ to make themselves appear taller and more authoritative. These elevated shoes were not only a fashion statement but also a social status symbol, with the height of the heels indicating the wearer’s rank in society.

High heels regained popularity in Europe during the 16th century, especially among the aristocracy. It was during this time that Catherine de’ Medici, the queen consort of King Henry II of France, made high heels fashionable for women. She introduced the Italian style of heel to the French court and set a trend that would continue for centuries to come.

In the 17th century, high heels became a fashionable accessory for both men and women. The height of the heels grew to extravagant proportions, with some reaching up to 30 inches. Such extreme heights required the use of canes or servants to assist with walking. It was during this period that the term ‘well-heeled’ was coined, referring to someone who could afford these luxurious and impractical shoes.

As the 18th century arrived, high heels became associated primarily with women. This shift can be attributed to the rise of Enlightenment ideas and the desire to portray women as delicate and refined. The French Revolution further solidified the gender divide in footwear, with heels being seen as a symbol of the old regime. However, this did not deter women from wearing heels, as the allure of the fashion statement remained strong.

The 19th century brought a mix of styles, with heels evolving from slender and delicate to chunky and practical. The industrial revolution led to the mass production of shoes, making them more readily available to a wider range of people. Heels were now seen as a fashion choice for working-class women, rather than just the elite.

The 20th century saw high heels rise to new heights of popularity. In the 1920s, the iconic flapper style emerged, featuring low-heeled shoes known as ‘Louis heels.’ However, as the fashion industry evolved, high heels became synonymous with glamour and sensuality. The stiletto heel, introduced in the 1950s by designer Roger Vivier, became a symbol of femininity and sophistication.

High heels continued to be a staple in women’s fashion throughout the latter half of the 20th century. They were seen as a way to express individuality, boost confidence, and make a fashionable statement. However, the comfort versus style debate became more prominent, with many women experiencing discomfort and even health issues from wearing high heels on a regular basis.

In recent years, there has been a shift towards more comfortable shoe options, such as flats and sneakers. However, high heels still hold a special place in the hearts of fashion enthusiasts. They continue to be worn on special occasions, in the workplace, and as a form of self-expression.

In conclusion, the history of high heels is a captivating tale of function turning into fashion. From their practical origins in ancient times to their ever-evolving designs in modern society, high heels have become an integral part of our culture. Whether you love them or hate them, there’s no denying the impact that high heels have had on the world of fashion.
