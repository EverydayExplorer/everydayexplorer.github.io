From Grain to Glass: The Journey of Distilling Vodka


Vodka is one of the most popular spirits in the world today. It’s clean, smooth, and incredibly versatile. From cocktail bars to homes, individuals, and hospitality business owners alike can’t get enough of it. Have you ever stopped to think about the journey of your vodka? The real work that goes into turning grain into a tasteless, odorless, and colorless spirit that has swept the world by storm? Let’s take a look at the process of distilling vodka from grain to glass, and the key elements that make it the top spirit in the world today.

Step 1: Choosing the Right Grain

The basis of all great vodka begins with choosing the right grain. Traditionally, wheat, rye, and barley are the most commonly used grains in the process of distilling vodka. At the beginning of the process, the grain needs to be fermented. First, it’s mixed with water to create a mash, which is then heated and cooled until it reaches an ideal temperature for the addition of yeast. This enables the conversion of sugar to alcohol.

Step 2: The Fermentation Process

The next stage is the fermentation process. During this, the yeast reacts with the sugar in the mixture, and the resulting alcohol level increases. This process can take anywhere from several days to a few weeks. When the process of fermentation ends, the resulting liquid is called a wash. It is this wash that is used to make vodka.

Step 3: Distilling the Wash

Distilling is the process of separating the alcohol from the water in the wash. This is the crucial stage in turning low alcohol content wash into a high-alcohol content liquor. Two methods can be used in the distillation process: pot still or continuous column still.

When using the pot still method, the wash is heated in a kettle, and the vapor is collected in a cooled tube. As the vapor cools, it re-condenses, creating a liquid with higher alcohol content, called distillate. The distillate is then passed through the still, several times if necessary, until the desired strength is achieved.

In the case of a continuous column still, the wash is evaporated and remains in the column, forming different levels of alcoholic vapor. The vapors in this continuous process are separated in a manner that allows the distiller to produce higher alcohol content more efficiently.

Step 4: Purification

Once the distillation process is complete, it’s time to purify the vodka. The distillate needs to go back through a purification stage to remove impurities such as flavors and odors. A carbon filtration step is used to remove the impurities and pollutants, and the vodka is filtered at least two to three times.

Step 5: The Bottling Process

The final stage is the bottling process. Vodka is bottled in a colorless glass bottle, free of any branding or identifying details. The bottle is usually accompanied by a label with information on the distiller, the type of grain used, and sometimes with information on the processes involved in its manufacturing.

Conclusion:

From grain to glass, this is the journey of distilling vodka. While this process may seem straightforward, it’s a highly complex and detailed process that requires skill and precision. Each step of the process is crucial and influences the final product. The result is a clear, pure, and high alcohol content beverage that is enjoyed by millions around the globe. So, whether you prefer your vodka in cocktails or on the rocks, the distillation process that transforms grain to vodka is the backbone to this popular spirit.
