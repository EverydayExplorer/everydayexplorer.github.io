The Pros and Cons of Variable Life Insurance


Variable life insurance is a type of life insurance policy that allows policyholders to invest in various funds to grow their cash value. In this type of policy, the cash value is invested in a selection of investment options, such as stocks, bonds, and mutual funds. The policyholder can choose how much of their premium will be invested and in which investment options. While this type of insurance policy offers flexibility, it also comes with certain risks and limitations. In this blog, we will explore the pros and cons of variable life insurance.

Pros of Variable Life Insurance:

1. Investment Options: Variable life insurance allows policyholders to invest in various investment options, providing an opportunity for potentially higher returns than traditional insurance policies that have fixed cash values. This flexibility can also allow for more customized investment strategies tailored to the policyholder’s financial goals and risk tolerance.

2. Tax Benefits: Variable life insurance policies provide tax-deferred growth of the cash value. This means that the policyholder won’t have to pay taxes on the cash value of the policy until they withdraw it. Additionally, policyholders won’t have to pay taxes on death benefits paid out to beneficiaries, making it a tax-efficient tool for estate planning.

3. Flexibility: Unlike traditional insurance policies, variable life insurance policies allow policyholders to change their premiums, death benefit, and investment options over time. This can be particularly useful for individuals with changing financial circumstances or goals.

4. Creditor Protection: In some states, variable life insurance policies provide creditor protection. This means that creditors cannot legally seize the cash value of the policy to pay off outstanding debts.

Cons of Variable Life Insurance:

1. Investment Risk: Variable life insurance policies are subject to market fluctuations and investment risk, meaning the cash value of the policy can increase or decrease based on the performance of the chosen funds. This can be a drawback for individuals who prefer stable returns and don’t want to take on investment risk.

2. Fees: Variable life insurance policies typically have higher fees than traditional insurance policies due to the investment component. Fees to consider include mortality and expense fees, administrative fees, and fund expenses. These fees can eat into the policy’s cash value and returns over time.

3. Complex Structure: Variable life insurance policies can be complex, with many investment options and potential risks and fees to consider. It’s important for policyholders to understand the structure of the policy and the potential risks before investing.

4. Limited Guarantees: Unlike traditional insurance policies, variable life insurance policies offer limited guarantees. The death benefit is guaranteed, but the cash value is subject to investment risk and is not guaranteed. Additionally, policyholders are responsible for managing their investments, which can be a burden for individuals who don’t have the time or expertise to do so.

In conclusion, variable life insurance can be a useful tool for individuals looking for an investment component in their life insurance policy. However, it’s important to consider the potential risks and fees before investing. For those who value flexibility and potential for higher returns, variable life insurance may be a good option. On the other hand, individuals who prefer stable returns and lower fees may be better suited for traditional insurance policies. It’s important to consult a financial advisor to determine which type of policy is best suited for your individual needs and goals.
