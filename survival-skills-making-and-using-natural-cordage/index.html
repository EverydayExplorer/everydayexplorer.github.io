Survival Skills: Making and Using Natural Cordage


In the wild, where resources may be scarce, survival skills are essential for anyone venturing into the great outdoors. One crucial skill to learn is making and using natural cordage. Cordage refers to any type of rope or string, and having the ability to create it from natural materials can be a game-changer in a survival situation. In this blog post, we will explore the art of making and using natural cordage, providing you with the knowledge to navigate through nature’s challenges.

Before we dive into the intricacies of creating natural cordage, let’s understand why it is such a valuable skill. In survival scenarios, cordage can be used for various purposes. You can fashion it into traps for catching food, create fishing lines to reel in your dinner, construct shelters, tie up gear, or even make tools. The applications are limited only by your imagination.

To start, you need to gather suitable materials. In nature, plants such as stinging nettles, dogbane, yucca, or even tree bark can provide strong fibers for cordage. Take care to select plants that have long, flexible fibers that can withstand tension. You’ll want to avoid using plants with brittle or short fibers, as they won’t hold up well when twisted into cordage.

Once you have your chosen plant material, the next step is to process it. Begin by removing any leaves, thorns, or other unwanted parts. Then, using a technique called retting, you can separate the fibers from the plant stalk. Retting involves soaking the plant material in water to break down the non-fibrous components, making it easier to extract the fibers. This process may take several days, so patience is key.

After retting, the fibers need to be extracted. This can be done by gently rubbing the stalks between your hands or by using a wooden stick to strip the fibers away. The goal is to have long, clean fibers that are ready for twisting.

Now comes the fun part: twisting the fibers into cordage. Take a handful of fibers and roll them between your palms, applying gentle pressure. As you roll, the fibers will start to twist together, forming a stronger and more durable cord. Continue this twisting motion until you have reached the desired thickness and length.

To add strength to your cordage, you can create multiple strands and twist them together. This process is called plaiting. By intertwining several twisted strands, you can create a thicker and more robust rope. Plaiting also adds an aesthetic touch to your cordage, making it visually appealing.

Once you have your natural cordage, it’s time to put it to use. Whether you need to secure a tarp over your shelter, create a snare to catch small game, or fashion a fishing line, your cordage will be indispensable. Remember to test the strength of your cordage before relying on it for critical tasks, as the quality of your workmanship will determine its effectiveness.

In conclusion, mastering the art of making and using natural cordage is an essential survival skill. By learning to identify suitable plant materials, processing fibers, and twisting them into cordage, you gain a versatile tool that can aid you in various wilderness scenarios. So, next time you find yourself in the great outdoors, take a moment to connect with nature and harness its resources to create your own cordage. It might just be the lifeline you need in a survival situation.
