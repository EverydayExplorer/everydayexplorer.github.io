Why do we feel weight when we jump? The science behind it


When we jump, we are often amazed by the sensation of weightlessness we experience as our bodies momentarily leave the ground. However, upon landing, we immediately feel the impact and weight of our bodies returning to the Earth. But have you ever wondered why we feel this weight when we jump? In this blog post, we will explore the science behind this phenomenon and shed light on the forces at play.

The Force of Gravity

To understand why we feel weight when we jump, we must first grasp the concept of gravity. Gravity is the force that attracts objects towards each other. On Earth, we experience gravity as the force that keeps us grounded and gives us weight. The Earth’s gravity pulls us towards its center, giving us our sense of weight and keeping our feet on the ground.

Jumping and Counteracting Gravity

When we jump, we exert force against the ground to propel ourselves upwards. At the moment of takeoff, the force we exert on the ground is greater than the force of gravity acting upon us. As a result, we are able to overcome gravity and lift off the ground.

However, as soon as we leave the ground, the force of gravity begins to exert its influence. Gravity pulls us back towards the Earth with an acceleration of approximately 9.8 meters per second squared. This acceleration causes us to experience a feeling of weight even while we are in mid-air.

Freefall and Terminal Velocity

During the time we are in mid-air after jumping, we enter a state of freefall. Freefall occurs when an object is only influenced by gravity, with no other forces acting upon it. During freefall, we appear weightless because the force of gravity is the only force affecting our bodies.

However, as we continue to fall, another force known as air resistance comes into play. Air resistance is the frictional force exerted by the air on a moving object. As we fall faster, air resistance increases and eventually becomes equal to the force of gravity. At this point, we reach our terminal velocity.

Terminal velocity is the maximum velocity an object can reach when falling through a fluid, such as air. At terminal velocity, the force of gravity pulling us down is balanced by the force of air resistance pushing against us. As a result, we no longer accelerate and continue to fall at a constant speed. At terminal velocity, we once again feel the sensation of weight because the force of gravity is equal to the force exerted by air resistance.

Impact and Return to Earth

Finally, when we land after a jump, we experience a forceful impact with the ground. This impact occurs because of the force exerted on our bodies as we collide with the Earth’s surface. The greater the height of our jump, the stronger the impact upon landing.

Upon landing, our muscles and joints absorb the impact force, allowing us to safely return to the ground. This is why we feel the weight of our bodies as we make contact with the Earth again. The force exerted by the ground on our bodies is greater than the force of gravity, leading to the sensation of weight.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the feeling of weight when we jump can be attributed to the force of gravity constantly acting upon our bodies. While we may experience a moment of weightlessness during freefall, the force of gravity and air resistance ultimately bring us back to Earth. The impact upon landing further reinforces the sensation of weight. Understanding the science behind why we feel weight when we jump allows us to appreciate the forces at play and marvel at the wonders of physics.
