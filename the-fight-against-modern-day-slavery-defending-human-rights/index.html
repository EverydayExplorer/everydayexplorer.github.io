The Fight Against Modern-Day Slavery: Defending Human Rights


In today’s world, it is hard to believe that slavery still exists. The word might conjure up images of shackled African slaves being shipped across the Atlantic in the 18th century, but the truth is that modern-day slavery thrives in many forms and is closer than we think. Millions of people worldwide are affected by slavery, and it is a grave violation of human rights that needs to be brought to light.

Modern-day slavery or human trafficking is defined as the recruitment, transportation, transfer, harboring, or receipt of persons by means of threat, use of force or other forms of coercion, abduction, fraud, deception, abuse of power or vulnerability, and giving or receiving payments or benefits to achieve the consent of a person having control over another person for the purpose of exploitation. Exploitation could take the form of forced labor, sexual exploitation, domestic servitude, forced begging, or child labor. It is a global problem, affecting every country in the world, including developed countries.

According to the International Labour Organization (ILO), an estimated 40.3 million people were living in modern slavery on any given day in 2016. This includes 24.9 million people in forced labor and 15.4 million in forced marriage. Women and girls account for over 70% of victims, and one in four victims of modern slavery are children. The numbers may seem staggering, but they only represent the tip of the iceberg. As victims are often hidden from plain sight, it is challenging to identify and prosecute perpetrators.

Several factors contribute to the persistence of modern slavery, including poverty, conflict, discrimination, and lack of education. People living in poverty are vulnerable to manipulation and deceit by traffickers promising better lives abroad or in the city. They may be forced into debt bondage, where they work to pay off money borrowed from their employers and are never able to leave. Armed conflicts or natural disasters can displace people from their homes, forcing them into situations where they are at risk of being trafficked. Women and girls are particularly affected by gender-based violence, including sexual exploitation and forced marriage. Lack of education can also perpetuate slavery, as it limits people’s ability to seek decent work and recognize the signs of exploitation.

Awareness and prevention are key to fighting modern-day slavery. Governments, civil society, and the private sector must work together to identify, investigate, and prosecute individuals and organizations involved in human trafficking. The United Nations has set up the Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) to provide technical assistance and support to countries in implementing the Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons, Especially Women and Children. The protocol aims to prevent human trafficking, protect victims, and bring perpetrators to justice.

Another critical aspect of the fight against modern slavery is protecting victims and providing them with support. Victims need access to medical care, counseling, and legal aid to recover from their traumatic experiences and rebuild their lives. Additionally, prevention programs should focus on education and awareness-raising activities aimed at empowering vulnerable populations.

The private sector has a role to play in the fight against modern slavery, with businesses taking steps to ensure that their operations and supply chains are free from exploitative practices. This includes conducting due diligence assessments, monitoring suppliers, and implementing ethical sourcing policies. By adopting these measures, companies can help prevent human trafficking and reduce the demand for forced labor.

In conclusion, modern-day slavery is a grave violation of human rights that requires a concerted effort from governments, civil society, and the private sector. The fight against slavery involves recognizing the problem, raising awareness, preventing victimization, prosecuting perpetrators, and supporting victims. By working together, we can ensure that every individual’s right to freedom and dignity is respected and protected.
