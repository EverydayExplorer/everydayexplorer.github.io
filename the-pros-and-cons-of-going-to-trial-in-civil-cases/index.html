The Pros and Cons of Going to Trial in Civil Cases


When individuals or businesses find themselves involved in a civil dispute, one of the most important decisions they will have to make is whether to go to trial or explore alternative methods of dispute resolution. Going to trial in a civil case can have its pros and cons, and it’s crucial to understand them before making a choice. In this article, we will explore the advantages and disadvantages of going to trial in civil cases.

Pros of Going to Trial

  1. Opportunity for a favorable outcome: Going to trial provides parties with an opportunity to present their case before a judge or jury, who will then make a determination based on the evidence and legal arguments presented. If you believe you have a strong case with substantial evidence, going to trial can increase your chances of obtaining a favorable outcome.

  2. Complete resolution: Trials offer a definitive and final resolution to a dispute. Once the trial is concluded and a judgment is made, the parties can move on knowing that a decision has been reached. This clarity can be beneficial, especially in complex cases where alternative dispute resolution methods may not provide the same level of finality.

  3. Public scrutiny and accountability: Trials are conducted in open court, allowing for public scrutiny and providing an opportunity to hold individuals or entities accountable for their actions. This public display of proceedings can have a significant impact on the reputation of the parties involved and encourage accountability.

  4. Opponent’s evidence exposure: Going to trial allows for the exposure of the opposing party’s evidence. This can be beneficial if you believe the other party has weak arguments or lacks substantial evidence. By forcing the other party to present their case, you may uncover weaknesses or inconsistencies that could strengthen your own position.

  5. Precedent-setting decisions: In some cases, a trial can result in a precedent-setting decision that can shape future legal interpretations and outcomes. This can be particularly advantageous if you are seeking to establish or clarify legal principles.

Cons of Going to Trial

  1. Time-consuming and costly: Trials can be lengthy and costly affairs. They require substantial preparation, including gathering evidence, hiring expert witnesses, and extensive legal representation. Furthermore, the court’s schedule and availability can significantly impact the duration of the trial, resulting in delays and increased expenses.

  2. Uncertain outcome: While trials offer an opportunity for a favorable outcome, there is always an element of uncertainty. Judges and juries are human, and their decisions can be influenced by various factors. Even with a strong case, there is no guarantee of success, making trials risky endeavors.

  3. Lack of control: Once a case goes to trial, control over the outcome shifts from the parties to the judge or jury. This loss of control can be frustrating for parties who wish to have a say in the resolution of their dispute. Moreover, the rules of evidence and procedure can restrict how parties present their case, further limiting control.

  4. Emotional toll: Trials can be emotionally draining for all involved parties. Testifying and being cross-examined can be highly stressful and intimidating. Additionally, the adversarial nature of trials can further heighten tension and animosity between the parties, potentially leading to strained relationships.

  5. Appeal process: If a party is not satisfied with the outcome of a trial, they may choose to appeal the decision, extending the litigation process further. Appeals can be time-consuming and add additional costs to the already burdensome financial burden associated with going to trial.

In conclusion, going to trial in a civil case has both pros and cons. While trials offer the opportunity for a favorable outcome, complete resolution, and public accountability, they are also time-consuming, costly, and uncertain. The decision to go to trial should not be taken lightly and should be carefully considered in light of the specific circumstances of the case.
